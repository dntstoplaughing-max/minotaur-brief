

ARGUMENT

This Court should find that Duquesne University acted with deliberate indifference to known acts of sexual harassment in its programs or activities, in violation of Title IX. As established in Davis v. Monroe County Board of Education, 526 U.S. 629 (1999), a funding recipient is liable for student-on-student harassment where it acts with deliberate indifference to known harassment that is severe, pervasive, and objectively offensive enough to deny access to educational opportunities. (Davis, 526 U.S. at 629).

Here, Plaintiff David Mullins reported harassment by a fellow graduate student to Dean Wasilko on June 30, 2025, describing a physical pursuit and confrontation, and requesting emergency housing assistance. (Exhibit B-0). This report provided Duquesne University with actual knowledge of the harassment, as defined in Exhibit Z-e. (Exhibit Z-e, Slide 30). Furthermore, Dean Wasilko's response, advising Mullins to file a police report and stating that the situation "doesn't sound like a code of conduct violation," demonstrates a clearly unreasonable response to the known harassment. (Exhibit B-0). This response is particularly egregious given the severity of the harassment, which included physical pursuit, confrontation, and a threat to Mullins' safety. (Exhibit D).

Despite Mullins' repeated requests for supportive measures, including emergency housing and a no-contact order, Duquesne University failed to provide any meaningful assistance. (Exhibits B-5, B-5A). In fact, Title IX Coordinator Alicia Simpson's response to Mullins' requests was to propose a meeting to discuss his concerns, rather than taking immediate action to address the harassment. (Exhibit B-5). This response is inconsistent with the mandatory obligations under 34 C.F.R. § 106.44(a), which requires the university to promptly contact the complainant to discuss supportive measures, consider the complainant's wishes, inform the complainant of the availability of supportive measures, and explain the process for filing a formal complaint. (Exhibit Z-c, Slide 38).

Moreover, Simpson's subsequent reversal of her jurisdictional determination, stating that the Title IX Office does not have jurisdiction over Mullins' report, was clearly unreasonable in light of the known circumstances. (Exhibit B-6). This reversal was made without any new factual information about the underlying complaint, and was instead prompted by Mullins' protected activity under 34 C.F.R. § 106.71. (Exhibits B-5A, B-6). This constitutes retaliation, which is prohibited under Title IX. (Exhibit Z-b, Slide 64).

The university's failure to provide supportive measures, combined with its clearly unreasonable response to the known harassment, demonstrates deliberate indifference to Mullins' safety and well-being. As established in Hall v. Millersville University, No. 19-3275 (3d Cir. Jan. 11, 2022), a federal funding recipient has adequate notice under Title IX that it may face monetary liability for deliberate indifference to known sexual harassment perpetrated by a non-student where the recipient exercises substantial control over the context and the harasser. (Hall, No. 19-3275). Here, Duquesne University exercised substantial control over the context of the harassment, as it occurred in a university-owned apartment and involved a fellow graduate student. (Exhibit D).

In conclusion, Duquesne University's deliberate indifference to known acts of sexual harassment in its programs or activities, as demonstrated by its clearly unreasonable response to Mullins' reports and its failure to provide supportive measures, constitutes a violation of Title IX. This Court should find in favor of Plaintiff David Mullins and hold Duquesne University liable for its deliberate indifference to the known harassment.
