

ARGUMENT

This Court should find that Duquesne University retaliated against Plaintiff David Mullins in violation of Title IX, 20 U.S.C. § 1681. The evidence demonstrates that Mullins engaged in protected activity, suffered adverse actions, and that a causal connection exists between the two.

First, Mullins engaged in protected activity by reporting sex-based harassment, opposing the University's handling of his complaint, and participating in the investigation process. (Exhibits B-0A, B-3A). As the Third Circuit has held, "Demanding compliance with Title IX is protected activity." Atkinson v. Lafayette College, 460 F.3d 447, 453 (3d Cir. 2006). Mullins's actions, including his OCR complaint and federal lawsuit, are protected under Title IX. (Exhibits B-3A, ECF docket).

Second, the University took adverse actions against Mullins that would deter a reasonable person from engaging in protected activity. The University's actions include removing OCR from the CC line on emails (Exhibit B-3), reversing a jurisdictional determination after Mullins engaged in protected activity (Exhibit B-6), threatening Mullins with conduct charges for emailing University officials about his safety concerns (Exhibit B-7), and revoking his Zoom access (Exhibit L). These actions, particularly the revocation of Zoom access, would deter a reasonable person from reporting sex-based harassment or opposing the University's handling of a complaint.

Third, a causal connection exists between Mullins's protected activity and the adverse actions. The temporal proximity between Mullins's protected activity and the adverse actions is unusually suggestive of retaliation. (Jalil v. Avdel Corp., 873 F.2d 701, 706 (3d Cir. 1989)). For example, the University removed OCR from the CC line on emails just two days after Mullins filed his OCR complaint (Exhibit B-3). Similarly, the University revoked Mullins's Zoom access just two days after he testified at the TRO hearing (Exhibit L). This temporal proximity, combined with the pattern of antagonism exhibited by the University, gives rise to the inference that the adverse actions were retaliatory. (Kachmar v. SunGard Data Systems, Inc., 109 F.3d 173, 177 (3d Cir. 1997)).

Furthermore, the University's inconsistent explanations for its actions provide additional evidence of retaliation. (Farrell v. Planters Lifesavers Co., 206 F.3d 271, 278 (3d Cir. 2000)). For example, Selcer's email to Mullins on February 13, 2026, fabricated "performance issues" and threatened Mullins's stipend, despite Faculty B's email just hours earlier requesting reassignment without mentioning performance issues (Exhibits X-7, X-8). This inconsistency suggests that the University's actions were motivated by a desire to retaliate against Mullins for his protected activity.

In conclusion, the evidence demonstrates that Duquesne University retaliated against Mullins in violation of Title IX. The University's actions, including removing OCR from the CC line on emails, reversing a jurisdictional determination, threatening Mullins with conduct charges, and revoking his Zoom access, would deter a reasonable person from engaging in protected activity. The temporal proximity between Mullins's protected activity and the adverse actions, combined with the pattern of antagonism and inconsistent explanations, gives rise to the inference that the adverse actions were retaliatory.
