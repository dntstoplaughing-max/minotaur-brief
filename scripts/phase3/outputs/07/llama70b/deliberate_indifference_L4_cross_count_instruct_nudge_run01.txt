

**ARGUMENT**

**COUNT I: DELIBERATE INDIFFERENCE TO SEXUAL HARASSMENT UNDER TITLE IX**

Plaintiff David Mullins brings this claim against Defendant Duquesne University of the Holy Spirit, alleging deliberate indifference to known sexual harassment in violation of Title IX. The evidence demonstrates that Duquesne University had actual knowledge of the harassment, failed to respond promptly and effectively, and thereby denied Plaintiff equal access to educational opportunities.

**Actual Knowledge**

Duquesne University had actual knowledge of the harassment through various reports and communications. On June 30, 2025, Plaintiff reported the harassment to Dean Adam Wasilko, describing a physical pursuit and confrontation by his roommate, Respondent (Exhibit B-0). On July 24, 2025, Plaintiff sent a formal Title IX escalation to Alicia Simpson, the Title IX Coordinator, citing 34 C.F.R. § 106.44(a) and demanding supportive measures (SAC ¶¶ 168-172). These reports triggered Duquesne University's duty to respond under Title IX.

**Clearly Unreasonable Response (Deliberate Indifference)**

Duquesne University's response to the harassment was clearly unreasonable under the known circumstances. Despite Plaintiff's repeated requests for supportive measures, the University failed to provide any meaningful assistance. On August 13, 2025, Simpson acknowledged that Plaintiff's concerns warranted further review, but then reversed her jurisdictional determination the next day, stating that the Title IX Office did not have jurisdiction over the report (Exhibits B-5 and B-6). This reversal was not based on any new factual information, but rather on Plaintiff's refusal to attend a meeting (Exhibits B-5A, B-5B, B-5C). This response was deliberately indifferent, as it ignored Plaintiff's safety concerns and failed to provide any supportive measures.

The Third Circuit has held that a university's response to sexual harassment is deliberately indifferent if it is "clearly unreasonable in light of the known circumstances." (Foster v. Board of Regents of University of Michigan, No. 19-1314 (6th Cir. Dec. 11, 2020) (en banc)). In this case, Duquesne University's response was clearly unreasonable, as it failed to provide any supportive measures or take any meaningful action to address the harassment.

**Severe, Pervasive, Objectively Offensive Harassment**

The harassment Plaintiff experienced was severe, pervasive, and objectively offensive. The Signal transcript and timeline (Exhibit D) demonstrate a pattern of harassment and intimidation by Respondent, including locking Plaintiff out of the apartment, following him, and making threatening comments. This behavior was not only severe and pervasive but also objectively offensive, as it created a hostile and intimidating environment for Plaintiff.

**Denial of Equal Educational Access**

Duquesne University's deliberate indifference to the harassment denied Plaintiff equal access to educational opportunities. Plaintiff was forced to flee his home and seek temporary housing, which disrupted his academic work and caused significant emotional distress (Exhibit A). The University's failure to provide supportive measures, such as a no-contact order or registrar separation, further exacerbated the situation and denied Plaintiff equal access to educational opportunities.

The Supreme Court has held that a university's deliberate indifference to known harassment can deny students equal access to educational opportunities. (Davis v. Monroe County Board of Education, 526 U.S. 629 (1999)). In this case, Duquesne University's deliberate indifference to the harassment denied Plaintiff equal access to educational opportunities, in violation of Title IX.

**CONCLUSION**

In conclusion, Duquesne University's deliberate indifference to known sexual harassment denied Plaintiff equal access to educational opportunities, in violation of Title IX. The evidence demonstrates that the University had actual knowledge of the harassment, failed to respond promptly and effectively, and thereby denied Plaintiff equal access to educational opportunities. Therefore, Plaintiff is entitled to relief under Title IX.
