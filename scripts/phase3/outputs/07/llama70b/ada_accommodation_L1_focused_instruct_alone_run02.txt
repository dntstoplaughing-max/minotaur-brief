

ARGUMENT

Count III of this complaint alleges that Defendant Duquesne University of the Holy Spirit ("Duquesne University") failed to accommodate Plaintiff David Mullins' disability in violation of Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act and the Americans with Disabilities Act ("ADA"). The undisputed facts demonstrate that Duquesne University had notice of Plaintiff's disability and his request for a reasonable accommodation, yet failed to engage in the interactive process and ultimately denied his request.

First, Plaintiff is a qualified individual with a disability. Exhibit F, a medical letter from Plaintiff's treating physician, Treating Physician, confirms that Plaintiff has a reasonable fear of a specific individual on campus and that this fear is likely to worsen his mental health conditions. (Exhibit F, Aug. 13, 2025). This medical documentation establishes that Plaintiff has a disability within the meaning of the ADA and Section 504.

Second, Plaintiff provided notice of his disability and his need for accommodation. On August 25, 2025, Plaintiff met with Defendant Selcer and explained that he did not feel safe on campus due to his fear of Respondent. (Exhibit I, Aug. 25, 2025). Plaintiff requested specific safety measures, including a no-contact order, a campus safety escort, and registrar separation. (Exhibit I, Aug. 25, 2025). This meeting and subsequent email exchange put Duquesne University on notice of Plaintiff's disability and his need for accommodation.

Third, Plaintiff requested a reasonable accommodation. Plaintiff requested Zoom access for class participation as a medically necessary accommodation. (Exhibit I, Aug. 25, 2025). This request was reasonable, as Zoom access is already in active use in the same department by Faculty D for comparable graduate coursework. (Exhibit N, Faculty D Syllabus).

Fourth, Duquesne University denied Plaintiff's request for accommodation. Despite Plaintiff's repeated requests, Defendant Selcer imposed a three-week countdown on Plaintiff's Zoom access, after which Plaintiff would be required to attend in person regardless of medical documentation. (Exhibit I, Aug. 25, 2025). This conditional, time-limited measure was designed to expire and coerce meeting attendance, rather than provide a genuine accommodation. (Exhibit I-1, Sep. 2, 2025).

Fifth, Duquesne University's denial of accommodation resulted in harm to Plaintiff. Plaintiff was forced to participate in classes remotely, which limited his access to departmental offices, the library, and in-person interactions with colleagues and faculty. (Exhibit I-1, Sep. 2, 2025). This denial of accommodation also caused Plaintiff significant stress and anxiety, exacerbating his mental health conditions. (Exhibit F, Oct. 1, 2025).

The Third Circuit has held that an employer who fails to engage in the interactive process and denies a reasonable accommodation may be liable under the ADA. Taylor v. Phoenixville School District, 184 F.3d 296, 313 (3d Cir. 1999). In this case, Duquesne University failed to engage in the interactive process, as required by Taylor. Defendant Selcer did not assess Plaintiff's specific limitations, evaluate possible accommodations, discuss alternatives with Plaintiff, or involve disability services. (SJ 7.2).

Moreover, the Supreme Court has held that ADA and Section 504 claims in educational settings use the same standards as claims in other disability discrimination contexts. A.J.T. v. Osseo Area Schools, 605 U.S. ___ (2025). Therefore, Duquesne University's denial of accommodation must be evaluated under the ordinary failure-to-accommodate analysis, without a heightened bad faith or gross misjudgment standard.

In conclusion, the undisputed facts demonstrate that Duquesne University failed to accommodate Plaintiff's disability in violation of Section 504 and the ADA. Duquesne University had notice of Plaintiff's disability and his request for a reasonable accommodation, yet failed to engage in the interactive process and ultimately denied his request. This denial of accommodation resulted in harm to Plaintiff, and Duquesne University is liable for its failure to comply with federal disability law.
