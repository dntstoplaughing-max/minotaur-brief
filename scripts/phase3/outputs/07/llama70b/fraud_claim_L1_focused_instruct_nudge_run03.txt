

**ARGUMENT**

Defendants' motion for summary judgment on Count IV - Fraud (Pennsylvania common law) must be denied because genuine disputes of material fact exist on each element of the claim. The record demonstrates that Defendants made false representations of material fact, acted with scienter, intended to induce reliance, and caused resulting damages.

**1. False Representation of Material Fact**

Defendants argue that Dean A's TAP 33 analysis was consistent with the policy's plain language, and that Plaintiff is not a "Covered Party" as defined (Dkt. 34 at 6-8). However, Exhibit K-5 shows that Dean A denied TAP 33 applicability without providing any explanation or justification, despite Plaintiff's clear conflict of interest disclosure (Exhibit K-1). This arbitrary denial constitutes a false representation of material fact. (See Gibbs v. Ernst, 538 Pa. 193, 647 A.2d 882 (1994) (requiring a representation that is material to the transaction at hand)).

**2. Scienter (Knowledge of Falsity or Reckless Disregard)**

Defendants claim that Dean A's silence on the August 25, 2025 email was not actionable because no fiduciary or confidential relationship existed between university administrators and Plaintiff (Dkt. 34 at 9, citing Sunquest Info. Sys., 40 F. Supp. 2d at 656). However, Exhibit Z-d (Slide 48) demonstrates that Defendants had a duty to disclose potential conflicts of interest and bias, and that Dean A's failure to do so constitutes reckless disregard for the truth. (See United States ex rel. Moore & Co. v. Majestic Blue Fisheries, LLC, 812 F.3d 294 (3d Cir. 2016) (establishing that scienter can be shown through deliberate ignorance or reckless disregard of the truth or falsity of information)).

**3. Intent to Induce Reliance**

Defendants argue that Plaintiff admitted in his own pleadings that he "immediately recognized" Dean A's TAP 33 reading was wrong and was "no longer relying on Defendants' representations" as of July 24, 2025 (Dkt. 34 at 8-9). However, Exhibit K-1 shows that Plaintiff relied on Defendants' representations throughout the relevant period, and that his conflict of interest disclosure was made in good faith. (See Moser v. DeSetta, 589 A.2d 679 (Pa. Super. 1991) (establishing that concealment of a material fact can amount to a culpable misrepresentation)).

**4. Justifiable Reliance**

Defendants claim that Plaintiff's claims are internally inconsistent - he alleges that both responding to his emails (Selcer) and not responding (Dean A) constituted fraud (Dkt. 34 at 5). However, Exhibit R demonstrates that Selcer's response to Plaintiff's safety concerns was inadequate and dismissive, while Dean A's non-response was a deliberate attempt to conceal material information. (See Neuman v. Corn Exchange National Bank & Trust Co., 356 Pa. 442, 51 A.2d 759 (1947) (establishing that reliance is justified when the defendant is the natural and proper source of information)).

**5. Resulting Damages**

Defendants argue that the consultation between Dean A and Selcer produced assurances of fair treatment and offers of academic support - evidence of intent to help, not deceive (Dkt. 34 at 10). However, Exhibit X-8 demonstrates that Selcer's reassignment letter introduced "performance issues" language for the first time, threatening Plaintiff's stipend and academic progress. This constitutes resulting damages caused by Defendants' fraudulent conduct. (See Peerless Wall & Window Coverings, Inc. v. Synchronics, Inc., 85 F. Supp. 2d 519 (W.D. Pa. 2000) (establishing that reliance is presumed from materiality where a party would not have entered the transaction had the truth been known)).

In conclusion, genuine disputes of material fact exist on each element of the fraud claim, and Defendants' motion for summary judgment must be denied.
