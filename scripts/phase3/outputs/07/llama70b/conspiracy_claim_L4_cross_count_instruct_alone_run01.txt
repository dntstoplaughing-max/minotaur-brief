

ARGUMENT

Defendants' motion for summary judgment on Count V — Civil Conspiracy (Pennsylvania common law and 42 U.S.C. § 1985) is denied. Genuine disputes of material fact preclude summary judgment.

I. Agreement between two or more actors

Defendants argue that Blair's consultation with Selcer was routine academic administration (Dkt. 34 at 10). However, Exhibit K-2 reveals that Blair's consultation with Selcer was not routine, as she explicitly stated that "both Dr. Selcer and I strongly concur" on the decision to keep Plaintiff enrolled in the required teaching seminar. This joint determination is evidence of an agreement between Blair and Selcer, which is a necessary element of a civil conspiracy claim. (Thompson Coal Co. v. Pike Coal Co., 488 Pa. 198, 412 A.2d 466, 472-73 (1979)).

Moreover, Exhibit K-5 shows that Blair denied the applicability of TAP 33, which governs conflicts of interest, despite Plaintiff's clear disclosure of a conflict of interest involving Selcer. This denial is evidence of a deliberate attempt to circumvent university policies and procedures, further supporting the existence of an agreement between Blair and Selcer.

II. Unlawful act or unlawful means

Defendants argue that no underlying tort exists to support the conspiracy because Blair's TAP 33 analysis was correct on the policy's plain terms (Dkt. 34 at 10). However, Exhibit K-5 reveals that Blair's analysis was flawed, as she incorrectly determined that Plaintiff was not a "Covered Party" under TAP 33. This error is evidence of an unlawful act, as it demonstrates a deliberate attempt to disregard university policies and procedures.

Furthermore, Exhibit B-6 shows that Simpson disclaimed jurisdiction over Plaintiff's Title IX complaint without any legal basis, which is an unlawful act that supports the conspiracy claim.

III. Overt acts in furtherance

Defendants argue that Selcer's continuation as Plaintiff's instructor was not retaliatory because he held that role before and after Plaintiff's protected activity (Dkt. 34 at 10 n.3). However, Exhibit K-2 reveals that Blair and Selcer jointly determined that Plaintiff should remain enrolled in the required teaching seminar, despite Plaintiff's clear disclosure of a conflict of interest involving Selcer. This joint determination is an overt act in furtherance of the conspiracy, as it demonstrates a deliberate attempt to disregard university policies and procedures.

Moreover, Exhibit X-8 shows that Selcer fabricated "performance issues" raised by two faculty members, which is an overt act in furtherance of the conspiracy.

IV. Malice (legal or actual)

Defendants argue that the consultation between Blair and Selcer evidenced intent to help, not malice (Dkt. 34 at 10). However, Exhibit K-2 reveals that Blair and Selcer jointly determined that Plaintiff should remain enrolled in the required teaching seminar, despite Plaintiff's clear disclosure of a conflict of interest involving Selcer. This joint determination is evidence of malice, as it demonstrates a deliberate attempt to disregard university policies and procedures.

Furthermore, Exhibit X-8 shows that Selcer fabricated "performance issues" raised by two faculty members, which is evidence of malice.

V. Resulting harm

Defendants argue that Plaintiff's characterization of coordinated institutional responses as "conspiracy" reflects his refusal to engage with well-established university processes, not evidence of unlawful agreement (Dkt. 34 at 6). However, Exhibit B-3A reveals that Plaintiff escalated his concerns to OCR, which is evidence of his good-faith effort to engage with university processes.

Moreover, Exhibit F shows that Plaintiff suffered emotional distress and academic disruption as a result of the conspiracy, which is evidence of resulting harm.

CONCLUSION

In conclusion, genuine disputes of material fact preclude summary judgment on Count V — Civil Conspiracy (Pennsylvania common law and 42 U.S.C. § 1985). Defendants' motion for summary judgment is denied.
