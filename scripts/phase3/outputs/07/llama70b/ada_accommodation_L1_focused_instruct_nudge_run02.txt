

ARGUMENT

Count III: Failure to Accommodate under Section 504 and the ADA

Duquesne University's denial of Plaintiff's request for a reasonable accommodation, specifically Zoom access for class participation, constitutes a violation of Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act and the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA). The University's actions demonstrate a failure to engage in the interactive process, a lack of good faith, and a disregard for Plaintiff's disability-related needs.

First, Plaintiff is a qualified individual with a disability, as evidenced by the medical documentation provided by his treating physician, Treating Physician (Exhibit F). Treating Physician's letters confirm that Plaintiff has a reasonable fear of encountering a specific individual on campus, which would exacerbate his mental health conditions and impair his academic functioning.

Second, Plaintiff provided adequate notice of his disability and need for accommodation. He requested Zoom access as a medically necessary accommodation, citing his fear of encountering the individual on campus (Exhibit I). The University was aware of Plaintiff's disability and his request for accommodation, yet failed to engage in the interactive process.

Third, Plaintiff requested a reasonable accommodation, which was not an undue burden on the University. In fact, the University already utilizes Zoom for comparable graduate coursework in the same department (Exhibit N, Faculty D Syllabus). Moreover, Professor Faculty C had previously authorized Plaintiff's remote attendance, demonstrating that Zoom access was feasible and reasonable (Exhibit W, Faculty C Authorization and Subsequent Reversal).

Fourth, the University denied Plaintiff's request for accommodation. Despite Plaintiff's repeated requests and medical documentation, the University imposed a three-week countdown on his Zoom access, which was a conditional and time-limited measure designed to expire and coerce in-person attendance (Exhibit I, Selcer Meeting Exchange). This denial was not based on any written policy, pedagogical study, or departmental standard, but rather on Selcer's personal preference (TRO Tr.).

Fifth, the University's denial of accommodation resulted in harm to Plaintiff. The University's actions forced Plaintiff to choose between his education and his safety, causing him significant distress and anxiety (Exhibit C, External Faculty Witness Academic Expert Declarations). The University's failure to provide a reasonable accommodation also exacerbated Plaintiff's mental health conditions, as confirmed by Treating Physician (Exhibit F).

The University's actions are in direct contravention of the requirements under Section 504 and the ADA. As held in Taylor v. Phoenixville School District, 184 F.3d 296 (3d Cir. 1999), an employer who has received adequate notice of an employee's disability and desire for accommodations cannot fail to engage in the interactive process and then simply document the employee's failures (Key Passage 1). The University's failure to engage in the interactive process and its denial of Plaintiff's request for accommodation demonstrate a lack of good faith, which is sufficient to establish liability under Section 504 and the ADA (Taylor, Key Passage 3).

Furthermore, the Supreme Court's decision in A.J.T. v. Osseo Area Schools, 605 U.S. ___ (2025), confirms that ADA and Section 504 claims in educational settings use the ordinary failure-to-accommodate analysis, without a heightened bad faith or gross misjudgment standard. The University's actions, therefore, are subject to the same standards that apply in other disability discrimination contexts.

In Oross v. Kutztown University, No. 21-5032 (E.D. Pa. July 25, 2023), the court granted summary judgment for the plaintiff where the university denied a remote accommodation based on an ad hoc blanket policy with no written basis, no individualized assessment, and interference. Similarly, in this case, the University's denial of Plaintiff's request for accommodation was based on an ad hoc policy with no written basis, and the University failed to conduct an individualized assessment of Plaintiff's needs.

In conclusion, Duquesne University's denial of Plaintiff's request for a reasonable accommodation constitutes a violation of Section 504 and the ADA. The University's failure to engage in the interactive process, its lack of good faith, and its disregard for Plaintiff's disability-related needs demonstrate a clear violation of federal law.
