

ARGUMENT

Count III: Failure to Accommodate under Section 504 (29 U.S.C. § 794) and the ADA

Plaintiff David Mullins, a doctoral candidate in the Department of Philosophy at Duquesne University, brings this claim against Defendant Duquesne University of the Holy Spirit for failing to accommodate his disability in violation of Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act and the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA).

To establish a prima facie case under Section 504 and the ADA, Plaintiff must demonstrate that: (1) he is a qualified individual with a disability; (2) he provided notice of his disability and need for accommodation; (3) he requested a reasonable accommodation; (4) the accommodation was denied; and (5) he suffered resulting harm. (See Taylor v. Phoenixville School District, 184 F.3d 296, 306 (3d Cir. 1999)).

First, Plaintiff is a qualified individual with a disability. His treating physician, Treating Physician, has provided documentation stating that Plaintiff "does not feel safe around a specific individual on campus, and from what he's described to me, I believe it's reasonable for him to have this fear." (Exhibit F). This medical documentation establishes that Plaintiff has a disability that requires accommodation.

Second, Plaintiff provided notice of his disability and need for accommodation. On June 30, 2025, Plaintiff emailed Dean Wasilko, Dr. Selcer, and Dr. Faculty A, reporting the incident with his roommate and requesting emergency housing assistance. (Exhibit B-0). This email provided sufficient notice of Plaintiff's disability and need for accommodation.

Third, Plaintiff requested a reasonable accommodation. On August 13, 2025, Plaintiff submitted an Emergency Removal Request seeking Title IX supportive measures, including housing. (Exhibit B-5A). He also requested Zoom access for class participation as a medically necessary accommodation. (Exhibit I).

Fourth, the accommodation was denied. Despite Plaintiff's requests, Defendant failed to provide him with a safe and accessible learning environment. Defendant's response was deliberately indifferent, as it failed to address Plaintiff's safety concerns and instead imposed a three-week countdown on Plaintiff's Zoom access. (Exhibit I). This countdown was a conditional, time-limited measure designed to expire and coerce Plaintiff into attending classes in person, despite his medical documentation.

Fifth, Plaintiff suffered resulting harm. As a result of Defendant's failure to accommodate, Plaintiff was forced to endure a hostile and inaccessible learning environment, which exacerbated his disability and caused him significant emotional distress. (Exhibit C, External Faculty Witness Academic Expert Declarations).

Defendant's actions were in clear violation of Section 504 and the ADA. The Supreme Court has held that claims under these statutes use the same standard as claims in other settings, rejecting the heightened "bad faith or gross misjudgment" standard. (A.J.T. v. Osseo Area Schools, 605 U.S. ___ (2025)). Defendant's deliberate indifference to Plaintiff's safety concerns and failure to provide a reasonable accommodation demonstrate a clear violation of these statutes.

Furthermore, Defendant's actions were not only discriminatory but also retaliatory. Plaintiff's requests for accommodation were met with resistance and hostility, and he was ultimately denied the accommodations he needed to access his education. (Exhibit I-1, Mullins Demands Retraction of Retaliatory Zoom Countdown). This retaliation is a clear violation of the ADA and Section 504.

In conclusion, Plaintiff has established a prima facie case under Section 504 and the ADA. Defendant's deliberate indifference to Plaintiff's safety concerns and failure to provide a reasonable accommodation demonstrate a clear violation of these statutes. The Court should find in favor of Plaintiff on Count III.
