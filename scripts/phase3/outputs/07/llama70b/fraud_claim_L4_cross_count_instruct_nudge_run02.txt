

**ARGUMENT**

**I. Defendants' Argument 1: Dean A's TAP 33 Analysis**

Defendants argue that Dean A's analysis of TAP 33 was consistent with the policy's plain language, and that Plaintiff is not a "Covered Party" as defined, and the instructor-student evaluative relationship during litigation is not a "Conflict of Interest" under the policy. However, this argument ignores the fact that Dean A consulted with the conflicted party, Dr. Selcer, about his own conflict, and failed to provide an independent review as required by TAP 33 § IV.4 (Exhibit K-2). Furthermore, Plaintiff corrected Dean A within 90 minutes with specific policy citations, and Dean A responded "no change" (Exhibit K-5). This demonstrates a clear disregard for the policy and a failure to address the conflict of interest.

**II. Defendants' Argument 2: Dean A's Silence on the August 25, 2025 Email**

Defendants argue that Dean A's silence on the August 25, 2025 email was not actionable because no fiduciary or confidential relationship existed between university administrators and Plaintiff to trigger a duty to speak. However, this argument is belied by the fact that Dean A had a duty to speak as the Dean of the McAnulty College of Liberal Arts, and her silence constituted a culpable misrepresentation (Moser v. DeSetta, 589 A.2d 679 (Pa. Super. 1991)). Furthermore, Plaintiff had a right to rely on Dean A's representation as the natural and proper source of information (Neuman v. Corn Exchange National Bank & Trust Co., 356 Pa. 442, 51 A.2d 759 (1947)).

**III. Defendants' Argument 3: Plaintiff's Admission of No Justifiable Reliance**

Defendants argue that Plaintiff admitted in his own pleadings that he "immediately recognized" Dean A's TAP 33 reading was wrong and was "no longer relying on Defendants' representations" as of July 24, 2025. However, this argument is taken out of context and ignores the fact that Plaintiff had already been harmed by Defendants' actions and was seeking to mitigate those harms. Furthermore, Plaintiff's continued reliance on Defendants' representations is presumed from the materiality of the misrepresentations (Peerless Wall & Window Coverings, Inc. v. Synchronics, Inc., 85 F. Supp. 2d 519 (W.D. Pa. 2000)).

**IV. Defendants' Argument 4: Plaintiff's Claims are Internally Inconsistent**

Defendants argue that Plaintiff's claims are internally inconsistent because he alleges that both responding to his emails (Selcer) and not responding (Dean A) constituted fraud. However, this argument ignores the fact that Selcer's responses were inadequate and failed to address Plaintiff's concerns, while Dean A's silence was a culpable misrepresentation. Furthermore, the fact that Plaintiff was forced to navigate multiple channels and receive inconsistent responses from Defendants' administrators demonstrates a clear pattern of deliberate indifference.

**V. Defendants' Argument 5: The Consultation between Dean A and Selcer Produced Assurances of Fair Treatment**

Defendants argue that the consultation between Dean A and Selcer produced assurances of fair treatment and offers of academic support. However, this argument ignores the fact that Dean A consulted the conflicted party about his own conflict, and failed to provide an independent review as required by TAP 33 § IV.4 (Exhibit K-2). Furthermore, the fact that Selcer's responses were inadequate and failed to address Plaintiff's concerns demonstrates a clear disregard for Plaintiff's rights.

**VI. Defendants' Argument 6: Courts Should Defer to Academic Institutions' Professional Judgment**

Defendants argue that courts should defer to academic institutions' professional judgment regarding academic decisions. However, this argument is belied by the fact that Defendants' actions were not based on any legitimate academic or pedagogical concerns, but rather on a deliberate indifference to Plaintiff's rights and well-being. Furthermore, the fact that Defendants' actions were in clear violation of federal law and university policy demonstrates a clear disregard for Plaintiff's rights.

In conclusion, Defendants' arguments are without merit, and Plaintiff has demonstrated genuine disputes of material fact on each element of the fraud claim. The Court should deny Defendants' motion for summary judgment and allow the case to proceed to trial.
