

ARGUMENT

Plaintiff David Mullins has established a prima facie case of retaliation under Title IX, 20 U.S.C. § 1681. The evidence demonstrates that Duquesne University engaged in a pattern of adverse actions in response to Mullins' protected activity, including reporting sex-based harassment, opposing the University's handling of his complaint, and participating in the Title IX process.

First, Mullins engaged in protected activity by reporting sex-based harassment to Wasilko (Exhibit B-0A), escalating his complaint to the Title IX office (Exhibit B-3A), and filing a complaint with the Office for Civil Rights (OCR) (Exhibit B-3A). This activity is protected under Title IX, as demanding compliance with the statute is a protected activity. Atkinson v. Lafayette College, 460 F.3d 447, 452 (3d Cir. 2006). Mullins also reasonably believed that the University's conduct violated Title IX, as required by Kengerski v. Harper, No. 20-1307, at *3 (3d Cir. 2021).

Second, the University took adverse actions that would deter a reasonable person from engaging in protected activity. These actions include removing OCR from the CC line on emails (Exhibit B-3), threatening Mullins with conduct charges for emailing University officials about his safety concerns (Exhibit B-7), imposing a retaliatory "countdown" on Mullins' Zoom access (Exhibit I-1), and reassigning Mullins' TA position and threatening his stipend (Exhibits X-7 and X-8). These actions would deter a reasonable person from reporting sex-based harassment or opposing the University's handling of a complaint.

Third, the evidence establishes a causal connection between Mullins' protected activity and the adverse actions. The temporal proximity between Mullins' OCR filing and the University's removal of OCR from the CC line on emails is "unusually suggestive" of retaliation. Jalil v. Avdel Corp., 873 F.2d 701, 706 (3d Cir. 1989). The University's inconsistent explanations for its actions, including Selcer's shifting justifications for denying Mullins' Zoom access (Exhibit L), also establish a causal link. Farrell v. Planters Lifesavers Co., 206 F.3d 271, 279 (3d Cir. 2000). Furthermore, the pattern of antagonism towards Mullins following his protected activity, including the University's refusal to provide supportive measures and its threats of conduct charges, gives rise to an inference of causation. Kachmar v. SunGard Data Systems, Inc., 109 F.3d 173, 177 (3d Cir. 1997).

The University's actions, taken as a whole, demonstrate a convincing mosaic of circumstantial evidence sufficient to establish causation for retaliation claims. Canada v. Samuel Grossi & Sons, Inc., No. 20-2747, at *4 (3d Cir. 2022). The evidence also shows that the University's motivation for taking these actions was to retaliate against Mullins for his protected activity, which is itself evidence of pretext. Id.

In conclusion, Mullins has established a prima facie case of retaliation under Title IX, and the evidence demonstrates that the University's actions were causally connected to his protected activity. The University's pattern of adverse actions, including its removal of OCR from the CC line on emails, threats of conduct charges, retaliatory "countdown" on Zoom access, and reassignment of Mullins' TA position and threat to his stipend, would deter a reasonable person from engaging in protected activity.
