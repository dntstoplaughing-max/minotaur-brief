

ARGUMENT

Count I: Deliberate Indifference to Sexual Harassment under Title IX (20 U.S.C. § 1681)

Plaintiff David Mullins brings this claim against Defendant Duquesne University of the Holy Spirit, alleging deliberate indifference to known sexual harassment in violation of Title IX. The evidence demonstrates that Duquesne University had actual knowledge of the harassment, responded in a clearly unreasonable manner, and thereby denied Mullins equal access to educational opportunities.

Actual Knowledge

Duquesne University had actual knowledge of the harassment through its officials, including Dean Adam Wasilko, Title IX Coordinator Alicia Simpson, and Executive Director of Student Conduct Anne Sawa. (Exhibit B-0; Exhibit B-5; Exhibit T). As defined in the PCHE Summer 2020 Title IX Training, actual knowledge means notice of sexual harassment or allegations of sexual harassment to a recipient's Title IX Coordinator or any official with authority to institute corrective measures. (Exhibit Z-e, Slide 30). Duquesne University's officials had authority to institute corrective measures, and their knowledge of the harassment imputed actual knowledge to the university. (Exhibit Z-e, Slide 32).

Clearly Unreasonable Response (Deliberate Indifference)

Duquesne University's response to the harassment was clearly unreasonable. Despite Mullins' repeated reports of harassment and requests for assistance, the university failed to provide supportive measures, such as housing assistance, no-contact orders, or academic accommodations. (Exhibit A; Exhibit B-5A; Exhibit F). The university's response was not only inadequate but also retaliatory. (Exhibit B-6; Exhibit S). As the Third Circuit held in Hall v. Millersville University, a university's failure to forward sexual misconduct reports to the Title IX Coordinator can constitute a clearly unreasonable response. (Hall v. Millersville University, No. 19-3275 (3d Cir. Jan. 11, 2022)).

Severe, Pervasive, Objectively Offensive Harassment

The harassment Mullins experienced was severe, pervasive, and objectively offensive. (Exhibit D; Exhibit S). The harassment included physical pursuit, intimidation, and credible fear for Mullins' safety. (Exhibit B-0; Exhibit R). As the Supreme Court held in Davis v. Monroe County Board of Education, funding recipients are liable for deliberate indifference to known acts of student-on-student harassment that are severe, pervasive, and objectively offensive. (Davis v. Monroe County Board of Education, 526 U.S. 629 (1999)).

Denial of Equal Educational Access

Duquesne University's deliberate indifference to the harassment denied Mullins equal access to educational opportunities. (Exhibit C; Exhibit R). Mullins was forced to flee his home, experienced housing instability, and was denied academic accommodations. (Exhibit A; Exhibit F). As the Third Circuit held in Doe v. University of the Sciences, a university's failure to provide supportive measures can deny a student equal access to educational opportunities. (Doe v. University of the Sciences, 961 F.3d 203 (3d Cir. 2020)).

Conclusion

In conclusion, Duquesne University's deliberate indifference to known sexual harassment violated Title IX. The university had actual knowledge of the harassment, responded in a clearly unreasonable manner, and denied Mullins equal access to educational opportunities. The evidence demonstrates that Duquesne University's actions were not only inadequate but also retaliatory, and the university's failure to provide supportive measures denied Mullins equal access to educational opportunities.
