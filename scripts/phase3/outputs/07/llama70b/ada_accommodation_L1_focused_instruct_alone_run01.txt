

ARGUMENT

Plaintiff David Mullins, a doctoral candidate in the Department of Philosophy at Duquesne University, has demonstrated that he is a qualified individual with a disability, as evidenced by the medical documentation provided by his treating physician, Treating Physician (Exhibit F). Treating Physician's letters confirm that Mullins has a reasonable fear of encountering a specific individual on campus, which has caused him significant stress and worsened his mental health conditions.

Mullins provided notice of his disability and need for accommodation to the University, as required by law. He requested a reasonable accommodation, specifically Zoom access for class participation, which is a medically necessary accommodation (Exhibit F). This request was made in good faith, and Mullins was willing to work with the University to find a solution.

However, the University failed to engage in the interactive process, as required by Taylor v. Phoenixville School District, 184 F.3d 296 (3d Cir. 1999). The University did not assess Mullins' specific limitations, evaluate possible accommodations, discuss alternatives with Mullins, or involve disability services (SJ 7.2). Instead, the University imposed a three-week countdown on Mullins' Zoom access, which was a conditional, time-limited measure designed to expire and coerce Mullins into attending classes in person (Exhibit I).

This denial of accommodation was not based on any written policy, pedagogical study, or departmental standard, but rather on Selcer's personal preference (TRO Tr. at *:*). The University's actions demonstrate a pattern of denial with no articulable basis, as evidenced by the denial of Zoom access to another student, Colleague A (Exhibit L).

The University's failure to provide a reasonable accommodation resulted in harm to Mullins. As a result of the University's actions, Mullins was forced to continue attending classes in person, despite his medical documentation and requests for accommodation. This has caused him significant distress and has impaired his ability to participate in his education.

The Supreme Court's decision in A.J.T. v. Osseo Area Schools, 605 U.S. ___ (2025), makes clear that student ADA and Section 504 claims use the same standard as claims in other settings, rejecting the heightened "bad faith or gross misjudgment" standard. Therefore, the University's actions must be evaluated under the ordinary failure-to-accommodate analysis.

In Oross v. Kutztown University, No. 21-5032 (E.D. Pa. July 25, 2023), the court held that a university's denial of a remote accommodation based on an ad hoc blanket policy with no written basis, no individualized assessment, and interference with the interactive process established a claim for failure to accommodate. Similarly, in this case, the University's denial of Mullins' request for Zoom access was based on an ad hoc policy with no written basis, and the University interfered with the interactive process by imposing a conditional, time-limited measure.

In conclusion, Mullins has demonstrated that he is a qualified individual with a disability, and that the University failed to provide a reasonable accommodation, resulting in harm. The University's actions were not based on any written policy or pedagogical study, but rather on personal preference. Therefore, the Court should find in favor of Mullins on Count III, Failure to Accommodate under Section 504 and the ADA.
