

ARGUMENT

This case presents a clear example of deliberate indifference to known sexual harassment, in violation of Title IX. The undisputed facts demonstrate that Duquesne University had actual knowledge of the harassment, yet responded in a clearly unreasonable manner, denying Plaintiff David Mullins equal access to educational opportunities.

First, the University had actual knowledge of the harassment. As defined in Exhibit Z-e, actual knowledge means notice of sexual harassment or allegations of sexual harassment to a recipient's Title IX Coordinator or any official with authority to institute corrective measures. Here, Plaintiff reported the harassment to Dean Wasilko, who had authority to act, and later to Title IX Coordinator Alicia Simpson (Exhibits B-0, B-1). The University's knowledge of the harassment is further established by the emails exchanged between Plaintiff and various University officials, including Dr. Selcer and Dr. Wasilko (Exhibits R, S).

Second, the University's response was clearly unreasonable. As stated in Davis v. Monroe County Board of Education, 526 U.S. 629 (1999), a funding recipient's response to harassment is deliberately indifferent if it is "clearly unreasonable in light of the known circumstances" (Key Passage 3). Here, the University's response was not only unreasonable but also retaliatory. After Plaintiff refused to attend a meeting with University officials, Simpson reversed her jurisdictional determination, stating that the Title IX Office did not have jurisdiction over the report (Exhibit B-6). This decision was made without any new factual information about the underlying complaint, and the only intervening events were Plaintiff's emails consisting entirely of protected activity under § 106.71 (Exhibits B-5A, B-5B, B-5C). This retaliation is a clear example of deliberate indifference.

Third, the harassment was severe, pervasive, and objectively offensive. As established in Exhibit D, the Signal Transcript & Timeline, Plaintiff was subjected to a prolonged period of harassment, including being locked out of his apartment, being followed, and being yelled at (Exhibit D). This behavior is objectively offensive and created a hostile environment that denied Plaintiff equal access to educational opportunities.

Finally, the University's failure to provide supportive measures further demonstrates its deliberate indifference. As stated in Exhibit Z-c, supportive measures are mandatory, non-punitive, and individualized, and are designed to restore or preserve equal access to the education program or activity (Slide 38). Here, the University failed to provide any supportive measures, despite Plaintiff's repeated requests (Exhibits B-5, B-5A). Instead, the University conditioned supportive measures on Plaintiff's attendance at a meeting, which is inconsistent with the duty to offer and implement supportive measures promptly (Exhibit B-5A).

In conclusion, the undisputed facts demonstrate that Duquesne University had actual knowledge of the harassment, responded in a clearly unreasonable manner, and failed to provide supportive measures. This deliberate indifference to known sexual harassment denied Plaintiff equal access to educational opportunities, in violation of Title IX. As stated in Doe v. University of the Sciences, 961 F.3d 203 (3d Cir. 2020), a federally-funded institution discriminates on the basis of sex when it is deliberately indifferent to sexual harassment (Key Passage 2). The Court should find in favor of Plaintiff on Count I.
