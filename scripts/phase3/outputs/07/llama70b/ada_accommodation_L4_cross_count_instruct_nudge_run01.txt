

ARGUMENT

Count III: Failure to Accommodate under Section 504 (29 U.S.C. § 794) and the ADA

Duquesne University's deliberate indifference to Plaintiff's known disability and requests for accommodation constitutes a clear violation of Section 504 and the ADA. As established in A.J.T. v. Osseo Area Schools, 605 U.S. ___ (2025), ADA and Section 504 claims in educational settings use the ordinary failure-to-accommodate analysis, with no heightened bad faith or gross misjudgment standard.

Here, Plaintiff provided medical documentation from his treating physician, Treating Physician, stating that Plaintiff does not feel safe around a specific individual on campus and that it is reasonable for him to have this fear (Exhibit F). This documentation clearly establishes that Plaintiff is a qualified individual with a disability.

Plaintiff requested a reasonable accommodation, specifically Zoom access for class participation, which is medically necessary to ensure his safety (Exhibit N). However, Defendants denied this request, imposing a three-week countdown on Plaintiff's Zoom access, after which he would be required to attend in person regardless of medical documentation (Exhibit I). This conditional, time-limited measure was designed to expire and coerce meeting attendance, rather than providing a genuine accommodation.

The University's actions demonstrate a clear failure to engage in the interactive process, as required by Taylor v. Phoenixville School District, 184 F.3d 296 (3d Cir. 1999). Defendants did not assess Plaintiff's specific limitations, evaluate possible accommodations, discuss alternatives with Plaintiff, or involve disability services (SJ 7.2). Instead, they imposed a blanket policy, denying individualized assessment and accommodation (Exhibit Oross v. Kutztown University, No. 21-5032 (E.D. Pa. July 25, 2023)).

The University's denial of accommodation was not based on any written policy, pedagogical study, or departmental standard, but rather on Dr. Selcer's personal preference (TRO Tr. at *:*). This arbitrary denial constitutes a clear violation of Plaintiff's rights under Section 504 and the ADA.

Furthermore, the University's actions demonstrate a pattern of denial of accommodations, as evidenced by Dr. Selcer's denial of Zoom access to another student, Colleague A, without articulable basis (Exhibit L). This pattern of behavior suggests a deliberate indifference to the needs of students with disabilities, in violation of Section 504 and the ADA.

In conclusion, Duquesne University's failure to accommodate Plaintiff's known disability and requests for accommodation constitutes a clear violation of Section 504 and the ADA. The University's deliberate indifference to Plaintiff's needs, demonstrated by their failure to engage in the interactive process and their arbitrary denial of accommodation, warrants liability under these statutes.

WHEREFORE, Plaintiff respectfully requests that this Court find in his favor on Count III and grant such relief as is just and proper.
