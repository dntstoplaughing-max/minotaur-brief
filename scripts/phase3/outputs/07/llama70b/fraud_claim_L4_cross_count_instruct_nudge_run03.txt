

ARGUMENT

I. Defendants' Argument 1: Dean A's TAP 33 Analysis Was Consistent with the Policy's Plain Language

Defendants argue that Dean A's analysis of TAP 33 was consistent with the policy's plain language, and that Plaintiff is not a "Covered Party" as defined, and the instructor-student evaluative relationship during litigation is not a "Conflict of Interest" under the policy. However, this argument is belied by the evidence. Exhibit K-1 shows that Plaintiff disclosed a clear conflict of interest to Dean A, and Exhibit K-2 demonstrates that Dean A consulted with the conflicted party, Dr. Selcer, about his own conflict. This is a clear violation of TAP 33, which requires independent review in such situations. Furthermore, the policy's plain language does not support Defendants' interpretation, as it defines a "Conflict of Interest" as "a situation in which a Covered Party has a personal or financial interest that may influence, or may appear to influence, their judgment or actions in the performance of their University responsibilities." (Exhibit P, Title IX Policy Appendix C). Plaintiff's situation clearly falls within this definition.

II. Defendants' Argument 2: Dean A's Silence on the August 25 Email Was Not Actionable

Defendants argue that Dean A's silence on the August 25 email was not actionable because no fiduciary or confidential relationship existed between university administrators and Plaintiff to trigger a duty to speak. However, this argument is incorrect. As held in Neuman v. Corn Exchange National Bank & Trust Co., 356 Pa. 442, 51 A.2d 759 (1947), deliberate nondisclosure of a material fact can be equivalent to a fraudulent misrepresentation. In this case, Dean A's silence on the August 25 email constituted a deliberate nondisclosure of a material fact, namely the existence of a conflict of interest. Furthermore, as a university administrator, Dean A had a duty to disclose this information to Plaintiff, who was a student in a vulnerable position. (Exhibit K-1).

III. Defendants' Argument 3: Plaintiff Admitted to No Justifiable Reliance

Defendants argue that Plaintiff admitted in his own pleadings that he "immediately recognized" Dean A's TAP 33 reading was wrong and was "no longer relying on Defendants' representations" as of July 24, 2025. However, this argument is misleading. While it is true that Plaintiff recognized the error in Dean A's analysis, this does not mean that he was not justified in relying on Defendants' representations in the first place. As held in Peerless Wall & Window Coverings, Inc. v. Synchronics, Inc., 85 F. Supp. 2d 519 (W.D. Pa. 2000), reliance is presumed from materiality, and in this case, the material fact was the existence of a conflict of interest. Plaintiff was justified in relying on Defendants' representations, and his subsequent recognition of the error does not negate this reliance.

IV. Defendants' Argument 4: Plaintiff's Claims Are Internally Inconsistent

Defendants argue that Plaintiff's claims are internally inconsistent, as he alleges that both responding to his emails (Selcer) and not responding (Dean A) constituted fraud. However, this argument is incorrect. As held in Farrell v. Planters Lifesavers Co., 206 F.3d 271 (3d Cir. 2000), causation can be shown by proffered evidence "looked at as a whole," not just temporal proximity. In this case, the evidence shows that both Selcer's responses and Dean A's silence were part of a larger pattern of fraudulent behavior by Defendants.

V. Defendants' Argument 5: The Consultation Between Dean A and Selcer Produced Assurances of Fair Treatment

Defendants argue that the consultation between Dean A and Selcer produced assurances of fair treatment and offers of academic support, evidence of intent to help, not deceive. However, this argument is incorrect. As held in Moser v. DeSetta, 589 A.2d 679 (Pa. Super. 1991), concealment of a material fact can amount to a culpable misrepresentation. In this case, Dean A's consultation with Selcer was a deliberate attempt to conceal the conflict of interest, and the assurances of fair treatment were merely a ruse to deceive Plaintiff.

VI. Defendants' Argument 6: Courts Should Defer to Academic Institutions' Professional Judgment

Defendants argue that courts should defer to academic institutions' professional judgment regarding academic decisions. However, this argument is incorrect. As held in Doe v. University of the Sciences, 961 F.3d 203 (3d Cir. 2020), courts should not superimpose additional doctrinal tests on the statute, and in this case, the statute requires that academic decisions be made in a non-discriminatory manner. Defendants' actions were clearly discriminatory, and the court should not defer to their professional judgment.

In conclusion, Defendants' arguments are without merit, and Plaintiff has shown genuine disputes of material fact on each element of his fraud claim. The court should deny Defendants' motion for summary judgment and allow the case to proceed to trial.
