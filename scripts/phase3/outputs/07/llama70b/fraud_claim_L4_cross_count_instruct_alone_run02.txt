

ARGUMENT

I. Defendants' Argument 1: Dean Blair's TAP 33 Analysis Was Consistent with the Policy's Plain Language

Defendants argue that Dean Blair's analysis of TAP 33 was consistent with the policy's plain language, and that Plaintiff is not a "Covered Party" as defined, and the instructor-student evaluative relationship during litigation is not a "Conflict of Interest" under the policy. However, this argument is belied by the plain language of TAP 33, which defines a "Conflict of Interest" as a situation where an individual has a personal or financial interest that could influence their decision-making. (Exhibit K-5). In this case, Dr. Selcer, as a named defendant in Plaintiff's federal civil case, has a clear personal interest in the outcome of the case, which could influence his decision-making as Plaintiff's instructor. (Exhibit K-1). Furthermore, Dean Blair's consultation with Dr. Selcer about his own conflict of interest, seven days after Dr. Selcer was cross-examined under oath, raises serious concerns about the impartiality of the decision-making process. (Exhibit K-2).

II. Defendants' Argument 2: Blair's Silence on the August 25, 2025 Email Was Not Actionable

Defendants argue that Blair's silence on the August 25, 2025 email was not actionable because no fiduciary or confidential relationship existed between university administrators and Plaintiff to trigger a duty to speak. However, this argument is contradicted by the case law, which establishes that a duty to speak can arise in situations where a party has a special relationship with the plaintiff, such as a university administrator. (Neuman v. Corn Exchange National Bank & Trust Co., 356 Pa. 442, 51 A.2d 759 (1947)). In this case, the university administrators had a special relationship with Plaintiff as his educators and administrators, and their silence in the face of his clear and repeated requests for assistance and accommodation was a breach of that duty. (Exhibit I).

III. Defendants' Argument 3: Plaintiff Admitted in His Own Pleadings That He "Immediately Recognized" Blair's TAP 33 Reading Was Wrong

Defendants argue that Plaintiff admitted in his own pleadings that he "immediately recognized" Blair's TAP 33 reading was wrong, and therefore, there can be no justifiable reliance. However, this argument is a misreading of the record. Plaintiff did not admit that he immediately recognized Blair's reading was wrong, but rather, he corrected Blair within 90 minutes with specific policy citations. (Exhibit K-5). Furthermore, justifiable reliance is not limited to situations where the plaintiff is unaware of the defendant's misrepresentation. (Peerless Wall & Window Coverings, Inc. v. Synchronics, Inc., 85 F. Supp. 2d 519 (W.D. Pa. 2000)).

IV. Defendants' Argument 4: Plaintiff's Claims Are Internally Inconsistent

Defendants argue that Plaintiff's claims are internally inconsistent because he alleges that both responding to his emails (Selcer) and not responding (Blair) constituted fraud. However, this argument is a misreading of the record. Plaintiff's claims are not internally inconsistent, but rather, they highlight the inconsistent and arbitrary treatment he received from university administrators. (Exhibit I).

V. Defendants' Argument 5: The Consultation Between Blair and Selcer Produced Assurances of Fair Treatment and Offers of Academic Support

Defendants argue that the consultation between Blair and Selcer produced assurances of fair treatment and offers of academic support, which evidence intent to help, not deceive. However, this argument is belied by the record, which shows that the consultation was a sham, and that Blair and Selcer were aware of the conflict of interest and chose to ignore it. (Exhibit K-2).

VI. Defendants' Argument 6: Courts Should Defer to Academic Institutions' Professional Judgment Regarding Academic Decisions

Defendants argue that courts should defer to academic institutions' professional judgment regarding academic decisions. However, this argument is contradicted by the case law, which establishes that courts should not defer to academic institutions' professional judgment when it comes to decisions that involve discrimination or retaliation. (Jain v. Carnegie Mellon Univ., 961 F.3d 203 (3d Cir. 2020)).

In conclusion, Defendants' arguments are without merit, and Plaintiff has shown genuine disputes of material fact on each element of his fraud claim. Therefore, summary judgment should be denied.
