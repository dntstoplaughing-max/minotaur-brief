

**Argument**

Defendants' motion for summary judgment on Count IV - Fraud (Pennsylvania common law) must be denied. Despite Defendants' assertions, genuine disputes of material fact exist on each element of the claim, precluding summary judgment.

**1. False Representation of Material Fact**

Defendants argue that Dean Blair's TAP 33 analysis was consistent with the policy's plain language, and that Plaintiff is not a "Covered Party" as defined. However, Exhibit K-5, Blair's email denying TAP 33 applicability, demonstrates a clear misinterpretation of the policy. Blair's statement that TAP 33 does not apply because Plaintiff is not a "Covered Party" is a false representation of material fact. (See Exhibit K-5). This misinterpretation is further evidenced by Blair's failure to follow the independent review process required by TAP 33 § IV.4. (See Exhibit K-2).

**2. Scienter (Knowledge of Falsity or Reckless Disregard)**

Defendants claim that Blair's silence on the August 25, 2025 email was not actionable because no fiduciary or confidential relationship existed between university administrators and Plaintiff. However, as held in Neuman v. Corn Exchange National Bank & Trust Co., 356 Pa. 442, 51 A.2d 759 (1947), reliance is justified when the defendant is the "natural and proper source of information." (See Key Passage 1). In this case, Blair, as Dean, was the natural and proper source of information regarding TAP 33 and its applicability. Her silence, therefore, constitutes a reckless disregard for the truth.

**3. Intent to Induce Reliance**

Defendants argue that Plaintiff admitted in his own pleadings that he "immediately recognized" Blair's TAP 33 reading was wrong and was "no longer relying on Defendants' representations" as of July 24, 2025. However, this argument is belied by Exhibit K-1, where Plaintiff explicitly discloses the conflict of interest and seeks guidance from Blair. (See Exhibit K-1). Plaintiff's actions demonstrate a clear intent to rely on Blair's representation, and Blair's subsequent actions, including her consultation with Selcer, demonstrate an intent to induce reliance.

**4. Justifiable Reliance**

Defendants claim that Plaintiff's claims are internally inconsistent, alleging both that responding to his emails (Selcer) and not responding (Blair) constituted fraud. However, as held in Moser v. DeSetta, 589 A.2d 679 (Pa. Super. 1991), concealment of a material fact can amount to a culpable misrepresentation. (See Key Passage 1). Blair's failure to respond to Plaintiff's email, combined with her subsequent misinterpretation of TAP 33, constitutes a concealment of material fact, justifying Plaintiff's reliance.

**5. Resulting Damages**

Defendants argue that the consultation between Blair and Selcer produced assurances of fair treatment and offers of academic support, demonstrating an intent to help, not deceive. However, as held in Gibbs v. Ernst, 538 Pa. 193, 647 A.2d 882 (1994), fraud requires a showing of resulting injury proximately caused by reliance. (See Key Passage 1). Plaintiff's reliance on Blair's misrepresentation led to his continued enrollment in the teaching seminar, despite the clear conflict of interest, resulting in damages.

In conclusion, genuine disputes of material fact exist on each element of the fraud claim, precluding summary judgment. Defendants' motion must be denied.
