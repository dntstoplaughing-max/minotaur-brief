

ARGUMENT

Count III: Failure to Accommodate under Section 504 (29 U.S.C. § 794) and the ADA

Plaintiff David Mullins, a doctoral candidate in the Department of Philosophy at Duquesne University, brings this claim against Defendants for their failure to accommodate his disability and provide him with equal access to education. The evidence demonstrates that Defendants had actual knowledge of Plaintiff's disability and his need for accommodation, yet they failed to provide him with reasonable accommodations, resulting in harm to his education and well-being.

First, Plaintiff is a qualified individual with a disability, as evidenced by the medical documentation provided by his treating physician, Treating Physician (Exhibit F). Treating Physician states that Plaintiff does not feel safe around a specific individual on campus and that it is reasonable for him to have this fear. This documentation establishes that Plaintiff has a disability that requires accommodation.

Second, Plaintiff provided Defendants with notice of his disability and his need for accommodation. In his email to Dean Wasilko on June 30, 2025, Plaintiff reported an incident involving his roommate, Respondent, which left him feeling physically threatened and unsafe in his own home (Exhibit B-0). He requested emergency housing options, protective campus measures, and relevant reporting mechanisms. This email put Defendants on notice of Plaintiff's disability and his need for accommodation.

Third, Plaintiff requested reasonable accommodations, including a no-contact order, registrar separation, and a campus safety escort (Exhibit I). These accommodations are designed to restore or preserve equal access to the education program or activity without unreasonably burdening the other party (Exhibit Z-c). However, Defendants denied these accommodations, citing a lack of jurisdiction (Exhibit B-6) and a failure to engage with the relevant university offices (Exhibit J).

Fourth, Defendants' denial of accommodation resulted in harm to Plaintiff's education and well-being. Plaintiff was forced to participate remotely in his classes, which limited his access to departmental offices, the library, and other educational resources (Exhibit I-1). He also experienced significant stress and anxiety due to the lack of safety measures, which negatively impacted his mental health and academic performance (Exhibit F).

The Supreme Court's decision in A.J.T. v. Osseo Area Schools, 605 U.S. ___ (2025), is directly applicable to this case. The Court held that ADA and Section 504 claims in educational settings use the ordinary failure-to-accommodate analysis, without a heightened bad faith or gross misjudgment standard. In this case, Defendants' failure to accommodate Plaintiff's disability and provide him with reasonable accommodations constitutes a clear violation of Section 504 and the ADA.

Furthermore, the Third Circuit's decision in Taylor v. Phoenixville School District, 184 F.3d 296 (3d Cir. 1999), is also relevant. The court held that an employer who has received adequate notice of an employee's disability and desire for accommodations cannot fail to engage in the interactive process, increase the disabled employee's job responsibilities, and then simply document failures. In this case, Defendants received adequate notice of Plaintiff's disability and his need for accommodation, yet they failed to engage in the interactive process and provide him with reasonable accommodations.

In conclusion, the evidence demonstrates that Defendants had actual knowledge of Plaintiff's disability and his need for accommodation, yet they failed to provide him with reasonable accommodations, resulting in harm to his education and well-being. This constitutes a clear violation of Section 504 and the ADA. The Court should find in favor of Plaintiff on Count III.
