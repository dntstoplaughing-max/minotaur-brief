

ARGUMENT

Plaintiff David Mullins, a doctoral candidate in the Department of Philosophy at Duquesne University, brings this claim under Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act and the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) for Defendant Duquesne University's failure to accommodate his disability. The evidence demonstrates that Duquesne University had notice of Plaintiff's disability and his need for accommodation, yet failed to engage in the interactive process and denied his request for a reasonable accommodation.

First, Plaintiff is a qualified individual with a disability. His treating physician, Treating Physician, provided documentation stating that Plaintiff has a reasonable fear of being on campus due to a specific individual, and that this fear is likely to worsen his mental health conditions (Exhibit F). This documentation establishes that Plaintiff has a disability within the meaning of the ADA and Section 504.

Second, Plaintiff provided notice of his disability and his need for accommodation. He informed Defendant of his fear and requested a no-contact order, a campus safety escort, and registrar separation (Exhibit I). He also requested Zoom access for class participation as a medically necessary accommodation (Exhibit I). These requests demonstrate that Plaintiff provided adequate notice of his disability and his need for accommodation.

Third, Plaintiff requested a reasonable accommodation. His request for Zoom access was reasonable, as it was already in active use in the same department by Faculty D for comparable graduate coursework (Exhibit N). Moreover, there was no university policy or departmental policy prohibiting individual student Zoom access (Exhibit Y). In fact, Professor Faculty C had previously authorized Plaintiff's remote attendance due to ongoing legal and safety issues (Exhibit W).

Fourth, Defendant denied Plaintiff's request for accommodation. Despite Plaintiff's requests, Defendant imposed a three-week countdown on Plaintiff's Zoom access, after which Plaintiff would be required to attend in person regardless of medical documentation (Exhibit I). This conditional, time-limited measure was designed to expire and coerce meeting attendance, rather than provide a genuine accommodation. Defendant's denial of Plaintiff's request for accommodation was not based on any written policy, pedagogical study, or departmental standard, but rather on Selcer's personal preference (TRO Tr. at *:*).

Fifth, Plaintiff suffered resulting harm due to Defendant's denial of accommodation. Plaintiff was forced to attend classes in person, despite his fear of being on campus, which caused him significant distress and anxiety (Exhibit C). Plaintiff's education was also severely impacted, as he was unable to fully participate in classes and was forced to seek alternative arrangements (Exhibit C).

In Taylor v. Phoenixville School District, 184 F.3d 296 (3d Cir. 1999), the Third Circuit held that an employer who has received adequate notice of an employee's disability and desire for accommodations cannot fail to engage in the interactive process, increase the disabled employee's job responsibilities, and then simply document failures. Here, Defendant failed to engage in the interactive process, despite Plaintiff's repeated requests for accommodation. Defendant's actions demonstrate a lack of good faith, as they did not make a genuine effort to assist Plaintiff in seeking accommodations.

In A.J.T. v. Osseo Area Schools, 605 U.S. ___ (2025), the Supreme Court held that ADA and Section 504 claims in educational settings use the same standards as claims in other settings, rejecting the heightened "bad faith or gross misjudgment" standard. Here, Defendant's denial of Plaintiff's request for accommodation was not based on any legitimate reason, but rather on Selcer's personal preference.

In Oross v. Kutztown University, No. 21-5032 (E.D. Pa. July 25, 2023), the court granted summary judgment for the plaintiff where the university denied remote accommodation based on an ad hoc blanket policy with no written basis, no individualized assessment, and interference. Here, Defendant's denial of Plaintiff's request for accommodation was based on a similar ad hoc policy, with no written basis or individualized assessment.

In conclusion, the evidence demonstrates that Defendant Duquesne University failed to accommodate Plaintiff's disability, despite his repeated requests for accommodation. Defendant's actions demonstrate a lack of good faith, and Plaintiff suffered resulting harm due to Defendant's denial of accommodation. Therefore, Plaintiff is entitled to relief under Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act and the ADA.
