

ARGUMENT

Defendants' motion for summary judgment on Count V - Civil Conspiracy (Pennsylvania common law and 42 U.S.C. § 1985) must be denied because genuine disputes of material fact preclude summary judgment. Defendants' arguments are rebutted below.

1. Blair's consultation with Selcer was routine academic administration.

This argument is belied by Exhibit K-2, where Blair admits to consulting Selcer about a conflict-of-interest disclosure concerning Selcer himself. This written admission of joint determination with the conflicted party ("both of us, together") rather than independent review, contradicts Defendants' claim of routine academic administration. Under TAP 33 § IV.4, independent review of conflict-of-interest disclosures is required, and Blair's "consult[ation]" with the conflicted party does not constitute independent review under any reading of the policy.

2. The consultation evidenced intent to help, not malice.

Exhibit K-2 shows that Blair's consultation with Selcer resulted in assurances of fair treatment and offers of academic support. However, this does not negate the fact that Blair's actions were part of a larger conspiracy to deny Plaintiff's rights. As the Pennsylvania Supreme Court held in Thompson Coal Co. v. Pike Coal Co., 488 Pa. 198, 412 A.2d 466, 472-73 (1979), proof of malice is essential in proof of a conspiracy. The fact that Blair's actions may have been intended to help does not preclude a finding of malice, as malice can be established by showing a reckless disregard for Plaintiff's rights.

3. No underlying tort exists to support the conspiracy.

Defendants argue that Blair's TAP 33 analysis was correct on the policy's plain terms. However, Exhibit K-5 shows that Blair denied the applicability of TAP 33, despite the clear conflict of interest presented by Selcer's role as both Plaintiff's instructor and a named defendant in Plaintiff's federal civil case. This denial of TAP 33's applicability is a tortious act that supports the conspiracy claim.

4. Plaintiff's characterization of coordinated institutional responses as "conspiracy" reflects his refusal to engage with well-established university processes.

Exhibits B-0 through B-7 demonstrate Plaintiff's repeated attempts to engage with university processes, only to be met with retaliation, obstruction, and denial of his rights. These exhibits show that Plaintiff did not refuse to engage with university processes, but rather, the university failed to provide him with a safe and equitable environment.

5. Selcer's continuation as Plaintiff's instructor was not retaliatory because he held that role before and after Plaintiff's protected activity.

Exhibit X-8 shows that Selcer fabricated "performance issues" as a pretext for reassigning Plaintiff, despite the fact that Plaintiff had consistently fulfilled his research and TA responsibilities without issue or complaint. This fabrication of "performance issues" is an overt act in furtherance of the conspiracy, and it is not justified by Selcer's prior role as Plaintiff's instructor.

In conclusion, genuine disputes of material fact preclude summary judgment on Count V - Civil Conspiracy. Defendants' arguments are rebutted by the evidence presented, and Plaintiff has shown that each element of the conspiracy claim is supported by material facts. Therefore, Defendants' motion for summary judgment must be denied.

REQUIRED LEGAL ELEMENTS

(1) Agreement between two or more actors: Exhibits K-2, X-8, and Z-d demonstrate the agreement between Defendants to deny Plaintiff's rights and retaliate against him for his protected activity.

(2) Unlawful act or unlawful means: Exhibits K-5, X-8, and Z-c demonstrate the unlawful acts committed by Defendants, including the denial of TAP 33's applicability, the fabrication of "performance issues," and the failure to provide supportive measures.

(3) Overt acts in furtherance: Exhibits X-8, Z-d, and Z-e demonstrate the overt acts committed by Defendants in furtherance of the conspiracy, including Selcer's fabrication of "performance issues" and the university's failure to provide supportive measures.

(4) Malice (legal or actual): Exhibits K-2, X-8, and Z-d demonstrate the malice committed by Defendants, including Blair's reckless disregard for Plaintiff's rights and Selcer's fabrication of "performance issues."

(5) Resulting harm: Exhibits F, X-4, and X-8 demonstrate the resulting harm suffered by Plaintiff, including his emotional distress, academic disruption, and financial losses.

ALSO CONSIDER: REQUIRED ELEMENTS FOR DRAFT RETALIATION CLAIM (COUNT II)

(1) Protected activity: Exhibits B-0 through B-7 demonstrate Plaintiff's protected activity, including his reports of sex discrimination and his opposition to Defendants' actions.

(2) Adverse action that would deter a reasonable person: Exhibits X-8 and Z-c demonstrate the adverse actions taken by Defendants, including the fabrication of "performance issues" and the failure to provide supportive measures.

(3) Causal connection between protected activity and adverse action: Exhibits X-8, Z-d, and Z-e demonstrate the causal connection between Plaintiff's protected activity and the adverse actions taken by Defendants.
