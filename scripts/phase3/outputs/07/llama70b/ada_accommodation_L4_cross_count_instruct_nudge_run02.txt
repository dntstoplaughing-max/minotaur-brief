Count III: Failure to Accommodate under Section 504 and the ADA

Duquesne University's deliberate indifference to Plaintiff's known disability and need for accommodation constitutes a clear violation of Section 504 and the ADA. As the Supreme Court held in A.J.T. v. Osseo Area Schools, 605 U.S. ___ (2025), ADA and Section 504 claims in educational settings use the same standards that apply in other disability discrimination contexts, with no heightened "bad faith or gross misjudgment" standard.

Here, Plaintiff provided medical documentation from his treating physician, Treating Physician, stating that Plaintiff does not feel safe around a specific individual on campus and that it is reasonable for him to have this fear (Exhibit F). This documentation, combined with Plaintiff's repeated requests for accommodation, including Zoom access for class participation (Exhibit I), put the University on notice of Plaintiff's disability and need for accommodation.

Despite this notice, the University failed to engage in the interactive process, increase Plaintiff's job responsibilities, and then simply document failures, as prohibited by Taylor v. Phoenixville School District, 184 F.3d 296 (3d Cir. 1999). Instead, the University imposed a three-week countdown on Plaintiff's Zoom access, after which Plaintiff would be required to attend in person regardless of medical documentation (Exhibit I). This conditional, time-limited measure was designed to expire and coerce meeting attendance, constituting a clear denial of accommodation.

The University's actions were not only unreasonable but also retaliatory. As Plaintiff argued in his email to Selcer, the three-week limit on Zoom access was "facially retaliatory and must be retracted" (Exhibit I-1). The University's failure to provide a legitimate reason for denying Plaintiff's accommodation request, combined with its retaliatory actions, demonstrates a clear disregard for Plaintiff's rights under Section 504 and the ADA.

Furthermore, the University's policy of requiring in-person attendance for seminar participation, as stated in the Faculty Handbook (Exhibit Y), does not justify the denial of accommodation. As the Third Circuit held in Oross v. Kutztown University, No. 21-5032 (E.D. Pa. July 25, 2023), a university's blanket policy cannot justify the denial of accommodation, especially when the policy is not based on any written basis or individualized assessment.

In conclusion, Duquesne University's deliberate indifference to Plaintiff's known disability and need for accommodation, combined with its retaliatory actions, constitutes a clear violation of Section 504 and the ADA. The University's failure to engage in the interactive process, provide a legitimate reason for denying accommodation, and its imposition of a retaliatory countdown on Plaintiff's Zoom access, demonstrate a clear disregard for Plaintiff's rights under the law.

WHEREFORE, Plaintiff respectfully requests that this Court find in his favor on Count III and grant him the relief to which he is entitled.
