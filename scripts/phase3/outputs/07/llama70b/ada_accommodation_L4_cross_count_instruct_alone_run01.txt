Count III: Failure to Accommodate under Section 504 (29 U.S.C. § 794) and the ADA

Plaintiff David Mullins, a doctoral candidate in the Department of Philosophy at Duquesne University, brings this claim against Defendant Duquesne University of the Holy Spirit, alleging that the University failed to accommodate his disability in violation of Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act and the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA).

To establish a prima facie case under Section 504 and the ADA, Plaintiff must demonstrate that: (1) he is a qualified individual with a disability; (2) he provided notice of his disability and need for accommodation; (3) he requested a reasonable accommodation; (4) the University denied the accommodation; and (5) he suffered resulting harm. See Taylor v. Phoenixville School District, 184 F.3d 296, 306 (3d Cir. 1999).

Plaintiff has met his burden of establishing a prima facie case. Exhibit F, a medical letter from Treating Physician, confirms that Plaintiff has a disability and requires accommodations to ensure his safety and well-being on campus. Specifically, Treating Physician states that Plaintiff "does not feel safe around a specific individual on campus, and from what he's described to me, I believe it's reasonable for him to have this fear." (Exhibit F). This documentation satisfies the first element of a prima facie case.

Plaintiff provided notice of his disability and need for accommodation through various emails and meetings with University officials, including Dr. Adam Wasilko, Alicia Simpson, and Dr. Daniel Selcer. (Exhibits B-0, B-1, B-5, and I). These communications demonstrate that Plaintiff informed the University of his disability and requested accommodations, including a no-contact order, registrar separation, and a campus safety escort. (Exhibit I).

Despite Plaintiff's requests, the University denied the accommodations. Dr. Selcer imposed a three-week countdown on Plaintiff's Zoom access, after which Plaintiff would be required to attend in person regardless of medical documentation. (Exhibit I). This conditional, time-limited measure was designed to expire and coerce meeting attendance, rather than provide a genuine accommodation. Furthermore, the University's denial of Plaintiff's request for a no-contact order and registrar separation demonstrates a failure to provide reasonable accommodations. (Exhibits B-5 and I).

The University's denial of accommodations resulted in harm to Plaintiff. Plaintiff was forced to endure a hostile and intimidating environment, which exacerbated his mental health conditions and impaired his ability to participate in his education. (Exhibits C and F). The University's actions also caused Plaintiff to experience stress, anxiety, and fear, which are all recognized as resulting harms under the ADA and Section 504. See Taylor v. Phoenixville School District, 184 F.3d at 306.

In addition to the prima facie case, Plaintiff also demonstrates that the University's actions were motivated by bad faith and a deliberate indifference to his disability. The University's failure to engage in the interactive process, as required by the ADA and Section 504, demonstrates a lack of good faith. See Taylor v. Phoenixville School District, 184 F.3d at 307. Furthermore, the University's imposition of a three-week countdown on Plaintiff's Zoom access, despite knowing that Plaintiff had a legitimate fear of attending in person, demonstrates a deliberate indifference to his disability. See A.J.T. v. Osseo Area Schools, 605 U.S. ___ (2025).

In conclusion, Plaintiff has established a prima facie case of failure to accommodate under Section 504 and the ADA. The University's denial of accommodations, despite Plaintiff's notice and request, resulted in harm to Plaintiff. Furthermore, the University's actions demonstrate bad faith and a deliberate indifference to Plaintiff's disability. Therefore, Plaintiff is entitled to relief under Count III.
