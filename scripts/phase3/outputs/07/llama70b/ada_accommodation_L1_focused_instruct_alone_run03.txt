

ARGUMENT

Count III: Failure to Accommodate under Section 504 (29 U.S.C. § 794) and the ADA

Plaintiff David Mullins, a doctoral candidate in the Department of Philosophy at Duquesne University, brings this claim against Defendant Duquesne University of the Holy Spirit for failing to accommodate his disability in violation of Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act and the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA). The undisputed evidence demonstrates that Duquesne University had notice of Plaintiff's disability and his request for a reasonable accommodation, yet failed to engage in the interactive process and denied his request, resulting in harm to Plaintiff.

First, Plaintiff is a qualified individual with a disability. His treating physician, Treating Physician, provided documentation stating that Plaintiff has a reasonable fear of being around a specific individual on campus, and that this fear is likely to worsen his mental health conditions (Exhibit F). This documentation establishes that Plaintiff has a disability within the meaning of the ADA and Section 504.

Second, Plaintiff provided notice of his disability and his need for accommodation. He requested Zoom access for class participation as a medically necessary accommodation, citing his fear of encountering the individual on campus (Exhibit I). This request was made in good faith, and Plaintiff provided medical documentation to support his request.

Third, Plaintiff requested a reasonable accommodation. His request for Zoom access was not only reasonable but also feasible, as evidenced by Faculty D's syllabus, which allows students to attend classes remotely via Zoom (Exhibit N). Moreover, Plaintiff's request was not a preference, but a necessity, as he had a legitimate fear of encountering the individual on campus.

Fourth, Duquesne University denied Plaintiff's request for accommodation. Despite receiving notice of Plaintiff's disability and his request for accommodation, the University failed to engage in the interactive process and instead imposed a three-week countdown on Plaintiff's Zoom access (Exhibit I). This countdown was retaliatory and unlawful, as it was designed to coerce Plaintiff into attending classes in person despite his legitimate fear (Exhibit I-1).

Fifth, Duquesne University's denial of accommodation resulted in harm to Plaintiff. The University's failure to provide a reasonable accommodation forced Plaintiff to choose between his education and his safety, causing him significant distress and anxiety (Exhibit C). Moreover, the University's actions interfered with Plaintiff's ability to participate in his education, as he was unable to attend classes in person due to his fear (Exhibit F).

The Supreme Court's decision in A.J.T. v. Osseo Area Schools, 605 U.S. ___ (2025), is instructive here. The Court held that ADA and Section 504 claims in educational settings use the same standard as claims in other settings, rejecting the heightened "bad faith or gross misjudgment" standard. This means that Duquesne University's actions will be judged under the ordinary failure-to-accommodate analysis.

The Third Circuit's decision in Taylor v. Phoenixville School District, 184 F.3d 296 (3d Cir. 1999), is also relevant. The court held that an employer who has received adequate notice of an employee's disability and desire for accommodations cannot fail to engage in the interactive process, increase the disabled employee's job responsibilities, and then simply document failures. Here, Duquesne University had notice of Plaintiff's disability and his request for accommodation, yet failed to engage in the interactive process and instead denied his request.

In Oross v. Kutztown University, No. 21-5032 (E.D. Pa. July 25, 2023), the court granted summary judgment for the plaintiff where the university denied a remote accommodation based on an ad hoc blanket policy with no written basis, no individualized assessment, and interference. Similarly, Duquesne University's denial of Plaintiff's request for accommodation was based on an ad hoc policy with no written basis, and the University failed to engage in the interactive process.

In conclusion, the undisputed evidence demonstrates that Duquesne University failed to accommodate Plaintiff's disability in violation of Section 504 and the ADA. The University had notice of Plaintiff's disability and his request for accommodation, yet failed to engage in the interactive process and denied his request, resulting in harm to Plaintiff.
