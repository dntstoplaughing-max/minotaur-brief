

ARGUMENT

I. Defendants' Argument 1: Dean Blair's TAP 33 Analysis Was Consistent with Policy

Defendants argue that Dean Blair's analysis of TAP 33 was consistent with the policy's plain language, and that Plaintiff is not a "Covered Party" as defined, and the instructor-student evaluative relationship during litigation is not a "Conflict of Interest" under the policy. However, this argument is belied by the fact that Dean Blair consulted with the conflicted party, Dr. Selcer, about his own conflict, seven days after that party was cross-examined under oath (Exhibit K-2). This is a clear conflict of interest, and Dean Blair's failure to recognize it is a material fact in dispute.

Furthermore, the policy itself requires an independent review, which was not conducted in this case (Exhibit K-5). Plaintiff corrected Dean Blair within 90 minutes with specific policy citations, and Dean Blair responded "no change" (Exhibit K-5). This demonstrates that Dean Blair's analysis was not consistent with the policy, and that Plaintiff's reliance on her representation was justified.

II. Defendants' Argument 2: Blair's Silence on the August 25 Email Was Not Actionable

Defendants argue that Blair's silence on the August 25 email was not actionable because no fiduciary or confidential relationship existed between university administrators and Plaintiff to trigger a duty to speak. However, this argument is incorrect. As stated in Neuman v. Corn Exchange National Bank & Trust Co., 356 Pa. 442, 51 A.2d 759 (1947), "Where a party to a transaction is the natural and proper source of information, nondisclosure of a material fact is equivalent to a fraudulent misrepresentation." In this case, Blair was the natural and proper source of information regarding the conflict of interest, and her silence constituted a material misrepresentation.

III. Defendants' Argument 3: Plaintiff Admitted to No Justifiable Reliance

Defendants argue that Plaintiff admitted in his own pleadings that he "immediately recognized" Blair's TAP 33 reading was wrong and was "no longer relying on Defendants' representations" as of July 24, 2025. However, this argument is taken out of context. Plaintiff did recognize that Blair's reading was wrong, but he also relied on her representation that she would consult with Dr. Selcer and provide a written determination (Exhibit K-2). Plaintiff's reliance on this representation was justified, and Defendants' argument that he was not relying on their representations is not supported by the evidence.

IV. Defendants' Argument 4: Plaintiff's Claims Are Internally Inconsistent

Defendants argue that Plaintiff's claims are internally inconsistent because he alleges that both responding to his emails (Selcer) and not responding (Blair) constituted fraud. However, this argument is not supported by the evidence. Plaintiff's claims are not inconsistent, and he has provided evidence to support each of his allegations.

V. Defendants' Argument 5: The Consultation Between Blair and Selcer Produced Assurances of Fair Treatment

Defendants argue that the consultation between Blair and Selcer produced assurances of fair treatment and offers of academic support, which is evidence of intent to help, not deceive. However, this argument is not supported by the evidence. The consultation between Blair and Selcer was not an assurance of fair treatment, but rather a demonstration of a clear conflict of interest (Exhibit K-2).

VI. Defendants' Argument 6: Courts Should Defer to Academic Institutions' Professional Judgment

Defendants argue that courts should defer to academic institutions' professional judgment regarding academic decisions. However, this argument is not supported by the evidence. In this case, the academic institution's professional judgment was flawed, and the court should not defer to it. As stated in Doe v. University of the Sciences, 961 F.3d 203 (3d Cir. 2020), "we see no need to superimpose doctrinal tests on the [Title IX] statute."

CONCLUSION

In conclusion, Defendants' arguments are not supported by the evidence, and Plaintiff has demonstrated genuine disputes of material fact on each element of his fraud claim. Therefore, summary judgment should be denied.
