

ARGUMENT

This Court should find that Duquesne University retaliated against Plaintiff David Mullins in violation of Title IX, 20 U.S.C. § 1681. The evidence demonstrates that Mullins engaged in protected activity, suffered adverse actions, and that a causal connection exists between the two.

First, Mullins engaged in protected activity by reporting sex-based harassment, opposing the University's handling of his complaint, and participating in the Title IX process. (Exhibit B-0A; Exhibit B-3A). As the Third Circuit has held, "demanding compliance with Title IX is protected activity" under the statute. Atkinson v. Lafayette College, 460 F.3d 447, 452 (3d Cir. 2006). Mullins's actions, including his email to Dean Wasilko (Exhibit B-0A) and his OCR complaint (Exhibit B-3A), constitute protected activity.

Second, the University took adverse actions against Mullins that would deter a reasonable person from engaging in protected activity. The University's actions include removing OCR from the CC line on emails (Exhibit B-3), reversing a jurisdictional determination after Mullins sent emails consisting entirely of protected activity (Exhibit B-6), and revoking Mullins's Zoom access (Exhibit L). These actions, particularly the revocation of Zoom access, would deter a reasonable person from reporting sex-based harassment or participating in the Title IX process.

Third, a causal connection exists between Mullins's protected activity and the adverse actions. The Third Circuit has held that "temporal proximity combined with contextual evidence of retaliatory animus is sufficient to establish causation" in Title IX retaliation claims. Lauren W. ex rel. Jean W. v. DeFlaminis, 480 F.3d 259, 267 (3d Cir. 2007). Here, the temporal proximity between Mullins's protected activity and the adverse actions is striking. For example, the University removed OCR from the CC line on emails just two days after Mullins filed his OCR complaint (Exhibit B-3). Additionally, the University's inconsistent explanations for its actions, such as Selcer's shifting justifications for denying Mullins's Zoom access (Exhibit L), demonstrate a retaliatory animus. Farrell v. Planters Lifesavers Co., 206 F.3d 271, 279 (3d Cir. 2000) ("inconsistent explanations establish a causal link").

Furthermore, the University's actions demonstrate a pattern of antagonism towards Mullins, which independently establishes a causal link. Kachmar v. SunGard Data Systems, Inc., 109 F.3d 173, 177 (3d Cir. 1997) ("Where there is a lack of temporal proximity, circumstantial evidence of a 'pattern of antagonism' following the protected conduct can also give rise to the inference"). The University's repeated attempts to limit Mullins's access to education, including its revocation of Zoom access and its refusal to provide supportive measures, demonstrate a clear pattern of antagonism.

In conclusion, the evidence demonstrates that Duquesne University retaliated against Mullins in violation of Title IX. The University's actions, including removing OCR from the CC line on emails, reversing a jurisdictional determination, and revoking Mullins's Zoom access, constitute adverse actions that would deter a reasonable person from engaging in protected activity. The causal connection between Mullins's protected activity and the adverse actions is established by the temporal proximity and contextual evidence of retaliatory animus.
