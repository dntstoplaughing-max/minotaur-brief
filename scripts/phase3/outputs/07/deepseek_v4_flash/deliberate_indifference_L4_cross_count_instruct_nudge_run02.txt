**ARGUMENT**

Plaintiff David Mullins satisfies each element of a Title IX deliberate indifference claim under *Davis v. Monroe County Board of Education*, 526 U.S. 629 (1999). Duquesne University had actual knowledge of severe, pervasive, and objectively offensive sexual harassment by a fellow graduate student and responded with a course of conduct that was clearly unreasonable in light of the known circumstances. As a result, Plaintiff was deprived of access to educational opportunities and benefits.

**I. Duquesne University Had Actual Knowledge of Harassment by an Official with Authority to Institute Corrective Measures**

Actual knowledge exists when notice of sexual harassment or allegations of sexual harassment is provided to a recipient's Title IX Coordinator or any official with authority to institute corrective measures. *Davis*, 526 U.S. at 650; *see also* Exhibit Z-e at Slide 30 (actual knowledge defined). Such officials include the Dean of Students and Title IX Coordinator. *Id.* at Slide 32.

Plaintiff provided actual knowledge to at least two such officials. On June 30, 2025, Plaintiff reported to Dean Wasilko—Associate Vice President for Student Life and Dean of Students—that a female roommate had aggressively pursued him through the apartment, followed him up the stairs, stated "I'm coming up," and caused Plaintiff to fear for his physical safety. Exhibit B-0. Plaintiff expressly stated he "no longer fe[lt] safe sharing a living space" and requested "emergency housing options" and "protective campus measures." *Id.* This report triggered actual knowledge because Dean Wasilko is an official with "authority to institute corrective measures." Exhibit Z-e at Slide 32 (identifying Dean of Students as an official with authority). On July 24, 2025, Plaintiff sent a formal Title IX escalation to Title IX Coordinator Simpson, the university president, provost, and legal affairs. SAC ¶¶ 168-172. On August 13, 2025, Plaintiff submitted an Emergency Removal Request to Simpson citing § 106.44(c) and attaching sworn declarations and a physician's letter documenting safety concerns. Exhibit F; Exhibit B-5A. Simpson acknowledged receipt, stating Plaintiff's "concerns and allegations warrant further information gathering and review." Exhibit B-5.

Duquesne's own training materials confirm that the Title IX Coordinator and Dean of Students are officials whose knowledge constitutes actual knowledge to the institution. Exhibit Z-e at Slides 32-33. Actual knowledge was therefore established as of June 30, 2025, and certainly no later than July 24, 2025.

**II. The Harassment Was Severe, Pervasive, and Objectively Offensive**

Under *Davis*, the harassment must be "so severe, pervasive, and objectively offensive that it can be said to deprive the victims of access to the educational opportunities or benefits provided by the school." *Davis*, 526 U.S. at 650.

The documented harassment meets this standard. On June 30, 2025, Respondent confronted Plaintiff in the kitchen, positioned herself "within inches of my face," yelled and used obscenities, and when Plaintiff attempted to leave, she "aggressively followed me through the apartment." Exhibit B-0. When Plaintiff asked if she was following him, "she confirmed it aloud." *Id.* She "pursued me up the stairs toward my room, saying 'I'm coming up,' and only stopped just short of entering." *Id.* Plaintiff "locked [him]self in [his] room and remained there, visibly shaken." *Id.* This physical pursuit and confrontation—where a roommate chased Plaintiff through the home and up the stairs—constitutes objectively offensive conduct that a reasonable person would find intimidating and threatening. *See Davis*, 526 U.S. at 651 (harassment must be "severe, pervasive, and objectively offensive").

The harassment extended beyond the June 30 incident. On June 27, Respondent locked Plaintiff out of the apartment. Exhibit D. After Plaintiff moved out on July 1, she harassed him throughout the move. *Id.* Plaintiff's academic mentor, External Faculty Witness, declared that Plaintiff expressed "fear of encountering his former roommate" and "ongoing anxiety about encountering her without protective measures in place," and on September 17, 2025, Respondent was "literally waiting outside" Plaintiff's seminar classroom. Exhibit C. Plaintiff's physician, Treating Physician, confirmed that Plaintiff "does not feel safe around a specific individual on campus" and that this fear is "reasonable." Exhibit F. Treating Physician further opined that "to share a living space with this individual would cause him a great deal of stress, would be highly likely to worsen his medical condition, and could impair his academic functioning." *Id.* The harassment caused Plaintiff to flee his home and endure homelessness for over 90 days. Exhibit A; Exhibit F (medical documentation confirming "homelessness since June 30th which has been very stressful and likely worsens his mental health conditions").

The severity is further underscored by Plaintiff's report that he believed Respondent "has an unregistered firearm." Exhibit I (Selcer meeting notes); Exhibit R (Plaintiff stating Respondent "expressed strong enthusiasm for the idea that the Second Amendment should mean firearm regulations should be less of a hassle"). As Treating Physician documented, Plaintiff's reasonable fear was such that he "ran out of lexapro earlier than planned" due to "proximity to his abuser" and was "taking more than prescribed." Exhibit F. The harassment was not merely offensive—it was physically threatening, persistent, and caused documented psychological and medical harm.

**III. Duquesne University Responded with Deliberate Indifference**

A funding recipient is deliberately indifferent when its "response to the harassment or lack thereof is clearly unreasonable in light of the known circumstances." *Davis*, 526 U.S. at 648. "[M]inimal, ineffective, or belated efforts to respond to sexual harassment are not clearly unreasonable as a matter of law." *Foster v. Board of Regents of University of Michigan*, No. 19-1314 (6th Cir. Dec. 11, 2020) (en banc) (dissent citing standard). The record demonstrates that Duquesne's response was not merely belated or ineffective—it was nonexistent as to supportive measures, followed by a jurisdictional reversal that effectively abandoned Plaintiff, and punctuated by retaliation.

**A. Defendant Failed to Provide Any Supportive Measures for 45 Days**

From June 30, 2025, when Plaintiff first reported the harassment, until August 14, 2025, when Simpson disclaimed jurisdiction, "no supportive measures were offered or provided to Plaintiff through the Title IX process." Statement of Undisputed Material Facts ¶ 8. During this period, Defendants' "communications consisted exclusively of requests to schedule meetings, directives to 'engage with' offices, and jurisdictional disclaimers." *Id.*

This failure is directly contrary to the mandatory obligations under 34 C.F.R. § 106.44(a), which requires the Title IX Coordinator to "promptly contact" the complainant, "discuss the availability of supportive measures," "consider the complainant's wishes," and "explain the process for filing a formal complaint." Exhibit Z-c at Slide 38 (supportive measures "available at any time after a report of sexual harassment," offered "promptly," and "designed to restore or preserve equal access"). Simpson performed zero of these five obligations. Statement of Undisputed Material Facts ¶ 10. She did not promptly contact Plaintiff to discuss supportive measures, did not consider his wishes, did not inform him of availability, and did not explain the process for filing a formal complaint. *Id.*

Instead, Duquesne's entire approach was to demand meetings. Dean Wasilko told Plaintiff to "file a police report" and said the conduct "doesn't sound like a code of conduct violation." Exhibit B-0. Wasilko later directed Plaintiff to "please make arrangements to meet with" Dr. Sawa and Simpson. Exhibit B-2. Simpson initially offered to meet, Exhibit B-1, and then on August 13, after Plaintiff submitted his Emergency Removal Request, she acknowledged the allegations warranted review and offered a joint meeting, Exhibit B-5. But when Plaintiff insisted on written communications and restored OCR to the CC line, Exhibits B-5A, B-5B, B-5C, Simpson reversed course entirely within 19 hours, stating "the Title IX Office does not have jurisdiction over your report and cannot take action or provide assistance as requested." Exhibit B-6. No new factual information about the underlying complaint entered the record between B-5 and B-6. Statement of Undisputed Material Facts ¶ 7. The only intervening events were Plaintiff's three emails consisting entirely of protected activity under § 106.71. *Id.* This sequence—acknowledge, demand meeting, disclaim jurisdiction when plaintiff exercises rights—is textbook deliberate indifference. *See Hall v. Millersville University*, No. 19-3275 (3d Cir. Jan. 11, 2022) (failure to forward reports to Title IX Coordinator and failure to contact victim can constitute clearly unreasonable response).

**B. Defendant's Response Was Clearly Unreasonable**

*Davis* requires that the response be "clearly unreasonable in light of the known circumstances." 526 U.S. at 648. The known circumstances here included: a student fled his home fearing for his physical safety after being pursued and followed up the stairs by a roommate; the student was homeless and had less than $40 in his bank account; the student provided sworn declarations and a physician's letter documenting safety concerns and medical harm; and the student repeatedly requested supportive measures including a no-contact order, registrar separation, and housing assistance. Exhibits A, B-0, B-0A, B-3A, B-5A, F.

Despite these circumstances, Duquesne offered nothing. Dean Wasilko told Plaintiff the available rooms were "in the first year buildings" and "that is not helpful at this juncture," Exhibit G, effectively conceding housing resources existed but were withheld. Dr. Selcer authorized remote attendance for only three weeks, conditioned on Plaintiff "fully engaging" with offices that had already disclaimed jurisdiction, Exhibit I, and when Plaintiff sought to extend that modest accommodation, Selcer terminated it after consulting with a professor who had already authorized Zoom attendance. Exhibits V, W. Dr. Sawa threatened Plaintiff with disciplinary charges for "sending mass emails to University officials" about his safety concerns. Exhibit B-7. This was not a good-faith effort to assist a student in crisis—it was a coordinated campaign of deflection and retaliation. *See Taylor v. Phoenixville School District*, 184 F.3d 296 (3d Cir. 1999) (employer cannot "fail to engage the employee in the interactive process" and "then simply document the employee's failures").

**C. The Jurisdictional Disclaimer Was Pretextual and Retaliatory**

Simpson's August 14 determination that the conduct "does not meet TAP 61, nor TAP 31's definitions of prohibited conduct, even if proven to be true," Exhibit B-6, is a legal conclusion unsupported by the record. Physical pursuit, following, intimidation, and confinement to one's bedroom—all documented in Plaintiff's June 30 report—fall squarely within any reasonable definition of harassment, stalking, or intimidation. The timing of the disclaimer—19 hours after Plaintiff refused a meeting and restored OCR to the CC line—supports an inference of retaliation. *See Jalil v. Avdel Corp.*, 873 F.2d 701 (3d Cir. 1989) (two-day temporal proximity between protected activity and adverse action sufficient to establish causation). Simpson did not offer Plaintiff appeal rights following the dismissal as required by § 106.45(b)(8), and did not send simultaneous written notice to the respondent as required by § 106.45(b)(3)(iii). Exhibit B-6; Statement of Undisputed Material Facts ¶ 9. This procedural failure compounds the substantive unreasonableness.

**IV. The Harassment Denied Plaintiff Access to Educational Opportunities**

The final element requires that the harassment "deprive the victims of access to the educational opportunities or benefits provided by the school." *Davis*, 526 U.S. at 650. Plaintiff's educational access was devastated. He was homeless for over 90 days. Exhibit A. His bank account fell to $16.65 and then negative $2.55 during this period. Statement of Undisputed Material Facts ¶ 11. Professor External Faculty Witness, an academic mentor of 19 years, declared that Plaintiff's "education and research have been severely derailed by his housing instability" and that "supportive housing and protective measures are necessary for him to resume his academic work." Exhibit C. Treating Physician documented that homelessness "worsens his mental health conditions" and that "[w]orsening his mental health conditions is expected to negatively impact his graduate education." Exhibit F. Plaintiff was unable to attend classes in person without safety measures, was forced to couch-surf, and ultimately had his Zoom access terminated by a department chair who told a professor who had already authorized remote attendance that there was "no compelling reason" to continue it. Exhibits V, W. This is precisely the kind of educational deprivation *Davis* contemplates. *See Hall*, No. 19-3275 (recipient liable where deliberate indifference denies access to educational opportunities).

**V. Duquesne University's Response Was Clearly Unreasonable Under Binding Third Circuit Precedent**

The Third Circuit has held that to state a claim under Title IX, "the alleged facts, if true, must support a plausible inference that a federally-funded college or university discriminated against a person on the basis of sex." *Doe v. University of the Sciences*, 961 F.3d 203 (3d Cir. 2020). This standard requires no "superimpos[ition of] doctrinal tests on the [Title IX] statute." *Id.* Here, the facts—if true—support a powerful inference of discrimination. Plaintiff expressly raised the question of sex discrimination in his July 23 email to Dean Wasilko: "I can't help but wonder whether your response would have been the same had the genders in this situation been reversed." Exhibit B-0A. Plaintiff later stated in his July 26 OCR escalation: "I believe this is chiefly because I am male. Had I been female and my roommate male, the response would have been immediate and protective." Exhibit B-3A. These statements, combined with the complete failure to provide any supportive measures to a male victim of harassment by a female perpetrator, support a plausible inference of sex-based discrimination. *See Price Waterhouse v. Hopkins*, 490 U.S. 228 (1989) (sex-stereotyping constitutes sex discrimination).

The Third Circuit's decision in *Hall v. Millersville University* is instructive. There, the court held that a federal funding recipient has adequate notice that it may face monetary liability for deliberate indifference to known sexual harassment perpetrated by a non-student "where the recipient exercises substantial control over the context in which the harassment occurs and the harasser." *Hall*, No. 19-3275. The court further held that failing "to forward sexual misconduct reports to Title IX Coordinator as required by policy can constitute clearly unreasonable response." *Id.* Here, Dean Wasilko did not forward Plaintiff's June 30 report to the Title IX office—instead, he minimized it as "not a code of conduct violation." Exhibit B-0. When Simpson finally received the report weeks later, she ultimately disclaimed jurisdiction in a manner that violated multiple regulatory requirements. Exhibit B-6. This is precisely the pattern condemned in *Hall*.

*McAvoy v. Dickinson College*, No. 23-2939 (3d Cir. Aug. 16, 2024), is distinguishable. There, the school provided a no-contact directive, academic and mental health accommodations, a five-month outside investigation, and a semester of probation—actions the Third Circuit found were "not clearly unreasonable." *Id.* Here, Duquesne provided nothing.
