**ARGUMENT**

**I. Defendant Duquesne University Acted with Deliberate Indifference to Known Sexual Harassment, Denying Plaintiff Equal Access to Education Under Title IX**

Title IX provides a private right of action for damages against funding recipients who act with deliberate indifference to known student-on-student sexual harassment. *Davis v. Monroe County Board of Education*, 526 U.S. 629, 650 (1999). To prevail on Count I, Plaintiff must establish: (1) actual knowledge by an official with authority to institute corrective measures; (2) a response that is "clearly unreasonable in light of the known circumstances"; (3) sexual harassment that was "severe, pervasive, and objectively offensive"; and (4) a resulting deprivation of "access to the educational opportunities or benefits provided by the school." *Id.* at 648, 650; *Hall v. Millersville University*, No. 19-3275, slip op. at 8 (3d Cir. Jan. 11, 2022). The Third Circuit has cautioned against superimposing additional doctrinal tests on the statute; "to state a claim under Title IX, the alleged facts, if true, must support a plausible inference that a federally-funded college or university discriminated against a person on the basis of sex." *Doe v. University of the Sciences*, 961 F.3d 203, 209 (3d Cir. 2020). The undisputed record demonstrates that Duquesne satisfies each of these elements.

**A. Duquesne Had Actual Knowledge of the Sexual Harassment Through Officials with Authority to Institute Corrective Measures**

Actual knowledge is established when a Title IX Coordinator or "any official of the recipient who has authority to institute corrective measures on behalf of the recipient" receives notice of sexual harassment or allegations of sexual harassment. *Davis*, 526 U.S. at 650; *Hall*, slip op. at 8. Multiple Duquesne officials with such authority received actual notice of Plaintiff's harassment.

On June 30, 2025, Plaintiff reported to Dean Wasilko—the Associate Vice President for Student Life and Dean of Students—that a fellow graduate student had pursued him through his home, confirmed she was following him, stated "I'm coming up" as she ascended the stairs toward his bedroom, and that he had locked himself in his room because he felt physically threatened. (Exhibit B-0). Dean Wasilko is an official with authority to institute corrective measures, including referring matters to Public Safety, Student Conduct, and Title IX. (Exhibit B-0). As the *Davis* Court recognized, a school's actual knowledge arises when officials who can "address the alleged discrimination and to institute corrective measures" receive notice. *Davis*, 526 U.S. at 650.

On July 24, 2025, Plaintiff escalated his complaint directly to Title IX Coordinator Alicia Simpson, the president, provost, and legal affairs, explicitly citing 34 C.F.R. § 106.44(a) and demanding supportive measures. (SAC ¶¶ 168-172). Simpson acknowledged receipt and her role: "My office is notified when there are reported incidents of alleged sexual harassment or sexual misconduct involving students. . . . One of my primary roles is to help ensure your health and safety. The Title IX office provides oversight to the University processes for Title IX sexual harassment and non-Title IX sexual misconduct, and I am here to provide you with support and talk with you about resources." (Exhibit B-1). Simpson is the Title IX Coordinator—the official with the *highest* authority to implement Title IX corrective measures. Actual knowledge is thereby established. *See Hall*, slip op. at 8 (actual knowledge established when Title IX Coordinator receives report).

On August 13, 2025, Plaintiff submitted an Emergency Removal Request under 34 C.F.R. § 106.44(c), attaching sworn declarations and a physician's letter documenting his reasonable fear of physical harm and the resulting homelessness. (Exhibit F; Exhibit B-5A). Simpson responded at 2:02 PM the same day, acknowledging that "your concerns and allegations warrant further information gathering and review" and specifically offering to "discuss your current needs relating to supportive measures"—the statutory term of art defined in 34 C.F.R. § 106.30. (Exhibit B-5). This acknowledgment constitutes actual knowledge of actionable sexual harassment.

Moreover, Duquesne itself trained its Title IX personnel that a Title IX Coordinator and officials like the Dean of Students have actual knowledge upon receipt of a report, and that a Responsible Employee program means "every employee who receives a report = actual knowledge to the institution." (Exhibit Z-e, Slides 30-34). Duquesne cannot now disclaim what it trained itself to know.

**B. Duquesne's Response Was "Clearly Unreasonable" and Thus Deliberately Indifferent**

A school is deliberately indifferent when its response "is clearly unreasonable in light of the known circumstances." *Davis*, 526 U.S. at 648. This standard does not require courts to conclude that only "minimal, ineffective, or belated efforts" are clearly unreasonable. *Foster v. Board of Regents of University of Michigan*, No. 19-1314, slip op. at 10 (6th Cir. Dec. 11, 2020) (en banc). The Third Circuit has held that "failure to forward sexual misconduct reports to Title IX Coordinator as required by policy can constitute clearly unreasonable response." *Hall*, slip op. at 8. Here, Duquesne's response was not merely inadequate—it was nonexistent for 45 days, followed by a retributive jurisdictional flip-flop, and then outright retaliation.

**1. Duquesne's 45-Day Failure to Offer Any Supportive Measures Was Objectively Unreasonable**

From June 30, 2025, through August 14, 2025, Duquesne offered Plaintiff zero supportive measures through the Title IX process. (Exhibits B-0 through B-6). The record shows that between these dates, Duquesne's communications consisted exclusively of requests to schedule meetings, directives to "engage with" various offices, and eventual jurisdictional disclaimers. *Id.* During this same period, Plaintiff's bank account fell to $16.65 (July 25) and negative $2.55 (August 12), while he lacked permanent housing. (Bank records). A physician certified that Plaintiff's homelessness "worsens his mental health conditions" and that "all measures to protect him from his abuser and to provide stable housing" were necessary. (Exhibit F). The *Davis* court recognized that a school cannot simply "do nothing" in the face of known harassment, and "in effect, 'subject' [its] students to discrimination" by refusing to act. *Davis*, 526 U.S. at 654.

Simpson performed zero of the five mandatory obligations under 34 C.F.R. § 106.44(a): she did not promptly contact Plaintiff to discuss supportive measures, did not consider Plaintiff's wishes, did not inform Plaintiff of the availability of supportive measures, and did not explain the process for filing a formal complaint. (Exhibits B-5, B-6). The preamble to the regulations makes clear these are not optional. Duquesne's own Title IX training material defines supportive measures as "available at any time after a report of sexual harassment," "non-disciplinary," "non-punitive," and required to be offered "promptly." (Exhibit Z-c, Slides 38-40). The training explicitly states that these measures are "Available to both Complainants and Respondents" and "Designed to restore or preserve equal access to the education program or activity." *Id.* Simpson's failure to offer *any* supportive measure for 45 days—despite Plaintiff's explicit requests for housing, no-contact orders, and safety measures—was thus not only contrary to the regulatory framework but contrary to Duquesne's own training.

**2. The Jurisdictional Reversal Constituted a Clearly Unreasonable Response**

On August 13, 2025, at 2:02 PM, Simpson acknowledged jurisdiction, stating that the allegations "warrant further information gathering and review" by the Title IX Office, Student Conduct, and Public Safety, and offered to discuss "supportive measures." (Exhibit B-5). At 4:20 PM, Plaintiff replied refusing the proffered meeting, demanding written communications, restoring OCR to the CC line, and citing §§ 106.44, 106.30, and 106.71. (Exhibit B-5A). Plaintiff sent two additional emails that evening, none of which contained any new facts about the underlying complaint. (Exhibits B-5B, B-5C).

Nineteen hours later—on August 14 at 9:42 AM—Simpson reversed course entirely, declaring: "the Title IX Office does not have jurisdiction over your report and cannot take action or provide assistance as requested." (Exhibit B-6). She stated the conduct "does not meet TAP 61, nor TAP 31's definitions of prohibited conduct, even if proven to be true." *Id.* No new factual information about the underlying complaint entered the record between B-5 and B-6. The only intervening events were Plaintiff's three emails consisting entirely of protected activity. (Exhibits B-5, B-5A, B-5B, B-5C, B-6).

This sequence—a Title IX Coordinator initially accepting a complaint, then reversing jurisdiction in 19 hours with no new facts—is "clearly unreasonable" by any measure. As the *Hall* court recognized, a school's failure to follow its own Title IX policy and procedures is evidence of deliberate indifference. *Hall*, slip op. at 8. Here, Simpson not only failed to follow her own procedures, but she also acknowledged that she made her "preliminary determination" "without a meeting" because Plaintiff was "unwilling to meet." (Exhibit B-6). Yet the regulations require a recipient to "respond promptly" to a report of sexual harassment, not condition that response on a meeting. *See* 34 C.F.R. § 106.44(a). Simpson also failed to offer Plaintiff appeal rights following the dismissal, as required by § 106.45(b)(8), and failed to send simultaneous written notice to the respondent, as required by § 106.45(b)(3)(iii). (Exhibit B-6). These omissions demonstrate a pattern of disregard for the regulatory framework.

**3. Duquesne's Response Is Distinguishable from the School's Response in *McAvoy* and Aligned with the Deliberate Indifference Found in *Foster***

In *McAvoy v. Dickinson College*, the Third Circuit affirmed summary judgment for the university because its response included a no-contact directive, academic and mental health accommodations, a five-month outside investigation, and a semester of probation for the harasser. No. 23-2939, slip op. at 3 (3d Cir. Aug. 16, 2024). The *McAvoy* court found that "delays alone do not establish deliberate indifference where school maintained ongoing communication and support." *Id.* Here, Duquesne provided *no* supportive measures, *no* no-contact directive, *no* investigation, and *no* accommodations. Its "ongoing communication" consisted entirely of demands that Plaintiff attend meetings and directives to engage with other offices. (Exhibits B-0 through B-6). Unlike the school in *McAvoy*, which "adopted escalating measures proportionate to the misconduct," Duquesne adopted *zero* measures. *See Foster*, slip op. at 10 (finding university response reasonable where it "adopted escalating measures proportionate to the misconduct").

*Foster* is also instructive. While the en banc Sixth Circuit ultimately affirmed summary judgment for the university, the dissent noted that "genuine disputes precluded SJ" in that case. *Foster*, slip op. at 28-30 (dissent). Critically, the *Foster* majority acknowledged that "minimal, ineffective, or belated efforts to respond to sexual harassment are not clearly unreasonable as a matter of law." *Foster*, slip op. at 10. Here, Duquesne's efforts were not just minimal and belated—they were *nonexistent*. No court has held that 45 days of complete inaction in the face of known sexual harassment and a reasonable fear of physical harm is not clearly unreasonable.

**4. The Retaliatory Conduct Further Demonstrates Clearly Unreasonable Response**

On August 15, 2025—the day after Simpson disclaimed jurisdiction—Executive Director of Student Conduct Dr. Anne Sawa threatened Plaintiff with disciplinary charges for "sending mass emails to University officials about your concerns" and for not "communicat[ing] directly with the resources offered to you." (Exhibit B-7). As the Third Circuit has held, "Demanding compliance with Title IX is protected activity." *Atkinson v. Lafayette College*, 460 F.3d 447, 456 (3d Cir. 2006). Sawa's email constitutes an admission that Duquesne's response was retaliatory, not remedial. This threat came in response to Plaintiff's advocacy for his own safety—advocacy that included restoring OCR to the email chain to enforce compliance. (Exhibit B-5A). The *Hall* court found that a school's failure to take reports seriously, combined with retaliatory conduct, can establish deliberate indifference. *Hall*, slip op. at 8.

**C. The Harassment Was Severe, Pervasive, and Objectively Offensive**

The *Davis* standard requires that the harassment be "so severe, pervasive, and objectively offensive that it can be said to deprive the victims of access to the educational opportunities or benefits provided by the school." *Davis*, 526 U.S. at 650. The record supports each element.

On June 30, 2025, Plaintiff's roommate pursued him through the apartment, confirmed she was following him, stated "I'm coming up" as she ascended the stairs toward his bedroom, and only stopped when Plaintiff locked his bedroom door. (Exhibit B-0; Exhibit D). Plaintiff reported to Dean Wasilko: "She approached me in the kitchen; when I calmly stated that I was not interested in talking, she began yelling at me, got within inches of my face, and aggressively followed me through the apartment. . . . She pursued me up the stairs toward my room, saying 'I'm coming up,' and only stopped just short of entering. I locked myself in my room and remained there, visibly shaken." (Exhibit B-0). Plaintiff later disclosed to his department chair that he feared the harasser possessed an unregistered firearm. (Exhibit I). The Third Circuit in *Hall* recognized that physical pursuit and confinement, combined with threats and fear of weapons, constitutes severe harassment. *Hall*, slip op. at 4-5.

The harassment was pervasive and ongoing. The harasser confronted Plaintiff again while he moved out on July 1, 2025, following him up and down stairs while he repeatedly stated he did not feel safe. (Exhibit D). On September 17, 2025, the harasser was found "literally waiting outside" Plaintiff's Philosophy seminar classroom, causing Plaintiff to suffer severe distress. (Exhibit C). A physician treating Plaintiff certified that "to share a living space with this individual would cause him a great deal of stress, would be highly likely to worsen his medical condition, and could impair his academic functioning." (Exhibit F, Medical Letter 1). A second physician letter confirmed that Plaintiff's homelessness since June 30 "has been very stressful and likely worsens his mental health conditions" and that "worsening his mental health conditions is expected to negatively impact his graduate education." (Exhibit F, Medical Letter 2).

The harassment was objectively offensive under any standard. As the *Davis* Court instructed, the harassment must be evaluated from the perspective of a reasonable person in the victim's position. *Davis*, 526 U.S. at 651. Here, a reasonable doctoral candidate who has been physically pursued, trapped in his bedroom, threatened with a firearm, and stalked outside his classroom would unquestionably find the conduct "objectively offensive." The university's own Title IX training materials define sexual harassment to include conduct that creates an intimidating, hostile, or offensive environment. (Exhibit Z-b, Slide 5; Exhibit Z-c). The June 30 incident alone—involving pursuit, verbal threats, and physical confrontation—satisfies this standard.

**D. Duquesne's Deliberate Indifference Deprived Plaintiff of Equal Access to Educational Opportunities**

The harassment and Duquesne's deliberate indifference directly caused Plaintiff to flee his home, become homeless for over 90 days, suffer severe financial distress (bank account falling to $16.65 and negative $2.55), and lose access to in-person educational participation. (Exhibit A; Bank records). *Davis* requires that the harassment "denies the victims access to the educational opportunities or benefits provided by the school." *Davis*, 526 U.S. at 650.
