**ARGUMENT**

Plaintiff David Mullins is entitled to judgment on Count III because Duquesne University violated his rights under Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act and the Americans with Disabilities Act by denying his requested reasonable accommodation—Zoom access to class—without engaging in an individualized, good-faith interactive process. The undisputed facts and controlling law establish each element of a failure-to-accommodate claim.

**A. Plaintiff is a Qualified Individual with a Disability.**

Plaintiff’s treating physician, Treating Physician, diagnosed him with ADHD and depression and confirmed that these conditions are exacerbated by the stress of homelessness and the fear of encountering his former roommate. Exhibit F (Medical Letters, Aug. 13 & Oct. 1, 2025). Treating Physician explicitly stated that worsening mental health “is expected to negatively impact his graduate education” and recommended “all measures to protect him from his abuser.” Exhibit F (Oct. 1 letter). Plaintiff’s disability—mental health conditions that substantially limit major life activities including learning and concentrating—renders him a qualified individual under the ADA and Section 504. See 29 U.S.C. § 705(20)(B); 42 U.S.C. § 12102(1)(A). Moreover, Plaintiff was enrolled as a doctoral candidate in good standing with no disciplinary record and no performance issues. TRO Tr. at *:*. He was fully qualified to participate in his graduate program with the accommodation of remote attendance.

**B. Duquesne Received Adequate Notice of Plaintiff’s Disability and His Need for Accommodation.**

Treating Physician’s August 13, 2025 letter notified Duquesne that Plaintiff “does not feel safe around a specific individual on campus” and that the physician believed that fear was “reasonable.” Exhibit F. The October 1, 2025 letter added specific diagnoses and the link between homelessness, stress, and educational harm. Exhibit F. Plaintiff repeatedly communicated his need for Zoom access as a medically necessary accommodation, explaining that in-person attendance without safety measures foreclosed his ability to participate. Exhibits I, I-1. Under *Taylor v. Phoenixville School District*, 184 F.3d 296, 312-13 (3d Cir. 1999), an entity “who has received adequate notice of an employee’s disability and desire for accommodations” cannot simply ignore that request. Here, the University had both the physician’s letter and Plaintiff’s explicit requests.

**C. Plaintiff Requested a Reasonable Accommodation.**

On August 25, 2025, Selcer acknowledged that Plaintiff requested remote attendance. Exhibit I. Plaintiff consistently sought Zoom access as a means to continue his education while avoiding the documented safety risk on campus. That request was objectively reasonable. Zoom was already in active use in the same department for comparable graduate coursework. Exhibit N (Faculty D Syllabus: “I will also keep a Zoom meeting running” for any student who is not feeling well). No university policy prohibits individual student Zoom access. The Faculty Handbook governs course delivery modality for faculty, not individual student attendance accommodations. Exhibit Y (Faculty Handbook 2025-2026, pp. 10-11). Selcer himself initially authorized Zoom for three weeks, confirming that the accommodation was feasible. Exhibit I.

**D. Duquesne Denied the Accommodation Without Engaging in the Interactive Process.**

Rather than conducting an individualized assessment, the University imposed a rigid, time-limited “concession” that was designed to expire and coerce Plaintiff into returning to campus. Selcer’s August 25 directive authorized Zoom “only for the first three weeks.” Exhibit I. When Plaintiff sought clarification, Selcer made clear that this was not a good-faith accommodation but a coercive deadline: “This is my final directive … we expect you to attend seminars in person from September 15 forward.” Exhibit J. Selcer later terminated Zoom access entirely after the TRO hearing, claiming that Faculty C found “no compelling reason” to continue it—even though Faculty C had independently authorized Zoom just days earlier. Exhibits Q, V, W. This pattern of ad hoc, conditional, and ultimately arbitrary denial mirrors the conduct condemned in *Oross v. Kutztown University*, No. 21-5032 (E.D. Pa. July 25, 2023), where the university “applied blanket no-remote policy with no written basis, no individualized assessment of plaintiff’s request,” making the accommodation request “dead on arrival.” *Oross* slip op. at *.

Moreover, Duquesne utterly failed to engage in the interactive process. Under *Taylor*, a disabled plaintiff must demonstrate: “(1) the employer knew about the employee’s disability; (2) the employee requested accommodations … ; (3) the employer did not make a good faith effort to assist … ; and (4) the employee could have been reasonably accommodated but for the employer’s lack of good faith.” 184 F.3d at 312. Here, the University knew of Plaintiff’s disability (Exhibit F), knew he requested Zoom (Exhibit I), yet never assessed his specific limitations, never discussed alternative accommodations, never involved Disability Services, and never offered any genuine alternative. SJ 7.2 (undisputed that “no interactive process was conducted”). Selcer testified under oath that his objection to Zoom was based on personal preference: he cited “divides attention” and a post-COVID departmental finding, but could not point to any written policy, pedagogical study, or departmental standard. TRO Tr. at *:*. Another student, Colleague A, was similarly denied Zoom with “I can’t explain why.” Exhibit L. Such conduct is the antithesis of good faith. As *Taylor* holds, “an employer who acts in bad faith in the interactive process will be liable if the jury can reasonably conclude that the employee would have been able to perform … with accommodations.” 184 F.3d at 313.

The University’s internal processes are no defense. *Oross* rejected the notion that a university can shield itself from ADA liability by pointing to a rigid policy that is never applied to the individual. See *Oross*, slip op. at *. Here, the Faculty Handbook does not prohibit individual Zoom accommodations; indeed, the same department already provided Zoom for Faculty D’s class. Exhibit N. The University’s failure to conduct an individualized assessment is the defining feature of its noncompliance.

**E. The Denial Caused Plaintiff Substantial Harm.**

The consequences of Duquesne’s refusal were severe. Plaintiff was forced to miss in-person classes, was cut off from remote access on October 17, 2025, and experienced increased stress, housing instability, and educational disruption. Exhibit Q (Zoom link removed); Exhibit C (External Faculty Witness Decl.: “his education and research have been severely derailed”); Exhibit F (physician’s warning that educational harm would result). The very purpose of the ADA’s accommodation mandate is to prevent such harm. See *A.J.T. v. Osseo Area Schools*, 605 U.S. ___ (2025) (applying ordinary failure-to-accommodate analysis in educational settings; no heightened “bad faith or gross misjudgment” standard). Under *Osseo*, Plaintiff need not show bad faith—only that the requested accommodation was reasonable and was denied. Here, the University’s conduct satisfies even the higher standard, as it acted without any written policy, individualized assessment, or good-faith engagement.

**F. The Applicable Legal Standard Confirms Liability.**

*Osseo* explicitly repudiates the notion that ADA and Section 504 claims in educational contexts require “a heightened showing of ‘bad faith or gross misjudgment.’” 605 U.S. at ___. Instead, “the same standards that apply in other disability discrimination contexts” govern. *Id.* Under those standards, Plaintiff has established all elements: (1) he is a qualified individual with a disability; (2) the University knew of his disability and need for accommodation; (3) he requested a reasonable accommodation; (4) the University denied that accommodation without an interactive process; and (5) he suffered harm as a result. *See Taylor*, 184 F.3d at 312-13; *Oross*, slip op. at *.

Because Duquesne’s denial of Zoom access was based on an ad hoc, unarticulated preference, and because the University refused to engage in any genuine interactive process even after Plaintiff provided medical documentation, the Court should find that Duquesne violated Section 504 and the ADA. Plaintiff is entitled to judgment on Count III.
