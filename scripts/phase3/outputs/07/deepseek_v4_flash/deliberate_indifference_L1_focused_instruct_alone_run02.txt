**ARGUMENT**

**A. Duquesne University Had Actual Knowledge of Actionable Sexual Harassment**

Title IX liability requires that a funding recipient have "actual knowledge" of sexual harassment by an official with authority to institute corrective measures. *Davis v. Monroe County Board of Education*, 526 U.S. 629, 641 (1999); *see also* Exhibit Z-e at Slide 30 (defining actual knowledge as notice to the Title IX Coordinator or any official with authority to institute corrective measures). Actual knowledge is triggered when "any person" reports sex discrimination to the Title IX Coordinator. *Id.* at Slide 31.

Here, actual knowledge was established no later than June 30, 2025, when Plaintiff David Mullins reported a physical pursuit and confrontation by his roommate to Dean Wasilko, an official with authority to institute corrective measures. Exhibit B-0. The report described the harasser approaching within inches of Plaintiff's face, yelling, following him through the apartment, verbally confirming she was following him, pursuing him up the stairs toward his bedroom, and stating "I'm coming up"—forcing Plaintiff to lock himself in his room. *Id.* That same evening, Plaintiff requested emergency housing assistance. *Id.*

By July 24, 2025, actual knowledge was independently established through direct notice to Title IX Coordinator Alicia Simpson. Exhibit B-1. Simpson acknowledged receipt of Plaintiff's allegations, confirming that her office "is notified when there are reported incidents of alleged sexual harassment or sexual misconduct involving students." *Id.* Simpson further acknowledged that Plaintiff alleged "noncompliance with Title IX regarding a situation with another student that has caused [him] to be displaced from [his] housing." *Id.*

On August 13, 2025, Plaintiff sent an Emergency Removal Request to Simpson under 34 C.F.R. § 106.44(c), attaching sworn declarations and a physician's letter documenting his reasonable fear of physical harm. Exhibit F; Exhibit B-5. Simpson responded that same day, expressly acknowledging that Plaintiff's "concerns and allegations warrant further information gathering and review." Exhibit B-5. She offered to "discuss your current needs relating to supportive measures"—a term of art defined exclusively in 34 C.F.R. § 106.30. *Id.; see also* Exhibit Z-c at Slide 38 (defining supportive measures as measures designed to "restore or preserve equal access to the education program or activity" and protect safety). Simpson's express acknowledgment that Plaintiff's allegations "warrant further information gathering and review" constitutes actual knowledge that the allegations, if true, could constitute sexual harassment under the University's own policies. Exhibit B-5.

**B. Duquesne University's Response Was Clearly Unreasonable and Thus Deliberately Indifferent**

A school is deliberately indifferent where its "response to the harassment or lack thereof is clearly unreasonable in light of the known circumstances." *Davis*, 526 U.S. at 648. "Neither does [the deliberate indifference standard] require courts to conclude that minimal, ineffective, or belated efforts to respond to sexual harassment are not clearly unreasonable as a matter of law." *Foster v. Board of Regents of University of Michigan*, No. 19-1314 (6th Cir. Dec. 11, 2020) (en banc) (Key Passage 2). The Third Circuit has expressly adopted a straightforward pleading standard: "to state a claim under Title IX, the alleged facts, if true, must support a plausible inference that a federally-funded college or university discriminated against a person on the basis of sex." *Doe v. University of the Sciences*, 961 F.3d 203, 209 (3d Cir. 2020). There is "no need to superimpose doctrinal tests on the statute." *Id.*; *see also Hall v. Millersville University*, No. 19-3275 (3d Cir. Jan. 11, 2022) (failure to forward reports to Title IX Coordinator as required by policy can constitute clearly unreasonable response).

**1. Duquesne University Imposed Unlawful Conditions on Access to Supportive Measures**

The Title IX Coordinator is responsible for "coordinating the effective implementation of supportive measures," which must be "offered as appropriate and as reasonably available, without fee or charge, designed to restore or preserve access." Exhibit Z-c at Slide 38. Supportive measures are "available at any time after a report of sexual harassment," are "non-disciplinary," and "individualized." *Id.* at Slides 38-39.

Despite actual knowledge, Simpson demanded that Plaintiff participate in a meeting before she would consider providing supportive measures. Exhibit B-5 ("it is necessary for representatives of these offices to meet with you"). This condition was unlawful. The regulatory framework does not permit a Title IX Coordinator to condition the provision of supportive measures—including basic safety measures like mutual no-contact orders, housing changes, and Public Safety alerts—on a meeting. *See* Exhibit Z-c at Slide 39 (supportive measures are available at any time). Indeed, the very purpose of supportive measures is to ensure safety while the process unfolds, not after the victim submits to a screening meeting.

Plaintiff made explicit that he "cannot delay resolution any further," and that his "bank account balance fell to $16.65 (July 25) and negative $2.55 (August 12), while he lacked permanent housing." Undisputed Material Fact ¶ 11. He had already been displaced from his home since June 30. *Id.* at ¶ 1. His physician warned that "to share a living space with this individual would cause him a great deal of stress, would be highly likely to worsen his medical condition, and could impair his academic functioning." Exhibit F (August 13, 2025 letter). In the face of this documented emergency, conditioning supportive measures on a meeting was not merely bureaucratic; it was "clearly unreasonable." *Davis*, 526 U.S. at 648.

**2. Duquesne University Performed Zero of Its Mandatory Obligations Under § 106.44(a)**

Under 34 C.F.R. § 106.44(a), upon receiving a report of sexual harassment, the Title IX Coordinator must: (1) promptly contact the complainant to discuss supportive measures; (2) consider the complainant's wishes; (3) inform the complainant of the availability of supportive measures; (4) explain the process for filing a formal complaint; and (5) respond in a manner that is not deliberately indifferent.

Simpson performed none of these obligations. Undisputed Material Fact ¶ 10. She did not promptly contact Plaintiff to discuss supportive measures—she proposed a meeting days later. She did not consider Plaintiff's wishes—she ignored his explicit refusal to meet and his demand for written action. She did not inform Plaintiff of the availability of supportive measures—she conditioned their provision on a meeting. She did not explain the process for filing a formal complaint—she instead reversed jurisdictional determination in a single email that omitted any process explanation. Exhibit B-6. This wholesale failure to comply with the governing regulation is, by definition, "clearly unreasonable in light of the known circumstances." *Davis*, 526 U.S. at 648.

**3. Duquesne University Reversed Its Jurisdictional Determination Based on Protected Activity**

On August 13, 2025, at 2:02 PM, Simpson acknowledged Plaintiff's allegations warranted "further information gathering and review" and offered to discuss supportive measures. Exhibit B-5. Between 4:20 PM and 7:31 PM that same day, Plaintiff sent three emails containing no new facts about the underlying complaint. Undisputed Material Fact ¶ 5. These emails consisted entirely of protected activity: citing Title IX regulations, demanding written action, and restoring the Office for Civil Rights to the CC line. Exhibit B-5A (citing 34 C.F.R. §§ 106.30, 106.44, 106.71). Retaliation is prohibited under 34 C.F.R. § 106.71. *Id.*

Nineteen hours later—on August 14, 2025, at 9:42 AM—Simpson reversed her jurisdictional determination. Exhibit B-6. She now stated that "the Title IX Office does not have jurisdiction over your report and cannot take action or provide assistance as requested." *Id.* She determined that the conduct "does not meet TAP 61, nor TAP 31's definitions of prohibited conduct, even if proven to be true." *Id.*

No new factual information about the underlying complaint entered the record between Simpson's acknowledgment (B-5) and her reversal (B-6). Undisputed Material Fact ¶ 7. The only intervening events were Plaintiff's protected activity. *Id.* A reasonable jury could infer that Simpson was not making a good-faith jurisdictional assessment but was instead retaliating against Plaintiff for exercising his rights under the Title IX regulatory framework. This is the very definition of "clearly unreasonable." *Davis*, 526 U.S. at 648; *Hall*, No. 19-3275 (finding deliberate indifference where officials "filed away" reports and failed to act).

**4. Duquesne University's Complete Failure to Act for 45 Days Was Clearly Unreasonable**

Between June 30, 2025 and August 14, 2025—a period of 45 days—no supportive measures were offered or provided to Plaintiff through the Title IX process. Undisputed Material Fact ¶ 8. Defendants' communications consisted exclusively of requests to schedule meetings, directives to "engage with" offices, and jurisdictional disclaimers. *Id.* Plaintiff's bank account fell to $16.65 and then negative $2.55 while he lacked permanent housing. *Id.* at ¶ 11.

*McAvoy v. Dickinson College*, No. 23-2939 (3d Cir. Aug. 16, 2024), is distinguishable. There, the court affirmed summary judgment for the university where the school provided a no-contact directive, academic and mental health accommodations, a five-month outside investigation, and a semester of probation—all while maintaining "ongoing communication and support." *Id.* (Key Passage 1). Here, Duquesne University provided nothing. No no-contact order was issued. No housing was provided. No Public Safety alert was placed. No academic accommodations were made. No process was initiated. The *McAvoy* court's affirmance was premised on the university's *ongoing provision of support*, not on the mere absence of punishment for delay. The present case is the polar opposite: 45 days of complete inaction while a documented safety emergency persisted.

As the *Foster* dissent recognized—a position the majority did not reject as a matter of law—"minimal, ineffective, or belated efforts to respond to sexual harassment are not clearly unreasonable as a matter of law." *Foster* (Key Passage 2). Here, there were *no* efforts at all. A complete failure to respond for 45 days to known sexual harassment is the archetype of "clearly unreasonable" conduct. *Davis*, 526 U.S. at 648.

**C. The Harassment Was Severe, Pervasive, and Objectively Offensive, and Denied Plaintiff Equal Educational Access**

For Title IX liability, the harassment must be "so severe, pervasive, and objectively offensive that it can be said to deprive the victims of access to the educational opportunities or benefits provided by the school." *Davis*, 526 U.S. at 650.

Plaintiff's harasser physically cornered him in the kitchen, got within inches of his face, yelled at him, followed him through the apartment, verbally confirmed she was following him, pursued him up the stairs toward his bedroom, and stated "I'm coming up"—forcing Plaintiff to lock himself in his room for safety. Exhibit B-0. This was not a single incident of unpleasantness; it was a physical pursuit and confrontation that created an objectively reasonable fear of imminent physical harm. *Id.* Plaintiff's physician confirmed this fear was reasonable, stating: "I believe its reasonable for him to have this fear" and recommending law enforcement involvement. Exhibit F (August 13, 2025 letter).

The harassment denied Plaintiff equal educational access. He was forced to flee his home on July 1. Undisputed Material Fact ¶ 1. His bank account fell to $16.65 (July 25) and negative $2.55 (August 12). *Id.* at ¶ 11. His physician warned that the situation would "cause him a great deal of stress, would be highly likely to worsen his medical condition, and could impair his academic functioning." Exhibit F (August 13, 2025 letter). A second medical letter confirmed that "homelessness since June 30th which has been very stressful and likely worsens his mental health conditions," and that "[w]orsening his mental health conditions is expected to negatively impact his graduate education." *Id.* (October 1, 2025 letter). Plaintiff's department chair assured him "it won't be a problem to ensure you don't share an office," Exhibit R, confirming that the harassment required separation to preserve Plaintiff's ability to participate in the academic program.

This case is controlled by *Davis*, which held that a student's denial of access to educational opportunities is actionable where the harassment "so severe, pervasive, and objectively offensive" that it effectively excludes the victim from school. 526 U.S. at 650. Plaintiff was displaced from his housing, financially devastated, medically impaired, and unable to safely participate in academic activities for 45 days without any institutional response.

**D. Duquesne University Exercises Substantial Control Over the Harasser and the Context**

The *Hall* court held that "it is an intentional violation of Title IX's terms for a funding recipient to act with deliberate indifference to known sexual harassment where the recipient exercises substantial control over the context in which the harassment occurs and the harasser, even if they are a third party." *Hall*, No. 19-3275 (Key Passage 1). Here, the harasser was a fellow graduate student at Duquesne University, subject to the University's disciplinary authority, code of conduct, and Title IX policies. The harassment occurred in University-provided housing—a program or activity of the University. The University's own Title IX policy, reproduced at Exhibit P, expressly covers student-on-student harassment and provides mechanisms for housing changes, no-contact orders, and emergency removals. Duquesne University exercises substantial control over its graduate students, its housing, and the safety of its campus. This element is satisfied.

**CONCLUSION**

Duquesne University had actual knowledge of severe, pervasive, and objectively offensive sexual harassment against Plaintiff David Mullins. For 45 days, it did nothing. It conditioned supportive measures on a meeting, reversed its jurisdictional determination based on Plaintiff's protected activity, performed zero of its mandatory obligations under § 106.44(a), and failed to provide any safety measures, housing assistance, or academic accommodations while Plaintiff was displaced, financially destitute, and medically at risk. This response—or lack thereof—was "clearly unreasonable in light of the known circumstances." *Davis*, 526 U.S. at 648. Plaintiff has satisfied all elements of Count I for deliberate indifference under Title IX.
