**ARGUMENT**

**COUNT III: FAILURE TO ACCOMMODATE UNDER SECTION 504 AND THE ADA**

**A. Applicable Legal Standard**

The Supreme Court recently clarified that student claims under Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act and the Americans with Disabilities Act are subject to the same standards applied in other disability discrimination contexts. *A.J.T. v. Osseo Area Schools*, 605 U.S. ___ (2025). There is no heightened "bad faith or gross misjudgment" requirement for educational accommodations claims. *Id.* Plaintiff need only establish the ordinary elements of a failure-to-accommodate claim: (1) that he is a qualified individual with a disability; (2) that the defendant had notice of his disability and his need for an accommodation; (3) that he requested a reasonable accommodation; (4) that the defendant denied that accommodation; and (5) that he suffered resulting harm.

**B. Plaintiff Is a Qualified Individual with a Disability**

Plaintiff David Mullins is a doctoral candidate in good academic standing in the Department of Philosophy at Duquesne University. Selcer testified at the TRO hearing that Plaintiff had no performance issues. (TRO Tr. at *:*; Statement of Undisputed Material Facts ¶ 2.) Plaintiff has been diagnosed with ADHD and depression by his treating physician, Treating Physician, who has opined that these conditions are worsened by Plaintiff's housing instability and proximity to his abuser. (Exhibit F, Medical Letter dated Oct. 1, 2025.) Treating Physician further documented that Plaintiff's mental health conditions "likely worsens his mental health conditions" due to homelessness and that "[w]orsening his mental health conditions is expected to negatively impact his graduate education." (*Id.*) These conditions substantially limit major life activities, including learning, concentrating, and interacting with others, thereby qualifying Plaintiff as an individual with a disability under Section 504 and the ADA. Duquesne University is a private university receiving federal financial assistance and is subject to Section 504. (SAC ¶ 2; Statement of Undisputed Material Facts ¶ 10.)

**C. Plaintiff Provided Adequate Notice of His Disability and Need for Accommodation**

Plaintiff provided his treating physician's documentation to the University. Treating Physician's August 13, 2025 letter states: "I am a physician treating David Mullins. He's informed me he does not feel safe around a specific individual on campus, and from what he's described to me, I believe its reasonable for him to have this fear. I have advised him to involve law enforcement for his protection in response to this reasonable fear." (Exhibit F, Medical Letter dated Aug. 13, 2025.) The letter further recommends that "every effort be made to avoid" sharing living space with the individual, and warns that doing so "would cause him a great deal of stress, would be highly likely to worsen his medical condition, and could impair his academic functioning." (*Id.*)

Under *Taylor v. Phoenixville School District*, 184 F.3d 296 (3d Cir. 1999), a defendant who "has received adequate notice of [the] disability and desire for accommodations" cannot simply ignore that notice and proceed with punitive measures. Here, Plaintiff's medical documentation was explicit: his diagnosed disability—ADHD and depression—required that he avoid proximity to his abuser. Plaintiff explained in multiple communications that his fear of encountering Respondent on campus prevented him from safely attending in-person classes without protective measures. (Exhibit I, Selcer Aug. 25 directive; Exhibit C, External Faculty Witness Declarations.) Selcer's own August 25, 2025 email acknowledges Plaintiff's explanation: "You explained that you do not feel safe on campus because you fear Respondent. You confided that you feel physically intimidated by her, and that you believe she has an unregistered firearm." (Exhibit I.) This constitutes more than adequate notice of both Plaintiff's disability and his need for accommodations to avoid contact with Respondent.

**D. Plaintiff Requested a Reasonable Accommodation—Zoom Access—That Was Denied**

Plaintiff requested Zoom access for class participation as a medically necessary accommodation. (Statement of Undisputed Material Facts ¶ 3.) Zoom was already in active use in the same department. Faculty D taught PHIL 520–420W Religious Platonism in Spring 2025 with a policy stating: "Although we will meet in person, I will also keep a Zoom meeting running, so if you are not feeling well, please attend that session remotely!" (Exhibit N, Faculty D Syllabus.) Zoom access was not novel, impractical, or contrary to university policy. No university policy or departmental policy prohibits individual student Zoom access; the Faculty Handbook governs faculty course delivery modality, not individual student attendance accommodations. (Faculty Handbook 2025-2026, pp. 10-11, SJ 7.3; Statement of Undisputed Material Facts ¶ 4.)

Despite this, Defendants denied Plaintiff a meaningful, permanent accommodation. On August 25, 2025, Selcer imposed a three-week countdown on Plaintiff's Zoom access, after which Plaintiff would be required to attend in person regardless of medical documentation. (Exhibit I; Statement of Undisputed Material Facts ¶ 6.) This was a "conditional, time-limited measure designed to expire and coerce meeting attendance." (*Id.*) Selcer testified under oath that his objection to Zoom was based on personal preference, citing "divides attention" and a post-COVID departmental finding, but could not identify any written policy, pedagogical study, or departmental standard supporting denial. (TRO Tr. at *:*; Statement of Undisputed Material Facts ¶ 7.) Colleague A, another student in Selcer's Graduate Teaching Seminar, separately requested Zoom access and was denied; Selcer "can't explain why" Zoom was not available to her either. (Exhibit L; Statement of Undisputed Material Facts ¶ 8.)

The denial culminated in Plaintiff's actual removal from Zoom access. Despite Faculty C separately authorizing Zoom for Plaintiff on October 6, 2025 ("Yes, Zooming in tomorrow would work fine"), Selcer subsequently intervened and revoked that access. (Exhibits Q, W, V.) On October 17, 2025, Selcer emailed Plaintiff: "I've discussed your remote attendance of his graduate seminar with Faculty C and he did not identify a compelling reason to continue it. We would both welcome your return to in-person attendance of our seminars." (Exhibit V.) Faculty C later expressed confusion about the revocation, stating: "Colleague B had surgery this past week, so I gave him the opportunity to Zoom for that one day, which you joined. So, please ask Dan about any need for continued online attendance." (Exhibit W.) This demonstrates that Selcer unilaterally terminated an accommodation that Faculty C had authorized, without any individualized assessment of Plaintiff's medical needs.

**E. Defendants Engaged in No Interactive Process and Acted in Bad Faith**

*Taylor v. Phoenixville School District* establishes that "an employer who has received adequate notice of an employee's disability and desire for accommodations, cannot fail to engage the employee in the interactive process of finding accommodations, increase the disabled employee's job responsibilities, and then simply document the employee's failures." 184 F.3d at 306. To show failure to participate in the interactive process, a plaintiff must demonstrate: "(1) the employer knew about the employee's disability; (2) the employee requested accommodations or assistance for his or her disability; (3) the employer did not make a good faith effort to assist the employee in seeking accommodations; and (4) the employee could have been reasonably accommodated but for the employer's lack of good faith." *Id.*

All four elements are satisfied here. First, as detailed above, Selcer and the Philosophy Department knew of Plaintiff's disability and his fear of Respondent. (Exhibits F, I.) Second, Plaintiff repeatedly requested Zoom access as an accommodation. (Exhibits I-1, Q.) Third, Defendants made no good faith effort whatsoever. As the Statement of Undisputed Material Facts confirms: "No interactive process was conducted. Defendants did not assess Plaintiff's specific limitations, evaluate possible accommodations, discuss alternatives with Plaintiff, or involve disability services." (Statement of Undisputed Material Facts ¶ 5.) Selcer imposed a unilateral three-week countdown without consulting Plaintiff, without conducting any individualized assessment, and without involving Disability Services. (Exhibit I; Exhibit J.) This is precisely the sort of "blanket no-remote policy with no written basis, no individualized assessment of plaintiff's request" that was condemned in *Oross v. Kutztown University*, No. 21-5032 (E.D. Pa. July 23, 2023). In *Oross*, the court held that the accommodation request was "dead on arrival" because the university interfered with the interactive process. The same is true here.

Fourth, Plaintiff could have been reasonably accommodated but for Defendants' lack of good faith. Zoom was already in active use in the same department for comparable graduate coursework. (Exhibit N.) The Faculty Handbook does not prohibit individual student Zoom access. (Faculty Handbook; SJ 7.3.) Professor Faculty C independently determined that Plaintiff could Zoom into his class. (Exhibits W, Q.) The accommodation was plainly feasible, and Defendants' refusal to consider it was not based on any legitimate pedagogical or policy concern, but on Selcer's personal preference. (TRO Tr. at *:*; Statement of Undisputed Material Facts ¶ 7.) *Taylor* holds that "an employer who acts in bad faith in the interactive process will be liable if the jury can reasonably conclude that the employee would have been able to perform the job with accommodations." 184 F.3d at 316. Here, Plaintiff *was* performing with Zoom—Selcer testified at the TRO hearing that Plaintiff had no performance issues (TRO Tr. at *:*; Statement of Undisputed Material Facts ¶ 2)—and his access was revoked not because of any academic deficiency but because of administrative retaliation.

**F. Plaintiff Suffered Actual Harm as a Result of the Denial**

Plaintiff suffered concrete harm from Defendants' failure to accommodate. After his Zoom access was revoked, Plaintiff was unable to attend his graduate seminars. (Exhibit Q, Zoom Removal Sequence Oct. 16-17, 2025.) On October 16, 2025, Plaintiff emailed Faculty C: "I attempted to join class through the usual Zoom link, but no meeting was available." (*Id.*) Plaintiff was forced to miss class and was excluded from his educational program. Treating Physician documented that Plaintiff's mental health conditions worsened as a result of the ongoing crisis: Plaintiff "reported to me he ran out of lexapro earlier than planned" and suffered worsening symptoms due to "proximity to his abuser" and "homelessness since June 30th." (Exhibit F, Oct. 1, 2025 Medical Letter.) Professor External Faculty Witness, Plaintiff's academic mentor for 19 years, declared that Plaintiff's "education and research have been severely derailed by his housing instability and the conflict with his former roommate" and that Plaintiff "cannot focus on his PhD research when he is experiencing encounters that cause this level of distress and require hours of recovery time." (Exhibit C, External Faculty Witness Declarations.) The harm is precisely the kind of educational deprivation that Section 504 and the ADA are designed to prevent.

**G. Conclusion**

Defendants knew Plaintiff was a qualified individual with a disability, knew that he needed accommodation to avoid contact with his abuser, and knew that Zoom was a feasible accommodation already in use in the department. Instead of engaging in the interactive process required by *Taylor*, they imposed a retaliatory, time-limited measure designed to expire, refused to conduct any individualized assessment, and ultimately revoked Plaintiff's access without any legitimate basis. Under *Osseo*, no heightened "bad faith" standard applies, but even under *Taylor*'s ordinary standard, Defendants acted in bad faith by failing to engage in the interactive process, imposing a blanket denial, and then reversing an accommodation that a professor had independently authorized. Plaintiff is entitled to judgment as a matter of law on Count III.
