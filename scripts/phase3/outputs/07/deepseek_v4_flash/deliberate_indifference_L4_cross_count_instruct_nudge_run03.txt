**ARGUMENT**

**A. Duquesne University Had Actual Knowledge of Severe Sexual Harassment Through Officials with Authority to Institute Corrective Measures.**

Under Title IX and *Davis v. Monroe County Board of Education*, 526 U.S. 629 (1999), a funding recipient is liable when it acts with deliberate indifference to known harassment by a student under the school's disciplinary authority. Actual knowledge arises when notice is provided to a Title IX Coordinator or any official with authority to institute corrective measures. See *Hall v. Millersville University*, No. 19-3275 (3d Cir. Jan. 11, 2022) (holding that failure to forward reports to Title IX Coordinator can constitute clearly unreasonable response; notice to any responsible official triggers actual knowledge).

Here, Plaintiff David Mullins provided actual knowledge to multiple officials on multiple occasions. On June 30, 2025, he emailed Dean of Students Adam Wasilko—an official with authority to implement housing and safety measures—describing a physical pursuit and confrontation by a fellow graduate student, and requesting emergency housing assistance. (Exhibit B-0.) Dean Wasilko acknowledged receipt and conceded the conduct "sound[ed] unsettling" but refused to treat it as a conduct violation, instead directing Plaintiff to file a police report. (Exhibit B-0.) Under *Hall*, this knowledge is imputed to the institution, and the failure to promptly contact the Title IX Coordinator or offer supportive measures is itself a red flag.

On July 24, 2025, Plaintiff sent a formal Title IX escalation to Title IX Coordinator Alicia Simpson, the university president, provost, and legal affairs, citing 34 C.F.R. § 106.44(a) and demanding supportive measures within 48 hours. (SAC ¶¶ 168-172; Exhibit B-1.) Simpson responded offering a meeting but did not offer any immediate supportive measures. On August 13, 2025, Plaintiff submitted an Emergency Removal Request under § 106.44(c), attaching sworn declarations and a physician's letter documenting safety concerns. (Exhibit F; Exhibit B-5.) Simpson acknowledged that Plaintiff's "concerns and allegations warrant further information gathering and review" and offered to "discuss your current needs relating to supportive measures." (Exhibit B-5.) At that moment, Simpson had actual knowledge of severe harassment and was statutorily obligated to promptly offer supportive measures—not merely to discuss them.

Additionally, Plaintiff reported his fear to Department Chair Daniel Selcer on July 20, 2025, expressly stating "I am scared of this person" and revealing that the harasser had expressed enthusiasm for the Second Amendment and the possibility of firearm possession. (Exhibit R.) Selcer, as chair, had authority to implement classroom modifications and refer to the Title IX office. Selcer's response—asking for a timeline and assuring Plaintiff he would not have to share an office—fell far short of mandatory reporting obligations. Under *Hall*, a funding recipient's officials cannot "file away" reports of sexual misconduct and hope the problem resolves itself.

**B. The University's Response Was Clearly Unreasonable and Constituted Deliberate Indifference.**

Deliberate indifference exists when a school's response to known harassment is "clearly unreasonable in light of the known circumstances." *Davis*, 526 U.S. at 648. Even minimal, belated, or ineffective responses can satisfy this standard. *Foster v. Board of Regents of University of Michigan*, No. 19-1314 (6th Cir. Dec. 11, 2020) (en banc) (dissent and majority recognizing that ineffective efforts can be clearly unreasonable). The question is objective: was the response unreasonable given what the school knew?

Here, the University's response was not merely inadequate—it was a textbook example of deliberate indifference spanning 45 days of inaction and procedural obstruction.

*First*, no supportive measures were offered or provided between June 30 and August 14, 2025. During this period, Plaintiff's bank account balance fell to $16.65 (July 25) and negative $2.55 (August 12), while he lacked permanent housing. (Bank records, Exhibits A, B-0 through B-6.) Medical documentation confirmed that homelessness was worsening his mental health conditions and that protective measures were necessary. (Exhibit F, Treating Physician letter: "I recommend all measures to protect him from his abuser and to provide stable housing be made.") The University's communications consisted solely of requests to schedule meetings and directives to "engage with" offices. (Exhibits B-1, B-2, B-3.) Conditioning supportive measures on a meeting—when the regulations expressly provide that supportive measures are available "with or without a formal complaint," 34 C.F.R. § 106.30—was clearly unreasonable. The Title IX training materials provided by the University itself confirm that supportive measures are "non-disciplinary, non-punitive, individualized" and "available at any time after a report." (Exhibit Z-c, Slides 38-40.) The University cannot withhold such measures pending a meeting, particularly when the complainant has already provided extensive documentation.

*Second*, Simpson's jurisdictional reversal on August 14, 2025, was not a good-faith policy determination but a retaliatory and clearly unreasonable response. Simpson initially acknowledged jurisdiction on August 13, stating that Plaintiff's allegations "warrant further information gathering and review" and offering to discuss supportive measures. (Exhibit B-5.) Nineteen hours later—after Plaintiff sent three emails (Exhibits B-5A, B-5B, B-5C) that contained no new facts but merely insisted on written communications, restored OCR to the CC line, and cited Title IX regulations—Simpson reversed herself, declaring that "the Title IX Office does not have jurisdiction over your report and cannot take action or provide assistance as requested." (Exhibit B-6.) The only intervening events were Plaintiff's protected activity. This abrupt about-face, without any change in the underlying facts, demonstrates that the University's decision was not based on the merits but was a punitive response to Plaintiff's insistence on his legal rights. In *Hall v. Millersville University*, the Third Circuit held that a university's failure to forward sexual misconduct reports to the Title IX Coordinator as required by policy constituted a clearly unreasonable response. Here, the Title IX Coordinator herself arbitrarily withdrew jurisdiction after receiving protected communications.

*Third*, the University's response was procedurally deficient in multiple respects. Simpson did not offer Plaintiff appeal rights following the dismissal, as required by 34 C.F.R. § 106.45(b)(8). She did not send simultaneous written notice to the respondent, as required by § 106.45(b)(3)(iii). (Exhibit B-6.) And she performed zero of the five mandatory obligations under § 106.44(a): she did not promptly contact Plaintiff to discuss supportive measures, did not consider Plaintiff's wishes, did not inform Plaintiff of the availability of supportive measures, and did not explain the process for filing a formal complaint. (Exhibits B-5, B-6.) These are not technicalities; they are the regulatory heart of the Title IX response.

*Fourth*, the University's subsequent actions confirm a pattern of obstruction. On August 15, 2025, Executive Director of Student Conduct Anne Sawa threatened Plaintiff with disciplinary charges for "mass emailing University officials," warning that "[f]ailure to do so may result in charges against you for violating the Student Code of Conduct." (Exhibit B-7.) This threat, made one day after Simpson's jurisdiction disclaimer and while Plaintiff remained homeless and in fear, constitutes retaliation under 34 C.F.R. § 106.71 and further evidence of deliberate indifference. Department Chair Selcer later imposed a three-week cap on remote attendance (Exhibit I), conditioned on Plaintiff's "full engagement" with the same offices that had just disclaimed jurisdiction and threatened him. This is not a reasonable response; it is a classic run-out-the-clock strategy.

**C. The Harassment Was Severe, Pervasive, and Objectively Offensive.**

Under *Davis*, the harassment must be "so severe, pervasive, and objectively offensive that it can be said to deprive the victims of access to the educational opportunities or benefits provided by the school." 526 U.S. at 650. The record establishes this threshold.

On June 30, 2025, the harasser physically pursued Plaintiff through the apartment, confirmed she was following him, and stated she was "coming up" to his room. (Exhibit B-0; Exhibit D, Signal Transcript.) Plaintiff locked himself in his bedroom and fled the apartment the next day. The harasser's conduct escalated after Plaintiff sought help: she harassed him while he moved out (Exhibit S), and the landlord witnessed her behavior. Plaintiff's sworn declaration and the physician's letter documented that Plaintiff feared the harasser had access to an unregistered firearm. (Exhibit R; Exhibit F.) This fear was objectively reasonable given the harasser's statements about the Second Amendment and her pattern of retaliation.

The harassment continued throughout the summer and fall. On September 17, 2025, the harasser waited outside Plaintiff's Philosophy seminar classroom, causing Plaintiff to experience severe distress and requiring hours of recovery time. (Exhibit C, External Faculty Witness Declarations; Exhibit D, Facebook Messenger conversation.) External Faculty Witness, an expert academic mentor, declared that Plaintiff cannot focus on his PhD research under these conditions. (Exhibit C.) Medical documentation confirms that "proximity to his abuser" led Plaintiff to take more medication than prescribed and that his mental health conditions worsened due to homelessness and fear. (Exhibit F, Treating Physician letter October 1, 2025.)

The harassment was severe in nature (physical pursuit, stalking, weapon concern), pervasive in duration (June through October and beyond), and objectively offensive—no reasonable person would tolerate being chased through their home and later stalked outside their classroom. The University never offered even a mutual no-contact order, the most basic supportive measure available under Title IX. (Exhibit Z-c, Slide 40.)

**D. The Harassment and the University's Deliberate Indifference Denied Plaintiff Equal Access to Education.**

*Davis* requires that the harassment "deprive the victims of access to the educational opportunities or benefits provided by the school." 526 U.S. at 650. Plaintiff was rendered homeless for over 90 days (Exhibit A), unable to attend classes in person without safety measures (Exhibit I), forced into precarious couch-surfing arrangements (Exhibit A), and ultimately had his Zoom access revoked by Department Chair Selcer on October 17, 2025, despite a faculty member having previously authorized it. (Exhibits Q, V, W.) Selcer's written directives conditioned Plaintiff's continued remote attendance on "full engagement" with offices that had already disclaimed jurisdiction and threatened him. (Exhibit J.)

Plaintiff's academic work suffered. Professor External Faculty Witness declared that Plaintiff's "education and research have been severely derailed." (Exhibit C.) Treating Physician warned that the stress of homelessness and fear "would impair his academic functioning." (Exhibit F.) The University's refusal to provide supportive measures directly caused this deprivation. Under *Davis*, institutional liability attaches where the school's deliberate indifference causes the victim to lose access to educational benefits. The University's failure to provide housing assistance, no-contact orders, or safety measures forced Plaintiff into a position where he could not safely attend classes, use library facilities, or engage in the PhD program's in-person expectations.

**E. The University's Conduct Discriminated on the Basis of Sex.**

Under *Davis* and *Doe v. University of the Sciences*, 961 F.3d 203 (3d Cir. 2020), a Title IX plaintiff must show that the school discriminated on the basis of sex. The Third Circuit emphasized that courts should not superimpose additional doctrinal tests; the alleged facts, if true, must support a plausible inference of sex discrimination. 961 F.3d at 209.

Here, Plaintiff explicitly raised the question of sex discrimination in his July 23, 2025 email to Dean Wasilko, in which he asked: "I can't help but wonder whether your response would have been the same had the genders in this situation been reversed." (Exhibit B-0A.) That question remains unanswered because the University never investigated the harassment on the merits. The record shows that the University's Title IX Coordinator initially accepted jurisdiction (Exhibit B-5) but then disclaimed jurisdiction after Plaintiff engaged in protected activity (Exhibit B-6). No legitimate, non-discriminatory reason explains why a case involving a male victim and female harasser—with physical pursuit, stalking, and a gun threat—was deemed outside TAP 61 or TAP 31's definition of prohibited conduct. Under *Price Waterhouse v. Hopkins*, 490 U.S. 228 (1989), and *Desert Palace, Inc. v. Costa*, 539 U.S. 90 (2003), a plaintiff need not produce direct evidence of discrimination; circumstantial evidence, including suspicious timing and disparate treatment, can support an inference of sex-based discrimination. The University's pattern of minimizing a male victim's reports while providing no supportive measures—despite having clear resources available (as evidenced by Wasilko's admission that "first year buildings" were available but "not helpful"—Exhibit G)—creates a plausible inference of sex discrimination.

Moreover, the Third Circuit in *Doe v. University of the Sciences* expressly rejected the requirement that a plaintiff "must show that the school's response was motivated by sex" at the pleading stage; instead, the alleged facts must "support a plausible inference that a federally-funded college or university discriminated against a person on the basis of sex." 961 F.3d at 209. Here, the facts—gender-reversal hypothetical, immediate dismissal, lack of any supportive measures for a male victim, retaliatory threat—satisfy this standard.

**Conclusion**

For the foregoing reasons, Plaintiff has established all four elements of deliberate indifference under Title IX: actual knowledge by officials with authority, a clearly unreasonable response (deliberate indifference), severe and pervasive harassment, and denial of equal educational access. The University's 45-day period of complete inaction, its arbitrary jurisdictional reversal after protected activity, its threat of disciplinary charges against the victim, and its refusal to provide even the most basic supportive measures—all while Plaintiff was homeless and medically compromised—constitute deliberate indifference as a matter of law. This Court should hold Defendant Duquesne University liable for violating Title IX.
