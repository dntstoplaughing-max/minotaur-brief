**ARGUMENT**

**A. Legal Standard**

Under Title II of the ADA and Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act, a plaintiff must establish that (1) he is a qualified individual with a disability; (2) the defendant had notice of his disability and his need for an accommodation; (3) he requested a reasonable accommodation; (4) the defendant denied that accommodation; and (5) he suffered harm as a result. *A.J.T. v. Osseo Area Schs.*, 605 U.S. ___ (2025). The Supreme Court recently made clear that these claims in educational settings are governed by the same ordinary failure-to-accommodate analysis that applies elsewhere; there is no heightened “bad faith or gross misjudgment” standard. *Id.* at *2–3. Accordingly, this Court applies the familiar framework.

**B. Plaintiff Is a Qualified Individual with a Disability**

Plaintiff David Mullins is a doctoral candidate in good academic standing and has been diagnosed by his treating physician with ADHD and depression. (Ex. F, Oct. 1, 2025 Letter from Treating Physician.) Treating Physician further opined that the stress of plaintiff’s housing instability and his fear of a specific individual on campus “worsens his mental health conditions” and “is expected to negatively impact his graduate education.” (*Id.*) The physician expressly recommended “all measures to protect him from his abuser and to provide stable housing.” (*Id.*) These conditions—ADHD and depression—substantially limit major life activities including learning, concentrating, and interacting with others. Plaintiff is thus an individual with a disability within the meaning of 42 U.S.C. § 12102 and 29 U.S.C. § 705(20).

**C. Defendants Had Adequate Notice of Plaintiff’s Disability and His Need for an Accommodation**

On August 13, 2025, plaintiff submitted medical documentation from Treating Physician stating that plaintiff “does not feel safe around a specific individual on campus” and that it is “reasonable for him to have this fear.” (Ex. F, Aug. 13 Letter.) The same letter recommended “every effort be made to avoid” sharing space with that individual. (*Id.*) Plaintiff thereafter requested remote (Zoom) attendance for his graduate seminars as a medically necessary accommodation. (Ex. I, Mullins Aug. 25 email; Ex. I-1, Sept. 2 email.) The record shows that defendant Duquesne University was aware of plaintiff’s disability diagnosis and his specific requested accommodation. Indeed, Department Chair Selcer acknowledged plaintiff’s “fear of Respondent” and his desire for “safety measures” including remote attendance. (Ex. I, Selcer Aug. 25 email.) Notice is indisputable.

**D. Plaintiff’s Request for a Reasonable Accommodation Was Denied**

Although defendant initially permitted a temporary three-week period of Zoom access, it imposed an arbitrary “countdown” expiring on September 12, 2025, after which plaintiff was required to return to in-person attendance regardless of his medical documentation. (Ex. I, Selcer Aug. 25 email (“This remote seminar attendance … is authorized only for the first three weeks … at this time.”); Ex. J, Selcer Sept. 3 email (“[W]e expect you to attend seminars in person from September 15 forward.”).) This conditional, time-limited measure was designed to expire, making the accommodation “dead on arrival.” *Oross v. Kutztown Univ.*, No. 21-5032 (E.D. Pa. July 25, 2023) (accommodation request “dead on arrival” where university imposed ad hoc blanket denial). Even after the temporary period ended, Selcer refused to continue Zoom access, telling plaintiff that unless university offices intervened, he must attend in person. (Ex. J.) When plaintiff later tried to attend a seminar via Zoom in October 2025, the professor who had previously authorized remote participation (Dr. Faculty C) was overridden by Selcer, who instructed Faculty C that no “compelling reason” existed and cut off the Zoom link. (Ex. Q, Oct. 16–17 email sequence; Ex. V, Selcer Oct. 17 email; Ex. W, Faculty C authorization and subsequent reversal.) The denial was complete and final.

**E. The Denial Was Unreasonable and Constituted a Failure to Engage in the Interactive Process**

Defendant never conducted an individualized assessment of plaintiff’s limitations or the feasibility of Zoom as an accommodation. *Oross*, slip op. at *4 (“University applied blanket no-remote policy with no written basis, no individualized assessment of plaintiff’s request.”). Selcer testified that his objection to Zoom was based on personal preference—“divides attention”—and a vague “post-COVID departmental finding” with no written policy, pedagogical study, or departmental standard to support the denial. (TRO Tr. at *:*; see also SJ 7.3–7.4.) The Faculty Handbook explicitly states that course modality governs faculty delivery, not individual student attendance accommodations; no policy prohibits individual Zoom use. (Ex. Y, Faculty Handbook 2025–2026 at 10–11.) To the contrary, the same department already uses Zoom for comparable graduate coursework: Faculty D’s syllabus states, “I will also keep a Zoom meeting running, so if you are not feeling well, please attend that session remotely!” (Ex. N, Faculty D Syllabus (Spring 2025).) Thus, Zoom was plainly reasonable and feasible.

Defendant also failed to engage in any interactive process. “To show that an employer failed to participate in the interactive process, a disabled employee must demonstrate: 1) the employer knew about the employee’s disability; 2) the employee requested accommodations or assistance for his or her disability; 3) the employer did not make a good faith effort to assist the employee in seeking accommodations; and 4) the employee could have been reasonably accommodated but for the employer’s lack of good faith.” *Taylor v. Phoenixville Sch. Dist.*, 184 F.3d 296, 316 (3d Cir. 1999). Here, all four elements are satisfied:

- Defendant knew of plaintiff’s disability (Ex. F; Ex. I);
- Plaintiff repeatedly requested Zoom as an accommodation (Ex. I, Aug. 25 email; Ex. I-1, Sept. 2 email);
- Defendant did not conduct any interactive process: it did not assess plaintiff’s specific limitations, evaluate possible accommodations, discuss alternatives, or involve disability services (SJ 7.2);
- Plaintiff could have been accommodated with Zoom—the same technology already in use by the same department (Ex. N)—but for defendant’s bad faith. *Taylor*, 184 F.3d at 316 (“[A]n employer who acts in bad faith in the interactive process will be liable if the jury can reasonably conclude that the employee would have been able to perform the job with accommodations.”).

Defendant’s imposition of a three-week countdown and its refusal to engage further is textbook bad faith. *See* Ex. I (Selcer conditioning continued Zoom on plaintiff’s participation in Title IX and Student Conduct proceedings, not on medical need); Ex. I-1 (plaintiff’s demand for retraction of retaliatory countdown); Ex. J (Selcer’s final directive refusing to extend Zoom). The university cannot “fail to engage the employee in the interactive process of finding accommodations, increase the disabled employee’s job responsibilities, and then simply document the employee’s failures.” *Taylor*, 184 F.3d at 312. Here, defendant increased plaintiff’s burden by requiring in-person attendance despite medical documentation, and then documented his failure to comply. That is precisely the conduct *Taylor* condemns.

**F. Plaintiff Suffered Harm as a Direct Result of the Denial**

The denial of Zoom access caused plaintiff substantial harm. Without the accommodation, plaintiff was forced either to forgo class attendance or to risk encountering a person whom his physician deemed a reasonable source of fear. This fear and the resulting stress “worsens his mental health conditions” and “negatively impact[ed] his graduate education.” (Ex. F, Oct. 1 Letter.) Plaintiff’s academic mentor, External Faculty Witness, attested that plaintiff’s “education and research have been severely derailed” and that he “cannot focus on his PhD research when he is experiencing encounters that cause this level of distress and require hours of recovery time.” (Ex. C, External Faculty Witness Decl. ¶ 3; Supp. Decl. ¶ 10.) The encounter on September 17, 2025, when plaintiff’s former roommate “literally waiting outside of second class” (Ex. C, Sept. 17 Facebook message), confirms the palpable, ongoing danger. Plaintiff also faced 90+ days of homelessness while the university refused to implement supportive measures. (Ex. A, Housing Crisis Timeline; Ex. F, Oct. 1 Letter (“He reports homelessness since June 30th … likely worsens his mental health conditions.”).) The denial of the requested accommodation—the simple, feasible provision of Zoom—directly contributed to this cascade of educational and personal harm.

For these reasons, defendant Duquesne University violated Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act and Title II of the Americans with Disabilities Act. Plaintiff respectfully requests that the Court deny summary judgment and find that defendant failed to accommodate his disability as a matter of law.
