# ARGUMENT

Plaintiff David Mullins respectfully submits that Defendants' Motion for Summary Judgment on Count V (Civil Conspiracy) must be denied because the record contains genuine disputes of material fact on every element of the claim. Under Pennsylvania law, a civil conspiracy requires "(1) a combination or agreement; (2) an unlawful act or an unlawful means; (3) overt acts in furtherance; (4) malice, i.e., intent to injure; and (5) resulting harm." *Thompson Coal Co. v. Pike Coal Co.*, 412 A.2d at 472-73. Each element is supported by record evidence that a reasonable jury could credit.

## I. DEFENDANTS' ARGUMENT THAT BLAIR'S CONSULTATION WITH SELCER WAS "ROUTINE ACADEMIC ADMINISTRATION" IS CONTRADICTED BY DIRECT EVIDENCE OF JOINT DETERMINATION WITH THE CONFLICTED PARTY

Defendants contend that Dean A's consultation with Dr. Selcer was merely routine academic administration—checking course availability and academic progress. Dkt. 34 at 10. The record flatly contradicts this characterization.

**The evidence shows coordinated decision-making, not independent review.** On October 22, 2025, Dean A wrote: "I did need to consult Dr. Selcer as instructor to secure some additional details. **Both Dr. Selcer and I strongly concur** that it is appropriate for you to remain enrolled in your required teaching seminar." Exhibit K-2 (emphasis added). This is a written admission of joint determination—"both of us, together"—rather than independent review. A reasonable jury could find this constitutes an agreement between two actors, satisfying the first element of conspiracy. *Thompson Coal*, 412 A.2d at 472-73.

**The consultation violated the University's own conflict-of-interest policy.** TAP 33 § IV.4 requires *independent* review of conflict-of-interest disclosures. Dean A's "consultation" with the very person against whom the conflict was disclosed—Selcer himself—does not constitute independent review under any reading of the policy. Exhibit K-2; TAP 33 § IV.4.

**The timing demonstrates retaliatory motive.** Dean A consulted Selcer about a conflict-of-interest disclosure concerning Selcer himself—seven days after Plaintiff cross-examined Selcer under oath at the TRO hearing on October 15, 2025. Exhibit K-2; TRO Tr. This temporal proximity alone supports an inference of retaliatory motive and malice. *Jalil v. Avdel Corp.*, 873 F.2d at 708 (two-day temporal proximity sufficient to establish causation); *Lauren W. ex rel. Jean W. v. DeFlaminis*, 480 F.3d at 267 (temporal proximity combined with contextual evidence of retaliatory animus sufficient to establish causation).

**Under the intracorporate conspiracy doctrine, this coordination remains actionable.** *Heffernan v. Hunter*, 189 F.3d at 412 (conspiracy possible where conduct occurs outside scope of employment or for personal benefit). A reasonable jury could find that Dean A's consultation with the conflicted party—contrary to written policy—was not "actuated, at least in part, by a purpose to serve the master," as required by *Brumfield v. Sanders*, 232 F.3d at 380 (quoting Restatement (Second) of Agency § 228). Rather, the joint determination served to protect Selcer and the institution from Plaintiff's conflict-of-interest disclosure.

## II. DEFENDANTS' ARGUMENT THAT THE CONSULTATION SHOWED "INTENT TO HELP, NOT MALICE" IS UNDERMINED BY THE PATTERN OF COORDINATED ADVERSE ACTIONS

Defendants contend the consultation "resulted in assurances of fair treatment and offers of academic support." Dkt. 34 at 10. This characterization ignores the broader pattern of coordinated conduct that a reasonable jury could find demonstrates malice—"intent to injure." *Thompson Coal*, 412 A.2d at 472-73.

**The conspiracy involved systematic removal of oversight.** Four defendants systematically removed OCR and senior administrative oversight from correspondence at critical junctures, preventing Plaintiff from receiving corrective information. Selcer removed Dean A herself from the September 3 "final directive." Exhibits B-3 through B-7; Selcer Sept. 3 email. A reasonable jury could find this pattern of removing oversight actors was designed to maintain defendants' control without accountability.

**Dean A's silence in the face of known violations furthered the conspiracy.** Plaintiff sent multiple escalations directly to Dean A citing federal law, identifying specific violations, and requesting intervention. Dean A did not intervene. Exhibits K-2, K-5; SAC ¶¶ 326, 338, 417. Dean A's silence, combined with her active coordination with Selcer, furthered the conspiracy's objective of maintaining defendants' control without independent oversight. *Moser v. DeSetta*, 589 A.2d at 682 (concealment of material fact amounts to culpable misrepresentation no less than intentional false statement).

**The "assurances of fair treatment" were illusory.** While Dean A and Selcer jointly determined that Plaintiff should remain enrolled, Selcer simultaneously imposed conditions that made successful completion impossible: attendance requirements, missed-content meetings, syllabus drafts, and teaching demonstrations. Exhibit K-2. A reasonable jury could find these were not good-faith accommodations but rather conditions designed to set Plaintiff up for failure, demonstrating the "malice" element. *Hutchison v. Luddy*, 870 A.2d at 772 (punitive damages appropriate where defendant acted in conscious disregard of risk of harm).

**The pattern of antagonism independently establishes malice.** *Kachmar v. SunGard Data Systems, Inc.*, 109 F.3d at 177 (circumstantial evidence of "pattern of antagonism" following protected conduct can give rise to inference of causation). Here, the pattern includes: Wasilko's housing denial (Exhibit G); Simpson's jurisdiction disclaimer after initially accepting jurisdiction (Exhibits B-5, B-6); Sawa's retaliation threat (Exhibit B-7); Selcer's "final directive" with three-week Zoom countdown (Exhibit J); and Selcer's fabrication of "performance issues" contradicted by the source he cited (Exhibits X-7, X-8). A reasonable jury could view this pattern as evidencing intent to injure.

## III. THE RECORD CONTAINS AMPLE EVIDENCE OF UNDERLYING TORTS SUPPORTING THE CONSPIRACY CLAIM

Defendants argue no underlying tort exists because "Dean A's TAP 33 analysis was correct on the policy's plain terms." Dkt. 34 at 10. This argument fails for multiple reasons.

**TAP 33 does not even apply to students.** Dean A herself admitted this in Exhibit K-5: "First, as a student, you are not a 'Covered Party' as defined by TAP 33. Second, the scenario... does not fall within the limited definition of a 'Conflict of Interest' in TAP 33." If the policy does not apply, then Dean A's analysis—correct or not—is irrelevant. The underlying torts are not about TAP 33 interpretation; they are about retaliation, fraud, and deliberate indifference.

**The underlying torts committed by co-conspirators include:** 36 false statements fabricating a meeting requirement (Conspiracy 1); systematic OCR removal across four actors (Conspiracy 2); and Selcer's fabrication of "performance issues" contradicted by the source he cited on the same day. Exhibits X-7, X-8. Under *Thompson Coal*, 412 A.2d at 472-73, once a defendant joins a conspiracy, that defendant is jointly and severally liable for all tortious acts committed by any co-conspirator in furtherance of the conspiracy. Dean A need not have personally committed these acts.

**The fabricated "performance issues" constitute fraud.** *Gibbs v. Ernst*, 647 A.2d at 889 requires: (1) a representation; (2) materiality; (3) falsity with knowledge or recklessness; (4) intent to mislead; (5) justifiable reliance; (6) resulting injury. On February 13, 2026, Selcer wrote that "there have now been performance issues raised by two of the three faculty members." Exhibit X-8. But on the *same day*, Faculty B wrote: "My office door is always open to you" and requested reassignment without any performance complaint. Exhibit X-7. And on February 10, 2026—just three days earlier—Selcer's meeting transcript contains no "performance issues" language. Exhibit X-6. A reasonable jury could find this representation was false, made with knowledge of its falsity, intended to threaten Plaintiff's stipend through 2029, and relied upon to Plaintiff's detriment.

**The "performance issues" threat itself is an overt act in furtherance of the conspiracy.** Selcer's email referenced a "recommendation to the Dean of Liberal Arts"—Dean A's exact title—demonstrating communication between Selcer and Dean A in the days preceding the fabricated threat. Exhibit X-8. This language did not appear in the February 10 meeting, proving intervening communication between Selcer and Dean A. Exhibits X-6, X-8. A reasonable jury could find this consultation and coordination was the agreement in action.

## IV. THE COORDINATED INSTITUTIONAL RESPONSES EVIDENCE AGREEMENT, NOT MERELY "WELL-ESTABLISHED PROCESSES"

Defendants argue that Plaintiff's characterization of coordinated institutional responses as "conspiracy" reflects his "refusal to engage with well-established university processes." Dkt. 25 at 6. This argument ignores the evidence of *abnormal* coordination that goes far beyond routine process.

**The evidence shows specific, documented coordination among defendants.** Dean A's admission that she consulted Selcer and they "strongly concur" is direct evidence of agreement. Exhibit K-2. Selcer's "recommendation to the Dean" language appearing only after intervening communication with Dean A is circumstantial evidence of ongoing coordination. Exhibits X-6, X-8. The systematic removal of OCR oversight across four actors—Wasilko, Selcer, Sawa, and Simpson—demonstrates a common plan. Exhibits B-3 through B-7.

**The intracorporate conspiracy doctrine does not bar this claim.** *Heffernan v. Hunter*, 189 F.3d at 412. Even if the doctrine applied to employees of the same entity, it requires that the challenged conduct occur within the scope of employment. *Brumfield v. Sanders*, 232 F.3d at 380. A reasonable jury could find that coordinating to remove OCR oversight, fabricating performance issues, and retaliating against a student for protected activity are not "actuated, at least in part, by a purpose to serve the master." *Id.* Such conduct serves the personal interests of the defendants—avoiding liability, maintaining control, and retaliating—not the university's legitimate interests. *Fitzgerald v. McCutcheon*, 410 A.2d at 1272 ("where the act of force is excessive and totally without responsibility or reason, the employer is not responsible as a matter of law").

**The "well-established processes" defense is contradicted by the evidence that defendants *departed from* those processes.** TAP 33 § IV.4 requires independent review. Dean A consulted the conflicted party. Exhibit K-2. Title IX regulations require supportive measures to be implemented promptly. 34 C.F.R. §106.44; Exhibit Z-c, Slide 38. Defendants conditioned supportive measures on meetings. Exhibits B-2, B-3, B-7. Student Conduct policy requires fair process. Sawa threatened charges for seeking help. Exhibit B-7. A reasonable jury could find these were not good-faith processes but coordinated acts of retaliation.

## V. SELCER'S CONTINUATION AS INSTRUCTOR WAS AN OVERT ACT IN FURTHERANCE OF THE CONSPIRACY

Defendants argue that Selcer's continuation as Plaintiff's instructor "was not retaliatory because he held that role before and after Plaintiff's protected activity—therefore not an overt act in furtherance of conspiracy." Dkt. 34 at 10 n.3. This argument ignores the nature of Selcer's continued instruction.

**The overt acts are not Selcer's mere existence as instructor, but the specific retaliatory conditions and actions he took in that role.** These include: (1) the September 3 "final directive" imposing a three-week Zoom countdown and threatening loss of remote access (Exhibit J); (2) the removal of Dean A from that directive's CC line, eliminating oversight (Exhibit J); (3) the October 17 revocation of Zoom access—two days after Selcer promised at the TRO hearing that Zoom would continue (Exhibits V, W, Q); (4) the February 13 fabrication of "performance issues" and threat to suspend stipend through 2029 (Exhibit X-8). Each of these is an overt act in furtherance of the conspiracy.

**Selcer's coordination with other defendants is the overt act.** The "recommendation to the Dean of Liberal Arts" (Dean A) demonstrates Selcer was acting in coordination with Dean A, not independently as an instructor. Exhibit X-8. The Zoom revocation involved Selcer contacting another professor (Faculty C), reframing the issue as a "compelling reason" test, and misrepresenting Faculty C's position. Exhibits V, W, Q. A reasonable jury could find these were overt acts of conspiracy, not routine instruction.

**The phrase "before and after protected activity" misstates the record.** While Selcer was Plaintiff's chair throughout, the *nature* of his conduct changed dramatically after Plaintiff's protected activity. After Plaintiff cross-examined Selcer at the TRO hearing (October 15), Selcer revoked Zoom access (October 17), coordinated with Dean A to deny the conflict-of-interest transfer (October 22), and fabricated performance issues (February 13). *Kengerski v. Harper*, 2021 WL 3619935, at *4 (protected activity need only be based on objectively reasonable belief); *Atkinson v. Lafayette College*, 460 F.3d at 456 (demanding Title IX compliance is protected activity); *Carvalho-Grevious v. Delaware State University*, 2017 WL 3446298, at *5 (at prima facie stage, plaintiff need only show protected activity was the "likely reason" for adverse action).
