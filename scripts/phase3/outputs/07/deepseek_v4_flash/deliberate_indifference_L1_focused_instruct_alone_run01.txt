**ARGUMENT**

Count I alleges that Duquesne University was deliberately indifferent to known sexual harassment in violation of Title IX, 20 U.S.C. § 1681. To establish liability under Title IX for student-on-student harassment, a plaintiff must demonstrate that (1) the funding recipient had actual knowledge of the harassment by an official with authority to institute corrective measures; (2) the recipient's response was clearly unreasonable in light of the known circumstances, constituting deliberate indifference; (3) the harassment was so severe, pervasive, and objectively offensive that it deprived the plaintiff of access to educational opportunities; and (4) the harassment occurred in the recipient's programs or activities. *Davis v. Monroe County Board of Education*, 526 U.S. 629, 650 (1999). The undisputed facts and documentary evidence establish each element.

**I. Duquesne Had Actual Knowledge Through Officials with Authority to Act**

Actual knowledge is established when notice of sexual harassment or allegations of sexual harassment is provided to "a recipient's Title IX Coordinator" or "any official of the recipient who has authority to institute corrective measures on behalf of the recipient." Exhibit Z-e (Training Slide 30, defining actual knowledge). The preamble to the 2020 Title IX regulations identifies as officials with authority to institute corrective measures: "Provost, VP for Student Affairs, Dean of Students, HR Director, etc." Exhibit Z-e (Slide 32).

Plaintiff's first report of harassment occurred on June 30, 2025, when he emailed Dean Wasilko, the Associate Vice President for Student Life and Dean of Students, describing a physical confrontation with his roommate Respondent—a fellow graduate student—during which Respondent "got within inches of my face, and aggressively followed me through the apartment," "pursued me up the stairs toward my room," and "only stopped just short of entering." Exhibit B-0. Plaintiff specifically stated he "no longer feel[s] safe" and requested "emergency housing options, protective campus measures, or relevant reporting mechanisms." *Id.* Dean Wasilko, as Dean of Students, is an official with authority to institute corrective measures, and his receipt of this report constituted actual knowledge. Exhibit Z-e (Slide 32). Furthermore, as noted in *Hall v. Millersville University*, where a university official receives a report of sexual misconduct and "filed it away" or failed to forward it, the university has actual knowledge for purposes of Title IX. *Hall v. Millersville University*, No. 19-3275, at *12 (3d Cir. Jan. 11, 2022).

On July 24, 2025, Plaintiff escalated his complaint directly to Title IX Coordinator Simpson, copying the university president, provost, and legal affairs, thereby providing actual knowledge to the official specifically designated to receive such reports. Exhibit B-1; Exhibit Z-e (Slide 30). Simpson acknowledged receipt and stated she was "in receipt of your email where you allege that there has been noncompliance with Title IX." Exhibit B-1. On August 13, 2025, Plaintiff submitted an Emergency Removal Request to Simpson, attaching sworn declarations and a physician's letter documenting safety concerns. Exhibit F; Exhibit B-5. Simpson responded the same day, acknowledging that Plaintiff's "concerns and allegations warrant further information gathering and review." Exhibit B-5. Actual knowledge is therefore undisputed.

**II. Duquesne's Response Was Clearly Unreasonable, Constituting Deliberate Indifference**

The Supreme Court in *Davis* defined deliberate indifference as a response that is "clearly unreasonable in light of the known circumstances." *Davis*, 526 U.S. at 648. The Third Circuit has adopted this standard and emphasized that courts must evaluate whether the university's actions were "clearly unreasonable"—not merely whether alternative responses might have been preferable. *Hall*, No. 19-3275, at *10. As the Sixth Circuit noted in *Foster v. Board of Regents of University of Michigan*, "minimal, ineffective, or belated efforts to respond to sexual harassment" are not exempt from being found clearly unreasonable as a matter of law. *Foster v. Board of Regents of University of Michigan*, No. 19-1314, at *11 (6th Cir. Dec. 11, 2020) (en banc). Here, Duquesne's conduct falls squarely within this proscription.

**A. Duquesne Provided No Supportive Measures for 45 Days Despite Repeated Requests**

Between June 30, 2025 and August 14, 2025—a period of 45 days—Defendants provided no supportive measures whatsoever. Statement of Undisputed Material Facts (SUMF) ¶ 8. This is despite Plaintiff's repeated, explicit requests for protective measures, including emergency housing, no-contact orders, and safety accommodations. Exhibit B-0 (June 30 request); Exhibit B-1 (July 24 escalation); Exhibit B-5A (August 13 detailed request specifying mutual no-contact orders, space separation, and Public Safety alert).

The Title IX Coordinator is responsible for "coordinating the effective implementation of supportive measures." Exhibit Z-c (Slide 38). Supportive measures are "available at any time after a report of sexual harassment," are "non-disciplinary" and "non-punitive," and must be "individualized" and "provided at no cost." Exhibit Z-c (Slides 38-39). Critically, these measures "are available with or without a formal complaint." Exhibit B-5A (Plaintiff's August 13 email citing 34 C.F.R. § 106.30). Yet Simpson performed "zero of the five mandatory obligations under § 106.44(a)": she did not promptly contact Plaintiff to discuss supportive measures, did not consider his wishes, did not inform him of the availability of supportive measures, and did not explain the process for filing a formal complaint. SUMF ¶ 10.

Instead, Defendants' communications consisted exclusively of requests to schedule meetings, directives to "engage with" offices, and jurisdictional disclaimers. SUMF ¶ 8. Dean Wasilko's initial response on July 1 was to tell Plaintiff the incident "doesn't sound like a code of conduct violation" and to suggest filing a police report. Exhibit B-0. On July 24, after Plaintiff contacted the Title IX Coordinator, Wasilko again directed Plaintiff to "please engage with these offices" and to schedule meetings. Exhibit B-2. On August 13, Simpson responded to Plaintiff's emergency removal request by again demanding a meeting, writing that "it is necessary for representatives of these offices to meet with you to gather additional information." Exhibit B-5. Conditioning supportive measures on a meeting is directly contrary to the regulatory requirement that such measures be offered promptly and without preconditions. *See* Exhibit Z-c (Slide 38) (supportive measures "available at any time after a report"); 34 C.F.R. § 106.30 (supportive measures "offered as appropriate and as reasonably available, without fee or charge").

**B. Simpson's Reversal of Jurisdiction Without New Facts Was Clearly Unreasonable**

On August 13 at 2:02 PM, Simpson acknowledged Plaintiff's concerns "warrant further information gathering and review" and offered to "discuss your current needs relating to supportive measures." Exhibit B-5. Nineteen hours later, on August 14 at 9:42 AM, after Plaintiff sent three emails refusing a meeting and demanding written communications, Simpson reversed her position, stating "the Title IX Office does not have jurisdiction over your report and cannot take action or provide assistance as requested." Exhibit B-6. Critically, "[n]o new factual information about the underlying complaint entered the record between B-5 and B-6." SUMF ¶ 7. The only intervening events were Plaintiff's emails—which consisted entirely of protected activity under § 106.71, including citing Title IX regulations, requesting written responses, and restoring OCR to the CC line. Exhibits B-5A, B-5B, B-5C. This reversal in response to Plaintiff's assertion of his procedural rights constitutes a clearly unreasonable response. As the Third Circuit recognized in *Doe v. University of the Sciences*, courts must evaluate whether alleged facts support "a plausible inference that a federally-funded college or university discriminated against a person on the basis of sex." *Doe v. University of the Sciences*, 961 F.3d 203, 209 (3d Cir. 2020). The timing and substance of Simpson's reversal support precisely such an inference.

Furthermore, Simpson's conclusion that "the reported alleged conduct does not meet TAP 61, nor TAP 31's definitions of prohibited conduct, even if proven to be true" reflects a fundamental misunderstanding of Title IX's requirements. Exhibit B-6. A student's physical pursuit, confrontation, and stalking—particularly when accompanied by a documented reasonable fear of harm—is precisely the type of conduct that Title IX regulations are designed to address. *See* 34 C.F.R. § 106.30 (defining sexual harassment to include "unwelcome conduct determined by a reasonable person to be so severe, pervasive, and objectively offensive that it effectively denies a person equal access to the recipient's education program or activity").

**C. Duquesne Failed to Follow Its Own Policies and Regulatory Requirements**

Duquesne's failures extended beyond the absence of supportive measures. Simpson failed to offer Plaintiff appeal rights following the B-6 dismissal, as required by § 106.45(b)(8). SUMF ¶ 9. She failed to send simultaneous written notice to the respondent, as required by § 106.45(b)(3)(iii). *Id.* These procedural failures are consistent with the institutional pattern of non-response that the Third Circuit found actionable in *Hall*—where Millersville's failure to forward a sexual misconduct report to the Title IX Coordinator and its failure to contact the victim constituted a clearly unreasonable response. *Hall*, No. 19-3275, at *12-13. Just as "Sehl did not forward Wiberg's report and did not discuss it with anyone until after Karlie's murder," *id.* at *12, Duquesne officials repeatedly failed to take appropriate action on Plaintiff's reports.

The contrast with *McAvoy v. Dickinson College* is instructive. In *McAvoy*, the court affirmed summary judgment for the university where the school implemented a no-contact directive, provided academic and mental health accommodations, conducted a five-month investigation, and imposed a semester of probation. *McAvoy v. Dickinson College*, No. 23-2939, at *2 (3d Cir. Aug. 16, 2024). Here, Duquesne did none of these things. The court in *McAvoy* emphasized that "[d]elays alone do not establish deliberate indifference" where the school "maintained ongoing communication and support." *Id.* But Duquesne maintained no communication or support—it merely demanded meetings and ultimately disclaimed jurisdiction entirely.

**III. The Harassment Was Severe, Pervasive, and Objectively Offensive**

The Supreme Court in *Davis* requires that harassment be "so severe, pervasive, and objectively offensive that it can be said to deprive the victims of access to the educational opportunities or benefits provided by the school." *Davis*, 526 U.S. at 650. Plaintiff's evidence satisfies this standard.

On June 30, 2025, Respondent confronted Plaintiff in the kitchen, "got within inches of my face," "aggressively followed me through the apartment," confirmed she was following him when asked, pursued him up the stairs toward his bedroom stating "I'm coming up," and only stopped when he locked himself in his room. Exhibit B-0; Exhibit D (Signal Transcript & Timeline). This encounter was the culmination of a pattern of escalating conduct: Respondent had previously locked Plaintiff out of the apartment (June 27), engaged in manipulative behavior regarding the air conditioning (June 19-25), and, after Plaintiff's move-out, harassed him throughout the process. Exhibit D.

The severity of the harassment is underscored by Plaintiff's documented fear. Plaintiff informed Dean Wasilko, his department chair, and the DGS that he "no longer feel[s] safe" and that he "fear[s] for my safety in any context where she knows my whereabouts." Exhibit B-0; Exhibit R. He specifically reported that Respondent had "expressed strong enthusiasm for the idea that, in her view, the Second Amendment should mean firearm regulations should be less of a hassle than they are." Exhibit R. Treating Physician, Plaintiff's treating physician, opined that Plaintiff's fear was "reasonable" and recommended "every effort be made to avoid" Plaintiff sharing a living space with Respondent, noting that doing so "would cause him a great deal of stress, would be highly likely to worsen his medical condition, and could impair his academic functioning." Exhibit F (Medical Letter, Aug. 13, 2025). A second medical letter confirmed that Plaintiff's homelessness since June 30 "has been very stressful and likely worsens his mental health conditions," and that "[w]orsening his mental health conditions is expected to negatively impact his graduate education." Exhibit F (Medical Letter, Oct. 1, 2025).

**IV. The Harassment Denied Plaintiff Equal Educational Access**

The harassment and Defendants' deliberate indifference directly denied Plaintiff access to educational opportunities. Plaintiff was forced to flee his home on July 1, 2025, at substantial personal cost. Exhibit B-0; Exhibit D. His bank account balance fell to $16.65 on July 25 and to negative $2.55 on August 12. SUMF ¶ 11. He lacked permanent housing throughout the 45-day period at issue. *Id.* The physician's letter directly links these conditions to impairment of academic functioning. Exhibit F. The Third Circuit in *Doe v. University of the Sciences* held that a plaintiff need not "superimpose additional doctrinal tests" onto Title IX; rather, "the alleged facts, if true, must support a plausible inference that a federally-funded college or university discriminated against a person on the basis of sex." *Doe*, 961 F.3d at 209. Here, the causal chain is direct: Respondent's harassment forced Plaintiff from his housing; Duquesne's deliberate indifference—its refusal to offer any supportive measures for 45 days—left him homeless, financially destitute, and medically impaired; and these conditions "would impair his academic functioning." Exhibit F.

The harassment occurred in Duquesne's programs and activities, including Plaintiff's on-campus housing and his graduate program in the Department of Philosophy. The university's disciplinary authority over Respondent is not in dispute; as the Supreme Court noted in *Davis*, liability attaches where "the harasser is under the school's disciplinary authority." *Davis*, 526 U.S. at 646. Respondent was a fellow graduate student at Duquesne, subject to the same Student Code of Conduct and Title IX policies. Exhibit S (Plaintiff listing multiple potential conduct violations).

**CONCLUSION**

Duquesne University had actual knowledge of severe, pervasive, and objectively offensive sexual harassment directed at Plaintiff. Despite this knowledge, the university failed to offer any supportive measures for 45 days, demanded meetings as a precondition for assistance, reversed its jurisdictional determination in response to Plaintiff's assertion of his rights, and ultimately disclaimed responsibility entirely. This response was clearly unreasonable under the known circumstances and constitutes deliberate indifference as a matter of law. Plaintiff respectfully requests that this Court deny summary judgment as to Count I and proceed to trial.
