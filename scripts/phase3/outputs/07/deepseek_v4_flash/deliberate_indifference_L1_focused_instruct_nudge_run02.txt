## ARGUMENT

Plaintiff David Mullins is entitled to judgment on Count I because Defendant Duquesne University acted with deliberate indifference to known, severe, and pervasive sexual harassment, thereby denying Plaintiff equal access to educational opportunities in violation of Title IX, 20 U.S.C. § 1681. The undisputed material facts establish each element of a Title IX deliberate indifference claim as articulated by the Supreme Court in *Davis v. Monroe County Board of Education*, 526 U.S. 629 (1999), and as applied by the Third Circuit.

### I. The University Had Actual Knowledge of Sexual Harassment Through Officials with Authority to Institute Corrective Measures

Actual knowledge exists when notice of sexual harassment or allegations of sexual harassment is provided to a recipient's Title IX Coordinator or any official with authority to institute corrective measures on behalf of the recipient. *See* Exhibit Z-e at Slide 30 (defining actual knowledge under 34 C.F.R. § 106.30). "This could include: Provost, VP for Student Affairs, Dean of Students, HR Director, etc." *Id.* at Slide 32.

Here, actual knowledge was established no later than June 30, 2025, when Plaintiff reported the physical confrontation, pursuit, and threat to Dean Wasilko, the Associate Vice President for Student Life and Dean of Students. Exhibit B-0. Dean Wasilko is an official with "authority to institute corrective measures" under any reasonable definition. *See* Exhibit Z-e at Slide 32. Moreover, Title IX Coordinator Alicia Simpson received Plaintiff's detailed report on July 24, 2025, and acknowledged that her office had been "notified" and that she was "in receipt of your email where you allege that there has been noncompliance with Title IX." Exhibit B-1. Simpson further acknowledged that her "primary roles" included ensuring Plaintiff's "health and safety." *Id.* Actual knowledge is therefore indisputable.

### II. The University's Response Was Clearly Unreasonable and Constituted Deliberate Indifference

A school is deliberately indifferent where its "response to sexual harassment is clearly unreasonable in light of the known circumstances." *Davis*, 526 U.S. at 648. The standard does not require courts to excuse "minimal, ineffective, or belated efforts to respond." *Foster v. Board of Regents of University of Michigan*, No. 19-1314 (6th Cir. Dec. 11, 2020) (en banc) (Key Passage 2). Here, the University's response falls far below any reasonable standard.

**A. No Supportive Measures Were Offered for 45 Days Despite Repeated Requests.**

Between June 30, 2025, and August 14, 2025—a period of 45 days—the University offered Plaintiff no supportive measures whatsoever. This is not a case where the school adopted escalating measures or maintained ongoing communication and support. *Cf. McAvoy v. Dickinson College*, No. 23-2939 (3d Cir. Aug. 16, 2024) (affirming summary judgment where school issued no-contact directive, provided academic and mental health accommodations, and conducted a five-month investigation). Rather, the University's communications consisted exclusively of requests to schedule meetings, directives to "engage with" offices, and jurisdictional disclaimers. Exhibits B-0 through B-6.

The Title IX Coordinator's mandatory obligations under 34 C.F.R. § 106.44(a) are clear: she must promptly contact the complainant to discuss supportive measures, consider the complainant's wishes, inform the complainant of the availability of supportive measures, and explain the process for filing a formal complaint. Exhibit Z-b at Slide 64 (required response includes "explaining and providing supportive measures"). Simpson performed zero of these obligations. Even when Plaintiff explicitly requested emergency removal and supportive measures on August 13, 2025, citing the specific regulatory framework, Exhibit B-5A, Simpson's response was not to implement measures but to demand a meeting. Exhibit B-5. She then reversed her preliminary jurisdictional determination just nineteen hours later, citing no new factual information about the underlying complaint, and disclaimed all responsibility. Exhibit B-6. The only intervening events were Plaintiff's three emails consisting entirely of protected activity under 34 C.F.R. § 106.71. Exhibits B-5A, B-5B, B-5C. A response that conditions supportive measures on meetings and then withdraws jurisdiction after the complainant engages in protected activity is the epitome of "clearly unreasonable." *See Hall v. Millersville University*, No. 19-3275 (3d Cir. Jan. 11, 2022) (Key Passage 1) (failure to forward reports and failure to contact victim can constitute clearly unreasonable response).

**B. Simpson's Jurisdictional Reversal Was Baseless and Procedurally Deficient.**

Simpson's August 14 determination that the conduct "does not meet TAP 61, nor TAP 31's definitions of prohibited conduct, even if proven to be true," Exhibit B-6, is indefensible. The conduct reported included physical pursuit, yelling, following, locking Plaintiff out of his apartment, and a verbal confrontation that caused Plaintiff to flee his home. Exhibits B-0, D. Such conduct falls squarely within the definition of sexual harassment because it was sex-based and created a hostile environment. The University's own training materials define supportive measures as "non-disciplinary, non-punitive, individualized" and "available at any time after a report of sexual harassment." Exhibit Z-c at Slides 38-39. Simpson's blanket refusal to provide any assistance—including a no-contact order, housing modifications, or safety planning—violated the University's duty to respond promptly and equitably.

Furthermore, Simpson failed to comply with mandatory procedural requirements. She did not offer Plaintiff appeal rights following the dismissal, as required by § 106.45(b)(8). She did not send simultaneous written notice to the respondent, as required by § 106.45(b)(3)(iii). Exhibit B-6. These failures, combined with the substantive refusal to act, demonstrate a response that is "clearly unreasonable in light of the known circumstances." *Davis*, 526 U.S. at 648.

**C. The University's Conduct Is Not Saved by Its Offers to Meet.**

The University's insistence on meetings as a precondition to providing supportive measures is itself unreasonable. Supportive measures are administrative directives that can be implemented immediately—"email orders to Public Safety/Registrar/Access Control; written notice from Title IX." Exhibit B-5A. The Title IX Coordinator's duty to "discuss your current needs relating to supportive measures," Exhibit B-5, is not a license to delay or deny those measures. *See* Exhibit Z-c at Slide 39 (supportive measures are "provided at no cost" and "available at any time after a report"). Conditioning safety measures on a meeting with the complainant who has already provided sworn declarations and a physician's letter, Exhibit F, is comparable to the conduct condemned in *Hall v. Millersville*, where the university "filed away" a report and failed to contact the victim. *Hall*, Key Passage 2. Here, Simpson acknowledged that Plaintiff's "concerns and allegations warrant further information gathering and review," Exhibit B-5, but then did nothing—no contact, no investigation, no interim safety measures—before reversing course entirely.

### III. The Harassment Was Severe, Pervasive, and Objectively Offensive

To establish deliberate indifference under Title IX, a plaintiff must show that the harassment was "so severe, pervasive, and objectively offensive that it can be said to deprive the victims of access to the educational opportunities or benefits provided by the school." *Davis*, 526 U.S. at 650. Plaintiff's evidence satisfies this standard.

The harassment began with a pattern of manipulation regarding air conditioning, escalated to a lockout, and culminated in a physical confrontation where the harasser "approached me in the kitchen; when I calmly stated that I was not interested in talking, she began yelling at me, got within inches of my face, and aggressively followed me through the apartment. . . . She pursued me up the stairs toward my room, saying 'I'm coming up,' and only stopped just short of entering." Exhibit B-0. Plaintiff was forced to lock himself in his room. *Id.* The harasser had previously expressed enthusiasm for firearm access, Exhibit R, and Plaintiff reasonably feared for his safety. He fled his home, became homeless, and his bank account balance fell to $16.65 and then negative $2.55. Statement of Undisputed Material Facts ¶ 11. His physician attested that sharing a living space with the harasser "would be highly likely to worsen his medical condition, and could impair his academic functioning." Exhibit F (August 13 letter). The harassment was objectively severe; no reasonable person would discount the physical pursuit and threat of invasion of a private room.

### IV. The University's Deliberate Indifference Denied Plaintiff Equal Educational Access

A school's deliberate indifference "must cause students to undergo harassment or make them liable or vulnerable to it" and must "subject" the student to discrimination. *Davis*, 526 U.S. at 644-45. Here, the University's failure to act directly caused Plaintiff's continued deprivation. Because of the harassment and the University's refusal to provide supportive measures, Plaintiff was forced to live in unstable housing, suffered severe financial distress, and experienced worsening mental health that negatively impacted his graduate education. Exhibit F (October 1 letter: "Worsening his mental health conditions is expected to negatively impact his graduate education"). The University's inaction effectively ratified the harassment and left Plaintiff without protection, denying him equal access to the educational programs and activities at Duquesne.

### V. The University Exercised Substantial Control Over the Context and the Harasser

*Davis* requires that the recipient exercise "substantial control over both the harasser and the context in which the known harassment occurs." *Davis*, 526 U.S. at 645. The harasser was a fellow graduate student at Duquesne, subject to the University's disciplinary authority. The harassment occurred in University-affiliated housing and in a context where the University exercised control through its student conduct code, housing policies, and Title IX procedures. See Exhibit S (Plaintiff identifying multiple student conduct violations that the University refused to enforce). The Third Circuit has held that a university exercises substantial control over a third-party harasser where the university's "Title IX Policy also required that victims of domestic or dating violence on campus be contacted by someone at [the university]." *Hall*, Key Passage 3. Here, Duquesne's own policies required a prompt and equitable response. The University's failure to act falls squarely within the scope of conduct that *Davis* and *Hall* hold actionable.

### VI. The University's Reliance on *McAvoy* and *Foster* Is Misplaced

In *McAvoy v. Dickinson College*, the Third Circuit affirmed summary judgment for the university where the school's response included a no-contact directive, academic accommodations, a five-month outside investigation, and a semester of probation. No. 23-2939 (3d Cir. Aug. 16, 2024) (Key Passage 1). The court held that "delays alone do not establish deliberate indifference where school maintained ongoing communication and support." *Id.* Here, the University provided no support at all—no no-contact order, no housing assistance, no safety planning, and no investigation. The only "communication" consisted of demands for meetings and jurisdictional disclaimers. *McAvoy* is therefore inapposite.

Similarly, *Foster v. Board of Regents* stands for the proposition that a school's response may be clearly unreasonable when it "knew its initial response was ineffective" and failed to take adequate subsequent action. *Foster*, Key Passage 3. Here, the University's initial response was nonexistent. It never even began a response that could be judged ineffective; it simply refused to act. The *Foster* majority's holding that genuine disputes may preclude summary judgment is not a shield for total inaction.

### CONCLUSION

For 45 days, Duquesne University had actual knowledge that Plaintiff was being sexually harassed by a fellow graduate student. For 45 days, the University did nothing—no supportive measures, no safety planning, no investigation, no contact with the harasser. When Plaintiff finally demanded action under the governing regulations, the Title IX Coordinator acknowledged that his concerns warranted review but then reversed jurisdiction hours later, citing no new facts and violating multiple procedural requirements. This conduct is "clearly unreasonable in light of the known circumstances," *Davis*, 526 U.S. at 648, and constitutes deliberate indifference as a matter of law.

Plaintiff respectfully requests that the Court find Defendant liable on Count I for deliberate indifference to sexual harassment under Title IX.
