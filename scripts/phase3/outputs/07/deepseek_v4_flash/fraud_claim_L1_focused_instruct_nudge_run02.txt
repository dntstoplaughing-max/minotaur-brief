# ARGUMENT

## I. Defendants' Contention That Dean A's TAP 33 Analysis Was Consistent With Policy Plain Language Is Controverted By Genuine Disputes of Material Fact

Defendants argue that Plaintiff is not a "Covered Party" under TAP 33 and that the instructor-student evaluative relationship does not constitute a "Conflict of Interest." (Dkt. 34 at 6-8). This argument fails because genuine disputes exist as to whether Dean A's interpretation was made in good faith, whether TAP 33 actually applies, and whether Dean A's conduct was a false representation of material fact.

**First, Dean A's TAP 33 analysis was a representation of material fact.** When Plaintiff disclosed to Dean A that Dr. Selcer was a named defendant in his federal lawsuit against Duquesne, Dean A responded by consulting Selcer himself—the very person about whom the conflict was raised—and then jointly determined with Selcer that it was "appropriate for you to remain enrolled." (Exhibit K-2). Dean A then issued a formal policy determination stating TAP 33 did not apply because Plaintiff was not a "Covered Party" and the situation did not fall within the "limited definition of a 'Conflict of Interest.'" (Exhibit K-5). These representations were material: they directly affected Plaintiff's ability to obtain an alternate instructor, his academic standing, and his access to an unbiased learning environment.

**Second, there is a genuine dispute as to whether Dean A's representation was false.** TAP 33's definition of "Covered Party" and "Conflict of Interest" must be read in light of federal Title IX training requirements. Exhibit Z-d explicitly defines a conflict of interest as "[a] conflict between the private interests and the official responsibilities of a person in a position of trust" and mandates that "[c]onflicts of interest must be avoided to: ... Ensure fairness; Maintain the trust, integrity, and credibility of the Title IX process; Comply with Title IX; [and] Avoid litigation." The same training instructs that "[w]hen in doubt, remember to treat parties the same" and that a conflict exists if "based on objective facts, a reasonable person would believe that a COI exists." (Exhibit Z-d, Slides 43, 46-48). A reasonable jury could find that having a department chair who is a named defendant in a pending federal lawsuit serve as the instructor of a required seminar—and then have the dean consult that same chair about his own conflict—creates an objective, reasonable belief of a conflict of interest. Dean A's flat denial that "TAP 33 does not apply to this situation" (Exhibit K-5) was therefore false or, at minimum, made with reckless disregard for the truth.

**Third, Dean A's consultation with Selcer before issuing her determination is itself evidence of falsity and scienter.** Dean A wrote: "I did need to consult Dr. Selcer as instructor to secure some additional details." (Exhibit K-2). Consulting the very person whose conflict was at issue—seven days after Selcer was cross-examined under oath in the TRO hearing (Material Fact No. 8)—demonstrates that Dean A's purported conflict review was not neutral or independent. TAP 33 § IV.4 requires independent review; Dean A bypassed that requirement entirely. (Material Fact No. 9). A jury could infer that Dean A knowingly or recklessly misrepresented the policy to protect Selcer and the University, not to provide Plaintiff with an unbiased process.

**Fourth, the falsity is further evidenced by Dean A's response after Plaintiff corrected her within 90 minutes with specific policy citations.** Plaintiff immediately identified the error; Dean A responded "no change." (Material Fact No. 9; Exhibit K-5). This refusal to reconsider—despite being presented with contrary policy language—supports an inference of scienter.

**Fifth, the TAP 33 analysis is not a "professional judgment" entitled to deference.** Defendants cite *Jain v. Carnegie Mellon Univ.* (Dkt. 34 at 8 n.2), but that deference applies to *bona fide* academic decisions, not to administrative determinations that are themselves alleged to be fraudulent. When a university administrator makes a false statement about policy applicability to induce a student to remain in a conflicted classroom, that is not an academic judgment—it is a misrepresentation. *Gibbs v. Ernst*, 538 Pa. 193, 647 A.2d 882 (1994) (fraud requires false representation of material fact). Defendants cannot insulate themselves from fraud liability by labeling a policy interpretation as "academic."

**Accordingly, genuine disputes of material fact exist as to whether Dean A's TAP 33 analysis was a false representation, made with scienter, and material to Plaintiff's decision to continue or withdraw from the course.**

## II. Defendants' Contention That Dean A's Silence Was Not Actionable Because No Fiduciary or Confidential Relationship Existed Ignores the "Proper Source" Doctrine

Defendants argue that Dean A's failure to respond to Plaintiff's August 25, 2025 email was not actionable because no fiduciary or confidential relationship existed between university administrators and Plaintiff. (Dkt. 34 at 9, citing *Sunquest Info. Sys.*, 40 F. Supp. 2d at 656). This argument is legally and factually flawed.

**First, the "proper source" doctrine, not a fiduciary relationship, governs here.** Under Pennsylvania law, reliance on a defendant's representations is justified when the defendant is "naturally the proper source of information." *Neuman v. Corn Exchange National Bank & Trust Co.*, 356 Pa. 442, 51 A.2d 759 (1947) ("Where a party to a transaction is the natural and proper source of information, nondisclosure of a material fact is equivalent to a fraudulent misrepresentation"). The duty to investigate does not apply where the defendant is the natural and proper source. *Duquesne Light Co. v. Westinghouse Electric Corp.*, 66 F.3d 604 (3d Cir. 1995). Dean A, as the chief academic officer of the college, was the natural and proper source for information about TAP 33, conflict-of-interest procedures, and academic accommodations. Plaintiff was not a sophisticated party in an arm's-length transaction; he was a student seeking guidance from a dean. *Duquesne Light*'s "no duty to speak between sophisticated parties" exception does not apply.

**Second, Dean A's silence was not mere inaction—it was a deliberate nondisclosure following an affirmative misrepresentation.** After Plaintiff sent his detailed August 25 email to Selcer (with Dean A copied) explaining that the three-week Zoom limit was illegitimate, that supportive measures are rights not contingent on process, and that the Title IX Office had wrongly denied jurisdiction (Exhibit I), Dean A never responded. But on October 22, Dean A *did* respond to a different email, issuing her TAP 33 determination. (Exhibit K-2). This selective responsiveness—silent on the substantive fraud allegations, vocal when reinforcing the fraudulent narrative—supports an inference of intentional concealment. *Moser v. DeSetta*, 589 A.2d 679 (Pa. Super. 1991) ("The concealment of a material fact can amount to a culpable misrepresentation no less than does an intentional false statement").

**Third, the fraud claims against Dean A are not based solely on silence; they are based on her affirmative false statements** (the TAP 33 analysis, consulting Selcer about his own conflict, and the joint determination that Plaintiff must remain enrolled) **and her concealment of material facts** (that the TAP 33 bypass was improper, that Selcer had a conflict, that Plaintiff's policy citations were correct). Dean A's silence on the August 25 email is additional evidence of fraudulent concealment, not the sole basis for liability.

**Accordingly, genuine disputes exist as to whether Dean A had a duty to speak and whether her silence constituted fraudulent concealment of material facts.**

## III. Defendants' Contention That Plaintiff Admitted He "Immediately Recognized" Dean A's Reading Was Wrong and Was "No Longer Relying" Mischaracterizes the Record

Defendants argue that because Plaintiff stated in his pleadings that he "immediately recognized" Dean A's TAP 33 reading was wrong and was "no longer relying on Defendants' representations" as of July 24, 2025, there can be no justifiable reliance. (Dkt. 34 at 8-9). This argument misstates both the law and the facts.

**First, justifiable reliance does not require that the plaintiff be fooled indefinitely.** Under Pennsylvania law, "[r]eliance is presumed from materiality where a party would not have entered the transaction had the truth been known." *Peerless Wall & Window Coverings, Inc. v. Synchronics, Inc.*, 85 F. Supp. 2d 519 (W.D. Pa. 2000). Once Plaintiff discovered the falsity of Dean A's representation, he immediately corrected her (Material Fact No. 9). But the damage was already done: Plaintiff had already refrained from seeking an alternate instructor, had already remained enrolled, and had already suffered academic consequences based on his initial reliance. Moreover, Plaintiff's immediate recognition that the representation was wrong does not negate his initial reliance on it—he believed the dean's representation initially, and only later discerned the error.

**Second, Plaintiff's statement about "no longer relying" refers to a specific time period and specific defendants—the July 2025 false meeting requirement representations by Simpson, Wasilko, Sawa, and Selcer.** The "July 24, 2025" date referenced by Defendants corresponds to Wasilko's housing denial and the early meeting requirement emails (Exhibits B-1, B-2). It does not refer to the later TAP 33 fraud (October 2025) or the August 25 Selcer fraud, or the February 2026 performance issues fabrication. Plaintiff's fraud claim encompasses multiple defendants and multiple false statements across an extended period. A plaintiff may justifiably rely on some representations and not others; the fact that he stopped relying on one set of false statements does not immunize other false statements made later.

**Third, even after Plaintiff identified the falsity, he continued to justifiably rely on other representations by the same defendants.** For example, Plaintiff relied on Selcer's August 25 email that promised remote participation "subject to the condition that you fully engage with the Title IX, Student Conduct, and Public Safety offices" (Exhibit I)—a condition that was itself based on the false meeting requirement narrative. Plaintiff relied on that condition by continuing to engage with those offices, only to be told later that his engagement was insufficient. Similarly, Plaintiff relied on Dean A's October 22 representation that the conflict was resolved and he should return to class, which caused him to remain in a conflicted learning environment. *Peerless*, 85 F. Supp. 2d at 519 (reliance presumed from materiality).

**Fourth, the question of justifiable reliance is inherently a jury question.** A reasonable jury could find that Plaintiff, as a doctoral candidate facing a powerful university administration, was justified in initially trusting his dean and chair, and that his immediate correction of Dean A's error does not eviscerate his reliance claim. *Gibbs*, 647 A.2d at 882 (justifiable reliance is element of fraud).

**Accordingly, genuine disputes of material fact exist as to whether Plaintiff justifiably relied on Defendants' false representations, and Defendants' cherry-picked language from the pleadings does not establish the absence of reliance as a matter of law.**

## IV. Defendants' Contention That Plaintiff's Claims Are Internally Inconsistent Misconstrues the Fraud Allegations

Defendants argue that Plaintiff cannot simultaneously claim that both responding to his emails (Selcer) and not responding (Dean A) constituted fraud. (Dkt. 34 at 5). This argument is a red herring.

**First, different defendants committed different fraudulent acts at different times.** Selcer's fraud consisted of affirmative false statements: the August 25 meeting summary that misrepresented what was discussed and imposed a three-week Zoom countdown without medical or policy basis (Exhibit I), the September 3 "final directive" that conditioned accommodations on engagement with offices that had already told Plaintiff they lacked jurisdiction (Exhibit J), and the February 13, 2026 letter fabricating "performance issues raised by two of the three faculty members" to threaten Plaintiff's stipend (Exhibit X-8). Dean A's fraud, as discussed above, consisted of her false TAP 33 analysis and her consultation with the conflicted party. These are not inconsistent—they are different fraudulent schemes by different actors.

**Second, there is no inconsistency in alleging that one defendant's response was fraudulent and another defendant's non-response was also fraudulent.** A response can be fraudulent if it contains false statements; silence can be fraudulent if there is a duty to speak. *Moser*, 589 A.2d at 679. Plaintiff's allegations are straightforward: Selcer's *active misrepresentations* about meeting requirements, performance issues, and the Zoom countdown constitute fraud; Dean A's *silence* on the August 25 email, combined with her *affirmative misrepresentations* in October, also constitute fraud. These are legally distinct theories properly pleaded in the alternative.

**Third, even if there were some perceived inconsistency, Federal Rule of Civil Procedure 8(d)(3) expressly permits alternative pleading.** Plaintiff is entitled to plead that Selcer's active misrepresentations were fraudulent *and* that Dean A's deceptive combination of silence and affirmative falsehoods was also fraudulent. A party is not required to elect a single consistent narrative at the pleading stage, and certainly not at summary judgment where the record must be viewed in the light most favorable to the non-movant.

**Accordingly, Defendants' inconsistency argument is meritless and does not entitle them to summary judgment.**

## V. Defendants' Contention That the Consultation Between Dean A and Selcer Was Evidence of Intent to Help, Not Deceive, Is Disputed by the Record

Defendants argue that the consultation between Dean A and Selcer produced "assurances of fair treatment" and offers of academic support, demonstrating intent to help rather than deceive. (Dkt. 34 at 10). This argument ignores the overwhelming evidence of fraudulent intent.

**First, the fact that Dean A consulted Selcer—the very person whose conflict was at issue—is itself evidence of deceptive intent, not helpful intent.** Any reasonable administrator would recognize that consulting the conflicted party about whether that party has a conflict is inherently circular and self-serving. A jury could infer that Dean A's consultation was designed to obtain Selcer's self-serving input to justify a predetermined outcome, not to conduct an objective review. Exhibit Z-d, Slide 48 specifically instructs that in scanning for conflicts of interest, one should "consider where there may be influence unrelated to the evidence in the case, such as personal relationships, financial relationships, or power dynamics." Dean A did the opposite: she amplified the influence by consulting the conflicted party.

**Second, the "assurances of fair treatment" were themselves false.** Selcer committed to "providing you with the same consideration and opportunities as any other student" (Exhibit K-2), yet just four months later, Selcer fabricated "performance issues" (Exhibit X-8) based on no factual basis—Faculty B's own email requested reassignment with "my office door is always open" and "this will not affect your funding" (Exhibit X-7), and Selcer himself confirmed in the February 10 meeting that Faculty B did not control the stipend and that no performance issues had been raised (Exhibit X-6). The assurances of fair treatment were a sham, designed to induce Plaintiff to remain in the course while Defendants built a false case against him.

**Third, the evidence of intent to deceive is overwhelming across multiple defendants.** Between June 30 and August 14, 2025, four defendants (Simpson, Wasilko, Sawa, and Selcer) made 36 nearly identical false statements fabricating a meeting requirement as a condition of receiving Title IX supportive measures—a requirement that appears nowhere in 34 C.F.R. Part 106 or TAP 61. (Material Fact No. 1; Exhibits B-0 through B-7). Wasilko told Plaintiff: "We cannot assist you or provide you with further guidance unless you are willing to engage with the appropriate offices" (Exhibit B-2)—a categorical falsehood. Simpson made a jurisdiction determination "entirely on the written record" with "no meeting" (Material Fact No. 5), proving that meetings were not actually necessary, but then continued to demand meetings. Four defendants systematically removed OCR from correspondence at critical junctures—eight or more instances—after Plaintiff repeatedly restored OCR to the CC line. (Material Fact No. 6). This pattern of systematic false statements, followed by concealment of those false statements from federal oversight, demonstrates intent to deceive, not intent to help.
