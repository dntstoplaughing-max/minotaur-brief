**ARGUMENT**

**III. DEFENDANTS VIOLATED SECTION 504 AND THE ADA BY DENYING PLAINTIFF'S REQUESTED REASONABLE ACCOMMODATION AND FAILING TO ENGAGE IN THE INTERACTIVE PROCESS**

Under *A.J.T. v. Osseo Area Schools*, 605 U.S. ___ (2025), student ADA and Section 504 claims are governed by the same ordinary failure-to-accommodate analysis that applies in other disability discrimination contexts. The Supreme Court explicitly rejected any heightened "bad faith or gross misjudgment" standard, holding that "[n]othing in the text of Title II of the ADA or Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act suggests that such claims should be subject to a distinct, more demanding analysis." *Id.* Accordingly, Plaintiff must establish: (1) he is a qualified individual with a disability; (2) Defendants had notice of his disability and need for accommodation; (3) he requested a reasonable accommodation; (4) Defendants denied that accommodation; and (5) he suffered harm as a result.

**A. Plaintiff Is a Qualified Individual with a Disability**

Plaintiff David Mullins is a doctoral candidate in the Department of Philosophy at Duquesne University, and at all relevant times was an enrolled graduate student in good academic standing with no disciplinary record. (Statement of Undisputed Material Facts ("SUMF") ¶ 2; TRO Tr. at *:* (Selcer testimony confirming no performance issues).) His treating physician, Treating Physician, diagnosed him with ADHD and depression—conditions that qualify as disabilities under the ADA and Section 504. (Exhibit F (Medical Documentation).) Treating Physician expressly attests that these conditions "likely worsen[] his mental health conditions" and that "[w]orsening his mental health conditions is expected to negatively impact his graduate education." (Exhibit F, Medical Letter 2 (Oct. 1, 2025).) The medical documentation further confirms that Plaintiff's homelessness—directly caused by Defendants' failures—"has been very stressful and likely worsens his mental health conditions." (*Id.*) Plaintiff is thus a qualified individual with a disability under the statutes.

**B. Defendants Had Adequate Notice of Plaintiff's Disability and Need for Accommodation**

Plaintiff provided his treating physician's documentation stating: "I am a physician treating David Mullins. He's informed me he does not feel safe around a specific individual on campus, and from what he's described to me, I believe it's reasonable for him to have this fear." (Exhibit F, Medical Letter 1 (Aug. 13, 2025).) Treating Physician further recommended "every effort be made to avoid" sharing space with the individual causing Plaintiff's fear. (*Id.*) This documentation was provided to Defendants, who were thus on notice of Plaintiff's disability-related need for an accommodation that would allow him to participate in his education while avoiding the individual who triggered his documented fear.

*Taylor v. Phoenixville School District*, 184 F.3d 296 (3d Cir. 1999), makes clear that once an employer—or here, a university—receives "adequate notice of an employee's disability and desire for accommodations," it cannot "fail to engage the employee in the interactive process of finding accommodations." *Id.* at 313. Defendants had such notice no later than August 13, 2025, when Plaintiff provided Treating Physician's medical documentation, and arguably as early as July 2025, when Plaintiff repeatedly communicated his safety concerns and need for protective measures to multiple university officials. (Exhibit B-0 (June 30 email to Wasilko); Exhibit R (July 20 email to Selcer); Exhibit S (July 21 email to Wasilko, Selcer, Faculty A).)

**C. Plaintiff Requested Zoom Attendance as a Reasonable Accommodation**

Plaintiff specifically requested Zoom access for class participation as a medically necessary accommodation. (SUMF ¶ 3.) This request was reasonable and feasible: Zoom was already in active use in the same department by Faculty D for comparable graduate coursework, as evidenced by the Faculty D Syllabus providing that "if you are not feeling well, please attend that session remotely." (Exhibit N.) The syllabus further states: "Although we will meet in person, I will also keep a Zoom meeting running." (*Id.*) No university policy or departmental policy prohibits individual student Zoom access. (SUMF ¶ 4; Exhibit Y (Faculty Handbook 2025-2026, pp. 10-11) (governing faculty course delivery modality, not individual student attendance accommodations).)

*Oross v. Kutztown University*, No. 21-5032 (E.D. Pa. July 23, 2023), is directly on point. There, the court entered summary judgment for the plaintiff where the university "applied blanket no-remote policy with no written basis, no individualized assessment of plaintiff's request." *Oross* at *1. The accommodation request was "dead on arrival"—a finding of interference with the interactive process. Here, as in *Oross*, Defendants applied an ad hoc, unwritten policy against remote attendance, with no individualized assessment of Plaintiff's medical documentation or specific needs. The accommodation was similarly "dead on arrival."

**D. Defendants Denied Plaintiff's Reasonable Accommodation**

Despite having notice and a specific request, Defendants denied Plaintiff's requested accommodation. On August 25, 2025, Dr. Selcer imposed a three-week countdown on Plaintiff's Zoom access, after which Plaintiff would be required to attend in person "regardless of medical documentation." (SUMF ¶ 6; Exhibit I (Selcer Aug. 25 directive).) This was a conditional, time-limited measure "designed to expire and coerce meeting attendance." (SUMF ¶ 6.) Selcer's September 3, 2025, directive confirmed: "Until September 12, you may continue to attend your seminars via Zoom if you wish. . . . Otherwise, we expect you to attend seminars in person from September 15 forward." (Exhibit J.)

Selcer testified under oath that his objection to Zoom was based on personal preference, citing that remote attendance "divides attention" and a post-COVID departmental finding—but he could not identify any written policy, pedagogical study, or departmental standard supporting denial. (SUMF ¶ 7; TRO Tr. at *:*.) This subjective preference, unsupported by any written policy or evidence, cannot justify denial of a reasonable accommodation under *Oross* and *Osseo*.

The denial was not an isolated instance. When another student, Colleague A, separately requested Zoom access from Selcer for the Graduate Teaching Seminar, Selcer denied her request and told her he "can't explain why" Zoom was not available—demonstrating a pattern of denial with no articulable basis. (SUMF ¶ 8; Exhibit L.) This pattern underscores that Defendants' denial was not based on individualized assessment but on an unwritten, discriminatory policy.

Furthermore, after the TRO hearing on October 15, 2025, where Selcer assured Plaintiff that Zoom would continue, the Zoom link was removed the very next day. (Exhibit Q; Exhibit W.) Selcer then claimed that Professor Faculty C "did not identify a compelling reason to continue" the remote attendance—but Faculty C himself had already authorized Zoom on October 6, writing: "Yes, Zooming in tomorrow would work fine." (Exhibit W, Email 2 of 6.) Selcer's intervention to revoke an already-granted accommodation constitutes further denial and retaliation.

**E. Defendants Failed to Engage in the Interactive Process**

*Taylor v. Phoenixville School District* establishes the framework for the interactive process: "To show that an employer failed to participate in the interactive process, a disabled employee must demonstrate: 1) the employer knew about the employee's disability; 2) the employee requested accommodations or assistance for his or her disability; 3) the employer did not make a good faith effort to assist the employee in seeking accommodations; and 4) the employee could have been reasonably accommodated but for the employer's lack of good faith." *Taylor*, 184 F.3d at 317. An employer "who acts in bad faith in the interactive process will be liable if the jury can reasonably conclude that the employee would have been able to perform the job with accommodations." *Id.*

Here, no interactive process was conducted. Defendants did not: assess Plaintiff's specific limitations; evaluate possible accommodations; discuss alternatives with Plaintiff; or involve disability services. (SUMF ¶ 5.) Instead, Defendants imposed a unilateral, conditional, time-limited measure with no collaboration or good-faith exploration of alternatives. This is precisely the type of bad-faith conduct that *Taylor* condemns. An employer "who has received adequate notice of an employee's disability and desire for accommodations, cannot fail to engage the employee in the interactive process of finding accommodations, increase the disabled employee's job responsibilities, and then simply document the employee's failures." *Taylor*, 184 F.3d at 313. Here, Defendants not only failed to engage but affirmatively imposed a countdown clock to coerce Plaintiff into abandoning his accommodation request. (Exhibit I; Exhibit J.)

The contrast with the available alternatives is stark. Zoom was already in use in the department. (Exhibit N.) The Faculty Handbook expressly governs faculty course delivery modality, not individual student attendance accommodations. (Exhibit Y.) No written policy prohibited Plaintiff's requested accommodation. (SUMF ¶ 4.) Plaintiff could have been reasonably accommodated but for Defendants' lack of good faith.

**F. Plaintiff Suffered Harm as a Result of the Denial**

Plaintiff's harm is well-documented and severe. He has been homeless since June 30, 2025, with "90+ days of homelessness while university maintains available housing resources but refuses compliance." (Exhibit A (Housing Crisis Timeline).) Treating Physician attests that homelessness "worsens his mental health conditions" and that "[w]orsening his mental health conditions is expected to negatively impact his graduate education." (Exhibit F, Medical Letter 2.) External Faculty Witness similarly declares: "David cannot focus on his PhD research when he is experiencing encounters that cause this level of distress and require hours of recovery time." (Exhibit C (Supplemental Declaration).)

The denial of Zoom access specifically caused Plaintiff to miss classes, suffer academic disruption, and experience ongoing stress and uncertainty about his ability to continue his program. The three-week countdown was "a textbook example of retaliation masquerading as accommodation" (Exhibit I-1), and the subsequent removal of Zoom access (Exhibit Q; Exhibit W) left Plaintiff unable to participate in his education.

Moreover, the retaliatory threat of stipend suspension by Selcer on February 13, 2026, based on fabricated "performance issues" (Exhibit X-8), demonstrates the escalating harm flowing from Defendants' failure to accommodate. Where Faculty B expressly stated her office door was "always open" and that the reassignment "will not affect your funding" (Exhibit X-7), Selcer unilaterally introduced "performance issues raised by two of the three faculty members" and threatened stipend suspension through 2029 (Exhibit X-8). This pattern of retaliation and harm is directly traceable to the original failure to accommodate.

**G. The Evidence Establishes a Genuine Dispute of Material Fact Precluding Summary Judgment**

*Oross v. Kutztown University* is directly analogous and controlling. There, the court granted summary judgment for the plaintiff because the university's "blanket no-remote policy with no written basis, no individualized assessment of plaintiff's request" and the fact that the "accommodation request was 'dead on arrival'" constituted interference with the interactive process as a matter of law. *Oross* at *1. Here, the same facts exist: a de facto policy against remote attendance; no individualized assessment; and a request that was "dead on arrival" from the start.

*Osseo* confirms that no heightened standard applies. The Supreme Court expressly held that "Schoolchildren bringing ADA and Rehabilitation Act claims related to their education are not required to make a heightened showing of 'bad faith or gross misjudgment' but instead are subject to the same standards that apply in other disability discrimination contexts." *Osseo*, 605 U.S. ___. Accordingly, Plaintiff need only show that his request was reasonable, that Defendants knew of his disability and need, and that Defendants failed to provide the accommodation or engage in the interactive process.

Viewing the evidence in the light most favorable to Plaintiff, a reasonable jury could conclude: (1) Plaintiff has a disability (ADHD and depression, per Exhibit F); (2) Defendants had notice (the medical documentation and repeated communications); (3) Plaintiff requested a reasonable accommodation (Zoom access, which already existed in the department per Exhibit N); (4) Defendants denied that accommodation (the three-week countdown and subsequent removal, per Exhibits I, J, Q, V, W); and (5) Plaintiff suffered harm (homelessness, academic disruption, mental health deterioration, and retaliation, per Exhibits A, F, C, X-8). Summary judgment on Count III must therefore be denied.
