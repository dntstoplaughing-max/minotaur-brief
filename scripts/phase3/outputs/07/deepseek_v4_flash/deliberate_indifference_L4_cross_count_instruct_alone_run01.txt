**ARGUMENT**

**I. Legal Standard for Deliberate Indifference Under Title IX**

Title IX provides a private right of action for damages against federally-funded educational institutions that act with deliberate indifference to known acts of student-on-student sexual harassment. The seminal standard is governed by *Davis v. Monroe County Board of Education*, 526 U.S. 629 (1999), which established that a funding recipient may be held liable "only where the funding recipient acts with deliberate indifference to known acts of harassment in its programs or activities" (Key Passage 1). Critically, the Supreme Court held that liability attaches where "the harasser is under the school's disciplinary authority" (Key Passage 2), and the recipient's response is "clearly unreasonable in light of the known circumstances" (Key Passage 3). The harassment must be "so severe, pervasive, and objectively offensive that it can be said to deprive the victims of access to the educational opportunities or benefits provided by the school" (Key Passage 4).

The Third Circuit, in *Doe v. University of the Sciences*, 961 F.3d 203 (3d Cir. 2020), adopted a straightforward pleading standard: "to state a claim under Title IX, the alleged facts, if true, must support a plausible inference that a federally-funded college or university discriminated against a person on the basis of sex" (Key Passage 2). The Third Circuit rejected superimposing "additional doctrinal tests on the [Title IX] statute" (Key Passage 1). This Court need only determine whether Duquesne University's actions, as reflected in the undisputed record, support a plausible inference of sex-based discrimination arising from its clearly unreasonable response.

In *Hall v. Millersville University*, No. 19-3275 (3d Cir. Jan. 11, 2022), the Third Circuit confirmed that a federal funding recipient has "adequate notice under Title IX that it may face monetary liability for deliberate indifference to known sexual harassment perpetrated by a non-student where the recipient exercises substantial control over the context and the harasser." The *Hall* court further held that "failure to forward sexual misconduct reports to Title IX Coordinator as required by policy can constitute clearly unreasonable response" and that filing away a report without action—coupled with failing to contact the victim—satisfies the deliberate indifference standard (Key Passages 2, 3).

**II. Duquesne University Had Actual Knowledge of Sexual Harassment Through Officials with Authority to Act**

The record demonstrates that multiple Duquesne officials with authority to institute corrective measures received actual notice of the harassment Plaintiff David Mullins suffered. On June 30, 2025, Plaintiff reported to Dean Adam Wasilko—Associate Vice President for Student Life and Dean of Students—that his roommate, Respondent, had physically pursued him through their apartment, followed him up the stairs, and confirmed she was following him, leaving Plaintiff "feeling physically threatened and unsafe" (Exhibit B-0). Plaintiff specifically requested "any emergency housing options, protective campus measures, or relevant reporting mechanisms" (Exhibit B-0). Dean Wasilko, an official with authority to institute corrective measures as defined by 34 C.F.R. § 106.44 and the university's own training materials (Exhibit Z-e, Slide 32—identifying Dean of Students as having authority), responded by minimizing the threat, stating the incident "doesn't sound like a code of conduct violation" (Exhibit B-0).

Plaintiff's report was further escalated to Title IX Coordinator Alicia Simpson on July 24, 2025 (Exhibit B-1). Simpson acknowledged receipt and stated she was "here to provide you with support" (Exhibit B-1). On August 13, 2025, Plaintiff submitted an Emergency Removal Request with sworn declarations and a physician's letter documenting safety concerns (Exhibit F; Exhibit B-5A). Simpson responded on August 13, acknowledging that Plaintiff's "concerns and allegations warrant further information gathering and review" and offering to "discuss your current needs relating to supportive measures" (Exhibit B-5). Simpson's use of the term "supportive measures" is significant—it is a term of art defined exclusively in 34 C.F.R. § 106.30, confirming her knowledge of the Title IX framework (Exhibit B-5). Under *Hall*, the Title IX Coordinator's actual knowledge triggered the university's duty to respond appropriately, not to file away or dismiss the report.

**III. Duquesne University's Response Was Clearly Unreasonable and Constituted Deliberate Indifference**

Duquesne University's response to Plaintiff's reports of sexual harassment was not merely insufficient—it was "clearly unreasonable in light of the known circumstances." *Davis*, 526 U.S. at 648. The record establishes four independent grounds supporting a finding of deliberate indifference.

**A. Defendants Failed to Provide Any Supportive Measures for 45 Days**

Between June 30, 2025, when Plaintiff first reported the harassment and requested housing assistance, and August 14, 2025, when the Title IX Office disclaimed jurisdiction, Defendants provided zero supportive measures (Statement of Facts ¶ 8; Exhibits B-0 through B-6). This 45-day period included:
- Plaintiff's explicit plea for housing assistance (Exhibit B-0)
- Plaintiff's warning of homelessness sent to senior leadership on July 21, 2025 (Exhibit A)
- Dean Wasilko's formal housing denial on July 24, 2025, despite confirming available rooms (Exhibit G)
- Plaintiff's bank account falling to $16.65 and negative $2.55 while homeless (Statement of Facts ¶ 11)
- Multiple emails demanding supportive measures including no-contact orders, registrar separation, and campus safety escorts (Exhibits B-5A, B-5B, B-5C)

The university's own Title IX training materials define supportive measures as "available at any time after a report of sexual harassment," are "non-disciplinary" and "non-punitive," and include "mutual no contact orders," "changes in housing," and "extensions of deadlines" (Exhibit Z-c, Slides 38-40). These measures are designed to "restore or preserve equal access to the education program or activity" (Exhibit Z-c, Slide 38). Duquesne's complete failure to provide any such measures for 45 days while Plaintiff descended into homelessness and financial ruin is the archetype of a "clearly unreasonable" response.

In *Hall*, the Third Circuit found deliberate indifference where university officials "did not forward Wiberg's report and did not discuss it with anyone until after Karlie's murder" (Key Passage 2) and where "Karlie was not contacted by someone at Millersville after Wiberg's report" (Key Passage 3). Here, the failures are even more egregious: Duquesne officials not only failed to contact Plaintiff with offers of support but actively denied housing despite confirming availability (Exhibit G), conditioned assistance on mandatory meetings (Exhibits B-2, B-3), and ultimately disclaimed jurisdiction entirely (Exhibit B-6).

**B. Defendants Reversed Their Jurisdictional Determination After Plaintiff Engaged in Protected Activity**

The 24-hour reversal of the Title IX Office's position on August 13-14, 2025, is particularly telling. On August 13 at 2:02 PM, Simpson acknowledged Plaintiff's allegations "warrant further information gathering and review" and offered to discuss "supportive measures" (Exhibit B-5). Between 4:20 PM and 7:31 PM on August 13, Plaintiff sent three emails that consisted entirely of protected activity: demanding written communications, restoring OCR to the CC line, and citing specific Title IX regulatory provisions (Exhibits B-5A, B-5B, B-5C). No new factual information about the underlying complaint was provided in those emails (Statement of Facts ¶ 7). Yet at 9:42 AM on August 14—just nineteen hours later—Simpson reversed her position, stating "the Title IX Office does not have jurisdiction over your report and cannot take action or provide assistance as requested" (Exhibit B-6).

The timing of this reversal—immediately following Plaintiff's demand for compliance and restoration of OCR oversight—supports an inference of retaliatory motive. Under *Atkinson v. Lafayette College*, 460 F.3d 447 (3d Cir. 2006), "demanding compliance with Title IX is protected activity" (Key Passage 1). The Third Circuit has held that "unusually suggestive" temporal proximity can establish a causal connection for retaliation. *Jalil v. Avdel Corp.*, 873 F.2d 701 (3d Cir. 1989) (two-day proximity sufficient). Here, the 19-hour proximity between Plaintiff's protected activity and the adverse jurisdictional reversal is highly suggestive.

Moreover, Simpson performed zero of the five mandatory obligations under 34 C.F.R. § 106.44(a): she did not promptly contact Plaintiff to discuss supportive measures, did not consider Plaintiff's wishes, did not inform Plaintiff of the availability of supportive measures, and did not explain the process for filing a formal complaint (Statement of Facts ¶ 10). She also failed to offer appeal rights as required by § 106.45(b)(8) and failed to send simultaneous written notice to the respondent as required by § 106.45(b)(3)(iii) (Statement of Facts ¶ 9). These procedural failures demonstrate a pattern of disregard for federal Title IX requirements.

**C. Defendants' Response Failed the "Clearly Unreasonable" Standard Under Governing Precedent**

The Third Circuit's decision in *McAvoy v. Dickinson College*, No. 23-2939 (3d Cir. Aug. 16, 2024), is distinguishable. In *McAvoy*, the court affirmed summary judgment for the university because the school's response—a no-contact directive, academic and mental health accommodations, a five-month outside investigation finding sexual assault, and a semester of probation—was not clearly unreasonable (Key Passage 1). Here, by stark contrast, Duquesne provided no supportive measures, no investigation, no no-contact directive, and no accommodations of any kind. The only "response" was a series of requests for meetings, jurisdictional disclaimers, and ultimately a jurisdictional reversal that left Plaintiff without any remedy.

The en banc Sixth Circuit's decision in *Foster v. Board of Regents of University of Michigan*, No. 19-1314 (6th Cir. Dec. 11, 2020), is also instructive. The *Foster* majority affirmed summary judgment for the university where it "adopted escalating measures proportionate to the misconduct." But the dissent recognized that "minimal, ineffective, or belated efforts to respond to sexual harassment are not clearly unreasonable as a matter of law" (Key Passage 2). Here, Duquesne's efforts were not merely minimal or belated—they were nonexistent. The university did not even attempt to provide any of the supportive measures it was trained to offer (Exhibit Z-c).

**D. Plaintiff Suffered Severe and Pervasive Harassment That Deprived Him of Educational Access**

The harassment Plaintiff experienced was severe, pervasive, and objectively offensive. On June 30, 2025, Respondent confronted Plaintiff in the kitchen, "got within inches of my face, and aggressively followed me through the apartment" (Exhibit B-0). She confirmed she was following him when asked, stated "I'm coming up" to his room, and stopped only when he locked himself in his bedroom (Exhibit B-0). Plaintiff fled his home at substantial cost to protect his physical safety (Exhibit A). Medical documentation from Treating Physician confirms Plaintiff has a "reasonable fear" and that sharing a living space "would cause him a great deal of stress, would be highly likely to worsen his medical condition, and could impair his academic functioning" (Exhibit F).

The harassment continued through the fall semester. On September 17, 2025, Respondent was "literally waiting outside" Plaintiff's philosophy seminar classroom (Exhibit C). External Faculty Witness, who has known Plaintiff for 19 years, attested that this encounter caused severe distress and required hours of recovery time, directly impeding Plaintiff's educational participation (Exhibit C, Supplemental Declaration ¶¶ 1-4). External Faculty Witness further opined that Plaintiff's "education and research have been severely derailed by his housing instability and the conflict with his former roommate" (Exhibit C). The October 1 medical letter from Treating Physician confirms Plaintiff "reports homelessness since June 30th which has been very stressful and likely worsens his mental health conditions," and that "[w]orsening his mental health conditions is expected to negatively impact his graduate education" (Exhibit F).

The harassment was not limited to the June 30 incident. Exhibit D establishes a pattern of escalating conduct dating to June 2025, including Respondent locking Plaintiff out of the apartment, manipulating the A/C to Plaintiff's detriment while changing it for her own comfort, and ultimately the physical confrontation on June 30 (Exhibit D). During the move-out on July 1, Respondent harassed Plaintiff as he attempted to leave, repeatedly demanding to know where he was going (Exhibit D).

**IV. Duquesne University's Deliberate Indifference Caused Denial of Equal Educational Access**

Plaintiff's educational access was severely compromised. He was forced to flee his home and became homeless for 90+ days (Exhibit A). His bank account balance fell to $16.65 on July 25 and negative $2.55 on August 12, 2025 (Statement of Facts ¶ 11). He was forced into "precarious couch-surfing arrangements" (Exhibit A). His ability to attend classes in person was foreclosed by the university's refusal to implement basic safety measures—a no-contact order, registrar separation, and campus safety escort—measures that are expressly listed as supportive measures in the university's own Title IX training materials (Exhibit Z-c, Slide 40).

The department chair, Dr. Selcer, imposed a three-week time limit on remote attendance, creating what Plaintiff accurately characterized as a "countdown clock designed to coerce compliance or force attrition" (Exhibit I-1). Selcer's final directive of September 3, 2025, warned that if Plaintiff did not "engage with" Title IX and Student Conduct meetings, he would lose remote access and be required to attend in person without safety measures (Exhibit J). This ultimatum put Plaintiff in an impossible position: attend in person without safety measures and risk encountering his harasser, or lose access to his education entirely.

When Plaintiff later obtained temporary Zoom access from Professor Faculty C on October 6, 2025 (Exhibit W), Selcer intervened, discussed the matter with Faculty C without Plaintiff's knowledge, and then informed Plaintiff that Faculty C "did not identify a compelling reason to continue it" (Exhibit V). The Zoom link was removed the same day Faculty C's authorization was overridden (Exhibit Q). This coordinated removal of Plaintiff's only means of educational access, following his repeated demands for Title IX compliance, constitutes a clear denial of educational opportunities.
