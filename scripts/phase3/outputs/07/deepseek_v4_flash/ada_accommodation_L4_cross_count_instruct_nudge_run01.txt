**ARGUMENT**

**A. Plaintiff States a Claim for Failure to Accommodate Under Section 504 and the ADA**

To establish a prima facie case for failure to accommodate under Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act (29 U.S.C. § 794) and the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA), Plaintiff must show: (1) he is a qualified individual with a disability; (2) the University had notice of his disability and his need for an accommodation; (3) he requested a reasonable accommodation; (4) the University denied that accommodation; and (5) he suffered harm as a result. *See A.J.T. v. Osseo Area Schools*, 605 U.S. ___ (2025) (applying ordinary failure-to-accommodate standard, rejecting heightened “bad faith or gross misjudgment” standard). Plaintiff has adduced undisputed evidence on each element, and Defendants’ failure to engage in any interactive process—coupled with an arbitrary, time-limited, and ultimately revoked accommodation—plainly violates federal law.

**B. Plaintiff Is a Qualified Individual with a Disability**

Plaintiff’s treating physician, Treating Physician, diagnosed him with ADHD and depression. (Exhibit F, Medical Letter Oct. 1, 2025.) Treating Physician attested that homelessness and proximity to the individual Plaintiff fears have “worsen[ed] his mental health conditions” and that “[w]orsening his mental health conditions is expected to negatively impact his graduate education.” (*Id.*) The same letter explicitly recommends “all measures to protect him from his abuser and to provide stable housing.” (*Id.*) An earlier letter from Treating Physician confirmed that Plaintiff “does not feel safe around a specific individual on campus” and that the physician believed this fear was “reasonable”; the letter further warned that sharing a living space with that individual “would cause him a great deal of stress, would be highly likely to worsen his medical condition, and could impair his academic functioning.” (Exhibit F, Medical Letter Aug. 13, 2025.) Plaintiff’s depression and ADHD—both recognized disabilities under the ADA and Section 504—substantially limit major life activities including learning, concentrating, and sleeping. Dr. Selcer himself testified that Plaintiff had “no performance issues” and was in good academic standing. (TRO Tr. at *:*; SAC ¶ 2.) Plaintiff is therefore a “qualified individual” under both statutes.

**C. The University Had Actual Notice of Plaintiff’s Disability and His Need for an Accommodation**

Notice may be provided by the plaintiff or by the plaintiff’s physician. *Taylor v. Phoenixville Sch. Dist.*, 184 F.3d 296, 313 (3d Cir. 1999). Here, Plaintiff submitted Treating Physician’s medical documentation to the University on August 13 and October 1, 2025. (Exhibit F.) The August 13 letter stated unequivocally that Plaintiff feared a specific individual and that the physician recommended “every effort be made to avoid” sharing space with that person. (*Id.*) Plaintiff also repeatedly informed Dr. Selcer, Dean Wasilko, and other administrators of his disability-related need to avoid in-person contact with his former roommate. (Exhibits R, S, I.) As early as July 20, 2025, Plaintiff wrote to Dr. Selcer: “I am scared of this person. I fear for my safety in any context where she knows my whereabouts.” (Exhibit R.) Dr. Selcer acknowledged the email and did not dispute the severity of the concern. (*Id.*) By August 25, 2025, Dr. Selcer was fully aware that Plaintiff sought to “attend classes remotely” because of a “medical accommodation” need. (Exhibit I.) The University therefore had actual knowledge of Plaintiff’s disability and his need for a remote-learning accommodation more than a month before the September 12 expiration of the temporary Zoom access.

**D. Plaintiff Requested a Reasonable Accommodation**

Plaintiff repeatedly requested permission to attend his graduate seminars via Zoom as a disability-related accommodation. On August 25, 2025, Dr. Selcer acknowledged this request: “You are temporarily authorized to remotely attend the two classes in which you are enrolled this semester … This remote seminar attendance for these FA25 classes is authorized only for the first three weeks of class at this time. … If you are requesting a medical accommodation, it is also conditioned on you fully engaging with the Disability Services Office.” (Exhibit I.) Plaintiff thereafter explicitly asked for indefinite Zoom access as a reasonable accommodation. (Exhibit I-1, demanding that “Zoom access indefinitely” be secured because the three-week cap was “retaliatory.”) On October 6, 2025, Plaintiff requested remote participation from Professor Faculty C, who authorized it. (Exhibit W, Oct. 6 email: “Yes, Zooming in tomorrow would work fine.”) The accommodation request was both clear and repeated.

**E. The University Denied the Requested Accommodation**

Denial is not limited to an outright refusal; imposing a conditional, time-limited measure that is designed to expire and that coerces the employee into abandoning the accommodation is itself a denial. *Taylor*, 184 F.3d at 312–13 (employer cannot “fail to engage the employee in the interactive process … and then simply document the employee’s failures”). Here, the University did precisely that.

On August 25, 2025, Dr. Selcer authorized only three weeks of Zoom access, with the explicit condition that Plaintiff “fully engage with the Title IX, Student Conduct, and Public Safety offices within the next week and beyond.” (Exhibit I.) That condition was not met—Plaintiff had already been threatened by the Student Conduct director, and the Title IX coordinator had disclaimed jurisdiction—and on September 3, 2025, Dr. Selcer issued a “final directive” stating that “[u]ntil September 12, you may continue to attend your seminars via Zoom if you wish” and that “[o]therwise, we expect you to attend seminars in person from September 15 forward.” (Exhibit J.) This was not a good-faith temporary measure; it was a “countdown clock designed to coerce compliance or force attrition.” (Exhibit I-1.)

The denial became outright revocation in October 2025. Although Plaintiff had obtained Zoom authorization directly from Professor Faculty C on October 6, 2025 (Exhibit W), and although Dr. Selcer testified at the TRO hearing on October 15 that Zoom would continue (Exhibit Q, TRO Tr.), on October 16 the Zoom link disappeared. (Exhibit Q, Oct. 16 email: “I attempted to join class through the usual Zoom link, but no meeting was available.”) On October 17, 2025, Dr. Selcer wrote: “I’ve discussed your remote attendance of his graduate seminar with Faculty C and he did not identify a compelling reason to continue it. We would both welcome your return to in-person attendance.” (Exhibit V.) Faculty C later clarified that he was “confused” and had not known his prior authorization was being recharacterized. (Exhibit W, Oct. 17 PM email.) The accommodation was therefore denied—first by a conditional time limit that was not removed, and then by outright revocation without any individualized assessment or interactive process.

**F. The University Failed to Engage in the Interactive Process as Required by Law**

Under *Taylor*, an employer who has “adequate notice of an employee’s disability and desire for accommodations” must engage in a good-faith interactive process to assess the employee’s limitations and explore possible accommodations. *Taylor*, 184 F.3d at 312. The Third Circuit holds that a disabled employee must demonstrate: (1) the employer knew about the employee’s disability; (2) the employee requested accommodations; (3) the employer did not make a good faith effort to assist; and (4) the employee could have been reasonably accommodated but for the employer’s lack of good faith. *Id.* at 312–13.

Here, it is undisputed that “[n]o interactive process was conducted. Defendants did not assess Plaintiff’s specific limitations, evaluate possible accommodations, discuss alternatives with Plaintiff, or involve disability services.” (SJ 7.2.) No individualized assessment of Plaintiff’s medical documentation was ever performed; instead, Dr. Selcer imposed a three-week, take-it-or-leave-it deadline. (Exhibits I, J.) When Plaintiff asked for a challenge or review, he was told the Zoom access would simply end. (Exhibit I-1.) This is the antithesis of good faith.

In *Oross v. Kutztown University*, No. 21-5032 (E.D. Pa. July 25, 2023), the court granted summary judgment for the plaintiff where a university denied a remote accommodation “based on ad hoc blanket policy with no written basis, no individualized assessment of plaintiff’s request,” and concluded that the “accommodation request was ‘dead on arrival’—interference with the interactive process established.” *Oross* is directly on point. As in *Oross*, the University here applied a “blanket no-remote policy” (or, more accurately, an ad hoc personal preference) with no written basis, no individualized assessment, and no meaningful engagement. Dr. Selcer testified that his objection to Zoom was based on “divides attention” and a post-COVID departmental finding—but he “could not identify any written policy, pedagogical study, or departmental standard supporting denial.” (TRO Tr.) The Faculty Handbook confirms that course-modality rules govern *faculty* assignments, not individual student accommodations. (Exhibit Y, Faculty Handbook 2025-2026, pp. 10-11.) And Zoom is already in active use in the same department for comparable graduate coursework. (Exhibit N, Faculty D Syllabus: “I will also keep a Zoom meeting running, so if you are not feeling well, please attend that session remotely.”) There was no pedagogical barrier, and no showing of undue burden.

**G. The University’s Denial Was Not Based on Any Legitimate, Nondiscriminatory Reason—It Was Arbitrary and Retaliatory**

The record demonstrates that the University’s denial of accommodation was pretextual. Not only did Dr. Selcer fail to articulate a valid pedagogical reason (TRO Tr.), but he also engaged in a pattern of denying Zoom access to other students without explanation. Colleague A, another student in Selcer’s Graduate Teaching Seminar, separately requested Zoom access; Selcer denied it and “told her he ‘can’t explain why’ Zoom was not available.” (Exhibit L.) That is direct evidence that the denial was arbitrary, not the product of any considered policy or individualized assessment.

Furthermore, the University’s imposition of a three-week “countdown” and its subsequent revocation of a previously authorized Zoom link (Exhibits Q, V) are retaliatory. The University conditioned Zoom access on Plaintiff’s engagement with offices that had already dismissed his claims or threatened him with conduct charges. (Exhibits B-6, B-7.) As Plaintiff explained, the conditional deadline was “a textbook example of retaliation masquerading as accommodation.” (Exhibit I-1.) Courts have held that demanding compliance with Title IX is protected activity, *Atkinson v. Lafayette College*, 460 F.3d 447, 457 (3d Cir. 2006), and that a plaintiff need only “reasonably believe” the conduct opposed violates the statute. *Kengerski v. Harper*, No. 20-1307 (3d Cir. 2021). The revocation of Zoom access within two days of Plaintiff’s TRO hearing (Oct. 15, 2025) is “unusually suggestive” temporal proximity sufficient to establish causation. *Jalil v. Avdel Corp.*, 873 F.2d 701, 708 (3d Cir. 1989). In any event, the “convincing mosaic” of circumstantial evidence—the pattern of antagonism, the unexplained revocation, the refusal to engage in the interactive process—more than satisfies the causation requirement. *Canada v. Samuel Grossi & Sons, Inc.*, No. 20-2747 (3d Cir. 2022).

**H. The Accommodation Plaintiff Requested—Zoom Access—Was Reasonable and Would Not Have Imposed an Undue Burden**

A reasonable accommodation is one that enables the qualified individual to enjoy equal access to the educational program. The ADA and Section 504 do not require a perfect accommodation, only one that is effective. Zoom access is an objectively reasonable accommodation that would allow Plaintiff to participate in class discussions, ask questions, and fulfill his coursework requirements. It imposes no cost on the University (Zoom is already available and used in the department), it does not disrupt the classroom (Faculty D’s syllabus demonstrates it can be offered seamlessly), and it does not require the University to alter any fundamental aspect of its program. The Faculty Handbook explicitly permits “rare, one-off occasions” of remote participation, and nothing in the policy prohibits individual student Zoom attendance. (Exhibit Y.) The University has identified no pedagogical or administrative burden that would make Zoom access unreasonable.

**I. Plaintiff Has Suffered Harm as a Direct Result of the Denial**

Plaintiff has suffered substantial harm. The October 17, 2025 revocation forced him to choose between his physical safety and his education. He was unable to attend classes in person because of a documented, reasonable fear of encountering his former roommate—a fear that a physician validated as reasonable. (Exhibit F.) The loss of Zoom access caused Plaintiff to miss class sessions, disrupt his academic progress, and experience worsening mental health. Dr. External Faculty Witness’s declaration confirms that “David cannot focus on his PhD research when he is experiencing encounters that cause this level of distress and require hours of recovery time.” (Exhibit C). The medical documentation states that homelessness and stress “worsen his mental health conditions” and that “worsening his mental health conditions is expected to negatively impact his graduate education.” (Exhibit F, Oct. 1 letter.) The harm is ongoing: Plaintiff was deprived of the reasonable accommodation that would have allowed him to continue his education without endangering his mental and physical well-being.

**J. Conclusion**

Plaintiff has established each element of a failure-to-accommodate claim under the ADA and Section 504. He is a qualified individual with a disability; the University had notice of his disability and his need for accommodation; he requested a reasonable accommodation (Zoom access); the University denied that accommodation by imposing a conditional time limit and then revoking previously authorized remote attendance; and Plaintiff suffered educational and emotional harm as a result. The University failed to engage in the interactive process at all, let alone in good faith. Under *A.J.T. v. Osseo Area Schools*, “schoolchildren bringing ADA and Rehabilitation Act claims related to their education are not required to make a heightened showing of ‘bad faith or gross misjudgment’” but are subject to ordinary analysis. 605 U.S. ___ (2025). Under ordinary analysis, the University’s conduct—no individualized assessment, no interactive process, an arbitrary three-week ultimatum, and a retaliatory revocation—plainly violates the law. Summary judgment should be denied on Count III.
