**ARGUMENT**

Plaintiff David Mullins is entitled to judgment on Count I because the undisputed record establishes that Duquesne University (“Duquesne”) had actual knowledge of severe, pervasive, and objectively offensive sexual harassment; responded in a manner that was clearly unreasonable under the circumstances; and thereby denied Mullins equal access to educational opportunities. Each element is satisfied.

**I. Duquesne Had Actual Knowledge of Sexual Harassment Through Officials with Authority to Institute Corrective Measures**

Actual knowledge arises when notice of sexual harassment or allegations of sexual harassment is provided to a recipient’s Title IX Coordinator or any official with authority to institute corrective measures. *See Davis v. Monroe County Bd. of Educ.*, 526 U.S. 629, 650 (1999) (actual knowledge triggers duty). The record here demonstrates multiple avenues of actual knowledge.

On June 30, 2025, Mullins reported to Dean Wasilko that his roommate “got within inches of my face, and aggressively followed me through the apartment,” “pursued me up the stairs toward my room,” and that Mullins “no longer feel[s] safe sharing a living space.” (Ex. B-0). Dean Wasilko is the Associate Vice President for Student Life and Dean of Students—an official with authority to institute corrective measures, including emergency housing and conduct referrals. (Ex. B-0). Under the definition adopted in Duquesne’s own Title IX training, “actual knowledge” includes notice to “any official of the recipient who has authority to institute corrective measures on behalf of the recipient.” (Ex. Z-e, Slide 30). Dean Wasilko’s receipt of this report conferred actual knowledge on Duquesne.

Moreover, on July 24, 2025, Mullins sent a formal escalation to Title IX Coordinator Alicia Simpson, her supervisor, the university president, and legal affairs. (Ex. B-1). Simpson acknowledged receipt and stated that “[m]y office is notified when there are reported incidents of alleged sexual harassment or sexual misconduct.” (Ex. B-1). Simpson, as Title IX Coordinator, is the “official of the recipient who has authority to institute corrective measures” *par excellence*. (Ex. Z-e, Slide 30; *see* 34 C.F.R. § 106.44(a)). From July 24 onward, Simpson—and thus Duquesne—had undisputed actual knowledge of the alleged harassment.

Finally, on August 13, 2025, Mullins submitted an Emergency Removal Request attaching sworn declarations and a physician’s letter documenting safety concerns. (Ex. F; Ex. B-5). Simpson again acknowledged the report and conceded that “your concerns and allegations warrant further information gathering and review.” (Ex. B-5). Actual knowledge was continuous and unequivocal.

**II. Duquesne’s Response Was Clearly Unreasonable and Thus Deliberately Indifferent**

Deliberate indifference exists where a funding recipient’s response to known sexual harassment “is clearly unreasonable in light of the known circumstances.” *Davis*, 526 U.S. at 648. The Third Circuit has held that “[f]ailure to forward sexual misconduct reports to Title IX Coordinator as required by policy can constitute clearly unreasonable response.” *Hall v. Millersville Univ.*, No. 19-3275 (3d Cir. Jan. 11, 2022). And even where a school takes *some* action, it may be deliberately indifferent if its initial response was ineffective and its subsequent actions were not reasonable. *See Foster v. Bd. of Regents of Univ. of Mich.*, No. 19-1314 (6th Cir. Dec. 11, 2020) (en banc) (minority and majority both agree that “minimal, ineffective, or belated efforts” can be clearly unreasonable).

Duquesne’s response fails at every turn.

**A. Duquesne Never Provided Any Supportive Measures Despite a 45-Day Period of Known Risk**

Between June 30, 2025 and August 14, 2025—a period of 45 days—Duquesne offered Mullins *zero* supportive measures. (Statement of Undisputed Material Facts ¶ 8). The Title IX Coordinator’s mandatory obligations under 34 C.F.R. § 106.44(a) include promptly contacting the complainant to discuss supportive measures, considering the complainant’s wishes, informing the complainant of the availability of supportive measures, and explaining the process for filing a formal complaint. Simpson performed none of these duties. (¶ 10). Instead, Duquesne’s “response” consisted entirely of requests that Mullins schedule meetings, combined with jurisdictional disclaimers and referrals to other offices. (Exs. B-0, B-1, B-2, B-5, B-6).

The importance of supportive measures cannot be overstated. Duquesne’s own Title IX training defines supportive measures as “designed to restore or preserve equal access to the education program or activity” and states that they are “available at any time after a report of sexual harassment,” are “non-disciplinary, non-punitive, individualized, and provided at no cost.” (Ex. Z-c, Slides 38-40). The training emphasizes that the Title IX Coordinator is “responsible for coordinating the effective implementation of supportive measures.” (Ex. Z-c, Slide 38). Simpson never coordinated any measures. She did not issue a no-contact order, did not adjust housing or class schedules, and did not even offer counseling referrals until Mullins’ urgent August 13 request—and then only conditioned such discussion on a meeting. (Ex. B-5). This is the antithesis of a “prompt” response. *See Davis*, 526 U.S. at 648 (response must not be clearly unreasonable).

**B. Duquesne Conditioned All Assistance on a Meeting, Despite Knowing of Mullins’ Trauma and Safety Fears**

Simpson’s August 13 email stated: “it is necessary for representatives of these offices to meet with you to gather additional information. Additionally, during our meeting, we can discuss your current needs relating to supportive measures.” (Ex. B-5). Duquesne’s consistent pattern—from Wasilko on June 30 through Simpson on August 13—was to demand an in-person or Zoom meeting before providing any support. But the Title IX regulations do not permit a recipient to condition the provision of supportive measures on the complainant’s willingness to meet. Supportive measures are “available at any time after a report of sexual harassment,” (Ex. Z-c, Slide 39), and the Title IX Coordinator must “promptly contact the complainant to *discuss* the availability of supportive measures”—not demand a meeting as a prerequisite. *See* 34 C.F.R. § 106.44(a). Moreover, Mullins had repeatedly made his preference for written communications clear. (See Exs. B-5A, B-5B, B-5C). Conditioning safety-related support on a face-to-face meeting when the complainant has stated he feels unsafe and refuses to meet is the definition of “clearly unreasonable.”

**C. Duquesne Retaliated Against Mullins’ Protected Activity by Immediately Dismissing His Complaint**

On August 13, Mullins sent three emails (Exs. B-5A, B-5B, B-5C) that consisted entirely of protected activity: he refused the meeting, demanded written communications, restored OCR to the CC line, and cited §§ 106.44, 106.30, and 106.71. No new facts about the underlying harassment were provided. Yet nineteen hours later, on August 14, Simpson reversed her prior jurisdiction determination and stated: “the Title IX Office does not have jurisdiction over your report and cannot take action or provide assistance as requested.” (Ex. B-6). The temporal proximity between protected activity and an adverse action—here, the complete withdrawal of any promised assistance—raises an inference of retaliation, which itself constitutes deliberate indifference. *See* 34 C.F.R. § 106.71 (retaliation prohibited). And Simpson’s dismissal violated multiple regulatory requirements: she failed to provide appeal rights (as required by § 106.45(b)(8)), failed to send simultaneous written notice to the respondent (as required by § 106.45(b)(3)(iii)), and made a jurisdictional determination without the investigation that her own email two weeks earlier had deemed necessary. (Exs. B-1, B-6). This abrupt about-face, taken without any new factual development, is clearly unreasonable.

**D. Duquesne’s Response Contrasts Sharply with the Cases Finding No Deliberate Indifference**

The cases in which universities avoided liability involved meaningful, escalating interventions. In *McAvoy v. Dickinson College*, No. 23-2939 (3d Cir. Aug. 16, 2024), the university issued a no-contact directive, provided academic and mental health accommodations, conducted an outside investigation that found sexual assault, and imposed a semester of probation. Here, Duquesne offered nothing. In *Foster*, the university adopted escalating measures proportionate to the misconduct. Here, Duquesne never took a single measure. And unlike in *Hall*, where the university failed to forward a report but the Third Circuit reversed summary judgment for the university, the facts here are even more egregious: Duquesne did not merely fail to forward a report; it actively declined to act after repeated explicit requests for emergency assistance.

The conduct here more closely resembles the dissent in *Foster*—where the university “knew its initial response was ineffective” and failed to take reasonable subsequent action. *Foster*, No. 19-1314, slip op. at 3 (dissent). Duquesne knew by July 24 that Mullins had been displaced from his home, had a negative bank account, and feared for his safety. (Exs. B-0, B-1, F, G). Yet it persisted in demanding meetings and disclaiming jurisdiction.

**III. The Harassment Was Severe, Pervasive, and Objectively Offensive**

Under *Davis*, actionable harassment must be “so severe, pervasive, and objectively offensive that it can be said to deprive the victims of access to the educational opportunities or benefits provided by the school.” *Davis*, 526 U.S. at 651. The record demonstrates that Respondent’s conduct meets this standard.

On June 30, Respondent confronted Mullins in the kitchen, screamed at him, got “within inches of my face,” “aggressively followed me through the apartment,” pursued him up the stairs, and explicitly stated she was “coming up” to his room. (Ex. B-0). She only stopped when Mullins locked himself in his bedroom. (Ex. D). This physical pursuit and intimidation was accompanied by a pattern of earlier conduct: locking Mullins out of the apartment (Ex. D), manipulating the air conditioning to his detriment (Ex. D), and expressing enthusiasm for the Second Amendment in a context that Mullins reasonably interpreted as a threat (Ex. R). After Mullins moved out on July 1, Respondent continued to harass him as he attempted to leave. (Ex. S). The harassment was not an isolated incident; it was a course of escalating conduct that Mullins reported repeatedly.

Mullins’ physician, Treating Physician, confirmed that Mullins’ fear was reasonable, that sharing space with Respondent “would cause him a great deal of stress,” would “worsen his medical condition,” and “could impair his academic functioning.” (Ex. F). The harassment was objectively offensive by any measure: it involved physical pursuit, verbal abuse, and threats (whether explicit or implicit) that would intimidate a reasonable person.

**IV. The Harassment Denied Mullins Equal Access to Educational Opportunities**

Because of the harassment and Duquesne’s deliberate indifference, Mullins suffered concrete educational deprivation. He was forced to flee his home and became homeless; his bank account balance fell to $16.65 on July 25 and to negative $2.55 on August 12. (Statement of Undisputed Material Facts ¶ 11). He could not afford to continue his graduate studies. His physician opined that homelessness and proximity to the abuser “worsens his mental health conditions” and that “[w]orsening his mental health conditions is expected to negatively impact his graduate education.” (Ex. F). The harassment directly caused Mullins to lose housing, fall into financial distress, and experience a deterioration in mental health that impaired his academic performance. Duquesne’s failure to provide supportive measures—which could have included a no-contact order, housing modifications, or academic accommodations—directly contributed to this deprivation. *See Davis*, 526 U.S. at 650-51 (deprivation of access includes circumstances where harassment “undermines” a student’s ability to participate in educational activities).

**V. Duquesne Exercised Substantial Control Over Both the Harasser and the Context**

The Third Circuit has held that deliberate indifference liability attaches where “the recipient exercises substantial control over the context in which the harassment occurs and the harasser, even if they are a third party.” *Hall*, slip op. at 10. Here, Respondent was a fellow graduate student at Duquesne, sharing housing arranged through the university. (Ex. D). Duquesne had disciplinary authority over her, as reflected in its Student Code of Conduct. (Ex. S). The harassment occurred in university-affiliated housing and on campus. Duquesne therefore had substantial control over both the harasser and the context. Its failure to exercise that control—by not issuing any conduct charges, not imposing a no-contact order, and not removing the harasser from shared spaces—was clearly unreasonable.

**VI. The Facts Support a Plausible Inference of Sex Discrimination**

Under *Doe v. University of the Sciences*, 961 F.3d 203 (3d Cir. 2020), a Title IX plaintiff need not “superimpose additional doctrinal tests on the statute”; it is sufficient that “the alleged facts, if true, must support a plausible inference that a federally-funded college or university discriminated against a person on the basis of sex.” *Id.* at 209. Mullins’ allegations—that he reported sexual harassment, that the university knew about it, that the university’s response was deliberately indifferent, and that the university’s response caused him to lose access to educational opportunities—plainly support such an inference.

**CONCLUSION**

Duquesne had actual knowledge of severe, pervasive, and objectively offensive harassment. It responded with a forty-five-day pattern of inaction, conditional meeting demands, retaliatory jurisdictional dismissals, and no supportive measures whatsoever. Its response was clearly unreasonable under the known circumstances. Mullins was deprived of educational access as a result. For these reasons, the Court should enter judgment for Plaintiff on Count I.
