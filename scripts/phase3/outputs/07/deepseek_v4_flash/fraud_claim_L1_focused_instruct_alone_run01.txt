**ARGUMENT**

**I. Defendants' Motion for Summary Judgment Must Be Denied Because Genuine Disputes of Material Fact Exist on Every Element of Plaintiff's Fraud Claim**

Under Pennsylvania law, a plaintiff alleging common law fraud must establish: (1) a representation; (2) material to the transaction; (3) made falsely, with knowledge of its falsity or recklessness as to its truth; (4) with intent to induce reliance; (5) justifiable reliance; and (6) injury proximately caused by that reliance. *Gibbs v. Ernst*, 538 Pa. 193, 647 A.2d 882, 889 (1994). Summary judgment is improper where genuine disputes of material fact exist on any element. Defendants' six arguments each fail because Plaintiff has adduced record evidence creating triable issues on all elements.

**A. Defendants' First Argument Fails: Dean A's TAP 33 Analysis Was Not "Consistent with Plain Language"—Genuine Disputes Exist as to Falsity and Materiality**

Defendants contend Dean A's October 22, 2025 determination that TAP 33 did not apply to Plaintiff's conflict-of-interest disclosure was "consistent with the policy's plain language." (Dkt. 34 at 6-8.) This argument fails on multiple grounds.

First, Dean A's own actions belie any claim of consistent application. Exhibit K-2 reveals that when Plaintiff raised a conflict of interest concerning Dr. Selcer—who is both the instructor of a required teaching seminar and a named defendant in Plaintiff's federal lawsuit—Dean A's response was to "consult Dr. Selcer as instructor to secure some additional details." She then announced that "Both Dr. Selcer and I strongly concur that it is appropriate for you to remain enrolled." This is not a good-faith policy analysis; it is a process where the conflicted party himself participated in adjudicating whether his own conflict was problematic. Exhibit Z-d, the University's own Title IX training materials, defines a conflict of interest as "[a] conflict between the private interests and the official responsibilities of a person in a position of trust" and mandates that COI be avoided "to ensure fairness" and "[m]aintain the trust, integrity, and credibility of the Title IX process." Dean A's consultation with Selcer about his own conflict is precisely the type of self-interested decision-making that TAP 33 and the training (Exhibit Z-d, Slides 46-48) exist to prevent.

Second, Dean A's determination was factually wrong. As Exhibit K-5 demonstrates, when Plaintiff corrected Dean A within 90 minutes with specific policy citations, Dean A simply responded "no change" without any independent analysis. The policy (TAP 33 § IV.4) required independent review; Dean A bypassed it entirely. A jury could find that Dean A's representation that TAP 33 "does not apply" was false—particularly because Plaintiff, as a student in a required academic program, clearly falls within the class of persons protected from decisions influenced by instructor conflicts of interest.

Third, this false representation was material. Whether Plaintiff could receive an impartial academic experience free from his litigation adversary's influence directly affected his academic standing, stipend, and ability to complete his Ph.D. A reasonable factfinder could conclude that no student would voluntarily remain in a required course taught by a defendant whom they are actively suing if they knew the administration's conflict review process was a sham. *Peerless Wall & Window Coverings, Inc. v. Synchronics, Inc.*, 85 F. Supp. 2d 519, 528-29 (W.D. Pa. 2000) ("Reliance is presumed from materiality where a party would not have entered the transaction had the truth been known").

**B. Defendants' Second Argument Fails: Dean A's Silence Was Actionable Because the University Was the "Natural and Proper Source" of Information**

Defendants contend Dean A's "silence on the August 25, 2025 email was not actionable because no fiduciary or confidential relationship existed." (Dkt. 34 at 9, citing *Sunquest Info. Sys.*, 40 F. Supp. 2d at 656.) This argument misstates Pennsylvania law.

In Pennsylvania, "[t]he concealment of a material fact can amount to a culpable misrepresentation no less than does an intentional false statement." *Moser v. DeSetta*, 589 A.2d 679, 682 (Pa. Super. 1991). More critically, "[w]here a party to a transaction is the natural and proper source of information, nondisclosure of a material fact is equivalent to a fraudulent misrepresentation." *Neuman v. Corn Exchange National Bank & Trust Co.*, 356 Pa. 442, 51 A.2d 759, 764 (1947). The Third Circuit has expressly affirmed this principle in the fraud context: "The duty to investigate does not apply where the defendant is the natural and proper source of the information." *Duquesne Light Co. v. Westinghouse Electric Corp.*, 66 F.3d 604, 612 (3d Cir. 1995).

Here, Defendants were the exclusive source of information regarding the University's Title IX processes, TAP policies, and conflict-of-interest procedures. Plaintiff, as a student, had no independent means to verify whether Defendants' representations about the meeting requirement, jurisdiction determinations, or conflict-of-interest analysis were accurate. The University is the "natural and proper source" of information about its own policies. When Dean A received Plaintiff's August 25 email—which detailed the Title IX Office's improper jurisdiction disclaimer (Exhibit B-6), the retaliatory threat from Student Conduct, and the three-week Zoom countdown (Exhibit I)—her silence was not a neutral act. It was a deliberate omission that allowed Plaintiff to continue relying on a false understanding of his rights.

The *Duquesne Light* court distinguished between sophisticated parties with equal access to information—where no duty to speak exists—and cases like this one, where one party is dependent on the other for accurate information. *Id.* at 611-12. Plaintiff is a doctoral candidate, not a Title IX expert. He was entitled to rely on the University administrators who control policy interpretation. By remaining silent after receiving Plaintiff's detailed factual allegations, Dean A implicitly endorsed the false narrative maintained by Simpson, Wasilko, Sawa, and Selcer. This nondisclosure is actionable under *Moser* and *Neuman*.

**C. Defendants' Third Argument Fails: Plaintiff's "Immediate Recognition" of Error Does Not Negate Justifiable Reliance**

Defendants argue that because Plaintiff "immediately recognized" Dean A's TAP 33 reading was wrong and was "no longer relying on Defendants' representations" as of July 24, 2025, his claim fails. (Dkt. 34 at 8-9.) This argument conflates two distinct time periods and ignores the record.

Plaintiff's July 24 "recognition" pertained to the false meeting-requirement representations made by Simpson, Wasilko, Sawa, and Selcer between June 30 and August 14, 2025. The record shows Plaintiff repeatedly corrected these defendants, restoring OCR to email chains (Exhibits B-3 through B-7), citing federal regulations (34 C.F.R. § 106.44(a); TAP 61), and explaining that no policy conditions supportive measures on meeting attendance. But Plaintiff's awareness of those particular falsehoods does not negate reliance on other, later misrepresentations.

Critically, the record reveals ongoing reliance throughout the relevant period:
- On July 24, Plaintiff arranged a Zoom meeting with Simpson (Exhibit B-1), indicating he still believed engaging with the Title IX process could yield relief.
- On August 14, after Simpson's jurisdictional disclaimer (Exhibit B-6), Plaintiff had no choice but to seek relief through alternative channels, relying on the implicit representation that the Title IX process was genuinely unavailable.
- On October 20-22, Plaintiff disclosed the conflict to Dean A and requested reassignment (Exhibits K-1, K-2), demonstrating he believed Dean A would conduct a good-faith TAP 33 review.
- Plaintiff's ongoing enrollment, attendance at classes, and continued engagement with University procedures all demonstrate reliance on Defendants' representations that the Title IX and conduct processes were legitimate, fair, and administered in good faith.

The *Peerless* standard governs: if Plaintiff would not have continued engaging—would not have "entered the transaction"—had he known the truth, reliance is "obvious." *Peerless*, 85 F. Supp. 2d at 528-29. A reasonable jury could find that had Plaintiff known the meeting requirement was fabricated, the jurisdictional determination was predetermined, and the conflict-of-interest review was a sham, he would have taken different actions—including seeking immediate injunctive relief rather than continuing to exhaust internal processes.

Moreover, even if Plaintiff suspected or "immediately recognized" some falsehoods, Pennsylvania law does not require that reliance be exclusive or absolute. It requires only that the misrepresentation was a substantial factor in Plaintiff's decision. *Gibbs*, 647 A.2d at 889. The record shows Plaintiff was caught in a coercive structure: Defendants conditioned all assistance on meeting attendance, yet when Plaintiff refused to meet, they made adverse decisions without meeting. This is the hallmark of a fraudulent scheme, not a failed reliance claim.

**D. Defendants' Fourth Argument Fails: Plaintiff's Claims Are Not Internally Inconsistent**

Defendants argue Plaintiff's claims are internally inconsistent because he "alleges that both responding to his emails (Selcer) and not responding (Dean A) constituted fraud." (Dkt. 34 at 5.) This argument mischaracterizes the theory of fraud and ignores the substantive falsity at issue.

First, the nature of fraud differs by defendant. Selcer committed fraud through affirmative misrepresentations—his August 25 meeting summary falsely omitted critical context (Exhibit I), his February 13 reassignment letter fabricated "performance issues" that did not exist (Exhibit X-8), and his August 25 email imposed a three-week Zoom countdown without medical or policy basis (Exhibit I). Dean A committed fraud through concealment—remaining silent on the August 25 email and then issuing a sham TAP 33 determination that consulted the conflicted party rather than conducting independent review (Exhibits K-2, K-5). These are different *modes* of fraud, not inconsistent theories.

Second, the actionable conduct is the *substance* of the false statements, not whether they were spoken or silent. The Simpson/Wasilko/Sawa/Selcer group made 36 false statements fabricating a meeting requirement that does not exist in federal regulation or policy (Exhibits B-0 through B-7; 34 C.F.R. § 106.44(a); TAP 61). Dean A's silence compounded these falsehoods by failing to correct them when she had authority and obligation to do so. Both affirmative misrepresentation and concealment are forms of fraud under Pennsylvania law. *Moser*, 589 A.2d at 682.

**E. Defendants' Fifth Argument Fails: The "Consultation" Between Dean A and Selcer Is Evidence of Deception, Not Assistance**

Defendants argue that "The consultation between Dean A and Selcer produced assurances of fair treatment and offers of academic support—evidence of intent to help, not deceive." (Dkt. 34 at 10.) This argument cannot withstand scrutiny of the record.

Exhibit K-2 shows that when Plaintiff raised a conflict-of-interest concern about Selcer—who is a named defendant in Plaintiff's lawsuit—Dean A's response was to "consult Dr. Selcer as instructor to secure some additional details." She then announced a "joint determination" with Selcer that he should remain enrolled. This is not assistance; it is the opposite of independent conflict review. The conflict here is that Selcer has a direct personal and financial interest in retaliating against a litigant suing him. Consulting him about whether to remove himself from the decision is like asking the fox whether he should guard the henhouse.

The supposed "assurances of fair treatment" are equally hollow. Dean A's October 22 email (Exhibit K-2) imposes four conditions for remaining in the course—including meeting with Selcer individually to review missed content—and warns that failure to attend class will result in a failing grade. By October 22, Selcer had already threatened Plaintiff with stipend suspension through 2029 (Exhibit X-8) and imposed a three-week Zoom countdown (Exhibit I). Dean A's "offer" to have Selcer provide catch-up sessions is, in context, a demand that Plaintiff submit to one-on-one meetings with his litigation adversary under threat of academic failure. No reasonable jury could view this as "help" rather than deception.

The *United States ex rel. Moore & Co.* standard governs scienter analysis here. The Third Circuit held that the scienter requirement is "met when the defendant acts with actual knowledge, deliberate ignorance, or reckless disregard of the truth or falsity of the information." *United States ex rel. Moore & Co. v. Majestic Blue Fisheries, LLC*, 812 F.3d 294, 302 (3d Cir. 2016). Dean A's demonstrated practice of consulting the conflicted party about his own conflict, then claiming the result was a "joint determination," shows at minimum a reckless disregard for the truth of her representation that TAP 33 was inapplicable.

**F. Defendants' Sixth Argument Fails: Deference to Academic Judgment Does Not Immunize Fraud**

Defendants argue that "[c]ourts should defer to academic institutions' professional judgment regarding academic decisions" (Dkt. 34 at 8 n.2, citing *Jain v. Carnegie Mellon Univ.*). This principle has no application to fraud claims.

Deference to academic judgment applies to substantive academic decisions—curriculum design, grading standards, degree requirements. It does not immunize administrators who fabricate policy requirements, conceal material facts, and issue false representations under the color of academic authority. As the Pennsylvania Superior Court has recognized, "[f]raud is composed of a misrepresentation fraudulently uttered with the intent to induce the action undertaken in reliance upon it, to the damage of its victim." *Moser*, 589 A.2d at 682. No academic deference principle insulates fraudulent conduct from tort liability.

Moreover, the conduct at issue here is not a close academic judgment call. The meeting requirement Simpson, Wasilko, Sawa, and Selcer fabricated exists nowhere in 34 C.F.R. Part 106 or TAP 61. Simpson herself testified at the TRO hearing that she made her jurisdictional determination "entirely on the written record" with "no meeting" prior to decision, and that she "could have emailed" Plaintiff for clarification but "chose not to." (TRO Tr. at *:*.) That testimony directly contradicts the position taken in Exhibits B-1 through B-5 that meetings were necessary prerequisites to providing supportive measures. There is no academic judgment to which a court could defer; there is only a demonstrably false representation that conditions Title IX compliance on a procedure the defendants themselves later admitted was unnecessary.
