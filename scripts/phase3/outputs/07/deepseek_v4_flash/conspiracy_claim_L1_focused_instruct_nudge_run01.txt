**ARGUMENT**

Plaintiff David Mullins has adduced substantial direct and circumstantial evidence creating genuine disputes of material fact on every element of civil conspiracy under Pennsylvania common law. *See Thompson Coal Co. v. Pike Coal Co.*, 412 A.2d 466, 472–73 (Pa. 1979) (requiring “combination or agreement with intent to do an unlawful act or to do an otherwise lawful act by unlawful means,” plus proof of malice). Direct evidence of an explicit agreement is not required; circumstantial evidence suffices. *See Desert Palace, Inc. v. Costa*, 539 U.S. 90, 101 (2003) (rejecting heightened direct-evidence requirement; “circumstantial evidence is not only sufficient, but may also be more certain, satisfying and persuasive than direct evidence”). Each of Defendants’ four arguments fails because the record contains specific, disputed facts that a reasonable jury could credit.

---

**I.   DEFENDANTS’ CLAIM THAT BLAIR’S CONSULTATION WITH SELCER WAS “ROUTINE ACADEMIC ADMINISTRATION” IS CONTRADICTED BY THE RECORD, WHICH SHOWS A COORDINATED JOINT DETERMINATION WITH THE CONFLICTED PARTY AND EVIDENCE OF AN UNLAWFUL AGREEMENT.**

Defendants argue that Dean Blair’s consultation with Chair Selcer was a normal check on course availability and academic progress. (Dkt. 34 at 10.) The record refutes that characterization.

First, the consultation violated the plain terms of TAP 33 § IV.4, which requires **independent** review of conflict-of-interest disclosures. (Plaintiff’s Counter-Statement of Material Facts [“PSMF”] ¶ 4.) Blair did not conduct an independent review; she “consult[ed]” the very person about whom Plaintiff had disclosed a conflict—Selcer—and then announced a **joint** determination. Exhibit K-2 (Blair email, Oct. 22, 2025) states: “I did need to consult Dr. Selcer . . . Both Dr. Selcer and I **strongly concur** that it is appropriate for you to remain enrolled.” (emphasis added). That is not “routine administration”; it is a written admission of an agreement between two Defendants to act together on a matter directly affecting Plaintiff.

Second, the timing underscores the retaliatory nature of this agreement. Blair consulted Selcer just **seven days** after Plaintiff cross-examined Selcer under oath at the TRO hearing on October 15, 2025. (PSMF ¶ 3; Exhibit K-2.) A jury could reasonably infer that the consultation was not a neutral administrative step but a coordinated response to Plaintiff’s protected activity.

Third, the record shows ongoing communication between Selcer and Blair that preceded the fabrication of “performance issues” against Plaintiff. Selcer’s February 13, 2026 email (Exhibit X-8) references “a recommendation to the Dean of Liberal Arts”—Blair’s exact title. That phrase did **not** appear in the February 10, 2026 meeting between Selcer and Plaintiff (Exhibit X-6), proving that Selcer communicated with Blair in the intervening days. (PSMF ¶¶ 5–6.) This is circumstantial evidence of an agreement to manufacture a pretext to harm Plaintiff.

---

**II.   DEFENDANTS’ CLAIM THAT THE CONSULTATION SHOWED “INTENT TO HELP, NOT MALICE” IS UNDERMINED BY THE PATTERN OF COORDINATED ACTIONS THAT INFLICTED HARM AND BY THE ABSENCE OF ANY INDEPENDENT OVERSIGHT.**

Defendants point to offers of academic support in Exhibit K-2 as proof of benign intent. But malice under Pennsylvania law can be shown by circumstantial evidence of an intent to injure, *Thompson Coal*, 412 A.2d at 472–73, and the record here contains multiple indicia of malice.

- **Systematic removal of OCR and senior administrative oversight:** Four Defendants—Selcer, Blair, Wasilko, and Sawa—repeatedly removed OCR and other oversight officials from correspondence at critical junctures. (PSMF ¶ 7; Exhibits B-3, B-4, B-5, B-6, B-7.) Selcer removed Blair herself from the September 3 “final directive” email (Exhibit J), even as Blair was simultaneously consulting with him. This pattern of exclusion prevented Plaintiff from receiving corrective information and was designed to maintain Defendants’ control without independent scrutiny.
- **Blair’s silence despite direct escalations:** Plaintiff sent multiple escalations directly to Blair citing federal law and identifying specific violations. (PSMF ¶ 8; Exhibits K-2, K-5.) Blair did not intervene. Her silence, combined with her active coordination with Selcer, furthered the conspiracy’s objective of avoiding accountability.
- **Fabrication of “performance issues”:** On February 13, 2026, Selcer wrote that “performance issues [had been] raised by two of the three faculty members” (Exhibit X-8). Yet just days earlier, on February 10, 2026, Selcer used **no** such language in his meeting with Plaintiff (Exhibit X-6), and Faculty B’s own email on February 13, 2026 (Exhibit X-7) said nothing about performance—only that she had “asked Dan to reassign your TA position” and that her door remained open. The “performance issues” statement was false; it was contradicted by the source Selcer himself cited. (PSMF ¶ 9.) A jury could find that Selcer and Blair agreed to create a false record to threaten Plaintiff’s stipend through 2029.

These coordinated actions—removing oversight, fabricating performance deficiencies, and imposing conditional “support”—are entirely consistent with malice (intent to injure) and inconsistent with a good-faith effort to help.

---

**III.   DEFENDANTS’ ARGUMENT THAT THERE IS NO UNDERLYING TORT BECAUSE BLAIR’S TAP 33 ANALYSIS WAS CORRECT IGNORES THE PLAINTIFF’S EVIDENCE OF UNLAWFUL ACTS BY CO-CONSPIRATORS, FOR WHICH BLAIR IS JOINTLY LIABLE.**

Defendants contend that Blair correctly applied TAP 33 and therefore no underlying tort exists. (Dkt. 34 at 10.) This argument fails for two independent reasons.

*First*, the TAP 33 analysis was not correct: it violated the policy’s express requirement of independent review. (PSMF ¶ 4; TAP 33 § IV.4.) Using unlawful means (violating university policy) to achieve an otherwise lawful end is itself an “unlawful act” for conspiracy purposes. *Thompson Coal*, 412 A.2d at 472.

*Second*, even if Blair’s own actions were technically lawful, she is jointly and severally liable for all tortious acts committed by any co-conspirator in furtherance of the conspiracy. *Thompson Coal*, 412 A.2d at 472–73. The record here identifies multiple overt, tortious acts committed by co-conspirators:

- **36 false statements fabricating a meeting requirement** (PSMF ¶ 9, “Conspiracy 1”);
- **Systematic removal of OCR from communications across four actors** (PSMF ¶ 9, “Conspiracy 2”);
- **Selcer’s fabrication of “performance issues”** contradicted by the source he cited on the same day (Exhibits X-7, X-8; PSMF ¶ 9).

Each of these acts is independently tortious (e.g., fraud, intentional interference with contractual relations, or retaliatory conduct violating federal law). Blair need not have personally committed them to be liable; the agreement itself supplies the requisite nexus. *Id.*

---

**IV.   DEFENDANTS’ CLAIM THAT COORDINATED INSTITUTIONAL RESPONSES ARE MERELY “UNIVERSITY PROCESSES” IGNORES THE EVIDENCE OF SPECIFIC AGREEMENTS TO UNDERMINE OVERSIGHT AND TO FABRICATE PRETEXTS.**

Defendants argue that Plaintiff’s characterization of their actions as a “conspiracy” is merely a refusal to engage with university processes. (Dkt. 25 at 6.) But the record shows conduct that goes far beyond routine process:

- **The Blair-Selcer joint determination** (Exhibit K-2) was not a procedural step; it was a direct violation of TAP 33’s independence requirement.
- **The removal of OCR from email chains** was not an innocent omission; it was a deliberate pattern by four different Defendants to keep corrective information from Plaintiff. (Exhibits B-3 through B-7; PSMF ¶ 7.)
- **Selcer’s “recommendation to the Dean” language** (Exhibit X-8) following a meeting that had no such language (Exhibit X-6) proves that Selcer and Blair were communicating behind the scenes to manufacture a basis for threatening Plaintiff’s funding.

These are not “well-established university processes”; they are coordinated acts designed to harm a student who engaged in protected activity. A reasonable jury could find that Defendants “combined or agreed with intent to do an unlawful act or to do an otherwise lawful act by unlawful means.” *Thompson Coal*, 412 A.2d at 472.

---

**V.   DEFENDANTS’ CLAIM THAT SELCER’S CONTINUATION AS INSTRUCTOR WAS NOT RETALIATORY MISCHARACTERIZES THE OVERT ACTS IN FURTHERANCE OF THE CONSPIRACY.**

Defendants argue that Selcer’s role as Plaintiff’s instructor before and after protected activity cannot be an overt act. (Dkt. 34 at 10 n.3.) This argument conflates the mere holding of a position with the **use** of that position to take adverse actions.

The overt acts alleged are not that Selcer remained instructor; they are that Selcer (1) fabricated “performance issues” that did not exist (Exhibits X-6, X-8), (2) threatened to recommend suspension of Plaintiff’s stipend through 2029 (Exhibit X-8), (3) coordinated with Blair to remove OCR oversight (Exhibits B-3–B-7), and (4) pressured another professor, Dr. Faculty C, to withdraw previously authorized Zoom access after Selcer had assured Plaintiff under oath that access would continue (Exhibit Q). Each of these acts occurred **after** Plaintiff’s protected activity and was taken in Selcer’s capacity as chair with the knowledge and assistance of other Defendants. The fact that Selcer also taught Plaintiff before the protected activity does not immunize his subsequent, conspiratorial conduct.

---

**CONCLUSION**

The record contains specific, admissible evidence of an agreement between multiple Defendants to take unlawful actions—including violating TAP 33, fabricating performance issues, removing oversight, and threatening Plaintiff’s stipend—with malice and resulting harm. Each element of civil conspiracy is genuinely disputed. Summary judgment on Count V must be denied.
