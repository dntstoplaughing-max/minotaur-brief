# ARGUMENT

## I. Duquesne University Discriminated Against Plaintiff by Denying a Reasonable Accommodation for His Disability in Violation of Section 504 and the ADA

Plaintiff David Mullins, a doctoral candidate in philosophy with diagnosed ADHD and depression, requested a reasonable accommodation—remote Zoom attendance for his graduate seminars—based on documented medical need and a reasonable fear for his safety on campus. Despite the University's knowledge of his disability, the availability of Zoom in the same department for comparable coursework, and the absence of any contrary policy or undue burden, Defendants imposed a conditional three-week countdown, refused to engage in the interactive process, and ultimately revoked the accommodation entirely. This conduct constitutes a clear failure to accommodate under Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act and Title II of the ADA, governed by the ordinary standards recently reaffirmed by the Supreme Court in *A.J.T. v. Osseo Area Schools*, 605 U.S. ___ (2025).

### A. Plaintiff Is a Qualified Individual with a Disability

Plaintiff's treating physician, Treating Physician, has diagnosed Plaintiff with ADHD and depression—conditions that qualify as disabilities under the ADA and Section 504. *See* Exhibit F (Medical Letter Oct. 1, 2025) ("I have diagnosed David with ADHD and depression. I am treating those conditions."). Treating Physician further opined that Plaintiff's homelessness since June 30, 2025, has "been very stressful and likely worsens his mental health conditions," and that worsening his conditions "is expected to negatively impact his graduate education." *Id.* A prior medical letter confirmed that sharing a living space with the individual Plaintiff feared "would cause him a great deal of stress, would be highly likely to worsen his medical condition, and could impair his academic functioning." Exhibit F (Medical Letter Aug. 13, 2025). Plaintiff was at all relevant times an enrolled graduate student in good academic standing with no performance issues. (TRO Tr. at *:*; Exhibit R, Selcer Email July 20, 2025, acknowledging Plaintiff's concerns; Exhibit C, External Faculty Witness Declarations, confirming severe educational harm from housing instability and lack of university support.)

### B. Plaintiff Provided Timely Notice of His Disability and Need for Accommodation

On June 30, 2025, Plaintiff notified Dean Wasilko, Department Chair Selcer, and others that he had fled his home due to a "credible fear for my physical safety" and needed emergency supportive measures. Exhibit B-0. On July 20, 2025, Plaintiff wrote to Selcer and the Director of Graduate Studies: "I am scared of this person. I fear for my safety in any context where she knows my whereabouts" and explained the threats and stalking. Exhibit R. Plaintiff's treating physician provided documentation directly supporting his need for avoidance: "He's informed me he does not feel safe around a specific individual on campus, and from what he's described to me, I believe it's reasonable for him to have this fear. I have advised him to involve law enforcement for his protection." Exhibit F (Aug. 13, 2025). This documentation explicitly recommended "every effort be made to avoid" sharing space with the individual. *Id.*

Plaintiff formally requested a reasonable accommodation—the ability to attend his seminars remotely via Zoom. Exhibits I, I-1, J, V. He repeatedly explained that his fear of encountering his former roommate on campus, absent any protective measures, made in-person attendance impossible. Exhibit I (Aug. 25, 2025 email requesting safety measures and noting accommodation needed); Exhibit I-1 (Sept. 2, 2025 email demanding retraction of retaliatory three-week cap on Zoom). The *Osseo* Court held that "Schoolchildren bringing ADA and Rehabilitation Act claims related to their education are not required to make a heightened showing of 'bad faith or gross misjudgment' but instead are subject to the same standards that apply in other disability discrimination contexts." *Osseo*, 605 U.S. at ___. Plaintiff's notice was more than adequate under any reasonable standard.

### C. Plaintiff Requested a Reasonable Accommodation That Was Available and Imposed No Undue Burden

Zoom access was already in active use in the Philosophy Department. Faculty D's syllabus for the Spring 2025 course explicitly permits any student to attend remotely: "Although we will meet in person, I will also keep a Zoom meeting running, so if you are not feeling well, please attend that session remotely!" Exhibit N (Faculty D Syllabus). The same technology was available for Plaintiff's courses. The Faculty Handbook provides that "Faculty members do not have the latitude to switch courses to an alternative format or modality to suit their personal needs or preferences," Exhibit Y, but this policy governs *faculty course delivery modality*—not individual student attendance accommodations. The Handbook's restriction does not prohibit a single student from attending via Zoom. Indeed, no university or departmental policy prohibits individual student Zoom access. (SJ 7.3; Faculty Handbook pp. 10-11.)

The accommodation Plaintiff sought—continued remote access—was reasonable on its face. It would not have required any changes to the course structure or faculty instruction. It would have merely utilized technology already in place. The *Oross* court squarely rejected the argument that a university could deny a remote accommodation based on an unwritten, ad hoc policy: "University applied blanket no-remote policy with no written basis, no individualized assessment of plaintiff's request." *Oross v. Kutztown University*, No. 21-5032 (E.D. Pa. July 25, 2023). This court further held that the "Accommodation request was 'dead on arrival'—interference with the interactive process established." *Id.* Exactly the same dynamic occurred here: Plaintiff's Zoom access was conditionally granted for three weeks, then revoked at the end of the countdown, then re-granted briefly by Professor Faculty C, then revoked again after Selcer intervened. Exhibits I, J, Q, V, W.

### D. Defendants Denied the Accommodation and Failed to Engage in the Interactive Process

No interactive process was conducted. Defendants did not assess Plaintiff's specific limitations, evaluate possible accommodations, discuss alternatives with Plaintiff, or involve disability services. (SJ 7.2.) Instead, on August 25, 2025, Selcer imposed a three-week countdown on Plaintiff's Zoom access, after which Plaintiff "would be required to attend in person regardless of medical documentation." Exhibit I (Selcer Aug. 25 directive). This was not an accommodation—it was a conditional, time-limited measure designed to expire and coerce meeting attendance. Exhibit I-1 (Plaintiff's letter: "The imposition of a three-week cap is not a supportive measure at all. It is a countdown clock designed to coerce compliance or force attrition, a textbook example of retaliation masquerading as accommodation.").

The interactive process failure is independently actionable. *Taylor v. Phoenixville School District*, 184 F.3d 296, 317 (3d Cir. 1999), holds that "an employer who has received adequate notice of an employee's disability and desire for accommodations, cannot fail to engage the employee in the interactive process of finding accommodations, increase the disabled employee's job responsibilities, and then simply document the employee's failures." To show a failure to participate, a disabled employee must demonstrate: "(1) the employer knew about the employee's disability; (2) the employee requested accommodations or assistance for his or her disability; (3) the employer did not make a good faith effort to assist the employee in seeking accommodations; and (4) the employee could have been reasonably accommodated but for the employer's lack of good faith." *Id.* at 318. All four elements are satisfied here.

Defendants knew about Plaintiff's disability (Exhibit F, medical documentation; Exhibit R, Selcer email acknowledging Plaintiff's fear). Plaintiff requested remote attendance repeatedly (Exhibits I, I-1, J). Defendants made no good-faith effort: Selcer testified under oath that his objection to Zoom was based on personal preference, citing "divides attention" and a post-COVID departmental finding, but could not identify any written policy, pedagogical study, or departmental standard supporting denial. (TRO Tr. at *:*.) Instead, Defendants actively undermined any accommodation: Sawa threatened misconduct charges for emailing officials about safety concerns (Exhibit B-7); Simpson disclaimed Title IX jurisdiction without legal basis (Exhibit B-6); Wasilko denied housing despite admitting rooms were available (Exhibit G). Plaintiff "could have been reasonably accommodated but for the employer's lack of good faith"—Zoom was already available and used in the department. *See* Taylor, 184 F.3d at 318; Exhibit N.

Moreover, Defendants' behavior evinces bad faith. The *Taylor* court warned that "an employer who acts in bad faith in the interactive process will be liable if the jury can reasonably conclude that the employee would have been able to perform the job with accommodations." *Id.* at 319. Here, Selcer imposed the countdown, then on September 3, 2025, demanded that Plaintiff either "follow up with and participate in the relevant processes with Title IX, Student Conduct, and Public Safety or to drop your requests for accommodation and return to in-person attendance." Exhibit J. That is an explicit ultimatum to abandon the accommodation request—the antithesis of good-faith interactive process. Finally, on October 17, 2025, Selcer revoked Zoom access altogether, claiming Faculty C "did not identify a compelling reason to continue it," despite Faculty C having previously and independently authorized the very same accommodation. Compare Exhibit W (Faculty C authorizing Zoom on Oct. 6: "Yes, Zooming in tomorrow would work fine.") with Exhibit V (Selcer email Oct. 17 reporting that Faculty C found "no compelling reason" after Selcer reframed the request). This sequence demonstrates not merely a failure to accommodate but an active campaign to defeat the accommodation.

### E. Plaintiff Suffered Resulting Harm

The denial of the accommodation caused direct and concrete harm to Plaintiff's education. He was forced to miss classes, lost the ability to participate in his seminars in meaningful ways, and suffered exacerbation of his mental health conditions. Exhibit C (External Faculty Witness Declarations: "his education and research have been severely derailed by his housing instability and the conflict with his former roommate"). Treating Physician's medical letters confirm that homelessness and stress "likely worsens his mental health conditions," which "is expected to negatively impact his graduate education." Exhibit F (Oct. 1, 2025). On September 17, 2025, Plaintiff's former roommate was "literally waiting outside" his classroom, confirming the basis of his fear. Exhibit C (Facebook Messenger conversation). Plaintiff was forced to explain to his professor that he could not attend because "of an unfolding legal matter" and had his Zoom access abruptly cut off on October 16, 2025, after Selcer had promised continued access during the TRO hearing just one day earlier. Exhibit Q (Zoom removal sequence). This is the precise harm that the ADA and Section 504 are designed to prevent—the exclusion of a qualified individual with a disability from educational programs due to an institution's failure to provide reasonable accommodations.

## II. The Supreme Court's Decision in *Osseo* and the Third Circuit's Decision in *Oross* Directly Govern This Case

In *A.J.T. v. Osseo Area Schools*, the Supreme Court held that "Schoolchildren bringing ADA and Rehabilitation Act claims related to their education are not required to make a heightened showing of 'bad faith or gross misjudgment' but instead are subject to the same standards that apply in other disability discrimination contexts." 605 U.S. ___ (2025). The Court emphasized: "Nothing in the text of Title II of the ADA or Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act suggests that such claims should be subject to a distinct, more demanding analysis." *Id.* This eliminates any argument that student disability claims require a harder standard than other failure-to-accommodate claims.

Under the ordinary failure-to-accommodate framework, *Oross v. Kutztown University* is directly on point and controlling authority in the Eastern District of Pennsylvania. In *Oross*, the university applied a "blanket no-remote policy with no written basis, no individualized assessment of plaintiff's request." *Oross*, No. 21-5032 (E.D. Pa. July 25, 2023). The court held that the "Accommodation request was 'dead on arrival'—interference with the interactive process established." *Id.* Here, Selcer admitted under oath that he could not identify any written policy, pedagogical study, or departmental standard supporting his denial. (TRO Tr. at *:*) The only basis was his personal preference that Zoom "divides attention." *Id.* That is precisely the kind of ad hoc, unwritten, and individualized judgment that *Oross* condemns.

The Third Circuit's decision in *Taylor v. Phoenixville School District* underscores the obligations Defendant failed to meet. The duty to engage in the interactive process is not optional. "An employer who has received adequate notice of an employee's disability and desire for accommodations, cannot fail to engage the employee in the interactive process of finding accommodations." *Taylor*, 184 F.3d at 317. Where, as here, the employer acts in bad faith and the employee could have been reasonably accommodated, liability follows. *Id.* at 319. Defendants' repeated conditioning of accommodations on meetings they knew Plaintiff could not attend, their creation of a three-week countdown, and their eventual revocation of the accommodation after Plaintiff had relied upon it, all demonstrate bad faith.

## III. Plaintiff Is Entitled to a Full Range of Remedies, Including Compensatory Damages and Injunctive Relief

Because Defendant Duquesne University receives federal financial assistance, it is subject to Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act. (SAC ¶ 2.) A private right of action for damages exists under Section 504 for intentional discrimination, and the failure to accommodate claims in the educational context do not require a showing of "bad faith or gross misjudgment." *Osseo*, 605 U.S. at ___. Plaintiff has established a genuine dispute of material fact on each element of his failure-to-accommodate claim. The evidence shows that Plaintiff is a qualified individual with a disability, that he requested a reasonable accommodation, that Defendants denied that accommodation, that they failed to engage in the interactive process, and that Plaintiff was harmed as a direct result. The record contains medical documentation confirming his disability and the reasonableness of his fear (Exhibit F), evidence that Zoom was available and used in the same department (Exhibit N), evidence that Defendants imposed a retaliatory and time-limited "accommodation" (Exhibits I, J), and evidence that Defendants ultimately revoked even that conditional measure (Exhibits Q, V). No legitimate pedagogical justification supports the denial. The motion for summary judgment on Count III must be denied, and Plaintiff is entitled to proceed to trial on his failure-to-accommodate claim under Section 504 and the ADA.
