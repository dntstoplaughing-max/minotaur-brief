**ARGUMENT**

**A. Legal Standard**

Under the Supreme Court's recent decision in *A.J.T. v. Osseo Area Schools*, 605 U.S. ___ (2025), claims brought under Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act and the ADA in educational settings are subject to the same ordinary failure-to-accommodate analysis that applies in other disability discrimination contexts. No heightened showing of "bad faith or gross misjudgment" is required. *Id.* (Key Passage 1). "Nothing in the text of Title II of the ADA or Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act suggests that such claims should be subject to a distinct, more demanding analysis." *Id.* (Key Passage 2). Accordingly, Plaintiff David Mullins need only establish the standard elements of a failure-to-accommodate claim: (1) he is a qualified individual with a disability; (2) Defendant Duquesne University received adequate notice of his disability and his need for an accommodation; (3) he requested a reasonable accommodation; (4) the accommodation was denied; and (5) he suffered harm as a result.

**B. Plaintiff Is a Qualified Individual with a Disability**

Plaintiff's treating physician, Treating Physician, has diagnosed Plaintiff with ADHD and depression—conditions that qualify as disabilities under the ADA and Section 504. (Exhibit F, Medical Letter 2, Oct. 1, 2025). Treating Physician attests that these conditions "likely worsen[]" due to ongoing stressors, and that "worsening his mental health conditions is expected to negatively impact his graduate education." (*Id.*) The University does not contest that Plaintiff is a qualified individual; indeed, Defendant Selcer testified at the TRO hearing that Plaintiff had no performance issues and was in good academic standing. (TRO Tr. at *:*; Statement of Undisputed Material Facts ¶ 2.) Plaintiff therefore satisfies the first element.

**C. Defendant Received Adequate Notice of Plaintiff's Disability and Need for Accommodation**

The Third Circuit has held that "an employer who has received adequate notice of an employee's disability and desire for accommodations, cannot fail to engage the employee in the interactive process of finding accommodations, increase the disabled employee's job responsibilities, and then simply document the employee's failures." *Taylor v. Phoenixville School District*, 184 F.3d 296, 315 (3d Cir. 1999) (Key Passage 1). Here, Plaintiff provided Defendant with medical documentation from Treating Physician explicitly stating: "I am a physician treating David Mullins. He's informed me he does not feel safe around a specific individual on campus, and from what he's described to me, I believe it's reasonable for him to have this fear. I have advised him to involve law enforcement for his protection." (Exhibit F, Medical Letter 1, Aug. 13, 2025.) Treating Physician further recommended "all measures to protect him from his abuser and to provide stable housing" and explicitly stated such measures would "benefit and protect his mental health and his performance in his graduate program." (Exhibit F, Medical Letter 2, Oct. 1, 2025.)

Defendant Selcer's own email correspondence confirms receipt of this information. On August 25, 2025, Selcer acknowledged that Plaintiff "shared that [his] second priority is to be back physically in the classroom" and that Plaintiff "do[es] not feel safe on campus because [he] fear[s] Respondent." (Exhibit I, Aug. 25, 2025 email.) This acknowledgment, combined with the medical documentation, establishes that Defendant had actual knowledge of Plaintiff's disability and his need for accommodation. *See Taylor*, 184 F.3d at 315 (knowledge of disability and desire for accommodation triggers obligation).

**D. Plaintiff Requested a Reasonable Accommodation**

Plaintiff specifically requested Zoom access for class participation as a medically necessary accommodation. (Statement of Undisputed Material Facts ¶ 3.) This request was reasonable on its face, as Zoom was already in active use in the same department by Faculty D for comparable graduate coursework. (Exhibit N, Faculty D Syllabus/Policy, Spring 2025, providing "[a]lthough we will meet in person, I will also keep a Zoom meeting running, so if you are not feeling well, please attend that session remotely!") No university policy or departmental policy prohibits individual student Zoom access; the Faculty Handbook governs "faculty course delivery modality, not individual student attendance accommodations." (Statement of Undisputed Material Facts ¶ 4; Exhibit Y, Faculty Handbook Course Modality Provision, 2025-2026.) As *Oross v. Kutztown University*, No. 21-5032 (E.D. Pa. July 25, 2023) held, a "blanket no-remote policy with no written basis" cannot justify denying an individualized accommodation request. (Key Passage 1.)

**E. Defendant Denied the Accommodation**

Despite Plaintiff's documented medical need and the feasibility of the requested accommodation, Defendant imposed a conditional, time-limited measure rather than granting the accommodation. On August 25, 2025, Selcer "temporarily authorized" remote attendance "only for the first three weeks of class," after which Plaintiff would be required to attend in person "regardless of medical documentation." (Exhibit I, Aug. 25, 2025 email; Statement of Undisputed Material Facts ¶ 6.) This constituted a denial of the requested accommodation, not a grant.

Defendant's final directive of September 3, 2025, reinforced this denial, stating: "Until September 12, you may continue to attend your seminars via Zoom if you wish. . . . Otherwise, we expect you to attend seminars in person from September 15 forward." (Exhibit J, Selcer Final Directive, Sept. 3, 2025.) Selcer further testified under oath that his objection to Zoom was based on personal preference—citing that Zoom "divides attention" and a post-COVID departmental finding—but "could not identify any written policy, pedagogical study, or departmental standard supporting denial." (Statement of Undisputed Material Facts ¶ 7; TRO Tr. at *:*.)

The denial became absolute in October 2025. After Selcer testified at the TRO hearing that he would continue Zoom access, the Zoom link was removed. (Exhibit Q, Zoom Removal Sequence, Oct. 6-17, 2025.) Selcer then informed Plaintiff that Dr. Faculty C "did not identify a compelling reason to continue" remote attendance—despite Faculty C having already independently authorized Zoom access on October 6, 2025. (Exhibit W, Faculty C Authorization, Oct. 6, 2025, stating "Yes, Zooming in tomorrow would work fine.") Selcer's manipulation of the process—reframing Faculty C's prior authorization as a "compelling reason" inquiry that Faculty C never knew he was administering—demonstrates precisely the kind of bad faith that the Third Circuit condemned in *Taylor*.

The *Oross* court's analysis applies directly here. The accommodation request was "dead on arrival" because of "interference with the interactive process." *Oross*, No. 21-5032 (Key Passage 2). Defendant "applied blanket no-remote policy with no written basis, no individualized assessment of plaintiff's request." *Id.* (Key Passage 1). No individualized assessment occurred here; indeed, "no interactive process was conducted." (Statement of Undisputed Material Facts ¶ 5.) Defendants "did not assess Plaintiff's specific limitations, evaluate possible accommodations, discuss alternatives with Plaintiff, or involve disability services." (*Id.*)

**F. Defendant Failed to Engage in the Interactive Process**

*Taylor* sets forth a four-part test for interactive process failure: "(1) the employer knew about the employee's disability; (2) the employee requested accommodations or assistance for his or her disability; (3) the employer did not make a good faith effort to assist the employee in seeking accommodations; and (4) the employee could have been reasonably accommodated but for the employer's lack of good faith." *Taylor*, 184 F.3d at 315 (Key Passage 2). All four elements are satisfied here.

First, as established above, Defendant knew about Plaintiff's disability. Second, Plaintiff requested Zoom access as an accommodation. Third, Defendant made no good faith effort to assist. Selcer's response was to impose a "three-week countdown" and demand that Plaintiff engage with Title IX, Student Conduct, and Public Safety offices—offices that had already disclaimed jurisdiction (Title IX stated "the Title IX Office does not have jurisdiction over your report and cannot take action or provide assistance as requested," Exhibit I, Aug. 25 email) or threatened retaliation (Student Conduct "threatened that if I continued emailing University officials about my safety and access, she would initiate conduct charges against me," *id.*). This was not a good faith attempt to assist; it was an attempt to "coerce compliance or force attrition," as Plaintiff correctly identified. (Exhibit I-1, Sept. 2, 2025 email.) Fourth, Plaintiff could have been accommodated—Zoom was available in the same department, for the same type of coursework, and no policy barred it. (Exhibit N; Statement of Undisputed Material Facts ¶¶ 3-4.)

*Taylor* is explicit: "an employer who acts in bad faith in the interactive process will be liable if the jury can reasonably conclude that the employee would have been able to perform the job with accommodations." *Taylor*, 184 F.3d at 315 (Key Passage 3). Here, the University's bad faith is manifest: Selcer admitted his objection was based on personal preference, not policy or pedagogy; no interactive process occurred; and the accommodation was already in use in the department. A reasonable factfinder could easily conclude Plaintiff could have participated in his education with the requested Zoom accommodation.

**G. Plaintiff Suffered Harm as a Result of the Denial**

The denial of accommodation directly caused Plaintiff harm. The three-week countdown itself was harmful: as Plaintiff explained, "Every day the countdown remains, I have to wonder whether my access will suddenly be cut off," creating "destabilizing" effects on "education and health." (Exhibit I-1, Sept. 2, 2025 email.) When the accommodation was ultimately revoked in October 2025, Plaintiff was excluded from class participation. (Exhibit Q, Zoom Removal Sequence, Oct. 16, 2025, showing Plaintiff unable to join class.) Treating Physician documented that the resulting stress "worsens his mental health conditions" and "is expected to negatively impact his graduate education." (Exhibit F, Oct. 1, 2025.) Professor External Faculty Witness, an academic mentor, confirmed that Plaintiff's "education and research have been severely derailed" and that "David cannot focus on his PhD research when he is experiencing encounters that cause this level of distress." (Exhibit C, External Faculty Witness Declarations, Sept. 30 & Oct. 1, 2025.)

This harm flows directly from Defendant's failure to accommodate. Had Plaintiff been permitted to continue Zoom access—a readily available, feasible accommodation already in use in the same department—he would have been able to continue his education without the destabilizing threat of in-person encounters that caused his documented distress.

**H. Conclusion**

For the foregoing reasons, Plaintiff has established all elements of a failure-to-accommodate claim under Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act and the ADA. Defendant Duquesne University, a recipient of federal financial assistance, was on notice of Plaintiff's disability and need for accommodation, refused to engage in any meaningful interactive process, imposed a retaliatory time-limited measure rather than granting a reasonable accommodation that was readily available in the same department, and ultimately revoked access entirely. The Supreme Court's decision in *Osseo* confirms that no heightened standard applies. The Third Circuit's decision in *Taylor* confirms that Defendant's bad faith in the interactive process establishes liability. This Court should accordingly find Defendant liable for failure to accommodate under Count III.
