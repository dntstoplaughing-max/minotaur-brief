**ARGUMENT**

Plaintiff David Mullins has demonstrated genuine disputes of material fact on every element of his Pennsylvania common law fraud claim, precluding summary judgment on Count IV. Defendants' six arguments each fail because they ignore the record evidence, misapply governing law, or both.

**I. Dean A's TAP 33 Analysis Was Not Consistent with the Policy's Plain Language, Creating a Genuine Dispute of Material Fact**

Defendants contend that Dean A's TAP 33 analysis was correct—that Plaintiff is not a "Covered Party" and that Selcer's role as instructor during litigation does not constitute a "Conflict of Interest." *See* Dkt. 34 at 6-8. But the record shows otherwise.

First, TAP 33 defines "Covered Party" to include students affected by decisions made under the policy. Exhibit K-5. Plaintiff is a doctoral candidate whose required teaching seminar is taught by a named defendant in his pending federal lawsuit against the University. Exhibit K-1. The conflict is self-evident: Selcer is simultaneously the instructor grading Plaintiff and a party adverse to Plaintiff in active litigation. The University's own Title IX training materials define a conflict of interest as "[a] conflict between the private interests and the official responsibilities of a person in a position of trust." Exhibit Z-d at Slide 46. Selcer's role as litigant-defendant and instructor creates precisely such a conflict.

Second, Dean A's process was fundamentally flawed. She admits: "I did need to consult Dr. Selcer as instructor to secure some additional details." Exhibit K-2. She then consulted the *conflicted party* about his *own conflict* and announced that "[b]oth Dr. Selcer and I strongly concur that it is appropriate for you to remain enrolled." *Id.* This is not the independent review required by TAP 33 § IV.4. Plaintiff corrected Dean A within 90 minutes with specific policy citations; she responded "no change." Exhibit K-5. A reasonable jury could find that Dean A's TAP 33 bypass was a false representation that the policy did not apply, made with reckless disregard for its actual requirements.

**II. Defendants Had a Duty to Speak Because They Were the Natural and Proper Source of Information About University Policies and Procedures**

Defendants argue that no fiduciary or confidential relationship existed and thus no duty to speak arose. Dkt. 34 at 9 (citing *Sunquest Info. Sys.*). This argument ignores controlling Pennsylvania law.

Under *Neuman v. Corn Exchange National Bank & Trust Co.*, 356 Pa. 442, 51 A.2d 759 (1947), "[w]here a party to a transaction is the natural and proper source of information, nondisclosure of a material fact is equivalent to a fraudulent misrepresentation." The *Duquesne Light Co.* court confirmed this principle: "The duty to investigate does not apply where the defendant is the natural and proper source of the information." *Duquesne Light Co. v. Westinghouse Electric Corp.*, 66 F.3d 604, 612 (3d Cir. 1995).

Here, Defendants were the *exclusive* source of information about University policies—TAP 33, TAP 61, Title IX procedures, and supportive measures. Plaintiff, as a student, had no independent means of verifying the University's policy interpretations. When Simpson, Wasilko, Sawa, and Selcer repeatedly stated that a meeting was required before Plaintiff could receive supportive measures (Exhibits B-1 through B-7), and when Dean A stated TAP 33 did not apply (Exhibit K-5), they were the "natural and proper source" of that information. Their silence about the actual policy provisions—which contain no meeting requirement—constitutes fraudulent concealment under *Moser v. DeSetta*, 589 A.2d 679, 682 (Pa. Super. 1991) ("The concealment of a material fact can amount to a culpable misrepresentation no less than does an intentional false statement").

The record shows that between June 30 and August 14, 2025, four defendants made 36 nearly identical false statements fabricating a meeting requirement that appears nowhere in 34 C.F.R. Part 106 or TAP 61. Plaintiff's Counter-Statement of Material Facts ¶ 1. Wasilko stated: "We cannot assist you or provide you with further guidance unless you are willing to engage with the appropriate offices." Exhibit B-2. Simpson's initial outreach requested a meeting to "gather more information and discuss options"—framing institutional obligations as discretionary. Exhibit B-1. These were not innocent invitations; they were false representations that Plaintiff's rights were contingent on compliance with a non-existent requirement.

**III. Plaintiff's "Immediate Recognition" of Dean A's Error Does Not Defeat Justifiable Reliance on Defendants' Other Misrepresentations**

Defendants seize on Plaintiff's statement that he "immediately recognized" Dean A's TAP 33 reading was wrong and "no longer relying on Defendants' representations" as of July 24, 2025. Dkt. 34 at 8-9. This argument fails for three reasons.

First, Plaintiff's recognition of *one* misrepresentation does not negate reliance on *others*. The fraud claim encompasses numerous distinct misrepresentations across multiple defendants over an extended period. Plaintiff corrected Dean A's TAP 33 error within 90 minutes on October 22, 2025. Exhibit K-5. But that says nothing about his reliance on the 36 false statements from Simpson, Wasilko, Sawa, and Selcer between June 30 and August 14, 2025—months earlier. Counter-Statement ¶ 1.

Second, reliance is "presumed from materiality where a party would not have entered the transaction had the truth been known." *Peerless Wall & Window Coverings, Inc. v. Synchronics, Inc.*, 85 F. Supp. 2d 519, 529 (W.D. Pa. 2000). Plaintiff was told his access to supportive measures—including housing, safety escorts, and no-contact orders—depended on meeting with university officials. Exhibit B-2. He was told his remote attendance was "authorized only for the first three weeks" and conditioned on meeting attendance. Exhibit I. Had he known the truth—that no meeting requirement exists under federal regulation or institutional policy—he would not have delayed pursuit of his Title IX rights or believed his academic standing was at risk.

Third, under *Neuman*, Plaintiff was entitled to rely on Defendants as "the natural and proper source of information" about University procedures. *Neuman*, 51 A.2d at 763. A university student dealing with university administrators about university policies is not a "sophisticated party" with "equal access to information" under *Duquesne Light*. *Id.* at 612 (no duty to speak "between sophisticated parties dealing at arm's length with equal access to information"). The duty-to-investigate exception does not apply here.

**IV. Plaintiff's Claims Are Not Internally Inconsistent—They Reflect a Coherent Theory of Fraud**

Defendants argue that Plaintiff's claims are inconsistent because he alleges both that Selcer responded to emails (and made false statements) and that Dean A did not respond (and remained silent). Dkt. 34 at 5. This is not inconsistency; it is alternative theories of fraud.

Under Pennsylvania law, fraud can be accomplished through affirmative misrepresentations *or* through concealment. *Moser*, 589 A.2d at 682. Selcer made affirmative false statements—his August 25, 2025 email misrepresented the meeting requirement and imposed a three-week Zoom countdown without medical or policy basis. Exhibit I. Dean A engaged in concealment—she failed to disclose that her TAP 33 analysis was procedurally defective and that she had consulted the conflicted party. Exhibits K-2, K-5. Both theories are valid under *Moser* and *Gibbs v. Ernst*, 538 Pa. 193, 647 A.2d 882 (1994).

Moreover, the four defendants who made the 36 false statements (Simpson, Wasilko, Sawa, Selcer) acted in concert. They systematically removed OCR from correspondence at critical junctures—eight or more instances across four actors—after Plaintiff repeatedly restored OCR to the CC line. Counter-Statement ¶ 6; Exhibits B-3 through B-7 series. This coordinated concealment evidences a fraudulent scheme, not inconsistency.

**V. The Consultation Between Dean A and Selcer Did Not Demonstrate Intent to Help—It Demonstrated Intent to Maintain Control**

Defendants argue that Dean A's consultation with Selcer and the resulting offer of "fair treatment" and "academic support" shows intent to help, not deceive. Dkt. 34 at 10. A reasonable jury could disagree.

Dean A consulted the very person about whom Plaintiff raised a conflict-of-interest concern—seven days after that person was cross-examined under oath. Exhibit K-2. She did not conduct an independent review; she sought "additional details" from the conflicted party and then announced a "joint determination" that the conflict did not exist. *Id.* This is not fair treatment; it is institutional self-protection.

The record shows a pattern: when Plaintiff raised concerns, Defendants responded with conditions and threats. Selcer's August 25 email authorized remote attendance for "only the first three weeks" and conditioned it on "fully engag[ing] with the Title IX, Student Conduct, and Public Safety offices within the next week." Exhibit I. When Plaintiff did not comply, Selcer issued a "final directive" threatening loss of remote access and implying conduct charges. Exhibit J. When Plaintiff raised the TAP 33 conflict, Dean A denied its applicability without independent review. Exhibit K-5.

Under *Gibbs*, intent to mislead may be inferred from "knowledge of its falsity or recklessness as to whether it is true or false." *Gibbs*, 647 A.2d at 890. The *Majestic Blue* court held that scienter is satisfied when a defendant acts with "actual knowledge, deliberate ignorance, or reckless disregard of the truth or falsity of the information." *United States ex rel. Moore & Co. v. Majestic Blue Fisheries, LLC*, 812 F.3d 294, 306 (3d Cir. 2016). A jury could find that Defendants acted with deliberate ignorance or reckless disregard when they fabricated a meeting requirement, consulted the conflicted party about his own conflict, and systematically excluded OCR from correspondence.

**VI. Deference to Academic Judgment Does Not Apply to Fraudulent Misrepresentations About Policy and Procedure**

Defendants invoke *Jain v. Carnegie Mellon Univ.* for the proposition that courts should defer to academic institutions' professional judgment. Dkt. 34 at 8 n.2. This argument misses the mark.

*Jain* concerned academic decisions about curriculum and grading—whether a university properly evaluated a student's academic performance. It did not address fraudulent misrepresentations about Title IX obligations, conflict-of-interest procedures, or meeting requirements that appear nowhere in policy. Here, Plaintiff does not challenge the University's academic judgment; he challenges its *false statements* about what its policies require. Deference to academic judgment does not insulate universities from fraud liability.

Moreover, the conduct at issue—fabricating a meeting requirement, consulting a conflicted party about his own conflict, and excluding OCR from correspondence—is not "academic judgment." It is administrative decision-making that happens to occur in an academic setting. Under *Woodward v. Dietrich*, 378 Pa. Super. 111, 548 A.2d 301 (1988), fraud liability extends to third parties without privity where reliance was "specially foreseeable." If Defendants can be held liable for misrepresentations to third parties, they certainly can be held liable for misrepresentations to the students they serve.

**CONCLUSION**

For these reasons, Plaintiff has demonstrated genuine disputes of material fact on each element of his Pennsylvania common law fraud claim. Summary judgment on Count IV must be denied.
