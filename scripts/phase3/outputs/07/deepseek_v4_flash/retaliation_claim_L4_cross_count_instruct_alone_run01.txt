**ARGUMENT**

Count II of Plaintiff's complaint asserts that Defendant Duquesne University retaliated against him for engaging in protected activity under Title IX, in violation of 20 U.S.C. § 1681. To establish a prima facie case of Title IX retaliation, Plaintiff must demonstrate: (1) protected activity; (2) an adverse action that would deter a reasonable person from engaging in protected activity; and (3) a causal connection between the protected activity and the adverse action. *Doe v. Mercy Catholic Medical Center*, No. 16-1247 (3d Cir. 2017) (applying Title VII burden-shifting framework); *Atkinson v. Lafayette College*, 460 F.3d 447, 456 (3d Cir. 2006) (holding that "demanding compliance with Title IX is protected activity"). As demonstrated below, the undisputed evidence establishes all three elements.

**I. Plaintiff Engaged in Continuous Protected Activity**

Plaintiff engaged in a sustained course of protected activity beginning June 30, 2025, and continuing through the present. The record reflects at least seven discrete protected acts: (1) reporting sex-based harassment to Dean Wasilko on June 30 (Ex. B-0); (2) escalating his Title IX complaint on July 24 (Ex. B-1); (3) filing a complaint with the U.S. Department of Education's Office for Civil Rights ("OCR") on July 26 (Ex. B-3A); (4) filing the instant federal lawsuit on September 9 (ECF docket); (5) filing a motion for temporary restraining order ("TRO") on October 8 (ECF docket); and (6) testifying at the TRO hearing on October 15 (TRO Tr.). *See Atkinson*, 460 F.3d at 456 ("Demanding compliance with Title IX is protected activity."). Plaintiff need only have held an "objectively reasonable belief, in good faith" that the conduct he opposed violated Title VII. *Kengerski v. Harper*, No. 20-1307 (3d Cir. 2021). Plaintiff's multiple reports to university officials, his OCR complaint, and his federal lawsuit all satisfy this standard.

**II. Plaintiff Suffered Multiple Adverse Actions That Would Deter a Reasonable Person**

The record is replete with adverse actions that a reasonable person would find deterring. First, university actors systematically removed OCR from the CC line on institutional correspondence. On July 28—just two days after Plaintiff filed his OCR complaint—Dean Wasilko removed OCR from the CC line (Ex. B-3). This pattern continued through at least eight separate occasions (Exs. B-3 through B-7 series). No federal authority or university policy authorized this conduct. Second, on August 13-14, Title IX Coordinator Simpson acknowledged Title IX jurisdiction over Plaintiff's complaint at 2:02 PM (Ex. B-5), then reversed her jurisdictional determination just nineteen hours later on August 14 at 9:42 AM, on an identical factual record (Ex. B-6). This reversal occurred immediately after Plaintiff sent three emails consisting entirely of protected activity (Exs. B-5A, B-5B, B-5C). Third, on August 15, Executive Director of Student Conduct Sawa threatened Plaintiff with disciplinary charges for seeking help from university officials (Ex. B-7). Fourth, on October 17—just two days after Plaintiff testified at the TRO hearing—Chair Selcer revoked Plaintiff's Zoom access to seminars (Ex. V). Selcer provided shifting justifications and ultimately "c[ould not] explain why" Zoom was denied (Ex. L; SJ 7.3). Fifth, in September 2025, Dean Blair threatened Plaintiff with academic failure if he did not attend Selcer's class in person during active federal litigation against Selcer (SAC ¶¶ 66, 76, 336). Sixth, on February 13, 2026, Selcer reassigned Plaintiff's TA position, fabricating "performance issues raised by Faculty B" that Faculty B herself never raised (Exs. X-7, X-8). Faculty B's same-day email described the reassignment as keeping a "door open" with "no performance complaint" (Ex. X-7). Selcer then threatened Plaintiff's stipend through Spring 2029 (Ex. X-8). Each of these actions—stripping Plaintiff of access, threatening discipline, fabricating performance issues, and threatening financial harm—"would deter a reasonable person" from engaging in protected activity. *Doe v. Mercy Catholic Medical Center*, No. 16-1247.

**III. The Causal Connection Is Established by Temporal Proximity and a Pattern of Antagonism**

Causation is established through multiple avenues. First, the temporal proximity between Plaintiff's protected activity and the adverse actions is "unusually suggestive." *Jalil v. Avdel Corp.*, 873 F.2d 701, 708 (3d Cir. 1989). The removal of OCR from the CC line occurred just two days after Plaintiff's OCR filing (Ex. B-3). The Zoom revocation occurred just two days after Plaintiff's TRO testimony (Ex. V; TRO Tr.). The fabricated performance issues and stipend threat followed immediately after Plaintiff's ongoing federal litigation (Exs. X-7, X-8). Second, the record reveals a clear "pattern of antagonism" following Plaintiff's protected activity. *Kachmar v. SunGard Data Systems, Inc.*, 109 F.3d 173, 178 (3d Cir. 1997). Between July 28 and October 2025, four institutional actors systematically removed OCR from correspondence on eight or more occasions—each time after Plaintiff restored OCR (Exs. B-3 through B-7 series). This pattern demonstrates a coordinated effort to exclude the very agency Plaintiff had invoked. Third, the "inconsistent explanations" for adverse actions independently establish causation. *Farrell v. Planters Lifesavers Co.*, 206 F.3d 271, 286 (3d Cir. 2000). Selcer provided shifting justifications for the Zoom denial (Ex. L; SJ 7.3). Selcer's February 13, 2026 email introduced "performance issues" language that Faculty B's same-day email directly contradicted (Exs. X-7, X-8). At the TRO hearing, Selcer testified that Plaintiff had no performance issues (TRO Tr. at *:*). These inconsistencies establish pretext and causal connection. *Canada v. Samuel Grossi & Sons, Inc.*, No. 20-2747 (3d Cir. 2022) ("The employer's motivation for investigating the plaintiff is itself evidence of pretext."). Fourth, the "proffered evidence, looked at as a whole, may suffice to raise the inference" of causation. *Kachmar*, 109 F.3d at 178; *Farrell*, 206 F.3d at 286. The entire record demonstrates that each escalation of protected activity was met with immediate retaliation. This "convincing mosaic of circumstantial evidence is sufficient to establish causation for retaliation claims." *Canada*, No. 20-2747.

**IV. The University's Response Was Clearly Unreasonable Under Davis**

The retaliatory response to Plaintiff's protected activity is "clearly unreasonable in light of the known circumstances." *Davis v. Monroe County Board of Education*, 526 U.S. 629, 648 (1999). The university had actual knowledge of Plaintiff's sex-based harassment and homelessness through multiple reports to Dean Wasilko (Exs. B-0, B-0A), Title IX Coordinator Simpson (Ex. B-1), and other senior leaders. Yet it refused to provide supportive measures—including housing and no-contact orders—that are mandatory under Title IX. *See* 34 C.F.R. § 106.30 (supportive measures are non-disciplinary, non-punitive, individualized, and available without fee or charge); Ex. Z-c (PCHE Training Slide 38-40 describing supportive measures as "mandatory," "non-punitive," and including "changes in housing"). The university's failure to forward Plaintiff's reports to the Title IX Coordinator, its refusal to implement supportive measures, and its active retaliation constitute a "clearly unreasonable" response. *Hall v. Millersville University*, No. 19-3275 (3d Cir. Jan. 11, 2022) (failure to forward sexual misconduct reports can constitute clearly unreasonable response). At the prima facie stage, Plaintiff need only show that protected activity was the "likely reason" for the adverse action—not the but-for reason. *Carvalho-Grevious v. Delaware State University*, No. 15-3521 (3d Cir. 2017). Plaintiff easily satisfies this standard.

**CONCLUSION**

For the foregoing reasons, Plaintiff has established a prima facie case of Title IX retaliation. The undisputed evidence demonstrates protected activity, multiple adverse actions, and a causal connection established through temporal proximity, a pattern of antagonism, and inconsistent explanations. Summary judgment should be denied.
