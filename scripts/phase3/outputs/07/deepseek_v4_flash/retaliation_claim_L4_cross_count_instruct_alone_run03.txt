**ARGUMENT**

**I. The Legal Standard for Title IX Retaliation**

Title IX retaliation claims are analyzed under the Title VII burden-shifting framework. *Doe v. Mercy Catholic Medical Center*, No. 16-1247 (3d Cir. 2017). To establish a prima facie case of retaliation, Plaintiff must show: (1) protected activity; (2) adverse action that would deter a reasonable person from engaging in protected activity; and (3) a causal connection between the protected activity and the adverse action. *See id.* At the summary judgment stage, Plaintiff need only demonstrate that protected activity was the "likely reason" for the adverse action—not the but-for reason. *Carvalho-Grevious v. Delaware State University*, No. 15-3521 (3d Cir. 2017).

**II. Plaintiff Engaged in Continuous Protected Activity**

Plaintiff engaged in a sustained pattern of protected activity beginning June 30, 2025 and continuing through the present. Exhibits B-0, B-0A, B-3A. "Demanding compliance with Title IX is protected activity." *Atkinson v. Lafayette College*, 460 F.3d 447 (3d Cir. 2006). Plaintiff need only hold an "objectively reasonable belief, in good faith" that the conduct opposed violates the statute. *Kengerski v. Harper*, No. 20-1307 (3d Cir. 2021).

Plaintiff's protected activity includes: reporting sex-based harassment to Dean Wasilko on June 30 (Exhibit B-0); formally escalating Title IX concerns citing 34 C.F.R. §106.44(a) on July 24 (Exhibit B-0A); filing a complaint with the Office for Civil Rights on July 26 (Exhibit B-3A); filing a federal lawsuit on September 9; filing a TRO motion on October 8; and testifying at the TRO hearing on October 15. Statements of Undisputed Material Facts ("SUMF") ¶ 1. Each of these actions constitutes protected activity because Plaintiff was reasonably opposing conduct he believed violated Title IX. *Kengerski*, No. 20-1307.

**III. Duquesne University Took Multiple Adverse Actions That Would Deter a Reasonable Person**

The record demonstrates a systematic pattern of adverse actions directly tied to Plaintiff's protected activity. "Adverse action" in the retaliation context is defined broadly: it is any action that would deter a reasonable person from engaging in protected activity. *See Doe v. Mercy Catholic Medical Center*, No. 16-1247.

**A. Removal of OCR from Institutional Correspondence**

On July 28, 2025—two days after Plaintiff's OCR filing—Dean Wasilko removed OCR from the CC line on his responsive email. SUMF ¶ 2; Exhibit B-3. Between July 28 and October 2025, four institutional actors (Wasilko, Simpson, Sawa, and others) systematically removed OCR from the CC line on eight or more occasions, each time after Plaintiff restored OCR. SUMF ¶ 3; Exhibits B-3 through B-7 series. No federal authority or Duquesne policy authorized removal of a federal enforcement agency from institutional correspondence. SUMF ¶ 3. This conduct was designed to sever Plaintiff's connection to his federal enforcement channel—a classic deterrent to future protected activity.

**B. Simpson's 19-Hour Jurisdictional Reversal**

On August 13, 2025, at 2:02 PM, Title IX Coordinator Simpson acknowledged Title IX jurisdiction using the §106.30 term of art "supportive measures." Exhibit B-5; SUMF ¶ 4. Between 4:20 PM and 7:31 PM that same day, Plaintiff sent three emails consisting entirely of protected activity and zero new facts about the underlying complaint. Exhibits B-5A, B-5B, B-5C. On August 14, at 9:42 AM—just nineteen hours later and on an identical factual record—Simpson reversed her jurisdictional determination, explicitly disclaiming jurisdiction over sex discrimination complaints. Exhibit B-6; SUMF ¶ 4.

The timing is dispositive. Simpson's reversal occurred immediately after Plaintiff engaged in protected activity—demanding OCR compliance, restoring OCR to the correspondence chain, and citing federal regulations. No new information supported the reversal; the factual record was identical. This is precisely the kind of "clearly unreasonable" response that constitutes retaliation. *See Davis v. Monroe County Board of Education*, 526 U.S. 629, 648 (1999) (response is deliberately indifferent if "clearly unreasonable in light of the known circumstances").

**C. Sawa's Threat of Disciplinary Charges**

On August 15, 2025, Executive Director of Student Conduct Sawa threatened Plaintiff with disciplinary charges for seeking help from university officials via mass email. Exhibit B-7. Sawa wrote: "you must stop emailing University officials about your concerns" and warned that "failure to do so may result in charges against you for violating the Student Code of Conduct." *Id.* This threat to punish Plaintiff for communicating with university officials about his Title IX concerns is a paradigmatic adverse action—it directly warns that further protected activity will result in punishment.

**D. Selcer's Zoom Revocation and Shifting Justifications**

On October 6, 2025, Professor Faculty C authorized Zoom access for Plaintiff. Exhibit Q, W. On October 8, Plaintiff filed his TRO motion. On October 15, Plaintiff testified at the TRO hearing. On October 17—two days after the hearing—Selcer manufactured Zoom revocation. SUMF ¶ 5; Exhibit V, Q. Selcer provided shifting justifications for the denial and ultimately told Colleague A he "can't explain why" Zoom was denied. SUMF ¶ 6. No university or departmental policy constrained Zoom access. SUMF ¶ 6.

The temporal proximity here is "unusually suggestive." *Jalil v. Avdel Corp.*, 873 F.2d 701, 708 (3d Cir. 1989) (two-day proximity between protected activity and adverse action sufficient to establish causation). Selcer testified under oath at the TRO hearing that Plaintiff could continue attending via Zoom, then revoked that access immediately after the hearing. Exhibit Q. The shifting explanations—first claiming Faculty C found "no compelling reason," then Faculty C stating he was unaware of the prior authorization—demonstrate pretext. *Farrell v. Planters Lifesavers Co.*, 206 F.3d 271, 286 (3d Cir. 2000) ("inconsistent explanations establish causal link").

**E. Dean A's Threat of Academic Failure**

In September 2025, Dean A threatened Plaintiff with academic failure if he did not attend Selcer's class in person during active federal litigation against Selcer. SUMF ¶ 7; Exhibits K-2, K-5. After Plaintiff disclosed a conflict of interest because Selcer was a named defendant in the federal lawsuit, Dean A consulted Selcer and jointly determined that Plaintiff must remain in the course or face failure and delayed graduation. Exhibit K-2. This is a direct threat to Plaintiff's academic standing for engaging in protected litigation activity.

**F. Selcer's Fabricated Performance Issues and Stipend Threat**

On February 13, 2026, Selcer reassigned Plaintiff's TA position and fabricated "performance issues raised by Faculty B." Exhibit X-8; SUMF ¶ 8. Faculty B's own same-day email made no mention of performance issues and described the reassignment request as keeping a "door open" with "no performance complaint." Exhibit X-7. Faculty B's email stated: "I have asked Dan to reassign your TA position (he says this will not affect your funding). My office door is always open to you during my office hours." *Id.* Selcer's email mid-sentence recharacterized "tensions and confrontations" as "performance issues" and threatened Plaintiff's stipend through Spring 2029. Exhibit X-8; SUMF ¶ 8. Critically, Selcer testified at the TRO hearing that Plaintiff had "no performance issues." SUMF ¶ 9; TRO Tr. at *:*.

The contrast between Faculty B's actual email and Selcer's mischaracterization—combined with Selcer's prior sworn testimony that no performance issues existed—establishes that the "performance issues" claim was a pretextual justification for retaliation. *Taylor v. Phoenixville School District*, 184 F.3d 296, 312 (3d Cir. 1999) (employer acting in bad faith cannot increase responsibilities and then document failures).

**IV. Strong Causal Connection Exists Between Protected Activity and Adverse Actions**

**A. Temporal Proximity**

The temporal proximity between Plaintiff's protected activity and each adverse action is striking:

- **OCR removal (July 28):** Two days after OCR filing (July 26). *See Jalil*, 873 F.2d at 708 (two days sufficient).
- **Sawa threat (August 15):** One day after Simpson's reversal and two days after Plaintiff's protected emails citing §106.44.
- **Zoom revocation (October 17):** Two days after Plaintiff's TRO testimony (October 15) and nine days after TRO filing (October 8). *Id.*
- **Fabricated performance issues (February 13, 2026):** Months after federal litigation began, but in the midst of active litigation. The Supreme Court recognizes that causation can be established through ongoing proximity. *See Price Waterhouse v. Hopkins*, 490 U.S. 228, 252 (1989) (gender played motivating part).

**B. Pattern of Antagonism**

Even if temporal proximity alone were insufficient, the record demonstrates a "pattern of antagonism" following Plaintiff's protected conduct. *Kachmar v. SunGard Data Systems, Inc.*, 109 F.3d 173, 177 (3d Cir. 1997). The pattern includes: (1) systematic removal of OCR from correspondence; (2) Simpson's 19-hour jurisdictional flip; (3) Sawa's threat to charge Plaintiff for emailing officials; (4) Selcer's Zoom revocation with shifting explanations; (5) Dean A's academic failure threat; and (6) Selcer's fabricated performance issues and stipend threat. As *Kachmar* holds, "circumstantial evidence of a 'pattern of antagonism' following the protected conduct can also give rise to the inference" of causation. *Id.* at 177.

**C. Shifting Explanations Establish Pretext**

Defendant's shifting, inconsistent explanations for adverse actions independently establish the causal link. "The employer's motivation for investigating the plaintiff is itself evidence of pretext." *Canada v. Samuel Grossi & Sons, Inc.*, No. 20-2747 (3d Cir. 2022). Selcer claimed Faculty C found "no compelling reason" for Zoom access, but Faculty C told Plaintiff he was unaware of the prior authorization and confused by Selcer's claim. Exhibit Q, W. Selcer claimed "performance issues raised by Faculty B," but Faculty B's same-day email said the opposite. Exhibits X-7, X-8. These inconsistent explanations create genuine disputes of material fact that preclude summary judgment. *Foster v. Board of Regents of University of Michigan*, No. 19-1314 (6th Cir. 2020) (en banc) (genuine disputes precluded summary judgment where university's response was ineffective).

**V. A Convincing Mosaic of Circumstantial Evidence Supports Causation**

The Third Circuit has held that "a convincing mosaic of circumstantial evidence is sufficient to establish causation for retaliation claims." *Canada*, No. 20-2747. The evidence here—viewed "as a whole," *Farrell*, 206 F.3d at 286—creates a compelling mosaic:

- Defendant removed OCR from correspondence immediately after OCR was added.
- Simpson acknowledged jurisdiction, then reversed after Plaintiff's protected emails—with no new facts.
- Sawa threatened Plaintiff for emailing about his safety concerns.
- Selcer promised Zoom access under oath, then revoked it two days after Plaintiff's testimony.
- Selcer fabricated performance issues despite Faculty B's explicit statement that no performance complaint existed—and despite Selcer's own prior sworn testimony.

"Temporal proximity combined with contextual evidence of retaliatory animus is sufficient to establish causation." *Lauren W. ex rel. Jean W. v. DeFlaminis*, 480 F.3d 259, 267 (3d Cir. 2007). The contextual evidence here includes the coordinated nature of Defendants' actions. Four institutional actors systematically removed OCR from eight or more occasions. SUMF ¶ 3. Dean A consulted Selcer before threatening Plaintiff with academic failure. Exhibit K-2. Selcer coordinated with Faculty C to revoke Zoom access. Exhibit V, Q. This pattern of coordination and shifting justifications establishes that the adverse actions were causally connected to Plaintiff's protected activity.

**VI. The Deliberate Indifference Standard Further Supports Plaintiff's Claim**

Under *Davis v. Monroe County Board of Education*, 526 U.S. 629 (1999), a funding recipient is liable where it acts with deliberate indifference to known harassment that denies access to educational opportunities. The Hall court clarified that "[i]t is an intentional violation of Title IX's terms for a funding recipient to act with deliberate indifference to known sexual harassment." *Hall v. Millersville University*, No. 19-3275 (3d Cir. Jan. 11, 2022).

Here, Defendant's response was "clearly unreasonable." *Davis*, 526 U.S. at 648. Defendant had actual knowledge of Plaintiff's harassment and safety concerns. Exhibit B-0 (June 30 report); Exhibit B-3A (July 26 OCR filing). Defendant had guidance through training materials requiring supportive measures to be "non-disciplinary, non-punitive, individualized, and available at no cost." Exhibits Z-c, Z-b. Yet Defendant did the opposite: it retaliated against Plaintiff for seeking those measures. The Title IX training materials explicitly state that supportive measures "must be documented" and are "designed to restore or preserve equal access." Exhibit Z-c at Slide 38-40. Defendant's response—threatening discipline, fabricating performance issues, revoking Zoom access—was the antithesis of a supportive measure.

**VII. Genuine Disputes of Material Fact Preclude Summary Judgment**

Summary judgment is inappropriate where, as here, the evidence creates genuine disputes on material facts. *McAvoy v. Dickinson College*, No. 23-2939 (3d Cir. Aug. 16, 2024) (affirming summary judgment only where no genuine dispute existed). Unlike *McAvoy*, where the court found the university's response—including a no-contact directive, accommodations, and a five-month investigation—was not clearly unreasonable, Defendant here provided none of those measures. Instead, Defendant systematically retaliated.

Whether Defendant's actions would deter a reasonable person is a question for the jury. Whether the causal connection exists is a question for the jury. Whether Defendant's shifting explanations are pretextual is a question for the jury. *Carvalho-Grevious*, No. 15-3521 (reversing summary judgment where plaintiff showed protected activity was "likely reason" for adverse action).

**CONCLUSION**

Plaintiff has established a prima facie case of retaliation under Title IX. He engaged in continuous protected activity. Defendant subjected him to multiple adverse actions—OCR removal, jurisdictional reversal, disciplinary threats, Zoom revocation, academic failure threats, and fabricated performance issues—that would deter any reasonable person. The temporal proximity, pattern of antagonism, and shifting explanations provide overwhelming circumstantial evidence of causation. Genuine disputes of material fact preclude summary judgment, and Count II must proceed to trial.
