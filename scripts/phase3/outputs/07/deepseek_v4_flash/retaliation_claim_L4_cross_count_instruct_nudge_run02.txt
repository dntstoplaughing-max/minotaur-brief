**ARGUMENT**

Count II of Plaintiff’s complaint alleges that Duquesne University retaliated against him for engaging in conduct protected under Title IX, 20 U.S.C. § 1681. Title IX retaliation claims are analyzed under the Title VII burden-shifting framework. *Doe v. Mercy Catholic Medical Center*, No. 16-1247 (3d Cir. 2017). To establish a prima facie case of retaliation, Plaintiff must demonstrate: (1) protected activity; (2) an adverse action that would deter a reasonable person from engaging in protected activity; and (3) a causal connection between the protected activity and the adverse action. *Id.* The record overwhelmingly establishes each element, and Defendant cannot articulate a legitimate, non-retaliatory reason for its conduct—or, if it attempts to do so, the proffered reasons are pretext.

**I. Plaintiff Engaged in Extensive, Continuous Protected Activity**

Protected activity under Title IX includes “demanding compliance with Title IX,” *Atkinson v. Lafayette College*, 460 F.3d 447 (3d Cir. 2006), as well as reporting sex-based harassment, filing complaints with the Office for Civil Rights, filing a federal lawsuit, and testifying at a hearing. A plaintiff need only hold “an objectively reasonable belief, in good faith” that the conduct opposed violates the statute. *Kengerski v. Harper*, No. 20-1307 (3d Cir. 2021).

Plaintiff’s protected activity began on June 30, 2025, when he reported sex-based harassment to Dean Wasilko (Ex. B-0). On July 24, he formally escalated under § 106.44(a) (Ex. B-0A). On July 26, he filed a complaint with the Office for Civil Rights (OCR) (Ex. B-3A). On September 9, he filed this federal lawsuit; on October 8, he moved for a temporary restraining order; and on October 15, he testified at the TRO hearing. (ECF docket.) This continuous chain of protected activity—spanning more than 90 days—is undisputed. Each instance constituted a demand for compliance with Title IX and opposition to what Plaintiff reasonably believed to be sex discrimination. *Atkinson*, 460 F.3d at 447; *Kengerski*.

**II. Duquesne Took Multiple Adverse Actions That Would Deter a Reasonable Person**

An adverse action in the retaliation context is one that “well might have dissuaded a reasonable [person] from making or supporting a charge of discrimination.” *Farrell v. Planters Lifesavers Co.*, 206 F.3d 271 (3d Cir. 2000). The record is replete with such actions:

- *OCR Exclusion from Communications:* On July 28—two days after Plaintiff’s OCR filing—Dean Wasilko removed OCR from the CC line on his email (Ex. B-3). Between July 28 and October 2025, four institutional actors (Wasilko, Simpson, Sawa, and others) systematically removed OCR from the CC line on eight or more occasions, each time after Plaintiff restored OCR. No policy or authority justified this removal (Ex. B-3 through B-7). A reasonable person would be deterred from continuing to involve a federal enforcement agency if the university repeatedly excised it from official correspondence.

- *Simpson’s 19-Hour Jurisdictional Reversal:* On August 13 at 2:02 PM, Simpson acknowledged Title IX jurisdiction and used the term of art “supportive measures” (Ex. B-5). Between 4:20 PM and 7:31 PM that same day, Plaintiff sent three emails consisting entirely of protected activity—citing federal regulations, demanding compliance, and copying OCR—and zero new facts about the underlying complaint (Ex. B-5A, B-5B, B-5C). On August 14 at 9:42 AM—just 19 hours later—Simpson reversed her jurisdictional determination on an identical factual record (Ex. B-6). The temporal proximity of Plaintiff’s protected emails to the reversal alone supports an inference of retaliation. *Jalil v. Avdel Corp.*, 873 F.2d 701 (3d Cir. 1989) (two-day proximity sufficient). No reasonable person would continue to press Title IX claims if the coordinator disclaims jurisdiction immediately after receiving protected communications.

- *Selcer’s Zoom Revocation:* On October 6, Professor Faculty C authorized Zoom access for Plaintiff (Ex. Q). On October 8, Plaintiff filed the TRO motion. On October 15, he testified at the TRO hearing. On October 17—just two days after the hearing—Selcer manufactured a revocation, claiming Faculty C found “no compelling reason” to continue remote attendance (Ex. V). Faculty C himself stated he had authorized Zoom for the semester and was confused by Selcer’s intervention (Ex. W). The revocation occurred within 48 hours of Plaintiff’s testimony and within days of the TRO filing—a classic “unusually suggestive” temporal proximity. *Jalil*, 873 F.2d at 701.

- *Dean A’s Threat of Academic Failure:* In September 2025, Dean A threatened Plaintiff with academic failure if he did not attend Selcer’s class in person, despite knowing that Selcer was a named defendant in the pending federal litigation (Ex. K-2, K-5). Forcing a plaintiff to sit in a classroom taught by the person he is suing under Title IX—and threatening his academic standing if he refuses—is an obvious deterrent to further litigation.

- *Selcer’s Fabricated Performance Issues and Stipend Threat:* On February 13, 2026, Selcer reassigned Plaintiff’s TA position and, for the first time, alleged “performance issues raised by Faculty B” (Ex. X-8). Yet Faculty B’s own same-day email made no mention of performance issues and described the reassignment as keeping a “door open” with “no performance complaint” (Ex. X-7). Selcer’s email mid-sentence recharacterized “tensions and confrontations” as “performance issues” and threatened Plaintiff’s stipend through Spring 2029 (Ex. X-8). At the TRO hearing, Selcer had testified under oath that Plaintiff had no performance issues (TRO Tr.). The discrepancy is powerful evidence that the performance rationale was manufactured in retaliation for Plaintiff’s ongoing litigation—a quintessential adverse action.

Each of these actions—individually and collectively—would deter a reasonable person from engaging in protected activity. *Farrell*, 206 F.3d at 271.

**III. Strong Causal Connection Exists Between Plaintiff’s Protected Activity and Each Adverse Action**

Causation can be established through “unusually suggestive” temporal proximity, *Jalil*, 873 F.2d at 701, a “pattern of antagonism” following protected conduct, *Kachmar v. SunGard Data Systems, Inc.*, 109 F.3d 173 (3d Cir. 1997), or “a convincing mosaic of circumstantial evidence,” *Canada v. Samuel Grossi & Sons, Inc.*, No. 20-2747 (3d Cir. 2022). At the prima facie stage, Plaintiff “need only show that the protected activity was the likely reason for the adverse action.” *Carvalho-Grevious v. Delaware State University*, No. 15-3521 (3d Cir. 2017).

- *Temporal Proximity:* The OCR removal occurred two days after the OCR filing (Ex. B-3, B-3A). The jurisdictional reversal occurred 19 hours after Plaintiff’s protected emails (Ex. B-5, B-5A, B-5C, B-6). The Zoom revocation occurred two days after the TRO hearing (Ex. V, W; ECF docket). This pattern of near-instantaneous retaliation following each escalation is sufficient to raise an inference of causation. *Jalil*, 873 F.2d at 701; *Lauren W. ex rel. Jean W. v. DeFlaminis*, 480 F.3d 259 (3d Cir. 2007) (temporal proximity plus contextual evidence sufficient).

- *Pattern of Antagonism:* The record shows a systematic campaign of retaliation. Every time Plaintiff invoked OCR or federal court, institutional actors responded with a new adverse action: first removing OCR, then disclaiming jurisdiction, then threatening conduct charges, then revoking Zoom, then fabricating performance issues. This uninterrupted “pattern of antagonism” independently establishes a causal link. *Kachmar*, 109 F.3d at 173.

- *Pretext and Shifting Justifications:* Selcer’s justifications for the Zoom revocation shifted and ultimately collapsed: he told Plaintiff that Faculty C found “no compelling reason,” but Faculty C had already authorized Zoom and expressed confusion (Ex. V, W). Selcer later told Colleague A he “can’t explain why” Zoom was denied (SJ 7.3). Similarly, Selcer fabricated “performance issues” from a supervisor who expressly disclaimed any performance complaint (Ex. X-7, X-8). The “employer’s motivation for investigating the plaintiff is itself evidence of pretext.” *Canada*, No. 20-2747. When “the proffered evidence, looked at as a whole,” suffices to raise the inference of causation, summary judgment must be denied. *Farrell*, 206 F.3d at 271.

**IV. Defendant Cannot Satisfy Its Burden of Articulating a Legitimate, Non-Retaliatory Reason; Even If It Could, the Reasons Are Pretextual**

Under the Title VII framework adopted for Title IX retaliation, *Doe v. Mercy Catholic Medical Center*, once Plaintiff establishes a prima facie case, the burden shifts to Defendant to articulate a legitimate, non-retaliatory reason for the adverse actions. Defendant cannot meet that burden here. There is no legitimate reason to remove a federal enforcement agency from institutional correspondence. There is no legitimate reason to reverse a jurisdictional determination 19 hours after receiving protected emails that added no new facts. There is no legitimate reason to revoke Zoom access two days after a plaintiff testifies at a TRO hearing, especially when the instructor who authorized it never withdrew that authorization.

Even if Defendant attempts to proffer reasons—such as “policy compliance” or “performance issues”—the record demonstrates pretext. Simpson’s claim that Plaintiff needed to meet before she could act is contradicted by her own August 13 email acknowledging jurisdiction (Ex. B-5). Selcer’s claim of “performance issues” is directly contradicted by his own testimony and Faculty B’s email (Ex. X-7; TRO Tr.). Where “inconsistent explanations” exist, a jury can infer retaliatory motive. *Farrell*, 206 F.3d at 271. The same is true for the shifting justifications for Zoom denial (Ex. V, W). “A convincing mosaic of circumstantial evidence” precludes summary judgment. *Canada*.

**CONCLUSION**

Plaintiff has presented undisputed evidence of continuous protected activity, a pattern of adverse actions that would deter any reasonable person, and a causal connection established by temporal proximity, a pattern of antagonism, and overwhelming evidence of pretext. Duquesne University is not entitled to summary judgment on Count II.
