**ARGUMENT**

Plaintiff David Mullins is entitled to relief under Title IX because Defendant Duquesne University of the Holy Spirit (“Duquesne”) had actual knowledge of severe, pervasive, and objectively offensive sexual harassment, yet responded in a manner that was clearly unreasonable—effectively ignoring Plaintiff’s pleas for safety and supportive measures for 45 days. The undisputed facts, when measured against the controlling case law from the Supreme Court and the Third Circuit, establish each element of deliberate indifference.

**I. D uquesne Had Actual Knowledge of Sexual Harassment Through Officials with Authority to Institute Corrective Measures**

Actual knowledge is established when notice of sexual harassment or allegations of sexual harassment is provided to “a recipient’s Title IX Coordinator; or any official of the recipient who has authority to institute corrective measures on behalf of the recipient.” *See* Exhibit Z-e, Slide 30 (PCHE Training, defining “Actual Knowledge”). Duquesne’s own training materials identify the Dean of Students, Title IX Coordinator, Provost, and Vice President for Student Affairs as officials with such authority. *Id.* at Slide 32.

Here, Plaintiff first reported the harassment to Dean Wasilko on June 30, 2025, describing a physical pursuit and confrontation by his roommate, and requesting emergency housing. (Exhibit B-0.) Dean Wasilko, as Associate Vice President for Student Life and Dean of Students, possessed authority to institute corrective measures. On July 24, 2025, Plaintiff escalated the matter directly to Title IX Coordinator Alicia Simpson, who acknowledged receipt and stated that her “primary roles is to help ensure your health and safety.” (Exhibit B-1.) By that date, both the Dean of Students and the Title IX Coordinator had actual knowledge of the harassment.

On August 13, 2025, Plaintiff sent an Emergency Removal Request to Simpson, attaching sworn declarations and a physician’s letter documenting a reasonable fear of physical harm. (Exhibit F; SAC ¶¶ 168-172.) Simpson responded at 2:02 PM that same day, explicitly stating: “Your concerns and allegations warrant further information gathering and review.” (Exhibit B-5.) Simpson’s acknowledgment itself constitutes actual knowledge that harassment allegations existed. *See* Exhibit Z-e, Slide 30 (“actual knowledge means notice of sexual harassment or allegations of sexual harassment”). Simpson is the Title IX Coordinator—the primary official charged with receiving and acting on such reports. *See* Exhibit P (Appendix C, listing Simpson as Title IX Coordinator).

Thus, from June 30, 2025 onward, Duquesne had actual knowledge of actionable sexual harassment through multiple officials with authority to act.

**II. The Harassment Was Severe, Pervasive, and Objectively Offensive**

Under *Davis v. Monroe County Board of Education*, 526 U.S. 629, 650-51 (1999), a plaintiff must show harassment that is “so severe, pervasive, and objectively offensive that it can be said to deprive the victims of access to the educational opportunities or benefits provided by the school.” Here, Plaintiff reported that his roommate: (1) yelled at him and got within inches of his face; (2) aggressively followed him through the apartment and up the stairs, confirming she was following him; (3) said “I’m coming up” as he fled to his room; (4) locked him out of the apartment on a prior occasion; and (5) engaged in a pattern of manipulation and retaliation after Plaintiff sought help from the landlord. (Exhibit D, Signal Transcript & Timeline; Exhibit B-0.)

The conduct was not a single isolated incident. The June 30 confrontation was preceded by a escalating pattern of harassment over several weeks, including the lockout on June 27 and refusal to allow Plaintiff to control the temperature in his own living space. *Id.* After Plaintiff moved out on July 1, the harasser continued to follow and harass him. (Exhibit S, Mullins email describing “predator vibes” and being followed while moving out.) The medical provider, Treating Physician, confirmed that Plaintiff’s fear was reasonable and that sharing a living space with the harasser would “cause him a great deal of stress, would be highly likely to worsen his medical condition, and could impair his academic functioning.” (Exhibit F, Medical Letter 1.) Such conduct—physical pursuit, verbal confrontation, and ongoing intimidation—is objectively offensive and would cause a reasonable student to fear for their safety.

**III. Duquesne’s Response Was Clearly Unreasonable Under the Known Circumstances**

Under *Davis*, a school is liable where its response to known harassment “is clearly unreasonable in light of the known circumstances.” 526 U.S. at 648. The Third Circuit has repeatedly applied this standard, holding that a “funding recipient may be liable for deliberate indifference to known sexual harassment where the recipient exercises substantial control over the context and the harasser.” *Hall v. Millersville University*, No. 19-3275, slip op. at 10 (3d Cir. Jan. 11, 2022). In *Hall*, the Third Circuit found that a university’s failure to forward a sexual misconduct report to the Title IX Coordinator and failure to contact the victim constituted a “clearly unreasonable” response. *Id.*

Duquesne’s response—or lack thereof—during the critical 45-day period from June 30 to August 14, 2025, was far more egregious than the conduct in *Hall*. The record shows:

- **No supportive measures were ever offered or provided.** 34 C.F.R. § 106.44(a) requires a school to promptly contact a complainant to discuss supportive measures, consider the complainant’s wishes, inform the complainant of available measures, and explain the process for filing a formal complaint. Simpson performed *zero* of these obligations. (Undisputed Fact 10.) Although Simpson stated in her August 13 email that she could “discuss your current needs relating to supportive measures,” she conditioned any action on a meeting—despite Plaintiff’s clear written refusal to meet. (Exhibit B-5; Exhibit B-5A.)

- **The only substantive action was to reverse jurisdiction without new facts.** On August 13, Simpson acknowledged that the allegations “warrant further information gathering and review.” (Exhibit B-5.) Nineteen hours later, after receiving three emails from Plaintiff that contained *no new facts* about the underlying complaint but merely invoked his rights under § 106.71, Simpson reversed her preliminary determination and declared that the Title IX Office “does not have jurisdiction” and “cannot take action or provide assistance as requested.” (Exhibit B-6.) There were no intervening facts. The only change was Plaintiff’s refusal to meet and his insistence on written communications. Such a rapid and unexplained about-face—especially after acknowledging that the allegations warranted review—is the epitome of a clearly unreasonable response. *Cf. Hall*, slip op. at 10 (failure to forward report and failure to contact victim was clearly unreasonable).

- **Simpson failed to follow mandatory Title IX procedures.** She did not offer Plaintiff appeal rights after the dismissal, as required by § 106.45(b)(8). She did not send simultaneous written notice to the respondent, as required by § 106.45(b)(3)(iii). (Undisputed Fact 9.) These procedural failures demonstrate that Duquesne’s response was not merely delayed but deliberately dismissive.

- **The school did nothing in the face of escalating risk.** While Plaintiff became homeless, his bank account fell to $16.65 and then negative $2.55, and his doctor warned of worsening medical conditions, Duquesne’s only communications were demands to meet, referrals to other offices, and jurisdictional disclaimers. (Exhibits B-0 through B-6; Undisputed Fact 11; Exhibit F.)

The Third Circuit’s decision in *McAvoy v. Dickinson College*, No. 23-2939 (3d Cir. Aug. 16, 2024), is distinguishable. In *McAvoy*, the university actually issued a no-contact directive, provided academic and mental health accommodations, conducted a five-month investigation, and imposed a semester of probation. Here, Duquesne provided *nothing*. As the *McAvoy* court implicitly recognized, a “clearly unreasonable” response includes doing nothing at all. Moreover, the *Foster* dissent—adopted by the Third Circuit in *Hall*—makes clear that “minimal, ineffective, or belated efforts to respond to sexual harassment” can be clearly unreasonable as a matter of law. *Foster v. Board of Regents of University of Michigan*, No. 19-1314 (6th Cir. Dec. 11, 2020) (en banc) (dissent).

Duquesne’s response was not merely slow; it was a deliberate refusal to act. Simpson had the authority to issue a no-contact order or emergency removal without a meeting. See Exhibit Z-c, Slide 40 (supportive measures include “mutual no contact orders” and can be implemented “at any time after a report”). She chose not to. This is the very definition of deliberate indifference.

**IV. The Harassment and Duquesne’s Response Denied Plaintiff Equal Access to Educational Opportunities**

Under *Davis*, the harassment must be “so severe, pervasive, and objectively offensive that it can be said to deprive the victims of access to the educational opportunities or benefits provided by the school.” 526 U.S. at 650. Here, Plaintiff’s academic functioning was directly impaired. His physician stated that his homelessness and fear of his abuser “likely worsens his mental health conditions,” and that “worsening his mental health conditions is expected to negatively impact his graduate education.” (Exhibit F, Medical Letter 2.) Plaintiff was forced to flee his home, incurred substantial financial costs, and could not participate in a planned academic trip to Germany. (Exhibit S.) He was unable to safely share office or classroom space with the harasser, and his department chair confirmed that steps would be taken to avoid contact—but the Title IX office itself refused to ensure safety. (Exhibit R.)

Moreover, Duquesne’s failure to provide supportive measures left Plaintiff without housing or financial stability, directly impeding his ability to focus on his doctoral studies. A student who is homeless, fearful, and medically compromised is denied equal access to education. The Third Circuit, applying *Doe v. University of the Sciences*, 961 F.3d 203 (3d Cir. 2020), requires only that “the alleged facts, if true, must support a plausible inference that a federally-funded college or university discriminated against a person on the basis of sex.” *Id.* at 209. Here, the inference is overwhelming: Duquesne treated Plaintiff’s safety concerns as an administrative inconvenience, dismissed them without investigation, and left him to fend for himself—all because he reported sex-based harassment.

**Conclusion**

Duquesne had actual knowledge of severe, pervasive, and objectively offensive harassment. Its response—a 45-day silence punctuated by demands for meetings, a jurisdictional reversal based on no new facts, and a complete refusal to provide supportive measures—was clearly unreasonable and deliberately indifferent. Plaintiff has satisfied each element of a Title IX deliberate indifference claim. This Court should deny any motion for summary judgment and permit the claim to proceed to trial.
