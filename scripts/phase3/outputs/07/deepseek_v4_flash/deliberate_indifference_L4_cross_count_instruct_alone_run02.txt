**ARGUMENT**

**I. Duquesne University Had Actual Knowledge of Sexual Harassment Through Officials with Authority to Institute Corrective Measures**

The undisputed facts demonstrate that Duquesne University received actual notice of actionable sexual harassment through multiple officials with authority to act. On June 30, 2025, Plaintiff David Mullins reported to Dean Wasilko—the Associate Vice President for Student Life and Dean of Students—that a fellow graduate student had physically pursued him through his home, yelled at him within inches of his face, followed him up the stairs when he attempted to retreat, and confirmed she was following him while stating "I'm coming up" to his bedroom. (Exhibit B-0.) Dean Wasilko is precisely the type of official with "authority to institute corrective measures" that triggers institutional knowledge under Title IX. *See* Exhibit Z-e at Slide 32 (identifying "Dean of Students" as an official with actual knowledge).

The Third Circuit has unequivocally held that a federal funding recipient has adequate notice under Title IX "where the recipient exercises substantial control over the context in which the harassment occurs and the harasser, even if they are a third party." *Hall v. Millersville University*, No. 19-3275, slip op. at *12 (3d Cir. Jan. 11, 2022). Here, both the harasser (Respondent) and the context (Duquesne University housing and campus) were under Duquesne's substantial control.

Moreover, actual knowledge was independently established when Plaintiff sent a formal escalation to Title IX Coordinator Alicia Simpson on July 24, 2025, citing 34 C.F.R. § 106.44(a) and demanding supportive measures. (SAC ¶¶ 168-172; Exhibit B-1.) Simpson herself acknowledged this report, stating she was "in receipt of your email where you allege that there has been noncompliance with Title IX regarding a situation with another student." (Exhibit B-1.) This constitutes undisputed actual knowledge by the Title IX Coordinator—the primary official charged with Title IX compliance. *See* Exhibit Z-e at Slide 30 ("Actual knowledge means notice of sexual harassment or allegations of sexual harassment to: A recipient's Title IX Coordinator").

Plaintiff further submitted an Emergency Removal Request on August 13, 2025, citing § 106.44(c) and attaching sworn declarations and a physician's letter documenting his safety concerns. (Exhibit F; Exhibit B-5A.) Simpson responded on August 13 acknowledging that Plaintiff's "concerns and allegations warrant further information gathering and review." (Exhibit B-5.) This acknowledgment independently confirms actual knowledge.

Under *Doe v. University of the Sciences*, 961 F.3d 203 (3d Cir. 2020), the Court held that "to state a claim under Title IX, the alleged facts, if true, must support a plausible inference that a federally-funded college or university discriminated against a person on the basis of sex." *Id.* at 209. The undisputed facts here more than satisfy this straightforward pleading standard—Duquesne knew of the harassment through its highest-ranking student affairs and Title IX officials.

**II. Duquesne's Response Was Clearly Unreasonable and Constitutes Deliberate Indifference as a Matter of Law**

Under *Davis v. Monroe County Board of Education*, 526 U.S. 629 (1999), a funding recipient is liable for deliberate indifference where "the recipient's response to the harassment or lack thereof is clearly unreasonable in light of the known circumstances." *Id.* at 648. The Third Circuit has made clear that "neither does [the deliberate indifference standard] require courts to conclude that minimal, ineffective, or belated efforts to respond to sexual harassment are not clearly unreasonable as a matter of law." *Foster v. Board of Regents of University of Michigan*, No. 19-1314 (6th Cir. Dec. 11, 2020) (en banc) (dissent, though recognizing that minimal efforts can be clearly unreasonable).

The record establishes a pattern of conduct that is the antithesis of a reasonable response:

**A. Complete Failure to Provide Supportive Measures During 45 Critical Days**

Between June 30, 2025 and August 14, 2025—a period of 45 days—Defendants offered and provided no supportive measures whatsoever through the Title IX process. (Exhibits B-0 through B-6.) The undisputed facts confirm that "Defendants' communications consisted exclusively of requests to schedule meetings, directives to 'engage with' offices, and jurisdictional disclaimers." (Statement of Undisputed Material Facts ¶ 8.)

This stands in stark contrast to what Title IX requires. As Exhibit Z-c makes clear, supportive measures are "available at any time after a report of sexual harassment" and include "mutual no contact orders," "changes in housing," "work or class schedule modifications," and "extensions of deadlines." (Exhibit Z-c at Slides 39-40.) The Title IX Coordinator bears "responsibility for coordinating the effective implementation of supportive measures." (Exhibit Z-c at Slide 38.) Simpson performed "zero of the five mandatory obligations under § 106.44(a): she did not promptly contact Plaintiff to discuss supportive measures, did not consider Plaintiff's wishes, did not inform Plaintiff of the availability of supportive measures, and did not explain the process for filing a formal complaint." (Statement of Undisputed Material Facts ¶ 10; Exhibits B-5, B-6.)

In *Hall v. Millersville University*, the Third Circuit held that failure to forward sexual misconduct reports to the Title IX Coordinator as required by policy "can constitute clearly unreasonable response." *Hall*, slip op. at *13. Here, the failure was far more egregious: the Title IX Coordinator herself received the reports and still took no action.

**B. Retaliatory Jurisdiction Reversal After Protected Activity**

The most damning evidence of deliberate indifference is the 19-hour jurisdiction reversal that occurred between August 13 and August 14, 2025. On August 13 at 2:02 PM, Simpson acknowledged Plaintiff's concerns and offered to "discuss your current needs relating to supportive measures." (Exhibit B-5.) Between 4:20 PM and 7:31 PM on August 13, Plaintiff sent three emails refusing the proposed meeting, demanding written communications, and citing §§ 106.44, 106.30, and 106.71. (Exhibits B-5A, B-5B, B-5C.) Critically, "[n]o new factual information about the underlying complaint entered the record between B-5 and B-6. The only intervening events were Plaintiff's three emails consisting entirely of protected activity under § 106.71." (Statement of Undisputed Material Facts ¶ 7.)

On August 14 at 9:42 AM—nineteen hours later—Simpson reversed her preliminary determination, stating "the Title IX Office does not have jurisdiction over your report and cannot take action or provide assistance as requested." (Exhibit B-6.) She further stated the conduct "does not meet TAP 61, nor TAP 31's definitions of prohibited conduct, even if proven to be true." (Exhibit B-6.) This reversal—with no new facts—can only be explained as retaliation for Plaintiff's exercise of his protected rights.

The Third Circuit has repeatedly held that retaliation for demanding Title IX compliance is actionable. *Atkinson v. Lafayette College*, 460 F.3d 447, 454 (3d Cir. 2006) ("Demanding compliance with Title IX is protected activity"). Moreover, the close temporal proximity between Plaintiff's protected activity and Simpson's adverse jurisdiction determination establishes causation. *Jalil v. Avdel Corp.*, 873 F.2d 701, 708 (3d Cir. 1989) (two-day temporal proximity between protected activity and adverse action sufficient to establish causation); *Lauren W. ex rel. Jean W. v. DeFlaminis*, 480 F.3d 259, 267 (3d Cir. 2007) ("Temporal proximity combined with contextual evidence of retaliatory animus is sufficient to establish causation").

The Third Circuit applies the Title VII retaliation framework to Title IX retaliation claims. *Doe v. Mercy Catholic Medical Center*, No. 16-1247 (3d Cir. 2017). Under this framework, at the prima facie stage, "the plaintiff need only show that the protected activity was the likely reason for the adverse action." *Carvalho-Grevious v. Delaware State University*, No. 15-3521 (3d Cir. 2017). Here, the evidence establishes that Simpson's jurisdiction reversal occurred only after Plaintiff engaged in protected activity, and no other intervening facts can explain the change.

**C. Failure to Provide Procedural Protections Following Dismissal**

Following the dismissal of Plaintiff's complaint, Simpson did not offer appeal rights as required by § 106.45(b)(8), and did not send simultaneous written notice to the respondent as required by § 106.45(b)(3)(iii). (Statement of Undisputed Material Facts ¶ 9; Exhibit B-6.) This failure to follow even basic procedural requirements further demonstrates the clearly unreasonable nature of Duquesne's response.

**D. The Hollow "Meeting Requirement" as a Barrier to Relief**

Throughout the 45-day period, Duquesne officials conditioned any assistance on Plaintiff's willingness to attend meetings. (Exhibits B-1, B-2, B-3, B-5.) However, Plaintiff had already provided extensive written documentation, including sworn declarations, medical records, and detailed factual accounts. (Exhibits B-0, B-5A, F.) Conditioning supportive measures on additional meetings—especially given Plaintiff's documented safety concerns and the fact that supportive measures are "available at any time after a report" without requiring a formal complaint—constitutes "clearly unreasonable" conduct. *See* Exhibit Z-c at Slide 39 ("Available at any time after a report of sexual harassment"); Exhibit Z-c at Slide 38 ("The Title IX Coordinator is responsible for coordinating the effective implementation of supportive measures").

The Third Circuit has recognized that "where the recipient's response to the harassment or lack thereof is clearly unreasonable in light of the known circumstances," liability attaches. *Davis*, 526 U.S. at 648. Duquesne's singular demand for meetings—repeatedly made while Plaintiff was experiencing homelessness, financial crisis, and documented safety fears—was the functional equivalent of putting up a procedural wall to avoid providing federally mandated relief.

**III. The Harassment Was Severe, Pervasive, and Objectively Offensive**

Under *Davis*, liability requires harassment that is "so severe, pervasive, and objectively offensive that it can be said to deprive the victims of access to the educational opportunities or benefits provided by the school." *Id.* at 650. The undisputed facts satisfy this standard.

The harassment included: (1) aggressive physical confrontation, including yelling within inches of Plaintiff's face; (2) physical pursuit through the home, with the harasser confirming she was following Plaintiff; (3) a verbal threat to enter Plaintiff's bedroom ("I'm coming up"); (4) continued harassment during Plaintiff's move-out, including following him up and down stairs while ignoring his repeated statements that he did not feel safe; and (5) an encounter on September 17, 2025, where the harasser was "waiting outside" Plaintiff's Philosophy seminar classroom. (Exhibits B-0, D, C.)

Plaintiff's doctor confirmed that Plaintiff's fear was reasonable, stating "I believe its reasonable for him to have this fear" and recommending "all measures to protect him from his abuser and to provide stable housing." (Exhibit F, Medical Letter 1, Aug. 13, 2025.) External Faculty Witness, an academic mentor of 19 years, declared that Plaintiff "cannot focus on his PhD research when he is experiencing encounters that cause this level of distress and require hours of recovery time." (Exhibit C, Supplemental Declaration.)

The severity of the harassment is further evidenced by the fact that Plaintiff fled his housing on June 30 and remained homeless for over 90 days, with his bank account dropping to negative $2.55 during this period. (Exhibits A, Statement of Undisputed Material Facts ¶ 11.) This is not a case of mere "teasing" or isolated incidents—it involves physical pursuit, threats, stalking, and psychological intimidation that caused a doctoral candidate to abandon his home and live in a state of financial and housing crisis.

**IV. Duquesne's Deliberate Indifference Denied Plaintiff Equal Educational Access**

The Third Circuit applies a straightforward pleading standard for Title IX claims: "to state a claim under Title IX, the alleged facts, if true, must support a plausible inference that a federally-funded college or university discriminated against a person on the basis of sex." *Doe v. University of the Sciences*, 961 F.3d at 209. The harm to Plaintiff's educational access is well-documented.

Plaintiff was forced to flee his housing; experienced homelessness for over 90 days; was denied supportive measures that would have allowed him to safely attend campus; was subjected to a retaliatory three-week cap on remote participation; and ultimately had his Zoom access terminated based on a manufactured "compelling reason" test. (Exhibits A, I, J, Q, V, W.)

His doctor opined that his homelessness "likely worsens his mental health conditions" and that "[w]orsening his mental health conditions is expected to negatively impact his graduate education." (Exhibit F, Medical Letter 2, Oct. 1, 2025.) Professor External Faculty Witness declared that Plaintiff's "education and research have been severely derailed by his housing instability." (Exhibit C, Declaration of External Faculty Witness.)

The *Davis* standard requires denial of "access to the educational opportunities or benefits provided by the school," 526 U.S. at 650. A doctoral candidate who cannot safely attend classes, cannot access campus, cannot focus on research, and is experiencing homelessness and financial destitution has clearly been denied educational access. As the Sixth Circuit recognized in *Foster*, "we are faced with a case where the University knew its initial response was ineffective and we must determine whether its subsequent actions were so reasonable—in light of that knowledge and the surrounding circumstances—as to warrant judgment in its favor as a matter of law. They were not." *Foster*, slip op. at *12.

**V. Duquesne's Conduct Demonstrates Gender-Based Discrimination**

The Third Circuit has rejected any requirement to superimpose "additional doctrinal tests on the [Title IX] statute." *Doe v. University of the Sciences*, 961 F.3d at 209. The facts, taken as a whole, support a plausible inference of sex-based discrimination.

Plaintiff specifically raised the gender issue in his July 23, 2025 email to Dean Wasilko, asking "whether your response would have been the same had the genders in this situation been reversed." (Exhibit B-0A.) In his July 26 OCR escalation, Plaintiff stated: "I believe this is chiefly because I am male. Had I been female and my roommate male, the response would have been immediate and protective." (Exhibit B-3A.)

Under *Price Waterhouse v. Hopkins*, 490 U.S. 228 (1989), sex-stereotyping—including assumptions about how male victims of harassment should be treated—constitutes sex discrimination. *Id.* at 251.
