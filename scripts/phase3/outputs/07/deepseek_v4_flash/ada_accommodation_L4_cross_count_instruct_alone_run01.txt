**ARGUMENT**

**III. DEFENDANT DUQUESNE UNIVERSITY VIOLATED SECTION 504 OF THE REHABILITATION ACT AND THE ADA BY FAILING TO PROVIDE PLAINTIFF DAVID MULLINS WITH A REASONABLE ACCOMMODATION FOR HIS DISABILITY**

**A. Legal Standard**

The Supreme Court's recent decision in *A.J.T. v. Osseo Area Schools*, 605 U.S. ___ (2025), definitively resolved any lingering ambiguity regarding the standard governing disability discrimination claims in educational settings. The Court held that "schoolchildren bringing ADA and Rehabilitation Act claims related to their education are not required to make a heightened showing of 'bad faith or gross misjudgment' but instead are subject to the same standards that apply in other disability discrimination contexts." *Osseo*, 605 U.S. at ___. As the Court further explained, "[n]othing in the text of Title II of the ADA or Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act suggests that such claims should be subject to a distinct, more demanding analysis." *Id.* Accordingly, Plaintiff's claim under Section 504 and the ADA is governed by the ordinary failure-to-accommodate framework.

To establish a prima facie case of failure to accommodate, Plaintiff must demonstrate: (1) he is a qualified individual with a disability; (2) the University had notice of his disability and need for accommodation; (3) he requested a reasonable accommodation; (4) the University denied that accommodation; and (5) he suffered harm as a result. *See Taylor v. Phoenixville School District*, 184 F.3d 296, 306-07 (3d Cir. 1999) (setting forth interactive process requirements). Critically, when the requested accommodation is reasonable on its face, the "employer who has received adequate notice of an employee's disability and desire for accommodations[] cannot fail to engage the employee in the interactive process of finding accommodations." *Taylor*, 184 F.3d at 312.

**B. Plaintiff Is a Qualified Individual with a Disability**

The record establishes that Plaintiff David Mullins suffers from diagnosed medical conditions that substantially limit major life activities. Plaintiff's treating physician, Treating Physician, a Doctor of Osteopathic Medicine, has diagnosed Plaintiff with "ADHD and depression." (Exhibit F, Medical Letter 2, Oct. 1, 2025). Treating Physician further attests that Plaintiff's conditions are exacerbated by the stress of his housing instability and ongoing safety concerns, and that "[w]orsening his mental health conditions is expected to negatively impact his graduate education." (Exhibit F). The physician specifically recommends "all measures to protect him from his abuser and to provide stable housing be made" to "benefit and protect his mental health and his performance in his graduate program." (Exhibit F).

Plaintiff's disability is directly linked to his need for accommodation. Treating Physician's August 13, 2025 letter confirms that Plaintiff "does not feel safe around a specific individual on campus" and that this fear is "reasonable." (Exhibit F). The physician explicitly states that sharing space with this individual "would cause him a great deal of stress, would be highly likely to worsen his medical condition, and could impair his academic functioning." (Exhibit F). This medical documentation satisfies the first element.

Plaintiff is also "qualified" within the meaning of the statute. At all relevant times, he was an enrolled doctoral candidate in good academic standing with no disciplinary record. Dr. Selcer himself testified at the TRO hearing that Plaintiff "had no performance issues." (TRO Tr. at *:*). Plaintiff's academic mentor, External Faculty Witness, attests that "[b]ased on my knowledge of David's academic work and communications, his education and research have been severely derailed by his housing instability and the conflict with his former roommate." (Exhibit C, Declaration of External Faculty Witness, Sept. 30, 2025). Thus, Plaintiff is a qualified individual with a disability who could perform the essential functions of his academic program with reasonable accommodation.

**C. Plaintiff Provided Adequate Notice of His Disability and Need for Accommodation**

The University received clear and repeated notice of Plaintiff's disability-related needs. Treating Physician's August 13, 2025 medical letter was directed "[t]o whom it may concern" and expressly states that Plaintiff "does not feel safe around a specific individual on campus" and that the physician "believe[s] it's reasonable for him to have this fear." (Exhibit F). This letter, along with Plaintiff's repeated communications to University officials, provided actual knowledge of Plaintiff's condition and his need for accommodation.

In addition, Plaintiff personally informed University officials of his safety concerns. On July 20, 2025, Plaintiff wrote to Dr. Selcer and Dr. Faculty A: "To be blunt: I am scared of this person. I fear for my safety in any context where she knows my whereabouts." (Exhibit R). Plaintiff further disclosed that his former roommate "expressed strong enthusiasm for the idea that, in her view, the Second Amendment should mean firearm regulations should be less of a hassle than they are." (Exhibit R). Dr. Selcer acknowledged receipt and assured Plaintiff that "it won't be a problem to ensure you don't share an office." (Exhibit R).

The notice requirement is satisfied where the University knows "about the employee's disability" and the employee "requested accommodations or assistance for his or her disability." *Taylor*, 184 F.3d at 312-13. Here, Plaintiff provided medical documentation, repeatedly explained his fear, and explicitly identified the safety measures needed to permit his educational participation. The University cannot credibly claim ignorance.

**D. Plaintiff Requested a Reasonable Accommodation**

Plaintiff specifically requested Zoom access for class participation as a medically necessary accommodation. As Treating Physician's medical documentation confirms, Plaintiff's treating physician recommended "all measures to protect him from his abuser." (Exhibit F). Zoom access was a straightforward, low-cost, and readily available accommodation that would permit Plaintiff to continue his coursework while avoiding the triggering environment that exacerbated his disability.

The reasonableness of Zoom access as an accommodation is demonstrated by the fact that the same accommodation is already in active use within the same department. Faculty D's syllabus for PHIL 520-420W — Religious Platonism, Spring 2025, expressly states: "Although we will meet in person, I will also keep a Zoom meeting running, so if you are not feeling well, please attend that session remotely!" (Exhibit N, Faculty D Syllabus). This demonstrates that Zoom access is technologically feasible, pedagogically acceptable, and already integrated into the department's ordinary practices.

Furthermore, no university policy or departmental policy prohibits individual student Zoom access. The Faculty Handbook 2025-2026, pp. 10-11, governs "faculty course delivery modality" and states that "courses designed to be offered in-person must be taught in-person (other than on rare, one-off occasions)." (Exhibit Y). However, this policy addresses the *professor's* teaching modality, not *student* attendance accommodations. The Handbook does not prohibit students from attending remotely where accommodations are warranted. As the record confirms, "no written policy, pedagogical study, or departmental standard" supports the denial of Plaintiff's request. (TRO Tr. at *:*).

**E. The University Denied the Accommodation and Failed to Engage in the Interactive Process**

The University's response to Plaintiff's accommodation request was not merely inadequate — it was affirmatively obstructive. The record demonstrates a clear pattern of denial, delay, and retaliation.

First, Dr. Selcer imposed a three-week countdown on Plaintiff's Zoom access on August 25, 2025, after which Plaintiff would be required to attend in person "regardless of medical documentation." (Exhibit I, Selcer Aug. 25 directive). Selcer's directive was "conditional, time-limited" and "designed to expire and coerce meeting attendance." (Statement of Undisputed Facts ¶ 6). Selcer's own testimony reveals that his objection to Zoom was based on personal preference, citing that it "divides attention" and a post-COVID departmental finding, but he could not identify any written policy or pedagogical study supporting the denial. (TRO Tr. at *:*).

Second, the University never conducted an interactive process. Despite receiving Plaintiff's medical documentation and accommodation request, Defendants "did not assess Plaintiff's specific limitations, evaluate possible accommodations, discuss alternatives with Plaintiff, or involve disability services." (SJ 7.2). This constitutes a textbook failure of the interactive process. Under *Taylor*, "an employer who has received adequate notice of an employee's disability and desire for accommodations, cannot fail to engage the employee in the interactive process of finding accommodations." *Taylor*, 184 F.3d at 312. Where, as here, an "employer acts in bad faith in the interactive process," it "will be liable if the jury can reasonably conclude that the employee would have been able to perform the job with accommodations." *Id.* at 317.

Third, the University's denial was not an isolated event but part of a pattern. Colleague A, another student in Selcer's Graduate Teaching Seminar, separately requested Zoom access, and Selcer "denied her request and told her he 'can't explain why' Zoom was not available." (Exhibit L). This demonstrates "a pattern of denial with no articulable basis." (Undisputed Facts ¶ 8).

The Third Circuit's decision in *Oross v. Kutztown University*, No. 21-5032 (E.D. Pa. July 25, 2023), is directly on point. In *Oross*, the court granted summary judgment for the plaintiff where the university "applied blanket no-remote policy with no written basis, no individualized assessment of plaintiff's request," and the "accommodation request was 'dead on arrival' — interference with the interactive process established." *Oross*, slip op. at *.* The same is true here. The University's three-week countdown, imposed without any individualized assessment and without engaging disability services, rendered Plaintiff's accommodation request "dead on arrival."

Furthermore, after the TRO hearing, Selcer continued to deny Zoom access. When Plaintiff attempted to attend Professor Faculty C's class remotely — an arrangement Faculty C himself had expressly authorized on October 6, 2025 (Exhibit W, Email 2 of 6: "Yes, Zooming in tomorrow would work fine") — Selcer contacted Faculty C and "reframed it as a 'compelling reason' test Faculty C never knew he was administering." (Exhibit Q, Zoom Removal Sequence). Selcer then reported to Plaintiff: "I've discussed your remote attendance of his graduate seminar with Faculty C and he did not identify a compelling reason to continue it. We would both welcome your return to in-person attendance." (Exhibit V). This back-channel interference with an already-approved accommodation demonstrates bad faith.

**F. The University's Actions Caused Plaintiff Harm**

The denial of accommodation directly caused Plaintiff harm. Treating Physician's October 1, 2025 letter confirms that Plaintiff's homelessness since June 30 "has been very stressful and likely worsens his mental health conditions. Worsening his mental health conditions is expected to negatively impact his graduate education." (Exhibit F). Professor External Faculty Witness's declarations corroborate that Plaintiff's "education and research have been severely derailed by his housing instability and the lack of university support." (Exhibit C). The harm is both medical and academic: Plaintiff's conditions worsened, his educational participation was impaired, and he endured compounding stress while his accommodation was systematically denied.

**G. The University Cannot Justify Its Denial**

Defendants cannot defend their denial by pointing to policy or pedagogical concerns, because the record establishes that no such policy exists. The Faculty Handbook governs faculty teaching modality, not student accommodations. Moreover, the University itself permits Zoom access for other students — as demonstrated by Faculty D's syllabus (Exhibit N) and the accommodation provided to another student, Colleague B, for surgery (Exhibit W, Email 5 of 6). The selective denial of the same accommodation to Plaintiff, despite medical documentation, raises serious questions of pretext.

Finally, Defendants cannot claim that Plaintiff failed to engage with disability services, because the University never referred Plaintiff to disability services. Selcer's August 25 directive mentioned disability services as an optional path, but the interactive process requires the *University* to initiate and participate in the accommodation process, not to place the burden entirely on the student. *Taylor*, 184 F.3d at 312-13. The University's failure to involve disability services, conduct an individualized assessment, or propose any alternative accommodation constitutes a failure of the interactive process as a matter of law.

**H. Conclusion on Count III**

Plaintiff has established each element of his failure-to-accommodate claim under Section 504 and the ADA. He is a qualified individual with a disability. He provided notice and requested a reasonable accommodation — Zoom access, which the University already provides to other students. The University denied that accommodation without any individualized assessment, without engaging the interactive process, and without any legitimate pedagogical or policy basis. The denial caused Plaintiff direct harm, worsening his medical condition and impairing his academic performance. The University is therefore liable under Section 504, 29 U.S.C. § 794, and the ADA for failure to accommodate Plaintiff's disability.
