**PLAINTIFF'S MEMORANDUM IN OPPOSITION TO DEFENDANTS' MOTION FOR SUMMARY JUDGMENT ON COUNT IV — FRAUD (PENNSYLVANIA COMMON LAW)**

**ARGUMENT**

**I. Genuine Disputes of Material Fact Exist as to Every Element of Pennsylvania Common Law Fraud, Precluding Summary Judgment.**

Under Pennsylvania law, the six elements of fraud are: (1) a representation; (2) material to the transaction; (3) made falsely, with knowledge of its falsity or reckless disregard for its truth; (4) intent to induce reliance; (5) justifiable reliance; and (6) resulting injury proximately caused by that reliance. *Gibbs v. Ernst*, 538 Pa. 193, 207, 647 A.2d 882, 889 (1994). "The concealment of a material fact can amount to a culpable misrepresentation no less than does an intentional false statement." *Moser v. DeSetta*, 589 A.2d 679, 682 (Pa. Super. 1991). Reliance is "presumed from materiality where a party would not have entered the transaction had the truth been known." *Peerless Wall & Window Coverings, Inc. v. Synchronics, Inc.*, 85 F. Supp. 2d 519, 528 (W.D. Pa. 2000).

Defendants' motion attempts to isolate narrow slices of a unified fraudulent scheme. The record—viewed in the light most favorable to Plaintiff—reveals a coordinated pattern of false representations, concealments, and procedural manipulations that directly satisfy each element of fraud. Summary judgment is improper.

**II. Defendants' Arguments Fail to Establish an Absence of Genuine Dispute.**

**A. Dean A's TAP 33 Analysis Was Not "Consistent" with Policy—It Was a False Representation Made with Reckless Disregard for the Truth.**

Defendants contend that Dean A's October 22, 2025 determination that TAP 33 did not apply to Plaintiff's conflict-of-interest disclosure was "consistent with the policy's plain language" (Dkt. 34 at 6-8). This assertion is itself a misrepresentation and is directly contradicted by the record.

On October 20, 2025, Plaintiff disclosed a conflict of interest to Dean A: Dr. Daniel Selcer, a named defendant in Plaintiff's pending federal lawsuit, was serving as Plaintiff's instructor in a required teaching seminar. (Exhibit K-1). Plaintiff explicitly requested an alternate instructor to "avoid any appearance of impropriety or further conflict." (Exhibit K-1). Dean A's response was to consult *the very same conflicted individual*—Dr. Selcer—about whether a conflict existed. (Exhibit K-2). Seven days earlier, Selcer had been cross-examined under oath in the TRO hearing. (Counterstatement ¶ 8). Dean A then announced a "joint determination" with Selcer that "it is appropriate for you to remain enrolled." (Exhibit K-2). This circular consultation is the antithesis of an independent conflict review.

When Plaintiff provided specific citations to TAP 33 within 90 minutes, Dean A issued a flat denial: "TAP 33 does not apply to this situation. First, as a student, you are not a 'Covered Party'... Second, the scenario... does not fall within the limited definition of a 'Conflict of Interest' in TAP 33." (Exhibit K-5).

A genuine dispute of material fact exists as to whether this representation was false. The Title IX training materials provided by Defendants themselves define a conflict of interest broadly as "[a] conflict between the private interests and the official responsibilities of a person in a position of trust" and emphasize that "COI must be avoided to... ensure fairness [and] maintain the trust, integrity, and credibility of the Title IX process." (Exhibit Z-d at Slide 46). The training further instructs that COI determinations should be "based on objective facts" and that "a reasonable person would believe that a COI exists" if the facts warrant it. (Exhibit Z-d at Slide 48). Having an active litigant evaluate the conflict complaint of his adversary—and then having the Dean rubber-stamp that evaluation—falls squarely within this definition. A jury could find that Dean A's statement that TAP 33 "does not apply" was not merely erroneous but was made with reckless disregard for the truth, given that she never conducted an independent review and ignored the plain language of the training materials her own institution provided.

This false representation was material: it denied Plaintiff access to a conflict-free educational environment and subjected him to instruction by an adverse party. *Gibbs*, 647 A.2d at 889 (materiality requires that the representation be "of such a character that if the truth had been known, the party would not have entered the transaction"). Plaintiff had specifically stated he would "refrain from attending to avoid any appearance of impropriety." (Exhibit K-1). The false representation directly affected his academic trajectory.

**B. Dean A's Silence on the August 25, 2025 Email Was Actionable Because She Was the "Natural and Proper Source" of Information.**

Defendants argue that no duty to speak existed because no fiduciary or confidential relationship existed between university administrators and Plaintiff. (Dkt. 34 at 9, citing *Sunquest Info. Sys.*, 40 F. Supp. 2d at 656). This misstates the law.

*Neuman v. Corn Exchange National Bank & Trust Co.*, 356 Pa. 442, 450, 51 A.2d 759, 763 (1947), holds that "[w]here a party to a transaction is the natural and proper source of information, nondisclosure of a material fact is equivalent to a fraudulent misrepresentation." The Third Circuit has confirmed that "the duty to investigate does not apply where the defendant is the natural and proper source of the information." *Duquesne Light Co. v. Westinghouse Electric Corp.*, 66 F.3d 604, 614 (3d Cir. 1995).

Dean A was Dean of the McAnulty College of Liberal Arts—the college in which Plaintiff was enrolled. She was the administrative head responsible for academic policy, including TAP 33 conflict-of-interest determinations. When Plaintiff explicitly requested her guidance on an alternative instructor (Exhibit K-1), she was the "natural and proper source" of that information. Her initial response (Exhibit K-2) misrepresented the process by consulting the conflicted party. Her subsequent silence—or her "no change" denial (Exhibit K-5)—concealed the material fact that her review was tainted and that a genuine conflict existed. Under *Moser*, "[t]he concealment of a material fact can amount to a culpable misrepresentation no less than does an intentional false statement." 589 A.2d at 682.

The cases distinguishing sophisticated parties dealing at arm's length (*Duquesne Light*, 66 F.3d at 614) are inapposite. This is not a commercial transaction between equals. It is a student-administrator relationship where the administrator is the sole authoritative source of policy interpretation. A jury could readily find that Dean A's silence—following her flawed consultation with the conflicted party and her refusal to correct course—constituted a fraudulent nondisclosure.

**C. Plaintiff's "Immediate Recognition" of the Error Does Not Negate Justifiable Reliance—It Confirms the Fraud and the Damaging Consequences.**

Defendants argue that because Plaintiff "immediately recognized" that Dean A's TAP 33 reading was wrong and stated he was "no longer relying on Defendants' representations" as of July 24, 2025, he cannot establish justifiable reliance. (Dkt. 34 at 8-9). This argument fails for three reasons.

*First*, the allegedly "immediate" recognition occurred only after Plaintiff received the false representation—at which point the damage was already done. Dean A's false denial (Exhibit K-5) was an official policy determination that remained in effect. Plaintiff was forced to participate in the teaching seminar under an adverse instructor, knowing his objection had been overruled based on a flawed and self-dealing process. The fact that Plaintiff could *identify* the error does not mean he was not injured by its imposition. *Peerless Wall & Window Coverings*, 85 F. Supp. 2d at 528 (reliance "presumed from materiality where a party would not have entered the transaction had the truth been known").

*Second*, Defendants conflate two separate fraudulent schemes. The July 24, 2025 communications relate to the *meeting requirement* fraud. The TAP 33 misrepresentation occurred on October 22, 2025—three months later. Plaintiff's statement in the SAC referenced the earlier scheme; it cannot be retroactively applied to immunize Dean A's later conduct.

*Third*, even as to the meeting requirement fraud, Plaintiff's recognition of the falsehood does not establish that he could have avoided its consequences. The meeting requirement was used to justify the denial of supportive measures (housing, academic accommodations) and to condition all university assistance on a meeting that had no policy basis. (Exhibits B-2, B-3, B-5). Plaintiff's recognition of the fraud did not undo the harm—the denial of supportive measures, the housing crisis, the medical exacerbation documented by Treating Physician (Exhibit F), and the academic jeopardy. *Gibbs*, 647 A.2d at 889 (damages must be "proximately caused by the reliance").

**D. Plaintiff's Claims Are Not Internally Inconsistent—They Describe a Coordinated Scheme.**

Defendants argue that Plaintiff "alleges that both responding to his emails (Selcer) and not responding (Dean A) constituted fraud." (Dkt. 34 at 5). This mischaracterizes the evidence.

Plaintiff alleges that *specific false representations* by multiple defendants—both affirmative misstatements and material omissions—constituted fraud. Selcer's August 25, 2025 "meeting summary" misrepresented what was discussed and imposed an arbitrary three-week Zoom countdown with no medical or policy basis. (Exhibit I). Dean A's TAP 33 analysis falsely claimed the policy did not apply and concealed her consultation with the conflicted party. (Exhibits K-2, K-5). Dean A also remained silent when Plaintiff explicitly sought an alternative accommodation. (Exhibits K-1, K-5). These are not inconsistent theories. They are different modalities of the same fraudulent scheme: misrepresenting policy, concealing conflicts, and imposing conditions on academic access that had no legitimate basis.

Defendants' argument ignores that fraud can be committed by both active misrepresentation *and* concealment. *Moser*, 589 A.2d at 682. A coordinated scheme involving multiple defendants using different tactics is not "inconsistent"—it is typical of fraud.

**E. The Consultation Between Dean A and Selcer Produced No Legitimate Assurance—It Was Evidence of a Tainted Process, Not Benign Intent.**

Defendants claim that the consultation between Dean A and Selcer "produced assurances of fair treatment and offers of academic support—evidence of intent to help, not deceive." (Dkt. 34 at 10). This is a factual assertion that must be weighed by a jury, not a legal conclusion that can be resolved on summary judgment.

The record demonstrates the opposite of "fair treatment." Dean A consulted the very person against whom Plaintiff had raised a conflict—a person who was simultaneously a cross-examined witness in ongoing litigation. (Counterstatement ¶ 8). The "offers of academic support" were conditional: Selcer's email demanded that Plaintiff "fully engage with Title IX, Student Conduct, and Public Safety offices within the next week" (Exhibit I)—the same offices that had just determined they lacked jurisdiction (Exhibit B-6). This is a Catch-22, not a help. Selcer's follow-up "final directive" (Exhibit J) imposed a September 12 deadline for compliance.

A reasonable jury could find that the consultation was not an effort to "help" but a procedural maneuver designed to insulate the university from liability by creating a paper trail of apparent procedural regularity while maintaining the substantive denial of Plaintiff's rights. *Gibbs* requires only that the false statement be made "with the intent of misleading another into relying on it." 647 A.2d at 889. The intent to deceive—or reckless disregard for the truth—can be inferred from the circumstances.

**F. Judicial Deference to Academic Institutions Does Not Immunize Fraud.**

Defendants invoke *Jain v. Carnegie Mellon Univ.*, arguing courts should defer to academic institutions' professional judgment regarding academic decisions. (Dkt. 34 at 8 n.2). This principle has no application here.

Plaintiff's fraud claim does not challenge the *substance* of an academic decision, such as a grade or curriculum. It challenges *false representations of policy and procedure*—Defendants telling Plaintiff that meetings were required for supportive measures (Exhibits B-2, B-3, B-5), that TAP 33 did not apply (Exhibit K-5), and that Title IX jurisdiction was lacking (Exhibit B-6). These are not discretionary academic judgments. They are factual representations about what institutional and federal policies require. Fraud is never entitled to academic deference.

**III. The Record Establishes Genuine Disputes on Every Element.**

**A. False Representations of Material Fact.**

The record is replete with false representations:
- Simpson, Wasilko, Sawa, and Selcer made 36 nearly identical statements fabricating a meeting requirement as a condition of supportive measures (Counterstatement ¶ 1; Exhibits B-0 through B-7).

- Selcer's August 25, 2025 "meeting summary" falsified what was discussed and imposed a three-week Zoom countdown (Exhibit I).
- Dean A's TAP 33 analysis falsely denied the policy's applicability (Exhibit K-5).

All of these representations were material—they directly affected Plaintiff's access to supportive measures, academic accommodations, and a conflict-free educational environment. *Gibbs*, 647 A.2d at 889.
