**ARGUMENT**

Plaintiff David Mullins respectfully submits that Defendants' Motion for Summary Judgment on Count V—Civil Conspiracy must be denied because genuine disputes of material fact exist on every element of the claim. Under Pennsylvania law, a civil conspiracy requires: (1) a combination or agreement between two or more persons; (2) with intent to do an unlawful act or to do a lawful act by unlawful means; (3) malice—an intent to injure; (4) an overt act in furtherance; and (5) resulting harm. *Thompson Coal Co. v. Pike Coal Co.*, 488 Pa. 198, 412 A.2d 466, 472-73 (1979). Once a conspiracy is shown, each defendant is jointly and severally liable for all tortious acts committed by any co-conspirator in furtherance of the conspiracy. *Id.* at 472-73.

**I. Defendants' Argument That Dean A's Consultation with Selcer Was Routine Academic Administration Is Contradicted by the Record and Creates a Genuine Dispute of Material Fact**

Defendants characterize Dean A's consultation with Selcer as "routine academic administration," arguing she could not respond to Plaintiff's transfer request without checking course availability and academic progress with the department chair. (Dkt. 34 at 10.) This characterization is flatly contradicted by the documentary evidence.

On October 20, 2025, Plaintiff disclosed to Dean A a direct conflict of interest: his required Ph.D. teaching seminar was taught by Dr. Daniel Selcer, a named defendant in Plaintiff's pending federal civil lawsuit against Duquesne University. (Exhibit K-1.) Plaintiff explicitly requested an alternate instructor or equivalent course. *Id.*

Dean A's response, dated October 22, 2025, states: "I did need to consult Dr. Selcer as instructor to secure some additional details. **Both Dr. Selcer and I strongly concur** that it is appropriate for you to remain enrolled in your required teaching seminar." (Exhibit K-2 (emphasis added).) This is a **written admission of joint determination** with the conflicted party—"both of us, together"—rather than an independent review. This matter is a genuine dispute of material fact sufficient to defeat summary judgment. *Thompson Coal*, 412 A.2d at 472-73.

The conflict here is not merely academic. Dean A consulted Selcer about a conflict-of-interest disclosure **concerning Selcer himself**—seven days after Plaintiff cross-examined Selcer under oath at the TRO hearing on October 15, 2025. (Exhibit K-2; TRO Tr.) The proximity between Plaintiff's protected activity and Dean A's consultation with the very party about whom Plaintiff raised the conflict creates a genuine dispute as to whether this consultation was routine administration or an act in furtherance of a conspiracy.

Moreover, TAP 33 § IV.4 requires **independent review** of conflict-of-interest disclosures. Dean A's "consult[ation]" with the conflicted party does not constitute independent review under any reading of the policy. (TAP 33 § IV.4; Exhibit K-2.) When Dean A later denied TAP 33's applicability (Exhibit K-5), she did so only after having already made a joint determination with Selcer—an impermissible process that Plaintiff is entitled to challenge before a jury.

**II. Defendants' Argument That the Consultation Evidenced Intent to Help, Not Malice, Creates a Genuine Dispute of Material Fact**

Defendants contend that Dean A's consultation "resulted in assurances of fair treatment and offers of academic support," evidencing intent to help rather than malice. (Dkt. 34 at 10.) This argument misstates both the record and the law.

**A. The Timing and Context of Dean A's "Assurances" Raise an Inference of Malice**

Proof of malice—"an intent to injure"—is essential to a civil conspiracy claim. *Thompson Coal*, 412 A.2d at 472-73. The record contains substantial evidence from which a reasonable jury could infer malice.

First, Dean A's consultation with Selcer occurred **seven days** after Plaintiff cross-examined Selcer under oath at the TRO hearing on October 15, 2025. (Exhibit K-2; TRO Tr.) This temporal proximity between Plaintiff's protected activity and the coordinated response is "unusually suggestive" and alone can establish a causal connection for retaliation purposes. *Jalil v. Avdel Corp.*, 873 F.2d 701, 708 (3d Cir. 1989). For a conspiracy claim, this timing supports an inference that the "joint determination" was motivated by animus toward Plaintiff for his litigation activity, not by legitimate academic concerns.

Second, the "assurances" Dean A offered were themselves conditional and coercive. She directed Plaintiff to "return to class today" and imposed four conditions including meeting with Selcer to "catch up on missed content." (Exhibit K-2.) She warned that failure to resume attendance would result in a failing grade. *Id.* These are not neutral offers of support—they are directives designed to force Plaintiff to continue under the instruction of a named defendant in his lawsuit.

Third, the broader pattern of conduct across multiple defendants supports an inference of malice. Four defendants systematically removed OCR and senior administrative oversight from correspondence at critical junctures, preventing reliance victims from receiving corrective information. (Exhibits B-3 through B-7; Selcer Sept 3 email.) Selcer removed Dean A herself from the September 3 "final directive." (Exhibit J.) This systematic pattern of isolating Plaintiff from oversight while coordinating among themselves is circumstantial evidence of an agreement with intent to injure. *Canada v. Samuel Grossi & Sons, Inc.*, No. 20-2747 (3d Cir. 2022) ("A convincing mosaic of circumstantial evidence is sufficient to establish causation for retaliation claims").

Fourth, Dean A's silence in the face of multiple escalations from Plaintiff citing federal law, identifying specific violations, and requesting intervention further supports an inference of malice. Plaintiff sent multiple escalations directly to Dean A; Dean A did not intervene. (Exhibits K-2, K-5; SAC ¶¶ 326, 338, 417.) Dean A's silence, combined with her active coordination with Selcer, furthered the conspiracy's objective of maintaining defendants' control without independent oversight. *Id.*

**B. The "Assurances" Were Themselves Part of the Conspiracy's Unlawful Means**

Even if one were to accept Defendants' characterization of the "assurances" as helpful, Pennsylvania law permits a conspiracy claim where defendants agree to do an otherwise lawful act by **unlawful means**. *Thompson Coal*, 412 A.2d at 472-73. Here, the means were unlawful. Dean A's consultation with the conflicted party violated TAP 33's requirement of independent review. (TAP 33 § IV.4.) The joint determination was reached through a process that precluded any meaningful independent assessment—a process that itself constituted a violation of university policy and federal obligations under Title IX.

As the Third Circuit has recognized, demanding compliance with Title IX is protected activity, and retaliation against those who do so is unlawful. *Atkinson v. Lafayette College*, 460 F.3d 447, 454 (3d Cir. 2006). Dean A's consultation with Selcer—the very person Plaintiff had cross-examined seven days earlier—was not neutral academic administration; it was a coordinated response designed to maintain Selcer's control over Plaintiff despite the obvious conflict.

**III. Defendants' Argument That No Underlying Tort Exists Because TAP 33 Analysis Was Correct Is Incorrect as a Matter of Law and Fact**

Defendants argue that no underlying tort supports the conspiracy because "Dean A's TAP 33 analysis was correct on the policy's plain terms." (Dkt. 34 at 10.) This argument fails for multiple reasons.

**A. The Underlying Torts Include Fraud, Retaliation, and Obstruction of Federal Rights**

The underlying torts committed by co-conspirators include: 36 false statements fabricating a meeting requirement (Conspiracy 1), systematic OCR removal across four actors (Conspiracy 2), and Selcer's fabrication of "performance issues" contradicted by the source he cited on the same day (Exhibits X-7, X-8). Dean A need not have personally committed these acts to be liable for them under *Thompson Coal*. 412 A.2d at 472-73.

Specifically, the record demonstrates:

- **Fraudulent Misrepresentations:** Defendants made multiple false statements, including fabricating a "meeting requirement" as a condition of receiving supportive measures. *Gibbs v. Ernst*, 538 Pa. 193, 647 A.2d 882, 887 (1994) (fraud requires false representation made with knowledge of falsity and intent to induce reliance). "The concealment of a material fact can amount to a culpable misrepresentation no less than does an intentional false statement." *Moser v. DeSetta*, 589 A.2d 679, 682-83 (Pa. Super. 1991).

- **Retaliation:** Demanding compliance with Title IX is protected activity. *Atkinson*, 460 F.3d at 454. The coordinated removal of Plaintiff's Zoom access, the imposition of coercive conditions, and Selcer's later fabrication of "performance issues" are adverse actions that would deter a reasonable person. *Lauren W. ex rel. Jean W. v. DeFlaminis*, 480 F.3d 259, 267 (3d Cir. 2007) (temporal proximity combined with contextual evidence of retaliatory animus sufficient to establish causation).

**B. Even If TAP 33 Analysis Were Correct, Other Unlawful Means Exist**

Defendants' focus on TAP 33 is a red herring. The conspiracy claim is not limited to the TAP 33 issue. The conspiracy encompasses multiple overt acts, including:

- **Systematic OCR Removal:** Four defendants removed OCR from correspondence at critical junctures, preventing corrective oversight. (Exhibits B-3 through B-7.) This is not a policy interpretation—it is an intentional act to deny Plaintiff access to federal enforcement mechanisms.

- **Sawa's Retaliation Threat:** On August 15, 2025, Sawa threatened Plaintiff with disciplinary charges for sending "mass emails" to university officials about his safety concerns. (Exhibit B-7.) This threat was issued in retaliation for Plaintiff's pursuit of Title IX protections and his refusal to be siloed into meetings without oversight. This is an overt act in furtherance of the conspiracy.

- **Selcer's Fabricated "Performance Issues":** On February 13, 2026, Selcer sent Plaintiff a reassignment email stating there had been "performance issues raised by two of the three faculty members." (Exhibit X-8.) This statement is contradicted by the source Selcer himself cited on the same day: Faculty B's February 13 email requested reassignment but stated "my office door is always open" and contained **no performance complaint whatsoever**. (Exhibit X-7.) Moreover, in the February 10 meeting between Selcer and Plaintiff, no "performance issues" language appeared; Selcer framed the situation as one he needed to understand and maintained a collegial tone. (Exhibit X-6.) The introduction of false "performance issues" three days later, combined with a threat to suspend Plaintiff's stipend through 2029 (Exhibit X-8), is textbook fraud.

**C. There Is a Genuine Dispute About the Correctness of Dean A's TAP 33 Analysis**

Even on the TAP 33 issue, there is a genuine dispute. TAP 33 § IV.4 requires independent review of conflict-of-interest disclosures. (TAP 33 § IV.4.) Dean A's admission that she "consult[ed]" Selcer—the conflicted party—before making her determination (Exhibit K-2) creates a genuine dispute about whether her analysis was correct. A reasonable jury could find that consulting the conflicted party does not constitute "independent review" under any reading of the policy. Summary judgment is therefore inappropriate.

**IV. Defendants' Argument That Coordinated Institutional Responses Do Not Constitute a Conspiracy Ignores the Specific Facts of This Case**

Defendants argue that Plaintiff's characterization of coordinated institutional responses as "conspiracy" reflects his "refusal to engage with well-established university processes, not evidence of unlawful agreement." (Dkt. 25 at 6.) This argument improperly seeks to have this Court resolve factual disputes on summary judgment.

**A. The Evidence Shows More Than Parallel Action—It Shows Deliberate Coordination**

The record demonstrates deliberate coordination, not merely parallel institutional action. On February 13, 2026, Selcer's reassignment email referenced a "recommendation to the Dean of Liberal Arts"—Dean A's exact title. (Exhibit X-8.) The "recommendation to the Dean" language did not appear in the February 10, 2026 meeting between Selcer and Plaintiff, proving intervening communication between Selcer and Dean A. (Exhibit X-6; Exhibit X-8.) A reasonable jury could infer from this sudden appearance of new language—mentioning Dean A's title after Selcer had made no mention of her in the February 10 meeting—that Selcer and Dean A had coordinated between February 10 and February 13.

Similarly, the Zoom removal sequence demonstrates coordination. On October 6, 2025, Professor Faculty C directly authorized Plaintiff's remote attendance. (Exhibit W.) On October 15, Selcer testified under oath at the TRO hearing that Plaintiff would continue to have Zoom access. (TRO Tr.; Exhibit Q.) Yet on October 17, Selcer sent an email stating he had "discussed your remote attendance of his graduate seminar with Faculty C and he did not identify a compelling reason to continue it." (Exhibit V.) When Plaintiff contacted Faculty C directly, Faculty C expressed confusion: "please ask Dan about any need for continued online attendance." (Exhibit W.) This sequence—Selcer going to a professor who had already authorized Zoom, reframing the request as a "compelling reason" test Faculty C never knew he was administering, and reporting the result as Faculty C's independent judgment—is evidence of coordination to achieve an outcome adverse to Plaintiff.

**B. The Law Does Not Require Explicit Agreement—Circumstantial Evidence Suffices**

Civil conspiracy may be proven by circumstantial evidence; an explicit agreement is not required. *Thompson Coal*, 412 A.2d at 472. The conduct of multiple defendants working in coordination to achieve a common objective is sufficient. Here, the common objective was maintaining Defendants' control without independent oversight—as demonstrated by the systematic removal of OCR and senior leadership from correspondence (Exhibits B-3 through B-7), the refusal to provide supportive measures unless Plaintiff submitted to siloed meetings (Exhibits B-2, B-3, B-7), and the coordinated response to Plaintiff's conflict-of-interest disclosure (Exhibits K-2, K-5).
