**ARGUMENT**

**COUNT III — FAILURE TO ACCOMMODATE UNDER SECTION 504 AND THE ADA**

**A. Legal Standard**

The Supreme Court has squarely rejected any heightened standard for disability discrimination claims in educational settings. In *A.J.T. v. Osseo Area Schools*, 605 U.S. ___ (2025), the Court held that "Schoolchildren bringing ADA and Rehabilitation Act claims related to their education are not required to make a heightened showing of 'bad faith or gross misjudgment' but instead are subject to the same standards that apply in other disability discrimination contexts." The Court emphasized that "[n]othing in the text of Title II of the ADA or Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act suggests that such claims should be subject to a distinct, more demanding analysis." *Id.* Accordingly, the ordinary failure-to-accommodate framework governs this claim.

To establish a prima facie case for failure to accommodate, Plaintiff must demonstrate: (1) he is a qualified individual with a disability; (2) Defendant had notice of his disability and need for accommodation; (3) he requested a reasonable accommodation; (4) Defendant denied that accommodation; and (5) Plaintiff suffered harm as a result. *Taylor v. Phoenixville School District*, 184 F.3d 296, 306-07 (3d Cir. 1999). Where the employer (or university) fails to engage in the interactive process in good faith, liability attaches if the employee could have been reasonably accommodated. *Id.* at 312.

**B. Plaintiff Is a Qualified Individual with a Disability**

Plaintiff's treating physician, Treating Physician, has diagnosed Plaintiff with ADHD and depression. (Exhibit F, Medical Letter 2, Oct. 1, 2025.) Treating Physician further states that Plaintiff "reports homelessness since June 30th which has been very stressful and likely worsens his mental health conditions." (*Id.*) The medical documentation confirms that "all measures to protect him from his abuser and to provide stable housing" are necessary to "benefit and protect his mental health and his performance in his graduate program." (*Id.*) These diagnoses substantially limit major life activities including concentrating, sleeping, and interacting with others, thereby constituting a disability under the ADA and Section 504.

Plaintiff was at all relevant times an enrolled graduate student in good academic standing with no disciplinary record. (Statement of Undisputed Material Facts ¶ 2; TRO Tr. at *:* (Selcer testifying Plaintiff had no performance issues).) He is therefore a "qualified individual" under the statutes.

**C. Defendant Had Notice of Plaintiff's Disability and Need for Accommodation**

Notice is established through multiple channels. Treating Physician's August 13, 2025 medical letter states: "I am a physician treating David Mullins. He's informed me he does not feel safe around a specific individual on campus, and from what he's described to me, I believe its reasonable for him to have this fear. I have advised him to involve law enforcement for his protection in response to this reasonable fear. To share a living space with this individual would cause him a great deal of stress, would be highly likely to worsen his medical condition, and could impair his academic functioning. I recommend every effort be made to avoid this." (Exhibit F, Medical Letter 1.)

This documentation was provided to Defendant, and Plaintiff repeatedly communicated his need for safety-related accommodations—including remote participation—to multiple university officials, including Dr. Selcer, Dean Wasilko, and Title IX Coordinator Simpson. (See, e.g., Exhibit I (Aug. 25, 2025 meeting exchange); Exhibit R (July 20, 2025 email to Selcer stating "I am scared of this person"); Exhibit B-0 (June 30, 2025 initial request for emergency housing).) Selcer acknowledged in his August 25 directive that Plaintiff had "explained that you do not feel safe on campus because you fear Respondent." (Exhibit I, Selcer Aug. 25 directive.) This actual knowledge satisfies the notice requirement. *Taylor*, 184 F.3d at 313 (employer "who has received adequate notice of an employee's disability and desire for accommodations" cannot fail to engage in the interactive process).

**D. Plaintiff Requested a Reasonable Accommodation**

Plaintiff specifically requested Zoom access for class participation as a medically necessary accommodation. (Statement of Undisputed Material Facts ¶ 3.) This request was made directly to Dr. Selcer, who had authority over course modality. (Exhibit I (Plaintiff's Aug. 25 email stating "remote access is not a choice and does not fulfill the University's obligations" and demanding retraction of the retaliatory three-week cap).) Plaintiff also requested baseline safety measures—no-contact order, campus safety escort, and registrar separation—without which he could not safely attend in person. (*Id.*)

The accommodation was undeniably reasonable. Zoom was already in active use in the same department by Faculty D for comparable graduate coursework. (Exhibit N, Faculty D Syllabus (stating "I will also keep a Zoom meeting running, so if you are not feeling well, please attend that session remotely!").) No university policy or departmental policy prohibits individual student Zoom access. The Faculty Handbook 2025-2026, pp. 10-11, "governs faculty course delivery modality, not individual student attendance accommodations." (Statement of Undisputed Material Facts ¶ 4; Exhibit Y, Faculty Handbook.) Indeed, Dr. Faculty C independently authorized Plaintiff's Zoom attendance in his Marion & Phenomenology seminar without any objection from the administration. (Exhibit W, Faculty C email Oct. 6, 2025 ("Yes, Zooming in tomorrow would work fine.").)

**E. Defendant Denied the Accommodation and Failed to Engage in the Interactive Process**

Defendant's response to Plaintiff's requests for accommodation was, at every turn, a denial veiled in procedural conditions that were never satisfied—conditions designed to fail.

*First*, no interactive process was conducted. Defendant "did not assess Plaintiff's specific limitations, evaluate possible accommodations, discuss alternatives with Plaintiff, or involve disability services." (Statement of Undisputed Material Facts ¶ 5.) The record is devoid of any individualized assessment of Plaintiff's documented disability. Instead, Defendant imposed a blanket condition: Plaintiff must meet with Title IX, Student Conduct, and Public Safety—offices that had already disclaimed jurisdiction or threatened retaliation. (Exhibit B-6 (Simpson stating Title IX "does not have jurisdiction over your report and cannot take action or provide assistance as requested"); Exhibit B-7 (Sawa threatening conduct charges for "mass emails").) This is the antithesis of good-faith engagement. *Taylor*, 184 F.3d at 312 ("an employer who has received adequate notice of an employee's disability and desire for accommodations, cannot fail to engage the employee in the interactive process").

*Second*, the only accommodation offered was conditional, time-limited, and coercive. On August 25, 2025, Selcer imposed a "three-week countdown" on Plaintiff's Zoom access, after which Plaintiff would be required to attend in person regardless of medical documentation. (Statement of Undisputed Material Facts ¶ 6; Exhibit I, Selcer Aug. 25 directive.) This was not a good-faith accommodation; it was "a conditional, time-limited measure designed to expire and coerce meeting attendance." (*Id.*) Selcer testified under oath that his objection to Zoom was based on personal preference, citing "divides attention" and a post-COVID departmental finding, but could not identify any written policy, pedagogical study, or departmental standard supporting denial. (Statement of Undisputed Material Facts ¶ 7; TRO Tr. at *:*.) Such an arbitrary, policy-less denial mirrors the "blanket no-remote policy" that the Eastern District of Pennsylvania found violated the ADA in *Oross v. Kutztown University*, No. 21-5032 (E.D. Pa. July 23, 2023), where the court held that the "accommodation request was 'dead on arrival'—interference with the interactive process established."

*Third*, the pattern of denial extends beyond Plaintiff. Colleague A, a student in Selcer's Graduate Teaching Seminar (Fall 2025), separately requested Zoom access. Selcer denied her request and told her he "can't explain why" Zoom was not available—demonstrating a pattern of denial with no articulable basis. (Statement of Undisputed Material Facts ¶ 8; Exhibit L.) This arbitrary denial of the same accommodation to another student confirms that Defendant's refusal is not based on pedagogical necessity but on an ad hoc, unstated policy of exclusion.

*Fourth*, after Selcer's three-week window expired, Defendant escalated the denial. Selcer surreptitiously contacted Dr. Faculty C—who had independently authorized Zoom—and obtained a reversal by claiming Faculty C "did not identify a compelling reason to continue it." (Exhibit V, Selcer Oct. 17, 2025 email; Exhibit Q, Zoom removal sequence.) Yet Faculty C later expressed confusion, stating he had authorized Zoom and was unaware of any "compelling reason" test. (Exhibit W, Faculty C Oct. 17, 2025 email ("Colleague B had surgery this past week, so I gave him the opportunity to Zoom for that one day, which you joined. So, please ask Dan about any need for continued online attendance.").) This coordinated revocation, after a professor had already granted the accommodation, demonstrates bad faith. *Taylor*, 184 F.3d at 312 ("an employer who acts in bad faith in the interactive process will be liable if the jury can reasonably conclude that the employee would have been able to perform the job with accommodations").

**F. Plaintiff Suffered Resulting Harm**

Defendant's denial directly caused harm. Plaintiff was forced into homelessness for over 90 days. (Exhibit A, Housing Crisis Timeline; Exhibit F, Treating Physician Oct. 1, 2025 letter ("homelessness since June 30th which has been very stressful and likely worsens his mental health conditions").) His mental health deteriorated: he "ran out of lexapro earlier than planned" and "was taking more than prescribed" due to "proximity to his abuser." (Exhibit F.) His academic functioning was impaired—Dr. External Faculty Witness, his academic mentor, declared that "his education and research have been severely derailed by his housing instability" and that "supportive housing and protective measures are necessary for him to resume his academic work." (Exhibit C, External Faculty Witness Declaration Sept. 30, 2025.) Plaintiff was deprived of in-person access to colleagues, faculty, departmental offices, and the library—all integral to his graduate education. (Exhibit I-1, Plaintiff's Sept. 2, 2025 email.) Finally, Defendant retaliated by issuing a written threat that "performance issues raised by two of the three faculty members" could result in "suspension of your stipend" through 2029. (Exhibit X-8, Selcer Feb. 13, 2026 reassignment letter.) This threat came after Faculty B expressly stated her "door is always open" and that reassignment "will not affect your funding" (Exhibit X-7), and after Selcer himself confirmed in a meeting that Faculty B "doesn't make decisions about your stipend" (Exhibit X-6). The creation of fabricated "performance issues" is direct evidence of retaliation for Plaintiff's pursuit of his rights.

**G. Defendant's Failure to Accommodate Was Clearly Unreasonable**

The Third Circuit's framework in *Taylor* makes clear that an institution that receives notice of a disability and a request for accommodation cannot simply list conditions and then terminate access when those conditions are unmet. *Taylor*, 184 F.3d at 313. Here, Defendant refused to engage in any individualized assessment, imposed a time limit on a concededly feasible accommodation, denied the same accommodation to another student without explanation, and then surreptitiously revoked the accommodation after a third-party professor had granted it. This is "clearly unreasonable in light of the known circumstances." *Davis v. Monroe County Board of Education*, 526 U.S. 629, 648 (1999); *Oross*, slip op. at *.* (applying similar standard to ADA claim).

**H. Conspiracy (Supporting Opposition to Summary Judgment on Count V)**

To the extent Defendant moves for summary judgment on the conspiracy claim, Plaintiff has demonstrated genuine disputes of material fact on each element: (1) an agreement among Selcer, Faculty C, and/or Faculty B to deny accommodation (Exhibit Q, Zoom removal sequence showing Selcer reframing Faculty C's authorization as a "compelling reason" test; Exhibit X-6, Selcer fabricating "performance issues" after Faculty B requested reassignment without performance complaints); (2) intent to do an unlawful act—denial of federally mandated accommodations and retaliation; (3) overt acts in furtherance—the three-week countdown, the surreptitious Zoom revocation, and the fabricated performance issues leading to stipend threats; (4) malice—the pattern of arbitrary denial, the threat to impose conduct charges (Exhibit B-7), and the stipend threat for future courses through 2029 (Exhibit X-8); and (5) resulting harm—homelessness, educational derailment, and retaliatory stipend jeopardy. *Thompson Coal Co. v. Pike Coal Co.*, 488 Pa. 198, 412 A.2d 466 (1979) (elements of civil conspiracy); *Canada v. Samuel Grossi & Sons, Inc.*, No. 20-2747 (3d Cir. 2022) ("convincing mosaic" of circumstantial evidence sufficient to oppose summary judgment).

**CONCLUSION**

For the foregoing reasons, Plaintiff has established each element of his failure-to-accommodate claim under Section 504 and the ADA. Defendant denied a reasonable accommodation without individualized assessment, without engaging in the interactive process, and on the basis of personal preference and ad hoc policy. The accommodation—Zoom attendance—was demonstrably feasible and already in use. The denial caused severe harm to Plaintiff's mental health, housing stability, and academic progress. Summary judgment should be denied, and this matter should proceed to trial.
