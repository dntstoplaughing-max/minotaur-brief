**ARGUMENT**

**I. The University Had Actual Knowledge of Severe, Pervasive, and Objectively Offensive Sexual Harassment Through Officials with Authority to Act**

Title IX requires that a funding recipient have "actual knowledge" of sexual harassment, meaning notice to "a recipient's Title IX Coordinator" or "any official of the recipient who has authority to institute corrective measures on behalf of the recipient." *See* Exhibit Z-e at Slide 30 (PCHE Summer 2020 Title IX Training, defining actual knowledge). The Third Circuit has clarified that no heightened pleading standard applies—this Court need only determine whether "the alleged facts, if true, must support a plausible inference that a federally-funded college or university discriminated against a person on the basis of sex." *Doe v. University of the Sciences*, 961 F.3d 203, 209 (3d Cir. 2020).

Here, the record demonstrates actual knowledge through multiple channels. On June 30, 2025, Plaintiff David Mullins reported to Dean Adam Wasilko—an official with "authority to institute corrective measures"—that his female roommate physically pursued him, confronted him within inches of his face, confirmed she was following him through the apartment, and stated "I'm coming up" as she advanced toward his bedroom, forcing him to lock himself in his room. Exhibit B-0. Dean Wasilko is Associate Vice President for Student Life and Dean of Students, placing him squarely within the category of officials with authority to act. *See* Exhibit Z-e, Slide 32 ("Provost, VP for Student Affairs, Dean of Students, HR Director, etc." have actual knowledge).

Plaintiff's report was not an isolated complaint. Over the following weeks, Plaintiff repeatedly escalated his concerns to Title IX Coordinator Alicia Simpson (Exhibit B-1), Dr. Anne Sawa, Executive Director of Student Conduct (Exhibit T), and Dr. Daniel Selcer, Chair of the Philosophy Department (Exhibit R). On July 24, 2025, Simpson herself acknowledged receipt of Plaintiff's allegations, stating: "My office is notified when there are reported incidents of alleged sexual harassment or sexual misconduct involving students." Exhibit B-1. On August 13, 2025, Simpson further confirmed that Plaintiff's "concerns and allegations warrant further information gathering and review." Exhibit B-5.

The underlying harassment was severe, pervasive, and objectively offensive under *Davis v. Monroe County Board of Education*, 526 U.S. 629, 650 (1999). The harasser—a fellow graduate student—physically pursued Plaintiff through their shared residence, yelled obscenities at him, followed him up the stairs after he attempted to retreat, and verbally confirmed she was stalking him ("I'm coming up"). Exhibit D (Signal Transcript & Timeline describing June 30 confrontation). Plaintiff expressed fear for his physical safety, noting that the harasser had expressed enthusiasm for minimal firearm regulations (Exhibit R), and a treating physician confirmed that Plaintiff "does not feel safe around a specific individual on campus," that his fear was "reasonable," and that sharing living space "would cause him a great deal of stress, would be highly likely to worsen his medical condition, and could impair his academic functioning." Exhibit F (Treating Physician Medical Letter, August 13, 2025). The harassment ultimately forced Plaintiff to flee his home on June 30, 2025, and left him without stable housing for over 90 days. Exhibit A (Housing Crisis Timeline).

Moreover, Plaintiff specifically raised the issue of gender discrimination. On July 23, 2025, Plaintiff asked Dean Wasilko: "I can't help but wonder whether your response would have been the same had the genders in this situation been reversed." Exhibit B-0A. This gender-based framing, combined with the University's materially different treatment of Plaintiff's complaint, supports "a plausible inference" that the University discriminated on the basis of sex. *University of the Sciences*, 961 F.3d at 209. As *Price Waterhouse v. Hopkins*, 490 U.S. 228, 250 (1989), established, when a plaintiff proves gender played a "motivating part" in a covered entity's decision, the burden shifts to the defendant to show it would have made the same decision absent the gender consideration. Here, the University's response—immediate dismissal of Plaintiff's complaint as not a "conduct violation" (Exhibit B-0), combined with Simpson's 24-hour jurisdictional flip (Exhibits B-5, B-6)—occurs in a context where Plaintiff explicitly flagged the gendered nature of the response.

**II. The University's Response Was Clearly Unreasonable—Not Merely Delayed, But Entirely Absent**

A funding recipient is deliberately indifferent when its "response to the harassment or lack thereof is clearly unreasonable in light of the known circumstances." *Davis*, 526 U.S. at 648. This standard does not require courts to conclude that "minimal, ineffective, or belated efforts to respond to sexual harassment are not clearly unreasonable as a matter of law." *Foster v. Board of Regents of University of Michigan*, No. 19-1314, at *9 (6th Cir. Dec. 11, 2020) (en banc). Indeed, *Hall v. Millersville University*, No. 19-3275 (3d Cir. Jan. 11, 2022), makes clear that a university's failure to forward sexual misconduct reports to the Title IX Coordinator can itself constitute a clearly unreasonable response.

Here, the University's response was not merely ineffective or belated—it was a complete absence of any meaningful support during the critical 45-day period from June 30 to August 14, 2025. During this entire period, the University offered Plaintiff zero supportive measures. Exhibit A; Statement of Undisputed Material Facts ¶ 8. Title IX Coordinator Simpson performed none of the five mandatory obligations under 34 C.F.R. § 106.44(a): she did not promptly contact Plaintiff to discuss supportive measures, did not consider Plaintiff's wishes, did not inform Plaintiff of the availability of supportive measures, and did not explain the process for filing a formal complaint. Statement of Undisputed Material Facts ¶ 10; Exhibits B-5, B-6.

The University's response consisted exclusively of three categories of non-action: (1) requests to schedule meetings; (2) directives to "engage with" other offices; and (3) jurisdictional disclaimers. Exhibits B-0 through B-6. This pattern is precisely what *Hall* condemned—a "filing away" of complaints without action. *Hall*, slip op. at 15 (describing how university officials "looked over [the] report and then filed it away" and "did not forward [the] report"). Here, Dean Wasiliko did not forward Plaintiff's June 30 report to Title IX. Exhibit B-0 (Wasilko response states "it doesn't sound like a code of conduct violation" and suggests only a police report). When Title IX Coordinator Simpson finally received the report, she spent weeks demanding meetings (Exhibits B-1, B-2, B-3) before reversing her own jurisdictional determination in 24 hours based on no new factual information (Exhibits B-5, B-6; Statement of Undisputed Material Facts ¶ 7).

The University's refusal to provide housing support is particularly egregious given that it had available housing resources. Exhibit G (Wasilko Housing Denial, July 24, 2025: "I heard back from housing, and it looks like right now the only rooms we have are in the first year buildings. Obviously, that is not helpful at this juncture. However, we will continue to work on this and see if we can find something."). Despite acknowledging available housing, the University never provided it. Exhibit A. Meanwhile, Plaintiff's bank account fell to $16.65 on July 25 and negative $2.55 on August 12, 2025. Statement of Undisputed Material Facts ¶ 11. Medical documentation confirms Plaintiff "reports homelessness since June 30th which has been very stressful and likely worsens his mental health conditions." Exhibit F (Treating Physician Medical Letter, October 1, 2025).

The University's response stands in stark contrast to the response deemed reasonable in *McAvoy v. Dickinson College*, No. 23-2939 (3d Cir. Aug. 16, 2024), where the college issued a no-contact directive, provided academic and mental health accommodations, conducted an investigation, and imposed probation. Here, the University provided nothing—no no-contact order, no housing assistance, no academic modifications, no safety plan. The University cannot rely on the fact that it offered meetings, because meetings are not supportive measures. The Title IX training materials themselves identify supportive measures as "non-disciplinary, non-punitive, individualized" services such as "work or class schedule modifications," "mutual no contact orders," "changes in housing or work locations," and "leaves of absence." Exhibit Z-c at Slides 38-40. Conditioning these measures on Plaintiff's willingness to submit to further investigatory meetings—while Plaintiff was homeless and without funds—is itself clearly unreasonable.

Most tellingly, Simpson's jurisdictional reversal on August 14, 2025—issued 19 hours after she acknowledged the report warranted "further information gathering and review" and stated she could "discuss your current needs relating to supportive measures" (Exhibit B-5)—was based on no new factual information about the underlying complaint. Statement of Undisputed Material Facts ¶ 7. The only intervening events were Plaintiff's three emails consisting "entirely of protected activity under § 106.71" (id.), in which Plaintiff refused a meeting, demanded written communications, and restored the Office for Civil Rights to the email chain. Exhibits B-5A, B-5B, B-5C. Simpson then not only dismissed the complaint but also failed to provide appeal rights as required by § 106.45(b)(8) and failed to send simultaneous written notice to the respondent as required by § 106.45(b)(3)(iii). Statement of Undisputed Material Facts ¶ 9. This combination of procedural irregularities and unexplained jurisdictional reversal supports a finding of deliberate indifference.

**III. The University's Response Was Motivated, at Least in Part, by Plaintiff's Gender and His Protected Activity**

*Price Waterhouse* holds that when a plaintiff proves gender played a "motivating part" in an adverse decision, "the defendant may avoid a finding of liability only by proving by a preponderance of the evidence that it would have made the same decision even if it had not taken the plaintiff's gender into account." 490 U.S. at 258. Here, Plaintiff explicitly raised the gender question on July 23, 2025, asking Dean Wasilko whether his response "would have been the same had the genders in this situation been reversed." Exhibit B-0A. The record contains no evidence that the University ever addressed this question.

Furthermore, the University's response—or lack thereof—can be understood as reflecting sex stereotyping. The harasser was female, Plaintiff was male, and the University dismissed the conduct as not rising to the level of a "conduct violation" (Exhibit B-0) and ultimately determined, even if proven true, the conduct "does not meet TAP 61, nor TAP 31's definitions of prohibited conduct" (Exhibit B-6). The inference that a female complainant reporting male-on-female harassment would have received a different response is supported by the totality of the circumstances.

Additionally, Plaintiff's protected activity—demanding Title IX compliance—was met with retaliation. *Atkinson v. Lafayette College*, 460 F.3d 447, 449 (3d Cir. 2006) ("Demanding compliance with Title IX is protected activity"). On August 15, 2025, immediately after Plaintiff's protected emails refusing meetings and demanding written process, Dr. Sawa threatened Plaintiff with conduct charges for "sending mass emails to University officials" and stated "you must stop emailing me." Exhibit B-7. Plaintiff correctly identified this as retaliatory under 34 C.F.R. § 106.71. Exhibit I (Mullins response, August 25, 2025). The three-week limit on remote participation imposed by Dr. Selcer (Exhibit I, August 25, 2025) was, as Plaintiff argued, "a countdown clock designed to coerce compliance or force attrition—a textbook example of retaliation masquerading as accommodation." Exhibit I-1 (September 2, 2025). The Chair's subsequent removal of Plaintiff's Zoom access after a Temporary Restraining Order hearing where he had promised to continue it further confirms a pattern of retaliatory conduct. Exhibits Q, V, W.

Under *Kachmar v. SunGard Data Systems, Inc.*, 109 F.3d 173, 177 (3d Cir. 1997), "circumstantial evidence of a 'pattern of antagonism' following the protected conduct can also give rise to the inference" of causation. Here, the pattern is unmistakable: every time Plaintiff asserted his Title IX rights, the University escalated its demands for meetings (Exhibits B-2, B-3), changed its jurisdictional position (Exhibit B-6), threatened conduct charges (Exhibit B-7), or imposed additional conditions on his continued academic participation (Exhibit I).

**IV. Plaintiff Has Suffered a Denial of Equal Educational Access**

To establish liability, Plaintiff must show the harassment was "so severe, pervasive, and objectively offensive that it can be said to deprive the victims of access to the educational opportunities or benefits provided by the school." *Davis*, 526 U.S. at 650. The record demonstrates that Plaintiff has been deprived of access in multiple ways: he was forced to flee his home and became homeless (Exhibit A); he was unable to participate in in-person classes for extended periods (Exhibit I); his academic work was severely derailed (Exhibit C, External Faculty Witness Declaration: "his education and research have been severely derailed by his housing instability"); his mental health deteriorated as his medical conditions worsened (Exhibit F, October 1, 2025: "worsening his mental health conditions is expected to negatively impact his graduate education"); and his physical safety on campus remained unaddressed (Exhibit C, Supplemental External Faculty Witness Declaration describing September 17, 2025 encounter where harasser waited outside Plaintiff's classroom).
