## ARGUMENT

### I. Duquesne University Had Actual Knowledge of Actionable Sexual Harassment

A Title IX deliberate indifference claim requires that a funding recipient have “actual knowledge” of sexual harassment. *Davis v. Monroe County Bd. of Educ.*, 526 U.S. 629, 650 (1999). Actual knowledge exists when notice is provided to “an official of the recipient who has authority to institute corrective measures on behalf of the recipient.” *Id.* at 642; *see also* Exhibit Z-e at Slide 30 (defining actual knowledge under 34 C.F.R. § 106.30). Duquesne’s own training materials confirm that responsible employees with such authority include Deans of Students and Title IX Coordinators. *Id.* at Slides 31–32.

Here, Plaintiff David Mullins provided actual knowledge of harassment to two separate officials vested with corrective authority. First, on June 30, 2025, Plaintiff reported to Dean of Students Dr. Adam Wasilko that a fellow graduate student had aggressively pursued him through the apartment, yelled at him within inches of his face, confirmed she was following him, and threatened to follow him to his bedroom—forcing Plaintiff to lock himself in his room for safety. Exhibit B-0. Dean Wasilko is the Associate Vice President for Student Life and Dean of Students, an official with “authority to institute corrective measures” under any definition. *See* Exhibit Z-e at Slide 32 (identifying Dean of Students as official with actual knowledge). Second, on July 24, 2025, Plaintiff reported the same conduct to Title IX Coordinator Alicia Simpson, who specifically acknowledged receipt of “alleged incidents of alleged sexual harassment or sexual misconduct” and confirmed that her office “provides oversight to the University processes for Title IX sexual harassment and non-Title IX sexual misconduct.” Exhibit B-1. Simpson also acknowledged that Plaintiff had reported being “displaced from your housing” as a result. *Id.*

Notice to either official satisfies the actual knowledge requirement. Under *Hall v. Millersville University*, No. 19-3275 (3d Cir. Jan. 11, 2022), a funding recipient has adequate notice when a report of sexual misconduct is made to a person with authority to act, and failure to forward that report or act upon it can itself constitute a clearly unreasonable response. Here, both Dean Wasilko and Title IX Coordinator Simpson had actual knowledge of the harassment from the outset. Additional reports followed, including a detailed timeline of stalking and threats (Exhibit D), a physician’s letter documenting reasonable safety fears (Exhibit F), and an email highlighting the harasser’s statements about firearms (Exhibit R). By no later than July 24, 2025, Duquesne indisputably had actual knowledge.

### II. Duquesne’s Response Was Clearly Unreasonable and Constituted Deliberate Indifference

A funding recipient is deliberately indifferent when its “response to the harassment or lack thereof is clearly unreasonable in light of the known circumstances.” *Davis*, 526 U.S. at 648; *see also Foster v. Bd. of Regents of Univ. of Mich.*, No. 19-1314 (6th Cir. Dec. 11, 2020) (en banc) (adopting same standard). The “clearly unreasonable” standard does not require courts to conclude that “minimal, ineffective, or belated efforts to respond to sexual harassment are not clearly unreasonable as a matter of law.” *Foster*, slip op. at *10 (emphasis in original). Rather, a school may be liable where it “knew its initial response was ineffective” and failed to act reasonably. *Id.* at *11.

Duquesne’s response was not merely slow or imperfect—it was entirely absent. Between June 30, 2025 (Exhibit B-0) and August 14, 2025 (Exhibit B-6)—a period of 45 days—Duquesne offered Plaintiff no supportive measures, no emergency housing, no no-contact order, and no meaningful assistance. See Statement of Undisputed Facts ¶ 8. The university’s communications consisted exclusively of requests to schedule meetings, directives to “engage with” other offices, and a final jurisdictional disclaimer that disclaimed any responsibility. *Id.*; Exhibits B-1, B-2, B-5, B-6.

Duquesne’s own Title IX policies and training materials define supportive measures as mandatory, non-disciplinary, individualized services that must be provided “promptly” and “at any time after a report.” Exhibit Z-c at Slides 38–40 (supportive measures “available at any time after a report of sexual harassment” and include housing changes, no-contact orders, and class modifications). The Title IX Coordinator is “responsible for coordinating the effective implementation of supportive measures.” *Id.* at Slide 38. Yet Simpson performed “zero of the five mandatory obligations under § 106.44(a)”: she did not promptly contact Plaintiff to discuss supportive measures, did not consider his wishes, did not inform him of available measures, and did not explain the process for filing a formal complaint. Statement of Undisputed Facts ¶ 10. Instead, Simpson conditioned any assistance on Plaintiff attending a meeting—a condition she maintained even after Plaintiff expressly stated he could not attend meetings due to safety concerns and requested written-only communications. Exhibits B-1, B-5, B-5A.

The sequence of events on August 13–14, 2025, is particularly damning. On August 13 at 2:02 PM, Simpson acknowledged that Plaintiff’s “concerns and allegations warrant further information gathering and review” and offered to “discuss your current needs relating to supportive measures.” Exhibit B-5. Then, after Plaintiff sent three emails consisting entirely of protected activity—citing Title IX regulations, restoring OCR to the CC line, and requesting written responses—Simpson reversed her jurisdictional determination at 9:42 AM the next day, claiming “the Title IX Office does not have jurisdiction over your report and cannot take action or provide assistance as requested.” Exhibit B-6. No new facts about the underlying complaint entered the record between the two emails. Statement of Undisputed Facts ¶ 7. The only intervening events were Plaintiff’s protected communications. *Id.* This temporal proximity between Plaintiff’s assertion of Title IX rights and the university’s abrupt withdrawal of any assistance supports an inference of retaliation and confirms that the university’s response was not made in good faith.
