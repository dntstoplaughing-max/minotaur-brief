**ARGUMENT**

**I. Applicable Legal Standard**

Under the Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA), 42 U.S.C. § 12132, and Section 504 of the Rehabilitation Act, 29 U.S.C. § 794, a plaintiff must prove: (1) he is a qualified individual with a disability; (2) the defendant knew of his disability and his need for an accommodation; (3) he requested a reasonable accommodation; (4) the defendant denied that accommodation; and (5) the denial caused him harm. In *A.J.T. v. Osseo Area Schools*, 605 U.S. ___ (2025), the Supreme Court expressly held that student ADA and Section 504 claims “use the same standard as claims in other settings” and rejected any heightened “bad faith or gross misjudgment” requirement. *Osseo*, 605 U.S. at ___. That holding governs this case. Defendant Duquesne University of the Holy Spirit receives federal financial assistance and is undisputedly subject to Section 504. (SAC ¶ 2.)

**II. Plaintiff Is a Qualified Individual With a Disability**

Plaintiff David Mullins was at all relevant times an enrolled graduate student in good academic standing with no disciplinary record. Selcer testified at the TRO hearing that Plaintiff “had no performance issues.” (TRO Tr. at *:*.) Plaintiff’s treating physician, Treating Physician, diagnosed him with ADHD and depression—both recognized disabilities under the ADA and Section 504. (Exhibit F, Oct. 1, 2025 letter: “I have diagnosed David with ADHD and depression. I am treating those conditions.”) Treating Physician further opined that Plaintiff’s condition “likely worsens his mental health conditions” and that “worsening his mental health conditions is expected to negatively impact his graduate education.” (*Id.*) The physician also confirmed that Plaintiff’s fear of a specific individual on campus is “reasonable” and that proximity to that individual “would cause him a great deal of stress, would be highly likely to worsen his medical condition, and could impair his academic functioning.” (Exhibit F, Aug. 13, 2025 letter.)

These medical facts establish that Plaintiff suffers from mental impairments that substantially limit major life activities such as learning, concentrating, and interacting with others. He is therefore a “qualified individual with a disability” within the meaning of the ADA and Section 504.

**III. Defendant Had Notice of Plaintiff’s Disability and Need for Accommodation**

On August 13, 2025, Plaintiff provided Defendant with Treating Physician’s medical letter stating that Plaintiff “does not feel safe around a specific individual on campus” and recommending that “every effort be made to avoid” sharing space with that individual. (Exhibit F, Aug. 13 letter.) On October 1, 2025, Plaintiff provided a second letter in which Treating Physician reiterated that Plaintiff suffers from ADHD and depression, that homelessness and proximity to his abuser exacerbate those conditions, and that “all measures to protect him from his abuser and to provide stable housing be made.” (Exhibit F, Oct. 1 letter.)

Defendant’s chair, Dr. Daniel Selcer, acknowledged receipt of this medical information. In his August 25, 2025 email, Selcer wrote: “You explained that you do not feel safe on campus because you fear Respondent.” (Exhibit I.) Selcer acknowledged that Plaintiff had “requested” safety measures and that Plaintiff sought remote attendance. (Exhibit I.) Thus, Defendant knew of Plaintiff’s disability and his need for a remote attendance accommodation. See *Taylor v. Phoenixville School District*, 184 F.3d 296, 315 (3d Cir. 1999) (employer who “received adequate notice of an employee’s disability and desire for accommodations” cannot fail to engage in the interactive process).

**IV. Plaintiff Specifically Requested a Reasonable Accommodation — Zoom Access for Class Participation**

Plaintiff explicitly requested remote Zoom access to attend his graduate seminars as a medically necessary accommodation. (Exhibit I; Exhibit I-1; Exhibit Q.) That request was reasonable. Zoom attendance was already in active use in the same Philosophy Department for comparable graduate coursework. Faculty D’s Spring 2025 syllabus for PHIL 520–420W states: “Although we will meet in person, I will also keep a Zoom meeting running, so if you are not feeling well, please attend that session remotely!” (Exhibit N.) No university or departmental policy prohibits individual student Zoom access. The Faculty Handbook 2025-2026 governs faculty course delivery modality, not individual student attendance accommodations. (Faculty Handbook, pp. 10-11; SJ 7.3.) And Selcer himself admitted that Zoom was “possible” for Plaintiff’s classes because “room and class size permits remote participation.” (Exhibit I.)

Thus, the accommodation was not only reasonable but was already implemented by the same department for other students and for one-off health needs. It would not fundamentally alter the nature of the program. *See Oross v. Kutztown University*, No. 21-5032 (E.D. Pa. July 25, 2023) (finding accommodation request was “dead on arrival” where university applied blanket no-remote policy without individualized assessment).

**V. Defendant Denied the Requested Accommodation and Failed to Engage in the Interactive Process**

Instead of granting Plaintiff’s reasonable request for indefinite Zoom access, Defendant imposed a conditional, time-limited measure designed to expire. On August 25, 2025, Selcer authorized remote attendance “only for the first three weeks of class,” subject to conditions that Plaintiff “fully engag[e] with the Title IX, Student Conduct, and Public Safety offices.” (Exhibit I.) Selcer’s September 3, 2025 directive made the conditional nature explicit: “This is my final directive . . . Until September 12, you may continue to attend your seminars via Zoom if you wish.” (Exhibit J.) Then, on October 17, 2025, Selcer terminated Plaintiff’s Zoom access entirely, claiming that Professor Faculty C “did not identify a compelling reason to continue it.” (Exhibit V.) But Faculty C had already authorized Zoom on October 6, 2025, writing: “Yes, Zooming in tomorrow would work fine.” (Exhibit W.) Selcer went behind Faculty C’s back, reframed the authorization as a “compelling reason” test, and then reported a negative result. (Exhibit Q, pattern summary.)

These actions constitute a clear denial of a reasonable accommodation. Moreover, Defendant never conducted an individualized assessment of Plaintiff’s specific limitations or possible accommodations. (SJ 7.2: “No interactive process was conducted.”) Selcer testified under oath that his objection to Zoom was based on personal preference, citing “divides attention” and a post-COVID departmental finding. Yet he “could not identify any written policy, pedagogical study, or departmental standard supporting denial.” (TRO Tr. at *:*.) Another student, Colleague A, separately requested Zoom access from Selcer, and he denied it, “telling her he ‘can’t explain why’ Zoom was not available.” (Exhibit L.) This demonstrates a pattern of denying remote accommodations with no articulable basis.

Under *Taylor v. Phoenixville School District*, 184 F.3d at 315, a defendant who “has received adequate notice of an employee’s disability and desire for accommodations, cannot fail to engage the employee in the interactive process…. [A disabled person] must demonstrate: 1) the employer knew about the employee’s disability; 2) the employee requested accommodations …; 3) the employer did not make a good faith effort to assist the employee in seeking accommodations; and 4) the employee could have been reasonably accommodated but for the employer’s lack of good faith.” All four elements are satisfied here. Defendant knew of Plaintiff’s disability and request (Elements 1 and 2). Defendant “did not assess Plaintiff’s specific limitations, evaluate possible accommodations, discuss alternatives with Plaintiff, or involve disability services” (SJ 7.2) — this is the antithesis of a good faith effort (Element 3). And Plaintiff could have been accommodated through Zoom, which was already in use in the department — “but for [Defendant’s] lack of good faith” (Element 4). *Taylor* further holds that “an employer who acts in bad faith in the interactive process will be liable if the jury can reasonably conclude that the employee would have been able to perform the job with accommodations.” *Id.* at 317.

*Oross v. Kutztown University* is directly on point. There, the court granted summary judgment for the plaintiff where the “university applied blanket no-remote policy with no written basis, no individualized assessment of plaintiff’s request,” and the “accommodation request was ‘dead on arrival’ — interference with the interactive process established.” *Oross*, No. 21-5032, slip op. at 12-13. Here, Defendant similarly applied an ad hoc, unwritten policy against remote attendance, never conducted an individualized assessment, and made the accommodation conditional and time-limited, effectively rendering it “dead on arrival.”

**VI. Defendant’s Denial Caused Plaintiff Harm**

The denial of Zoom access caused immediate and ongoing harm to Plaintiff’s education. After Defendant terminated his remote access on October 17, 2025, Plaintiff could not attend his graduate seminars. (Exhibit Q, Oct. 16 email: “I attempted to join class through the usual Zoom link, but no meeting was available.”) Plaintiff’s academic mentor, External Faculty Witness, declared that Plaintiff’s “education and research have been severely derailed” and that he “cannot focus on his PhD research when he is experiencing encounters that cause this level of distress.” (Exhibit C, External Faculty Witness declarations.) Treating Physician attested that worsening of Plaintiff’s mental health conditions, caused by the lack of protective measures, “is expected to negatively impact his graduate education.” (Exhibit F, Oct. 1 letter.)

The harm is not hypothetical. Plaintiff was forced to choose between his physical safety and his education. Defendant’s refusal to provide a reasonable accommodation directly impaired Plaintiff’s ability to participate in and benefit from his academic program. This constitutes the quintessential harm that the ADA and Section 504 are designed to prevent.

**VII. No Heightened Standard Applies**

Defendant may attempt to argue that a heightened “bad faith or gross misjudgment” standard applies in the educational setting. *Osseo* squarely forecloses that argument: “Schoolchildren bringing ADA and Rehabilitation Act claims … are not required to make a heightened showing of ‘bad faith or gross misjudgment’ but instead are subject to the same standards that apply in other disability discrimination contexts.” *Osseo*, 605 U.S. at ___. The same reasoning applies to graduate students. Plaintiff need only prove the ordinary elements of a failure-to-accommodate claim, which he has amply done here.

**CONCLUSION**

For the foregoing reasons, Plaintiff has established each element of his failure-to-accommodate claim under Section 504 and the ADA. Defendant had notice of Plaintiff’s disability, received a specific request for a reasonable accommodation, denied that accommodation through a conditional time-limited measure and subsequent removal, failed to engage in any interactive process, and caused Plaintiff significant educational harm. The Court should accordingly find Defendant liable on Count III.
