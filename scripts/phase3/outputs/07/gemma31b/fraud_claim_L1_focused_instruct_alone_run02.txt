**ARGUMENT**

Defendants’ Motion for Summary Judgment must be denied because genuine disputes of material fact exist regarding every element of Plaintiff’s fraud claim. Under Pennsylvania law, fraud requires: (1) a representation; (2) which is material to the transaction; (3) made falsely, with knowledge of its falsity or recklessness; (4) with the intent of misleading another into relying on it; (5) justifiable reliance; and (6) resulting injury proximately caused by the reliance. *Gibbs v. Ernst*, 538 Pa. 193, 647 A.2d 882 (1994).

**I. Defendants’ Fabricated “Meeting Requirement” Constitutes a Material Misrepresentation Made with Reckless Disregard for the Truth.**

Defendants argue that their actions were consistent with policy or intended to help. However, the evidence demonstrates a systematic campaign of misrepresentation. Between June 30 and August 14, 2025, four defendants made 36 nearly identical false statements claiming that meeting attendance was a condition for receiving Title IX supportive measures (Fact 1). This is contradicted by the plain language of 34 C.F.R. Part 106 and TAP 61, neither of which conditions supportive measures on meetings (Fact 1).

The materiality of these statements is clear: they were designed to influence Plaintiff’s access to safety and housing. Under *United States ex rel. Moore & Co. v. Majestic Blue Fisheries, LLC*, materiality is satisfied when a statement has a "natural tendency to influence" a decision. 812 F.3d 294 (3d Cir. 2016). Here, Dr. Wasilko explicitly stated, “We cannot assist you or provide you with further guidance unless you are willing to engage with the appropriate offices” (Ex. B-2). By conditioning all university assistance on a non-existent policy requirement, Defendants materially misled Plaintiff.

Furthermore, the "scienter" element is satisfied because Defendants acted with "reckless disregard of the truth." *Moore*, 812 F.3d at 294. This is evidenced by the fact that Alicia Simpson testified under oath that she made her jurisdictional determination "entirely on the written record" with "no meeting," admitting she "could have emailed" Plaintiff for clarification but "chose not to" (Fact 5). This admission directly contradicts the narrative pushed in Exhibits B-1 through B-5 that meetings were "necessary" and "essential" (Ex. B-5). Such a contradiction creates a genuine dispute of material fact as to whether Defendants knowingly fabricated the meeting requirement to obstruct Plaintiff’s access to supportive measures.

**II. Dean Blair’s TAP 33 Analysis and Nondisclosure Constitute Culpable Misrepresentation.**

Defendants contend that Dean Blair’s determination that Plaintiff was not a “Covered Party” under TAP 33 was a correct application of policy. However, the process by which Blair reached this conclusion creates a strong inference of fraud. 

First, Blair admitted that in preparing her response to Plaintiff’s conflict-of-interest disclosure, she "did need to consult Dr. Selcer as instructor" (Ex. K-2). Dr. Selcer was the very party whose conflict was being questioned and a named defendant in Plaintiff's federal litigation. This action directly violates the University’s own training on avoiding bias and conflicts of interest, which mandates that the Title IX Team "identify and avoid... Conflicts of interest" and "avoid leading questions" (Ex. Z-d, Slides 43, 45). Specifically, the training warns that a conflict exists where there are "power dynamics" and that determinations should be based on "objective facts" (Ex. Z-d, Slides 47-48). Consulting the conflicted party to determine his own conflict is the antithesis of an objective determination.

Second, Defendants argue that Blair’s silence regarding the August 25 email was not actionable. This ignores Pennsylvania law. Where a party is the "natural and proper source of information," nondisclosure of a material fact is equivalent to a fraudulent misrepresentation. *Neuman v. Corn Exchange National Bank & Trust Co.*, 356 Pa. 442, 51 A.2d 759 (1947). As the Dean of the College of Liberal Arts, Blair was the natural and proper source for the interpretation of TAP 33. Under *Duquesne Light Co. v. Westinghouse Electric Corp.*, 66 F.3d 604 (3d Cir. 1995), the "duty to investigate" does not apply when the defendant is the proper source of information. Furthermore, the "concealment of a material fact can amount to a culpable misrepresentation no less than does an intentional false statement." *Moser v. DeSetta*, 589 A.2d 679 (Pa. Super. 1991).

**III. Plaintiff’s Reliance Was Justifiable and Presumed from Materiality.**

Defendants argue that Plaintiff admitted he "immediately recognized" Blair’s TAP 33 reading was wrong and therefore did not rely on it. This argument is a red herring. Reliance is not limited to a single interaction but extends to the overall transactional relationship with the University.

Under *Peerless Wall & Window Coverings, Inc. v. Synchronics, Inc.*, reliance is "presumed from materiality where a party would not have entered the transaction had the truth been known." 85 F. Supp. 2d 519 (W.D. Pa. 2000). Plaintiff would not have remained in a required seminar under a conflicted instructor—knowing that his safety and academic standing were at risk—had the University provided a truthful, independent analysis of TAP 33 rather than a joint determination made by the Dean and the conflicted professor (Ex. K-2).

Moreover, the Defendants' claim that Plaintiff's allegations are "internally inconsistent" because he alleges fraud both when they responded (Selcer) and when they did not (Blair) is legally baseless. These are distinct acts of misrepresentation. Selcer’s response on August 25 was a "falsified meeting summary" that imposed a baseless "three-week Zoom countdown" (Fact 7, Ex. I). Blair’s failure to act on the COI disclosure was a separate concealment of a material policy violation. Both are actionable under *Moser* and *Neuman*.

**IV. Academic Deference Does Not Shield Fraudulent Conduct.**

Defendants rely on *Jain v. Carnegie Mellon Univ.* to argue for deference to academic judgment. However, academic deference applies to pedagogical and professional judgments, not to the fabrication of federal compliance requirements or the fraudulent concealment of conflicts of interest. The determination of whether a student is a "Covered Party" under TAP 33 or whether supportive measures are conditioned on meetings under 34 C.F.R. § 106.44(a) are questions of law and policy, not academic discretion.

**V. The Evidence Rebuts the "Intent to Help" Defense.**

Defendants point to the Blair-Selcer consultation as evidence of an "intent to help." On the contrary, the evidence suggests an intent to deceive. The consultation between the Dean and the conflicted party (Ex. K-2) occurred seven days after that party had been cross-examined under oath (Fact 8). This suggests a coordinated effort to maintain a false narrative regarding the conflict of interest. When combined with the fact that four defendants systematically removed the Office for Civil Rights (OCR) from correspondence to conceal the false meeting-requirement narrative from federal oversight (Fact 6, Ex. B-3 through B-7), the "intent to help" argument is belied by a pattern of deceptive conduct.

Because there are genuine disputes of material fact regarding the falsity of the representations, the scienter of the Defendants, and the justifiability of Plaintiff's reliance, summary judgment is improper.
