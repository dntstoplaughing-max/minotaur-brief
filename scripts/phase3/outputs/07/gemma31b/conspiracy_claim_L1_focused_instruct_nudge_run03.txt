**ARGUMENT**

Defendants are not entitled to summary judgment because genuine disputes of material fact exist regarding every element of Plaintiff’s civil conspiracy claim. Under Pennsylvania law, a civil conspiracy requires (1) a combination or agreement between two or more persons (2) with the intent to do an unlawful act or to do a lawful act by unlawful means, (3) involving overt acts in furtherance of the conspiracy, (4) motivated by malice (intent to injure), (5) resulting in harm. *Thompson Coal Co. v. Pike Coal Co.*, 488 Pa. 198, 412 A.2d 466, 472-73 (1979). Once a defendant joins a conspiracy, they are jointly and severally liable for all tortious acts committed by any co-conspirator in furtherance of that conspiracy. *Id.*

**I. Dean A’s consultation with Dr. Selcer was not "routine administration" but a violation of University policy and evidence of an unlawful agreement.**

Defendants argue that Dean A’s consultation with Dr. Selcer was a routine administrative necessity to check course availability. (Dkt. 34 at 10). This characterization is contradicted by the record. 

First, the subject of the consultation was a conflict-of-interest disclosure concerning Dr. Selcer himself. (Counter-Statement Fact 3). TAP 33 § IV.4 explicitly requires an *independent* review of such disclosures. (TAP 33 § IV.4). By her own admission, Dean A did not conduct an independent review; instead, she "did need to consult Dr. Selcer" and subsequently announced that "Both Dr. Selcer and I strongly concur" on the determination. (Exhibit K-2). This is not routine administration; it is a failure to adhere to the mandatory independence required by TAP 33 § IV.4, creating a genuine dispute as to whether Dean A agreed to bypass university policy to protect a co-conspirator. (Counter-Statement Fact 4).

**II. The timing and nature of the consultation evidence malice, not an "intent to help."**

Defendants contend the consultation resulted in assurances of fair treatment, proving a lack of malice. (Dkt. 34 at 10). However, the timing of this "coordination" suggests a retaliatory motive. Dean A’s joint determination with Selcer occurred on October 22, 2025—exactly seven days after Plaintiff cross-examined Selcer under oath at the TRO hearing on October 15, 2025. (Exhibit K-2; TRO Tr.; Counter-Statement Fact 3). The "support" offered was conditional upon Plaintiff returning to a classroom environment with the very individual who was the subject of the conflict-of-interest disclosure. This sequence of events creates a triable issue of fact regarding whether the "concurrence" between Dean A and Selcer was intended to injure Plaintiff by forcing him into a hostile environment as retaliation for his legal actions.

**III. An underlying tort exists, and Dean A is liable for the acts of her co-conspirators under *Thompson Coal*.**

Defendants argue that no underlying tort exists because Dean A’s analysis of TAP 33 was "correct." (Dkt. 34 at 10). This argument fails on two grounds. First, as noted above, any analysis that involves "consulting" the conflicted party is inherently incorrect under the "independent review" mandate of TAP 33 § IV.4. (Exhibit K-2; TAP 33 § IV.4).

Second, under *Thompson Coal*, a co-conspirator is liable for *all* tortious acts committed by any member of the conspiracy in furtherance of their common goal. 412 A.2d at 472-73. Even if Defendants dispute Dean A's individual role in specific acts, she is liable for the overt acts of her co-conspirators, including:
*   The fabrication of 36 false statements regarding meeting requirements;
*   The systematic removal of OCR and senior administrative oversight from correspondence to prevent corrective intervention (Exhibits B-3 through B-7); and
*   Dr. Selcer’s fabrication of "performance issues" to threaten Plaintiff’s stipend (Exhibits X-6, X-8). (Counter-Statement Fact 9).

**IV. The coordinated institutional responses were not "university processes" but a concerted effort to conceal and isolate.**

Defendants claim that Plaintiff’s characterization of their actions as a "conspiracy" is merely a refusal to engage with university processes. (Dkt. 25 at 6). The evidence demonstrates that the "process" was designed to exclude oversight. Four defendants systematically removed OCR and senior administration from critical emails. (Counter-Statement Fact 7). Specifically, Dr. Selcer removed Dean A herself from the "final directive" on September 3, 2025. (Selcer Sept 3 email). 

Furthermore, while Defendants claim they wanted Plaintiff to "engage," the record shows that when Plaintiff did escalate concerns directly to Dean A citing federal law and specific violations, Dean A remained silent and failed to intervene. (Exhibits K-2, K-5; Counter-Statement Fact 8). This combination of active coordination with Selcer and strategic silence toward Plaintiff’s legal pleas indicates an agreement to maintain control without independent oversight, rather than a good-faith application of university policy.

**V. Dr. Selcer’s role as instructor was used as a vehicle for retaliatory overt acts.**

Defendants argue that Selcer’s continuation as instructor was not an overt act because he held the role before and after the protected activity. (Dkt. 34 at 10 n.3). This ignores how Selcer utilized that role to further the conspiracy.

First, Selcer used his position to manipulate Plaintiff's access to education. In Exhibit Q, Selcer informed Plaintiff that Dr. Faculty C "did not identify a compelling reason" to continue Zoom access. However, Dr. Faculty C’s own email reveals he was confused by Selcer's framing and was unaware that he had been asked to make a "compelling reason" determination. (Exhibit Q). This demonstrates an overt act of deception to restrict Plaintiff's access.

Second, Selcer used his supervisory role to fabricate threats. On February 10, 2026, Selcer told Plaintiff that Faculty B "doesn't make decisions about your stipend" and used a "collegial tone." (Exhibit X-6). Yet, three days later, Selcer emailed Plaintiff claiming "performance issues raised by two of the three faculty members" and threatened the suspension of Plaintiff's stipend through 2029. (Exhibit X-8). This "performance issue" claim was directly contradicted by Faculty B, who, on the same day, told Plaintiff her "door is always open" and expressed no performance complaints. (Exhibit X-7).

These fabrications and manipulations constitute overt acts in furtherance of a conspiracy to injure Plaintiff's academic standing and financial stability. Because these facts are in dispute, summary judgment must be denied.
