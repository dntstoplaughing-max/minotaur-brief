**ARGUMENT**

Defendants’ Motion for Summary Judgment on Count IV (Fraud) must be denied because genuine disputes of material fact exist regarding every element of the claim. Under Pennsylvania law, fraud requires: (1) a representation; (2) which is material to the transaction at hand; (3) made falsely, with knowledge of its falsity or recklessness; (4) with the intent of misleading another into relying on it; (5) justifiable reliance; and (6) resulting injury proximately caused by the reliance. *Gibbs v. Ernst*, 538 Pa. 193, 647 A.2d 882 (1994).

**I. Genuine Disputes Exist Regarding the Falsity and Materiality of Dean A’s TAP 33 Analysis**

Defendants contend that Dean A’s determination—that Plaintiff is not a "Covered Party" and that the instructor-student relationship during litigation is not a "Conflict of Interest"—was consistent with the plain language of TAP 33. This is a disputed issue of material fact. 

First, the process by which Dean A reached this conclusion was fundamentally flawed. Dean A admitted that in preparing her response, she "did need to consult Dr. Selcer as instructor" (Exhibit K-2). This means the Dean consulted the very party whose conflict was being questioned—a party who had been cross-examined under oath only seven days prior (Fact 8). 

Second, Plaintiff corrected Dean A within 90 minutes of her determination, providing specific policy citations to demonstrate the error (Fact 9). Despite this, Dean A responded "no change" (Exhibit K-5). Whether this representation was "made falsely, with knowledge of its falsity or recklessness" is a question for the jury. *Gibbs*, 647 A.2d at 882. The fact that the Dean relied on the conflicted party to determine the existence of a conflict creates a genuine dispute as to whether the representation was reckless or intentionally misleading.

**II. Dean A’s Silence Constitutes Actionable Misrepresentation Under Pennsylvania Law**

Defendants argue that Dean A’s silence regarding the August 25 email was not actionable because no fiduciary relationship existed. This argument ignores established Pennsylvania precedent. 

Under *Neuman v. Corn Exchange National Bank & Trust Co.*, 356 Pa. 442, 51 A.2d 759 (1947), "where a party to a transaction is the natural and proper source of information, nondisclosure of a material fact is equivalent to a fraudulent misrepresentation." As the Dean of the College of Liberal Arts, Dean A is the natural and proper source of information regarding the application of University policies (TAP 33) to a student’s academic requirements. 

Furthermore, the "duty to investigate" does not apply where the defendant is the natural and proper source of the information. *Duquesne Light Co. v. Westinghouse Electric Corp.*, 66 F.3d 604 (3d Cir. 1995). By remaining silent or providing false assurances while withholding the correct policy application, Defendants engaged in "culpable misrepresentation." *Moser v. DeSetta*, 589 A.2d 679 (Pa. Super. 1991).

**III. Plaintiff’s Reliance Was Justifiable and Presumed from Materiality**

Defendants claim that because Plaintiff "immediately recognized" the error in the TAP 33 reading, he could not have justifiably relied on it. This is a misstatement of the law. 

In Pennsylvania, "reliance is presumed from materiality where a party would not have entered the transaction had the truth been known." *Peerless Wall & Window Coverings, Inc. v. Synchronics, Inc.*, 85 F. Supp. 2d 519 (W.D. Pa. 2000). The "transaction" here is Plaintiff’s continued enrollment in a required seminar under an adversarial defendant. Had the truth—that a conflict of interest existed under TAP 33—been acknowledged, Plaintiff would not have been forced to remain in a course taught by a named defendant in his own federal lawsuit. 

The fact that Plaintiff recognized the error does not negate the fraud; rather, it highlights the Defendants' scienter. The fraud lies in the Defendants' insistence that "no change" would be made (Exhibit K-5) despite being provided with the correct policy citations, thereby coercing Plaintiff to remain in a conflicted environment to avoid academic failure (Exhibit K-2).

**IV. Plaintiff’s Claims Are Not Internally Inconsistent**

Defendants argue that Plaintiff cannot allege that both responding (Selcer) and not responding (Dean A) constituted fraud. This is a false dichotomy. 

Fraud may be committed through affirmative misrepresentation or through the concealment of material facts. *Moser*, 589 A.2d at 679. Here, Plaintiff alleges two distinct fraudulent schemes:
1. **Affirmative Misrepresentation:** Dr. Selcer issued a "falsified meeting summary" on August 25, misrepresenting agreements and imposing a baseless "Zoom countdown" (Fact 7, Exhibit I).
2. **Culpable Concealment:** Dean A and other administrators used silence and the denial of policy applicability to maintain a status quo that benefited the University and the conflicted instructor (Exhibit K-2, K-5).

These are not inconsistent; they are complementary methods of deception used by different actors to obstruct Plaintiff’s access to education and safety.

**V. The Consultation Between Dean A and Selcer Evidence Intent to Deceive, Not Help**

Defendants point to the consultation between Dean A and Selcer as evidence of an "intent to help." To the contrary, this is evidence of a conspiracy to mislead. 

Consulting the conflicted party (Selcer) about his own conflict—especially after that party had been cross-examined under oath—demonstrates a total abandonment of impartiality. This consultation resulted in a "joint determination" that it was "appropriate" for Plaintiff to remain under the instruction of a defendant in his own lawsuit (Exhibit K-2). Such a determination is not an act of "help," but a calculated effort to maintain a power imbalance and ignore institutional conflict-of-interest policies.

**VI. Academic Deference Does Not Shield Fraudulent Representations of Policy**

Defendants cite *Jain v. Carnegie Mellon Univ.* to argue for deference to academic judgment. However, the issues at hand are not "academic judgments" (such as grading or curriculum), but rather the fraudulent representation of University policy and federal law.

The Defendants fabricated a "meeting requirement" as a condition for receiving Title IX supportive measures (Fact 1, Exhibit B-2, B-5, B-6). No such requirement exists in 34 C.F.R. Part 106 or TAP 61 (Fact 1). The determination that reported stalking did not meet the definition of prohibited conduct "even if proven to be true" (Exhibit B-6) was a legal error, not an academic one (Fact 4). 

Courts do not defer to "professional judgment" when that judgment is used as a cloak for the fraudulent misrepresentation of a student's legal rights or the fabrication of policy requirements to deny federally mandated supportive measures.
