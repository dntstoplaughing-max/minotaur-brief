**ARGUMENT**

Defendants’ Motion for Summary Judgment must be denied because the record presents genuine disputes of material fact regarding every element of Plaintiff’s civil conspiracy claim. Under Pennsylvania law, a civil conspiracy requires (1) a combination or agreement between two or more persons (2) with the intent to do an unlawful act or to do a lawful act by unlawful means, (3) overt acts in furtherance of that agreement, (4) malice, and (5) resulting harm. *Thompson Coal Co. v. Pike Coal Co.*, 488 Pa. 198, 412 A.2d 466, 472-73 (1979). Once a defendant joins a conspiracy, they are jointly and severally liable for all tortious acts committed by any co-conspirator in furtherance of the conspiracy. *Id.*

**I. Dean Blair’s Consultation with Dr. Selcer Was Not “Routine Administration” but an Agreement to Bypass Independent Oversight.**

Defendants argue that Dean Blair’s consultation with Dr. Selcer regarding Plaintiff’s transfer request was “routine academic administration.” (Dkt. 34 at 10). This characterization is belied by the evidence. 

Plaintiff’s request was specifically a conflict-of-interest disclosure regarding Dr. Selcer himself, who was a named defendant in a federal lawsuit against the University. (Exhibit K-1). TAP 33 § IV.4 explicitly requires an *independent* review of conflict-of-interest disclosures. (Counter-Statement ¶ 4). Rather than conducting an independent review, Dean Blair admitted in writing: “I did need to consult Dr. Selcer as instructor... Both Dr. Selcer and I strongly concur that it is appropriate for you to remain enrolled.” (Exhibit K-2). 

This is not “routine administration”; it is a written admission of a joint determination made with the very party whose conflict was being disclosed. By coordinating her decision with the conflicted party, Blair abandoned the independence required by TAP 33, providing direct evidence of an agreement to maintain the status quo regardless of policy or conflict.

**II. The Coordination Between Blair and Selcer Evidenced Malice and a Pattern of Retaliation, Not an “Intent to Help.”**

Defendants contend that the consultation between Blair and Selcer evidenced an intent to provide academic support. (Dkt. 34 at 10). However, the timing and subsequent actions of the parties demonstrate a malicious intent to fabricate a record of failure to justify future adverse actions.

On February 10, 2026, Dr. Selcer met with Plaintiff and maintained a collegial tone, explicitly stating that Faculty B “doesn’t make decisions about your stipend.” (Exhibit X-6). Yet, only three days later, on February 13, 2026, Selcer issued a written threat to suspend Plaintiff’s stipend through 2029, citing “performance issues raised by two of the three faculty members.” (Exhibit X-8). Crucially, Selcer’s February 13 email referenced a “recommendation to the Dean of Liberal Arts”—Dean Blair’s exact title. (Exhibit X-8). 

Because the “performance issues” language was entirely absent from the February 10 meeting (Exhibit X-6) and contradicted by Faculty B’s own contemporaneous email—which requested reassignment but expressed no performance complaints and noted her “door is always open” (Exhibit X-7)—the intervening communication between Selcer and Blair is evidence of a coordinated effort to fabricate a pretext for financial retaliation. This “convincing mosaic” of circumstantial evidence is sufficient to establish a causal link to retaliatory animus. *Canada v. Samuel Grossi & Sons, Inc.*, No. 20-2747 (3d Cir. 2022).

**III. Genuine Disputes Exist Regarding the Underlying Torts, Including Title IX Violations and Fraud.**

Defendants argue that no underlying tort exists because Blair’s TAP 33 analysis was correct. (Dkt. 34 at 10). This argument fails for two reasons. First, under *Thompson Coal*, Blair is liable for the torts of her co-conspirators even if she did not personally commit every act. 412 A.2d at 472-73. Second, the record establishes multiple underlying torts:

*   **Deliberate Indifference (Title IX):** Defendants had actual knowledge of Plaintiff’s safety fears and homelessness. (Exhibits R, S). Despite this, they failed to provide mandated supportive measures, such as a no-contact order or stable housing, which are “non-disciplinary” and “non-punitive.” (Exhibit Z-c). Instead, they conditioned help on meetings that Plaintiff reasonably feared. (Exhibit B-7). A response is “clearly unreasonable” when the institution acts with deliberate indifference to known harassment that denies access to educational opportunities. *Davis v. Monroe County Board of Education*, 526 U.S. 629 (1999).
*   **Fraud/Misrepresentation:** Dr. Selcer’s fabrication of “performance issues” to threaten Plaintiff’s stipend (Exhibit X-8), while simultaneously knowing that Faculty B had raised no such issues (Exhibit X-7), constitutes a material misrepresentation intended to mislead and injure. *Gibbs v. Ernst*, 538 Pa. 193, 647 A.2d 882 (1994).

**IV. The Coordinated Institutional Response Was Not “University Process” but a Conspiracy to Obstruct Federal Oversight.**

Defendants characterize the coordinated responses as “well-established university processes.” (Dkt. 25 at 6). However, “process” does not include the systematic removal of the Office for Civil Rights (OCR) and senior leadership from email chains at critical junctures. (Counter-Statement ¶ 7). 

For example, Dr. Selcer removed Dean Blair herself from the September 3 “final directive” email. (Exhibit J; Counter-Statement ¶ 7). This pattern of removing oversight occurred across four different defendants. (Counter-Statement ¶ 7). This is not administration; it is a coordinated effort to prevent reliance victims from receiving corrective information and to shield the conspiracy from federal scrutiny. Such conduct, combined with Dr. Sawa’s threat to initiate conduct charges against Plaintiff for emailing university officials about his safety (Exhibit B-7), demonstrates a malicious agreement to silence the Plaintiff and obstruct justice.

**V. Dr. Selcer’s Role as Instructor Was Used as a Tool for Retaliation and an Overt Act in Furtherance of the Conspiracy.**

Defendants argue that Selcer’s role as instructor was not an overt act because he held the role before and after the protected activity. (Dkt. 34 at 10 n.3). This ignores the *manner* in which that role was used to execute the conspiracy.

The “Zoom removal” sequence (Exhibit Q) proves that Selcer used his authority as instructor to execute a retaliatory strike. After Selcer testified under oath at a TRO hearing that he would continue Zoom access (Exhibit Q), he immediately coordinated with Dr. Faculty C to cut that access. (Exhibit V). Selcer then misrepresented Dr. Faculty C’s position, claiming Faculty C found “no compelling reason” to continue Zoom (Exhibit V), while Faculty C’s own email revealed he was confused and unaware that a formal request for remote attendance due to safety issues had even been made. (Exhibit W).

This manipulation of faculty and the subsequent denial of access—after a court hearing—is a classic “pattern of antagonism” that establishes a causal link to retaliation. *Kachmar v. SunGard Data Systems, Inc.*, 109 F.3d 173 (3d Cir. 1997). The use of the instructor role to lie to the court and coerce the Plaintiff’s return to a hostile environment constitutes an overt act in furtherance of the conspiracy.

Because genuine disputes of material fact exist regarding the agreement, the unlawful means, the overt acts, and the malice of the Defendants, summary judgment must be denied.
